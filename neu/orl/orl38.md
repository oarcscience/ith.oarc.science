<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>To Arles the Child, to Charles Marphisa wends,<br />
To be baptized, with Bradamant for guide.<br />
Astolpho from the holy realm descends;<br />
Through whom with sight the Nubian is supplied:<br />
Agramant’s land he with his troop offends;<br />
But he is of his Africk realm so wide,<br />
With Charles he bargains, that, on either side,<br />
Two knights by strife their quarrel should decide.</em></span></td>
</tr>

<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>Ye courteous ladies, who unto my strain<br />
Kind audience lend—I read it in your cheer—<br />
That good Rogero should depart again<br />
So suddenly, from her that held him dear,<br />
Displeases ye, and scarce inflicts less pain<br />
Than that which Bradamant endured whilere:<br />
I read you also argue, to his shame,<br />
That feebly burned in him the amorous flame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>If from her side for other cause had gone,<br />
Against that lady’s will, the youthful lord;<br />
Though in the hope more treasure to have won<br />
Than swelled rich Croesus’ or rich Crassus’ hoard,<br />
I too should deem the dart, by Cupid thrown,<br />
Had not the heart-core of Rogero gored.<br />
For such a sovereign joy, a prize so high<br />
No silver and no gold could ever buy.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>Yet to preserve our honour not alone<br />
Deserves excuse, it also merits praise:<br />
This to preserve, I say, when to have done<br />
In other wise, might shame and scandal raise;<br />
And had fair Bradamant reluctance shown,<br />
And obstinately interposed delays,<br />
This, as a certain sign, had served to prove<br />
That lady’s little wit or little love.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>For if his life, whom gentle woman loves,<br />
As her own life she values, or before;<br />
(I speak of one at whom young Cupid roves<br />
With arrows which beneath the mantle gore)<br />
His honour to his pleasure it behoves<br />
That woman to prefer, by so much more,<br />
As man beyond his life his honour treasures,<br />
Esteemed by him above all other pleasures.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>His duty good Rogero satisfied,<br />
Following the royal lord with whom he came;<br />
For having no fair cause to quit his side,<br />
He could not leave the Paynim without shame;<br />
And, if his sire had by Almontes died,<br />
In this, King Agramant was not to blame;<br />
Who for his parents’ every past offence<br />
Had made Rogero mighty recompense.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>He will perform his duty to repair<br />
To his liege-lord; so did that martial maid;<br />
Who had not with reiterated prayer<br />
(As so she might have done) Rogero stayed.<br />
The stripling may appay the warlike fair<br />
In other season, if not now appaid;<br />
But twice two hundred years will not atone<br />
The crying sin of honour once foregone.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>To Arles-town whither had his king conveyed<br />
His remnant of a host, he pricked anew;<br />
While they that, since their kindred was displayed,<br />
Had a close friendship formed—the damsels two—<br />
Thither together go where Charles had made<br />
His mightiest effort, with the Christian crew;<br />
Hoping by siege or fight to break the foe,<br />
And free his kingdom form so long a woe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>Bradamant, when she in the camp appeared,<br />
Was greeted with a welcome warm and kind.<br />
On all sides was she hailed, by all was cheered;<br />
And she in this or that her head inclined.<br />
Rinaldo, when he of her coming heard,<br />
Met her; nor young Richardo stayed behind;<br />
Nor Richardet; nor others of her race;<br />
And all received the maid with joyful face.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>When next ’tis known, the second of the twain<br />
Is that Marphisa, so in arms renowned,<br />
Who from Catày unto the bounds of Spain<br />
Had journeyed, with a thousand laurels crowned,<br />
Nor rich nor poor within their tents remain:<br />
The curious crowd, encompassing them round,<br />
Press, harm, and heave each other here and there,<br />
In the sole wish to see so bright a pair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>By them was Charles saluted reverently,<br />
And the first day was this (has Turpin shown)<br />
Marphisa had been seen to bend her knee:<br />
For Pepin’s royal son to her, alone,<br />
Deserving of such duty seemed to be,<br />
Mid emperors or kings that filled a throne,<br />
Baptized or infidel, of all those named<br />
For mighty riches, or for valour famed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>Her kindly Charlemagne received, and wide<br />
Of the pavilions met, in open view;<br />
And, above king, and prince, and peer, beside<br />
Himself the monarch placed that damsel true.<br />
Who go not, are dismist; so none abide<br />
In little time, except the good and few.<br />
The Paladins and lords remain; without,<br />
Is left the unrespected rabble-rout.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>Marphisa first began in grateful strain:<br />
“Unconquered Caesar, glorious and august,<br />
Who, to Alcides’ strait from Indian main,<br />
Mak’st Scythian’s pale and Aethiop’s race adust<br />
Revere thy Christian cross of snowy grain,<br />
—Of earthly monarchs thou most sage and just—<br />
Hither thy glory, which no limits bound,<br />
Has brought me from the world’s extremest ground;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>“And (to avow the truth) in jealous mood<br />
Alone I came, alone with thee to fight;<br />
Because I grudged that king so puissant shou’d<br />
Exist on earth, save he observed my rite.<br />
Hence reek they ravaged fields with Christian blood;<br />
And yet with greater rancour and despite,<br />
Like cruel foe, I purposed to offend,<br />
But that it chanced, one changed me to a friend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>“When to worst harm and scaith thy bands I doom,<br />
I find (as at my leisure I will show)<br />
Rogero of Risa was my father, whom<br />
An evil brother traitorously laid low.<br />
Me my sad mother carried in her womb<br />
Beyond the sea, and bore in want and woe.<br />
Till my seventh year by wizard nourished, I<br />
Was stolen from him by thieves of Araby.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>“They to a king in Persia vended me,<br />
That after died beneath my faulchion, who<br />
Would fain have taken my virginity.<br />
When grown, that king and all his court I slew;<br />
Chased his ill race, and seized his royalty;<br />
And—such my fortune—by a month or two,<br />
I eithteen years had not o’erpast, before<br />
I added to my realm six kingdoms more;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>“And, moved by envy of thy glorious fame<br />
I in my heart resolved (as thou hast heard)<br />
To abate the grandeur of they mighty name:<br />
I haply so had done; I haply erred.<br />
But now a chance has served that will to tame,<br />
And clip my fury’s wings; the having heard<br />
Since I arrived in Christendom, how we<br />
Are bound by ties of consanguinity;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>“And, for my father thee, as kinsman, served,<br />
So thou a kin and servant hast in me;<br />
And I that envy, that fierce hate, which nerved<br />
Mine arm whilere, now blot from memory.<br />
Nay, these for evil Agramant reserved,<br />
And for his sire’s and uncle’s kin shall be;<br />
They who were whilom guilty of the death<br />
Of that unhappy pair, who gave me breath.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>She adds, the Christian faith she will receive,<br />
And, after having spent king Agramant,<br />
Will home return, with royal Charles’s leave,<br />
Her kingdom to baptize in the Levant,<br />
And war upon whatever nation cleave<br />
To cheating Mahound or to Termagant;<br />
Promising that whate’er her arms obtain<br />
Shall be the Christian faith’s and empire’s gain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>Charles, no less eloquent upon his side,<br />
Than bold in deed and prudent in design,<br />
Much that illustrious lady magnified,<br />
And much her father, much her noble line:<br />
He courteously to every point replied;<br />
And of his heart his open front was sign.<br />
As his last words, that he received the maid<br />
As kinswoman and child, the monarch said.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>Then rose and locked her in a new embrace,<br />
And kissed her, like a daughter, on the brow.<br />
Morgana and Clermont’s kin, with joyful face,<br />
All thither troop; ’twere tedious to tell how<br />
Rinaldo did the gentle damsel grace;<br />
For he had oftentimes espied ere now<br />
Her martial prowess, tried by goodly test,<br />
When they with girding siege Albracca pressed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>’Twere long to tell how, with those worthies met,<br />
Guido rejoiced to see Marphisa there;<br />
Gryphon and Aquilant, and Sansonet,<br />
That with her in the cruel city were;<br />
Vivian, and Malagigi, and Richardet;<br />
Who, when Maganza’s traitors made repair,<br />
With those ill purchasers of Spain to trade,<br />
Found such a faithful comrade in the maid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>They deck the ground for the ensuing day;<br />
And Charlemagne takes care himself to see<br />
That they the place shall sumptuously array,<br />
Wherein Marphisa’s baptism is to be.<br />
Bishops are gathered, learned clerks, and they<br />
Who ken the laws of Christianity;<br />
That taught in all its doctrine by their care<br />
And holy skill may be that martial fair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>In sacred stole, pontifical, arraid,<br />
Her the archbishop Turpin did baptize;<br />
Charlemagne from the healthful font the maid<br />
Uplifted with befitting ceremonies.<br />
But it is time the witless head to aid<br />
With that, which treasured in the phial lies,<br />
Wherewith Astolpho, from the lowest star,<br />
Descended in Elias’ fiery car.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>The duke descended from the lucid round,<br />
On this our earthly planet’s loftiest height.<br />
Wither he with that blessed vase was bound,<br />
Which was the mighty champion’s brain to right.<br />
A herb of sovereign virtue on that ground<br />
The apostle shows, and with it bids the knight<br />
The Nubian’s eyeballs touch, when him anew<br />
He visits, and restore that sovereign’s view.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>That he, for this and for his first desert,<br />
May give him bands, Biserta to assail;<br />
And shows him how that people inexpert<br />
He may to battle train, in plate and mail;<br />
And how to pass the deserts, without hurt,<br />
Where men are dazzled by the sandy gale.<br />
The order that throughout should be maintained<br />
From point to point, the sainted sire explained;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>Then made him that plumed beast again bestride,<br />
Rogero’s and Atlantes’ steed whilere.<br />
By sainted John dismist, his reverend guide,<br />
Those holy regions left the cavalier;<br />
And coasting Nile, on one or the other side,<br />
Saw Nubia’s realm before him soon appear;<br />
And there, in its chief city, to the ground<br />
Descended, and anew Senapus found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>Great was the joy, and great was the delight,<br />
Wherewith that king received the English lord;<br />
Who well remembered how the gentle knight<br />
Had from the loathsome harpies freed his board.<br />
But when the humour, that obscured his sight,<br />
Valiant Astolpho scaled, and now restored<br />
Was the blind sovereign’s eyesight as before,<br />
He would that warrior as a god adore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>So that not only those whom he demands<br />
For the Bisertine war, he gives in aid;<br />
But adds a hundred thousand from his bands,<br />
And offer of his royal person made.<br />
Scarce on the open plain embattled stands,<br />
—All foot—the Nubian host, for war arraid.<br />
For few the horses which that region bore;<br />
Of elephants and camels a large store.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>The night before the day, when on its road<br />
The Nubian force should march, Astolpho rose,<br />
And his winged hippogryph again bestrode:<br />
Then, hurrying ever south, in fury goes<br />
To a high hill, the southern wind’s abode;<br />
Whence he towards the Bears in fury blows:<br />
There finds a cave, through whose strait entrance breaks<br />
The fell and furious Auster, when he wakes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>He, as his master erst instruction gave,<br />
With him an empty bladder had conveyed;<br />
Which, at the vent of that dim Alpine cave,<br />
Wherein reposed the wearied wind, was laid<br />
Quaintly and softly by the baron brave;<br />
And so unlooked for was the ambuscade,<br />
That, issuing forth at morn, to sweep the plains,<br />
Auster imprisoned in the skin remains.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>To Nubia he, rejoicing in his prey,<br />
Returns; and with that very light the peer,<br />
With the black host, sets out upon his way,<br />
And lets the victual follow in his rear.<br />
Towards Mount Atlas with his whole array<br />
In safety goes the glorious cavalier.<br />
Through shifting plains of powdery sand he past,<br />
Nor dreaded danger from the sultry blast;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>And having gained the mountain’s hither side,<br />
Whence are discerned the plain, and distant brine,<br />
He chooses from the swarm he has to guide<br />
The noblest and most fit for discipline;<br />
And makes them, here and there, in troops divide,<br />
At a hill’s foot, wherewith the plains confine;<br />
Then leaves his host and climbs the hill’s ascent,<br />
Like one that is on lofty thoughts intent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>After he, lowly kneeling in the dust,<br />
His holy master had implored, in true<br />
Assurance he was heard, he downward thrust<br />
A heap of stones. O what things may he do<br />
That in the Saviour wholly puts his trust!<br />
The stones beyond the use of nature grew;<br />
Which rolling to the sandy plain below,<br />
Next, neck and muzzle, legs and belly show.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>They, neighing shrill, down narrow paths repair,<br />
With lusty leaps; and lighting on the plain,<br />
Uplift the croup, like coursers as they are,<br />
Some bay, some roan, and some of dapple stain.<br />
The crowds that waiting in the valleys were,<br />
Layed hands on them, and seized them by the rein.<br />
Thus in a thought each soldier had his horse,<br />
Born ready reined and saddled for the course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>He fourscore thousand of his Nubian power,<br />
One hundred and two footmen, in a day<br />
To horsemen changes, who wide Afric scour,<br />
And, upon every side, sack, burn, and slay.<br />
Agramant had intrusted town and tower,<br />
Till his return, to king Branzardo’s sway,<br />
To Fersa’s king, and him of the Algaziers;<br />
And these against Astolpho lead their spears.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>Erewhile a nimble bark, with sail and oar,<br />
They had dispatched, which, stirring feet and wings,<br />
News of the Nubian monarch’s outrage bore<br />
To Agramant from his vicegerent kings,<br />
That rests not, night nor day, till to the shore<br />
Of Provence she her doleful tiding brings;<br />
And finds her monarch half subdued in Arles,<br />
For camped within a mile was conquering Charles.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>Agramant, hearing in what peril lies<br />
His realm, through his attack on Pepin’s reign,<br />
Him in this pressing peril to advise,<br />
Calls kings and princes of the paynim train;<br />
And when he once or twice has turned his eyes<br />
On sage Sobrino and the king of Spain,<br />
—Eldest and wisest they those lords among—<br />
The monarch so bespeaks the assembled throng:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“Albeit if fits not captain, as I know,<br />
To say, ‘on this I thought not,’ this I say;<br />
Because when from a quarter comes the blow,<br />
From every human forethought far away,<br />
’Tis for such fault a fair excuse, I trow;<br />
And here all hinges; I did ill to lay<br />
Unfurnished Africk open to attack,<br />
If there was ground to fear the Nubian sack.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“But who could think, save only God on high<br />
Prescient of all which is to be below,<br />
That, from land, beneath such distant sky,<br />
Such mighty host would come, to work us woe?<br />
’Twixt shifting sands, which restless whirlwinds blow:<br />
Yet they their camp have round Biserta placed,<br />
And laid the better part of Africk waste.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“I now on this, O peers! your counsel crave.<br />
If, bootless, homeward I should wend my way,<br />
Or should not such a fair adventure wave,<br />
Till Charles with me a prisoner I convey;<br />
Or how I may as well our Africk save,<br />
And ruin this redoubted empire, say.<br />
Who can advise, is prayed his lore to shew,<br />
That we may learn the best, and that pursue.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>He said; and on Marsilius seated nigh<br />
Next turned his eyes, who in the signal read,<br />
That it belonged to him to make reply<br />
To what the king of Africa had said.<br />
The Spaniard rose, and bending reverently<br />
To Agramant the knee as well as head,<br />
Again his honoured seat in council prest,<br />
And in these words the Moorish king addrest:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“My liege, does Rumour good or ill report,<br />
It still increases them; hence shall I ne’er,<br />
Under despondence, lack for due support,<br />
Nor bolder course than is befitting steer,<br />
For what may chance, of good or evil sort;<br />
Weighing in even balance hope and fear,<br />
O’errated still; and which we should not mete<br />
By what I hear so many tongues repeat;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“Which should so much more doubtfully be viewed,<br />
As it seems less with likelihood to stand.<br />
Now it is seen, if there be likelihood,<br />
That king who reigns in so remote a land,<br />
Followed by such a mighty multitude,<br />
Should set his foot on warlike Africk’s strand;<br />
Traversing sands, to which in evil hour<br />
Cambyses trusted his ill-omened power.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“I well believe, that from some neighbouring hill<br />
The Arabs have poured down, to waste the plain;<br />
Who, for the country was defended ill,<br />
Have taken, burnt, destroyed and sacked and slain;<br />
And that Branzardo, who your place doth fill,<br />
As viceroy and lieutenant of the reign,<br />
Has set down thousands, where he tens should write;<br />
The better to excuse him in your sight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“The Nubian squadrons, I will even yield,<br />
Have been rained down on Africk from the skies;<br />
Or haply they have come, in clouds concealed,<br />
In that their march was hidden from all eyes:<br />
Think you, because unaided in the field,<br />
Your Africk from such host in peril lies?<br />
Your garrisons were sure of coward vein,<br />
If they were scared by such a craven train.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“But will you send some frigates, albeit few,<br />
(Provided that unfurled your standards be)<br />
No sooner shall they loose from hence, that crew<br />
Of spoilers shall within their confines flee;<br />
—Nubians are they, or idle Arabs—who,<br />
Knowing that you are severed by the sea<br />
From your own realm, and warring with our band,<br />
Have taken courage to assail your land.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“Now take your time for vengeance, when the son<br />
Of Pepin is without his nephew’s aid.<br />
Since bold Orlando is away, by none<br />
Of the hostile sect resistance can be made.<br />
If, through neglect or blindness, be foregone<br />
The glorious Fortune, which for you has stayed,<br />
She her bald front, as now her hair, will show,<br />
To our long infamy and mighty woe.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>Thus warily the Spanish king replied,<br />
Proving by this and other argument,<br />
The Moorish squadrons should in France abide,<br />
Till Charlemagne was into exile sent.<br />
But King Sobrino, he that plainly spied<br />
The scope whereon Marsilius was intent,<br />
To public good preferring private gain,<br />
So spake in answer to the king of Spain:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“My liege, when I to peace exhorted you,<br />
Would that my prophecy had proved less just!<br />
Of, if I was to prove a prophet true,<br />
Ye in Sobrino had reposed more trust,<br />
Than in King Rodomont and in that crew,<br />
Alzirdo, Martasine and Marbalust!<br />
Whom I would here see gladly, front to front;<br />
But see most gladly boastful Rodomont.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“To twit that warrior with his threat to do<br />
By France, what by the brittle glass is done;<br />
And throughout heaven and hell your course pursue,<br />
Yea (as the monarch said) your course outrun.<br />
Yet lapt in foul and loathsome ease, while you<br />
So need his help, lies Ulien’s lazy son;<br />
And I, that as a coward was decried<br />
For my true prophecy, am at your side;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“And ever will be while this life I bear;<br />
Which, albeit ’tis with yours sore laden, still<br />
Daily for you is risked with them that are<br />
The best of France; and—be he who he will—<br />
There is not mortal living, who will dare<br />
To say Sobrino’s deeds were ever ill:<br />
Yea, many who vaunt more, amid your host,<br />
Have not so much, nay lighter, cause for boast.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“I speak, these words to show that what whilere<br />
I said and say again, has neither sprung<br />
From evil heart, nor is the fruit of fear;<br />
But that true love and duty move my tongue.<br />
You homeward with what haste you may to steer,<br />
I counsel, your assembled bands among;<br />
For little is the wisdom of that wight,<br />
Who risks his own to gain another’s right.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“If there be gain, ye know, Late thirty-two,<br />
Your vassal kings, with you our sails we spread;<br />
Now, if we pause to sum the account anew,<br />
Hardly a third survives; the rest are dead.<br />
May it please Heaven no further loss ensue!<br />
But if you will pursue your quest, I dread<br />
Lest not a fourth nor fifth will soon remain;<br />
And wholly spent will be your wretched train.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>“Orlando’s absence so far aids, that where<br />
Our troops are few, there haply none would be;<br />
But not through this removed our perils are,<br />
Though it prolongs our evil destiny.<br />
Behold Rinaldo! whom his deeds declare<br />
No less than bold Orlando; of his tree<br />
There are the shoots; with paladin and peer,<br />
Our baffled Saracens’ eternal fear;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“And the other Mars (albeit against my heart<br />
It goes to waste my praise upon a foe);<br />
I speak of the redoubted Brandimart,<br />
Whose feats no less than fierce Orlando’s show;<br />
Whose mighty prowess I have proved in part,<br />
In part, at others’ cost I see and know.<br />
Then many days Orlando has been gone;<br />
Yet we have lost more fields than we have won.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>“I fear, if heretofore our band has lost,<br />
A heavier forfeit will henceforth be paid.<br />
Blotted is Mandricardo from our host;<br />
Martial Gradasso hath withdrawn his aid;<br />
Marphisa, at our worst, has left her post;<br />
So Argier’s lord; of whom it may be said,<br />
Where he as true as strong, we should not need<br />
Gradasso and the Tartar king, to speed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>“While aids like these are lost to our array,<br />
While on our side such slaughtered thousands lie,<br />
Those looked-for are arrived, nor on her way<br />
Is any vessel fraught with new supply—<br />
Charles has been joined by four, that, as they say,<br />
Might with Orlando or Rinaldo vie;<br />
With reasons, for from hence to Bactrian shore,<br />
Ill would you hope to find such other four.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>“I know not if you know who Guido are,<br />
Sansonet, and the sons of Olivier.<br />
For these I more respect, more fear I bear,<br />
Than any warlike duke or cavalier,<br />
Of Almayn’s or of other lineage fair,<br />
Who for the Roman empire rests the spear,<br />
Though I misrate not those of newer stamp,<br />
That, to our scathe, are gathered in their camp.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>“As often as ye issue on the plain,<br />
Worsted so oft, or broken, shall you be.<br />
If oft united Africa and Spain<br />
Were losers, when sixteen to eight were we,<br />
What will ensue, when banded with Almayn<br />
Are England, Scotland, France, and Italy?<br />
When with our six twice six their weapons cross,<br />
What else can we expect but shame and loss?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>“You lose your people here, and there your reign,<br />
If you in this emprize are obstinate;<br />
—Returning—us, the remnant of your train,<br />
You save, together with your royal state.<br />
It were ill done to leave the king of Spain,<br />
Since all for this would hold you sore ingrate;<br />
Yet there’s a remedy in peace; which, so<br />
It pleases but yourself, will please the foe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>“But, if, as first defeated, on your part<br />
It seems a shame to offer peace, and ye<br />
Have war and wasteful battle more at heart,<br />
Waged hitherto with what success you see,<br />
At least to gain the victory use art,<br />
Which may be yours, if you are ruled by me.<br />
Lay all our quarrel’s trial on one peer,<br />
And let Rogero be that cavalier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>“Such our Rogero is, ye know and I,<br />
That—pitted one to one in listed fight—<br />
Not Roland, not Rinaldo stands more high,<br />
Nor whatsoever other Christian knight.<br />
But would ye kindle warfare far and nigh,<br />
Though superhuman be that champion’s might,<br />
The warrior is but one mid many spears,<br />
Matched singly with a host of martial peers.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>“Meseemeth, if to you it seemeth good,<br />
Ye should propose to Charles the war to end;<br />
And that, to spare the constant waste of blood,<br />
Which his, and countless of your warriors spend,<br />
He—by a knight of yours to be withstood—<br />
A champion, chosen from his best should send;<br />
And those two all the warfare wage alone,<br />
Till one prevails, and one is overthrown;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>“On pact the king, whose champion in the just<br />
Is loser, tribute to that other pay.<br />
Nor will this pact displease King Charles, I trust,<br />
Though his was the advantage in the fray.<br />
Then of his arms Rogero so robust<br />
I deem, that he will surely win the day;<br />
Who would prevail (so certain is our right)<br />
Though Mars himself should be his opposite.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>With these and other sayings yet more sound,<br />
So wrought Sobrino, he his end obtained;<br />
And on that day interpreters were found,<br />
And they that day to Charles their charge explained.<br />
Charles, whom such matchless cavaliers surround.<br />
Believes the battle is already gained;<br />
And chooses good Rinaldo for the just,<br />
Next to Orlando in his sovereign’s trust.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>In this accord like cause for pleasure find,<br />
As well the Christian as the paynim foe:<br />
For, harassed sore in body and in mind,<br />
Those warriors all were weary, all were woe.<br />
Each in repose and quietude designed<br />
To pass what time remained to him below:<br />
Each cursed the senseless anger and the hate<br />
Which stirred their hearts to discord and debate.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>Rinaldo felt himself much magnified,<br />
That Charles, for what in him so strong weighed,<br />
More trusted him than all his court beside,<br />
And glad the honoured enterprise assayed:<br />
Rogero he esteemed not in his pride,<br />
And thought he ill could keep him from his blade.<br />
Nor deemed the Child could equal him in fight,<br />
Albeit he slew in strife the Tartar knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>Rogero, though much honoured, on his part,<br />
That him his king has chosen from the rest,<br />
To whom a trust so weighty to impart,<br />
As of his many martial lords the best,<br />
Yet shows a troubled face; not that the heart<br />
Of that good knight unworthy fears molest;<br />
Not only none Rinaldo would have bred;<br />
Him, with Orlando leagued, he would not dread—</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>But because sister of the Christian knight<br />
(He knows) is she, his consort true and dear;<br />
That to the stripling evermore did write,<br />
As one sore injured by that cavalier.<br />
Now, if to ancient sins he should unite<br />
A mortal combat with Montalban’s peer,<br />
Her, although loving, will he anger so,<br />
Not lightly she her hatred will forego.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>If silently Rogero made lament<br />
That he in his despite must battle do;<br />
In sobs his consort dear to hers gave vent,<br />
When shortly to her ears the tidings flew.<br />
She beat her breast, her golden tresses rent:<br />
Fast, scalding tears her innocent cheeks bedew:<br />
She taxes young Rogero as ingrate,<br />
And aye cries out upon her cruel fate.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>Nought can result to Bradamant but pain,<br />
Whatever is the doubtful combat’s end.<br />
She will not think Rogero can be slain;<br />
For this, ’twould seem, her very heart would rend;<br />
And should our Lord the fall of France ordain,<br />
That kingdom for more sins than one to amend,<br />
The gentle maid, beside a brother’s loss,<br />
Would have to weep a worse and bitterer cross.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>For, without shame and scorn, she never may,<br />
Not without hatred of her kin combined,<br />
To her loved lord return in such a way<br />
As that it may be known of all mankind;<br />
As, thinking upon this by night and day,<br />
She oftentimes had purposed in her mind;<br />
And so by promise both were tied withal,<br />
Room for repentance and retreat was small.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>But she, that ever, when things adverse were,<br />
With faithful succour Bradamant had stayed,<br />
I say the weird Melissa, could not bear<br />
To hear the wailings of the woeful maid;<br />
She hurried to console her in her care,<br />
And proffered succour in due time and said,<br />
She would disturb that duel ’twixt the twain,<br />
The occasion of such grief and cruel pain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>Meanwhile their weapons for the future fray<br />
Rogero and Duke Aymon’s son prepared;<br />
The choice whereof with that good warrior lay,<br />
The Roman empire’s knight by Charles declared;<br />
And he, like one that ever from the day<br />
He lost his goodly steed afoot had fared,<br />
Made choice, afoot and fenced with plate and mail,<br />
His foe with axe and dagger to assail.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Whether Chance moved Mountalban’s martial lord,<br />
Or Malagigi, provident and sage,<br />
That knew how young Rogero’s charmed sword<br />
Cleft helm and hauberk in its greedy rage,<br />
One and the other warrior made accord,<br />
(As said) without their faulchions to engage.<br />
The place of combat chosen by that twain<br />
Was near old Arles, upon a spacious plain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>Watchful Aurora hardly from the bower<br />
Of old Tithonus hath put forth her head,<br />
To give beginning to the day and hour<br />
Prefixed and ordered for that duel dread,<br />
When deputies from either hostile power,<br />
On this side and on that forth issuing, spread<br />
Tents at each entrance of the lists; and near<br />
The two pavillions, both, an altar rear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>After short pause, was seen upon the plain<br />
The paynim host in different squadrons dight.<br />
Rich in barbarick pomp, amid that train,<br />
Rode Africk’s monarch, ready armed for fight:<br />
Bay was the steed he backed, with sable mane;<br />
Two of his legs were pied, his forehead white<br />
Fast beside Agramant, Rogero came,<br />
And him to serve Marsilius thought no shame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>The casque that he from Mandricardo wrung<br />
In single combat with such travel sore,<br />
The casque that (as in loftier strain is sung)<br />
Cased Hector’s head, a thousand years before,<br />
Marsilius carried, by his side, among<br />
Princes and lords, that severally bore<br />
The other harness of Rogero bold,<br />
Enriched with precious pearls and rough with gold.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>On the other part, without his camp appears<br />
Charles, with his men at arms in squadrons dight;<br />
Who in such order led his cavaliers,<br />
As they would keep, if marshalled for the fight.<br />
Fenced is the monarch with his famous peers,<br />
And with him wends, all armed, Montalban’s knight,<br />
Armed, save his helmet, erst Mambrino’s casque;<br />
To carry which is Danish Ogier’s task;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>And, of two axes, hath Duke Namus one,<br />
King Salamon the other: Charlemagne<br />
Is to this side, with all his following, gone,<br />
To that wend those of Africk and of Spain.<br />
In the mid space between the hosts is none;<br />
Empty remains large portion of the plain;<br />
For he is doomed to death who thither goes,<br />
By joint proclaim, except the chosen foes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>After the second choice of arms was made<br />
By him, the champion of the paynim clan,<br />
Thither two priests of either sect conveyed<br />
Two books; that, carried by one holy man,<br />
—Him of our law—Christ’s perfect life displayed;<br />
Those others’ volume was their Alcoran.<br />
The emperor in his hands the Gospel took,<br />
The king of Africa that other book.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>Charlemagne, at his altar, to the sky<br />
Lifted his hands, “O God, that for our sake”<br />
(Exclaimed the monarch) “wast content to die,<br />
Thyself a ransom for our sins to make;<br />
—O thou that found such favour in his eye,<br />
That God from thee the flesh of man did take,<br />
Borne for nine months within thy holy womb,<br />
While aye thy virgin flower preserved its bloom,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>“Hear, and be witnesses of what I say,<br />
For me and those that after me shall reign,<br />
To Agramant and those that heir his sway,<br />
I twenty loads of gold of perfect grain<br />
Will every year deliver, if to-day<br />
My champion vanquished in the lists remain;<br />
And vow I will straightway from warfare cease,<br />
And from henceforth maintain perpetual peace;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>“And may your joint and fearful wrath descend<br />
On me forthwith, if I my word forego!<br />
And may it me and mine alone offend,<br />
And none beside, amid this numerous show!<br />
That all in briefest time may comprehend,<br />
My breach of promise has brought down the woe.”<br />
So saying, in his hand the holy book<br />
Charles held, and fixed on heaven his earnest look.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>This done, they seek that altar, sumptuously<br />
Decked for the purpose, by the pagan train;<br />
Where their king swears, that he will pass the sea,<br />
With all his army, to his Moorish reign,<br />
And to King Charles will tributary be;<br />
If vanquished, young Rogero shall remain;<br />
And will observe the truce for evermore<br />
Upon the pact declared by Charles before;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>And like him, nor in under tone, he swears,<br />
Calling on Mahound to attest his oath;<br />
And on the volume which his pontiff bears,<br />
To observe what he has promised plights his troth.<br />
Then to his side each hastily repairs;<br />
And mid their several powers are harboured both.<br />
Next these, to swear arrive the champions twain;<br />
And this the promise which their oaths contain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>Rogero pledges first his knightly word,<br />
Should his king mar, or send to mar, the fray,<br />
He him no more as leader or as lord<br />
Will serve, but wholly Charlemagne obey.<br />
—Rinaldo—if in breach of their accord,<br />
Him from the field King Charles would bear away,<br />
Till one or the other is subdued in fight,<br />
That he will be the Moorish monarch’s knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>When ended are the ceremonies, here<br />
And there, to seek their camps the two divide.<br />
Nor long, therein delayed; when trumpets clear<br />
The time for their encounter signified:<br />
Now to the charge advanced each cavalier,<br />
Measuring with cautious care his every stride.<br />
Lo! the assault begins; now low, now high,<br />
That pair the sounding steel in circles ply.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>Now with the axe’s blade, now with its heel<br />
Their strokes they at the head or foot address;<br />
And these so skilfully and nimbly deal,<br />
As needs must shock all credence to express.<br />
The Child, that at her brother aims the steel,<br />
Who doth his miserable soul possess,<br />
Evermore with such caution strikes his blow,<br />
That he is deemed less vigorous than his foe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XC.</td>
<td>Rather to parry then to smite intent,<br />
He know not what to wish; that low should lie<br />
Rinaldo, would Rogero ill content,<br />
Nor willingly the Child by him would die.<br />
But here I am at my full line’s extent,<br />
Where I must needs defer my history.<br />
In other canto shall the rest appear,<br />
If you that other canto please to hear.</td>
</tr>
