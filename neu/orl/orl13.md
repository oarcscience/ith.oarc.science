<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>The Count Orlando of the damsel bland<br />
Who loves Zerbino, hears the piteous woes.<br />
Next puts to death the felons with his hand<br />
Who pent her there. Duke Aymon’s daughter goes,<br />
Seeking Rogero, where so large a band<br />
The old Atlantes’ magic walls enclose.<br />
Her he impounds, deceived by fictions new.<br />
Agramant ranks his army for review.</em></span></td>

<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>Those ancient cavaliers right happy were,<br />
Born in an age, when, in the gloomy wood,<br />
In valley, and in cave, wherein the bear,<br />
Serpent, or lion, hid their savage brood,<br />
They could find that, which now in palace rare<br />
Is hardly found by judges proved and good;<br />
Women, to wit, who in their freshest days<br />
Of beauty worthily deserve the praise.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>Above I told you how a gentle maid<br />
Orlando had discovered under ground,<br />
And asked, by whom she thither was conveyed?<br />
Pursuing now my tale, I tell, how drowned<br />
In grief (her speech by many a sob delayed),<br />
The damsel fair, in sweet and softest sound,<br />
Summing them with what brevity she might,<br />
Her ills recounted to Anglantes’ knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>“Though I am sure,” she said, “O cavalier,<br />
To suffer punishment for what I say;<br />
Because I know, to him who pens me here,<br />
This woman quickly will the fact display;<br />
I would not but thou shouldst the story hear.<br />
—And let my wretched life the forfeit pay!<br />
For what can wait me better than that he,<br />
My gaoler, should one day my death decree?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>“Lo! I am Isabel, who once was styled<br />
The daughter of Gallicia’s hapless king:<br />
I said aright who was; but now the child<br />
(No longer his) of care and suffering:<br />
The fault of Love, by whom I was beguiled;<br />
For against him alone this charge I bring.<br />
Who sweetly, at the first, our wish applauds,<br />
And weaves in secret but deceit and frauds.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>“Whilom I lived, content in Fortune’s smile,<br />
Rich, blameless, fair, and young; to sad reverse<br />
Condemned, I now am wretched, poor, and vile,<br />
And in worse case, if any yet be worse.<br />
But it is fitting, I to thee this while<br />
From their first root my troubles should rehearse.<br />
And it will soothe me, though of thee I borrow<br />
No help, that thou compassionate my sorrow.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>“My father in his city of Bayonne,<br />
(To-day will be twelve months) a tourney dight;<br />
Hence, led by spreading rumour to our town,<br />
To joust, from different lands came many a knight;<br />
Mid these (was it his manifest renown,<br />
Or was it love which so deceived my sight)<br />
Praise in my eyes alone Zerbino won,<br />
Who was the mighty king of Scotland’s son.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>“When him I after in the field espied,<br />
Performing wondrous feats of chivalry,<br />
I was surprised by Love, ere I descried<br />
That freedom in my Love, so rash a guide,<br />
I lay this unction to my phantasy,<br />
That no unseemly place my heart possest,<br />
Fixed on the worthiest in the world and best.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>“In beauty and in valour’s boast above<br />
Those other lords the Scottish prince stood high.<br />
He showed me, and, I think, be bore me love,<br />
And left no less an ardent flame than I.<br />
Nor lacked there one who did between us move,<br />
To speak our common wishes frequently,<br />
So could we still in heart and mind unite,<br />
Although disjoined from one another’s sight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>“Hence, when concluded was the festal show,<br />
And to his home Zerbino was returned,<br />
If thou know’st what is love, thou well may’st know<br />
How night and day I for the warrior yearned;<br />
And was assured, no less on him did prey<br />
The flame, that in his constant bosom burned.<br />
He, save a way to have me with him, nought<br />
For solace of his restless passion sought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>“For different faith forbade him (on my side<br />
I was a saracen, a Christian he)<br />
To ask me of my father as a bride,<br />
By stealth he purposed to elope with me.<br />
Amid green fields, our wealthy town beside,<br />
I had a garden, seated by the sea,<br />
Upon the pleasant shore; from whence the eye<br />
Might ocean and the hills about descry.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“A fitting place to effect what different creed<br />
And law forbade us, he esteemed this site,<br />
And showed the order taken for the deed,<br />
Which was to make our future life’s delight;<br />
And how, near Santa Martha, for our need,<br />
A bark was with arm’d men in ambush dight,<br />
Under Sir Odoric of Biscay’s command;<br />
A leader he, approved by sea and land!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“Unable in his person this to do,<br />
For by his father he was forced to wend<br />
In succour of the king of France, in lieu<br />
This Odoric for the purpose he would send;<br />
Chosen, of all his faithful friends and true,<br />
As his most faithful and his truest friend:<br />
And such had been, if benefits could bind<br />
And goodly deeds the friendship of mankind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>“At the time fixed to bear me thence away,<br />
This chief would anchor on the destined ground.<br />
—And thus it was arrived the wished for day,<br />
Then I of them was in my garden found.<br />
Sir Odoric, at night, with fair array<br />
Of valiant men, by land and sea renowned,<br />
In the near river from his bark descends,<br />
And thence in silence to my garden wends.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>“To the pitched bark with me his party sped,<br />
Before the city knew what was at hand;<br />
Some of the house, disarmed and naked, fled,<br />
And some were slain; while of the helpless band,<br />
With me, another part was captive led.<br />
So was I severed from my native land,<br />
Hoping in brief Zerbino to possess,<br />
I cannot tell thee with what happiness.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>“Scarcely was Mongia by our galley doubled,<br />
Ere a squall took us on the larboard side,<br />
Which round about the clear horizon troubled,<br />
And stirred and tost heaven-high the foaming tide.<br />
Smote with a north-west wind, next, ocean bubbled,<br />
Which on her other beam the vessel plied:<br />
This evermore increases, with such force,<br />
Starboard or larboard, boots not which our course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>“It steads not to strike sail, nor lash the mast,<br />
Lowered on the gang-board, nor our castles fell;<br />
The bark, in our despite, is hurried fast<br />
Towards the pointed rocks about Rochelle:<br />
Save He, above, assist us at the last,<br />
The cruel storm will us ashore impel;<br />
Driven thither by ill wind with mightier speed<br />
Than ever bow-string gave to whistling reed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>“Our peril well does the Biscayan note,<br />
And tries what often has an evil end;<br />
Lowers down the galley’s skiff, and, when afloat,<br />
Descends into it, and makes me descend:<br />
Two follow, and a troop would throng the boat,<br />
Did not the first prevent them, and defend<br />
The entrance with their naked faulchions; we<br />
Sever the rope forthwith, and put to sea.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>“Driven landward, on the shore we safely light<br />
Who in the skiff embarked; while of our band<br />
The rest in the split vessel sink outright;<br />
Our goods sea-swallowed all. Upon the strand<br />
To Eternal Love, To Goodness Infinite,<br />
I offer up my thanks, with outstretched hand,<br />
That I was doomed not ’mid the watery roar<br />
To perish, nor behold Zerbino more.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>“Though I had left on shipboard matters rare,<br />
And precious in their nature, gem and vest,<br />
So I might hope Zerbino’s lot to share,<br />
I was content the sea should have the rest.<br />
No dwelling on the beach appears, nor there<br />
Is any pathway seen, by footsteps pressed;<br />
Only a hill, whose woody top is beat<br />
By ceaseless winds, the waters bathe its feet.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>“Here the fell tyrant Love, aye prompt to range,<br />
And faithless to his every promise still,<br />
Who watches ever how he may derange<br />
And mar our every reasonable will,<br />
Converts, with woeful and disastrous change,<br />
My comfort to despair, my good to ill:<br />
For he, in whom Zerbino put his trust,<br />
Cooled in his loyal faith, and burned with lust.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>“Whether he his desire had nursed at sea,<br />
And had not dared exhibit it before;<br />
Or that it sprung from opportunity,<br />
Suggested by that solitary shore;<br />
Without more pause, in that lone desert, he<br />
Would sate his greedy passion; but forbore<br />
Till he of one could rid him, of the twain,<br />
Who in the boat with us had scaped the main.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>“A man of Scotland he, Almonio hight,<br />
Who to Zerbino seemed great faith to bear;<br />
And as a perfect warrior by the knight,<br />
Praised, when to Odoric given, his trust to share:<br />
To him (the Spaniard said) it were a slight<br />
If I unto Rochelle afoot should fare;<br />
And prayed, that he before would thither speed,<br />
And forward thence some hackney, for my need.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>“Almonio, who in this suspects no ill,<br />
Forthwith, before our party, wends his way<br />
To the town, hidden by the wooded hill,<br />
And which not more than six miles distant lay.<br />
To the other finally his wicked will<br />
Sir Odoric took courage to display;<br />
As well because he could not rid him thence,<br />
As that in him he had great confidence.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>“He that remained with us, of whom I said<br />
Before, Corebo was of Bilbao hight,<br />
Who with him under the same roof was bred<br />
From infancy, and the ungrateful wight<br />
Deemed that the thought he harboured in his head,<br />
He could impart in safety to the knight,<br />
Who would prefer, neglected of his trust,<br />
The pleasure of his friend to what was just.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>“Not without high disdain Corebo heard<br />
(Who kind and courteous was) the Biscayneer,<br />
And termed him traitor; and by deed and word<br />
Withstood the purpose of his foul compeer.<br />
This mighty wrath in either warrior stirred;<br />
In sign whereof their naked brands they rear.<br />
At sight of their drawn swords, in panic, I<br />
Turn shortly through the gloomy wood to fly.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>“Sir Odoric in war well taught and bred,<br />
Gained in few blows such vantage in the fray,<br />
He left Corebo on the field for dead,<br />
And, following in my steps, pursued my way.<br />
Love lent to him (unless I am misled)<br />
Pinions, that he might overtake his prey;<br />
And many a prayer and glozing flattery taught,<br />
Wherewith I to compliance might be wrought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>“But all in vain, for I was fixed and bent,<br />
Rather than sate his ill desire, to die.<br />
When menace had by him been vainly spent,<br />
And every prayer and every flattery,<br />
He would by open force his will content;<br />
Nor boots it aught that I entreaties try;—<br />
Of his lord’s faith in him the wretch remind,<br />
And how myself I to his hands resigned.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>“When I perceived that fruitless was my prayer,<br />
And that I could not hope for other aid;<br />
For he assailed me like a famished bear,<br />
With hands and feet I fierce resistance made,<br />
As he more brutal waxed, and plucked his hair,<br />
And with my teeth and nails his visage flayed:<br />
This while I vent such lamentable cries,<br />
The clamour echoes to the starry skies.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>“Were they by chance conducted, or my shriek,<br />
Which might have well been heard a league around,<br />
(Or, was it they were wont the shore to seek,<br />
When any vessel split or ran aground)<br />
I saw a crowd appear upon the peak,<br />
Which, to the sea descending, towards us wound.<br />
Them the Biscayan say, and at the sight<br />
Abandoned his design, and turned to flight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>“This rabble, sir, against that treacherous man<br />
Comes to my aid; but in such guise, that I<br />
The homely saw, of falling from the pan<br />
Into the fire beneath, but verify.<br />
’Tis true so lost I was not, nor that clan<br />
Accursed with minds of such iniquity,<br />
That they to violate my person sought;<br />
Though nothing good or virtuous on them wrought:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“But that they knew, for me preserved a maid,<br />
As yet I am, they higher price might crave.<br />
Eight months are past, the ninth arrived, since, stayed<br />
By them, alive I languish in this grave.<br />
All hope is lost of my Zerbino’s aid:<br />
For from their speech I gather, as a slave,<br />
I am bartered to a merchant for his gold;<br />
By whom I to the sultan shall be sold.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>The gentle damsel so her tale pursues,<br />
While sobs and sighs oft interposing break<br />
Her soft angelic voice, which might infuse<br />
Compassion into asp, or venomed snake.<br />
What time she so her piteous grief renews,<br />
Or haply does her bitter anguish slake,<br />
Some twenty men the gloomy cavern fill;<br />
This armed with hunting-spear, and that with bill.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>With squinting look and dark, and but one eye,<br />
The leader of the troop, of brutish cheer<br />
Was he, the foremost of the company;<br />
By a blow blinded, which from nose to ear<br />
Had cleft his jaw: when he did so descry<br />
Seated beside the maid, that cavalier,<br />
He turned about and said: “Lo! in the net<br />
Another bird for whom it was not set!”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>Then to the County cried: “I never knew<br />
A man more opportune my wants to stead;<br />
I know not whether any one to you<br />
Perchance may have announced my pressing need<br />
Of such fair arms,—or you conjectured true,—<br />
As well as of that goodly sable weed.<br />
You verily arrived in season are<br />
My needs (pursued the losel) to repair.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>With bitter smile, upstarting on his feet,<br />
Orlando to the ruffian made reply:<br />
“Thou at a price at which no chapman treat,<br />
Unmarked in merchant’s books, these arms shalt buy.”<br />
With that he snatched a brand, which, full of heat<br />
And smoke, was smouldering in the chimney nigh,<br />
Threw it, and smote by chance the knave half blind,<br />
Where with the nose the meeting brows confined.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>The brand discharged by him, hit either brow,<br />
But most severely on the left did smite;<br />
For that ill feature perished by the blow,<br />
Which was the thief’s sole minister of light.<br />
Nor is the stroke content to blind the foe;<br />
Unsated, save it register his sprite<br />
Among those damned souls, whom Charon keeps,<br />
With their companions, plunged in boiling deeps.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>A spacious table in mid cavern stood,<br />
Two palms in thickness, in its figure square;<br />
Propt on one huge, ill fashioned food and rude,<br />
Which held the thief and all who harboured there.<br />
Even with such freedom as his dart of wood<br />
We mark the nimble Spaniard launch through air,<br />
The heavy table Roland seized and threw,<br />
Where, crowded close together, stood the crew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>One had his belly crushed, and one his breast;<br />
Another head or arm, or leg and thigh.<br />
Whence some were slain outright, and maimed the rest,<br />
While he who was least injured sought to fly.<br />
’Tis so sometimes, with heavy stone oppressed,<br />
A knot of slimy snakes is seen to lie,<br />
With battered heads and loins where, winter done,<br />
They lick their scales, rejoicing in the sun.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>I could not say what mischiefs these offend;<br />
One dies, and one departs without its tail;<br />
Another crippled cannot move an-end,<br />
And wriggling wreathes its length without avail:<br />
While this, whom more propitious saints befriend,<br />
Safe through the grass drags off its slimy trail.<br />
Dire was the stroke; yet should no wonder breed,<br />
Since good Orlando’s arm achieved the deed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>Those whom the board had little maimed or nought,<br />
(Turpin says there were seven) in craven wise,<br />
Their safety in their feet, yet vainly, sought;<br />
For to the cavern’s door Orlando hies.<br />
And having them without resistance caught,<br />
Fast with a rope their hands behind them ties;<br />
A rope, which in the cavern on the ground,<br />
Convenient for his purpose he had found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>He after drags them bound without the cave,<br />
Where an old service-tree its shadow throws.<br />
Orlando lops the branches with his glaive,<br />
And hangs the thieves, a banquet for the crows:<br />
Nor chain and crook for such a deed did crave:<br />
For ready hooks the tree itself bestows,<br />
To purge the world; where by the chin up-hung,<br />
These, on the branches, bold Orlando strung.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>The ancient woman, the assassin’s friend,<br />
Escapes when she perceives that all are dead,<br />
And, threading that green labyrinth without end,<br />
Laments, and plucks the hair from off her head,<br />
By fear impelled, through paths which sore offend<br />
Her feet, till she, beside a river’s bed,<br />
Encounters with a warrior: but to say<br />
Who was the stranger champion I delay;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>And turn to her, who to the count applied,<br />
Praying he would not leave her there alone,<br />
And vowed to follow whither he would guide.<br />
Orlando her consoles in courteous tone:<br />
And thence, when, with a wreath of roses tied<br />
About her brows, and robed in purple gown,<br />
On wonted journey white Aurora starts,<br />
The paladin with Isabel departs.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>Without encountering aught that might appear<br />
Worthy of note, they wended many a day;<br />
And finally the twain a cavalier,<br />
As prisoner led, encountered by the way.<br />
Who shall be told; but, tale to you as dear<br />
Now calls me from the beaten path away;<br />
—Of Aymon’s daughter,—whom I left above,<br />
Languid and lost in all the pains of love.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>The beauteous lady who desires in vain,<br />
Rogero should not his return delay,<br />
Lies in Marseilles, from whence the paynim train<br />
She harasses, nigh each returning day;<br />
(What time they robbing aye, by hill and plain,<br />
Scower fruitful Languedoc and Provence gay)<br />
And the true duty executes aright<br />
Of a sage leader and a valiant knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>The time long past, she, lying in that place,<br />
Had hoped that her Rogero would appear,<br />
She, not beholding him in all that space,<br />
Of many evil chances lived in fear.<br />
One day, mid others that her woeful case<br />
The lady wept alone, to her drew near<br />
The dame, who with that healing ring made sound<br />
The bosom rankling with Alcina’s wound.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>When her she saw, without her love returned,<br />
(Such time elapsed, her mission incomplete),<br />
Sore trembling, faint, and pale, her heart so yearned,<br />
She scarce had strength to stand upon her feet.<br />
But the enchantress kind, when she discerned<br />
Her fear, advanced with smiles the maid to meet;<br />
And to console her such glad visage wore<br />
As messenger who joyful tidings bore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“Fear not for thy Rogero: he is well<br />
And safe (she cried), and ever worships thee,<br />
As wonted; but thy foe, that wizard fell,<br />
Him yet again deprives of liberty.<br />
And it behoves thee now to climb the sell,<br />
Would’st thou posses him, and to follow me;<br />
For if thou wendest with me, I will lead<br />
Whither, by thee Rogero shall be freed.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>And next pursued, relating to her all<br />
The frauds and magic of Atlantes hoar,<br />
That wearing her fair face, who seemed the thrall<br />
Of an ill giant, him had through the door<br />
Of gold, enticed into the enchanted hall,<br />
And after disappeared, the youth before;<br />
And told how dames and cavaliers he cheats<br />
Who thither make resort, with like deceits.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>Seeing the sage, all think they see a squire,<br />
Companion, lady-love, or absent friend;<br />
Whatever is each several wight’s desire:<br />
Since to our scope our wishes never tend.<br />
Hence searching every where, themselves they tire<br />
With labour sore, and frustrate of their end;<br />
And cannot, (so Desire and Hope deceive),<br />
Without the missing good, that palace leave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“As soon as thou (pursued the dame) art near<br />
The place where he has built the magic seat,<br />
Resembling thy Rogero in his cheer<br />
And every look, Atlantes thee shall meet,<br />
And make himself by his ill art appear<br />
As suffering from some stronger arm defeat;<br />
That thou may’st aid him in the peril feigned,<br />
And thus among those others be detained.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“To the end thou may’st escape his ambush, where<br />
So many and so many, thus betrayed,<br />
Have fallen; though he Rogero seem, beware<br />
To lend him faith, who will demand thine aid:<br />
Nor, when the sage presents himself, forbear<br />
To take his worthless life with lifted blade.<br />
Nor think to slay Rogero with the blow,<br />
But him who works thee still such cruel woe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“Hard will it seem to slay, full well I know,<br />
The wight, in whom Rogero you descry:<br />
But, for truth is not in the lying show,<br />
Trust not to sight where magic blears the eye.<br />
Fix, ere with me you to the forest go,<br />
To change not when the traitorous foe is nigh:<br />
For never shall with you Rogero wive,<br />
If weakly you the wizard leave alive.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>The valorous maid with the intent to slay<br />
The false enchanter, on her plan decides,<br />
Snatches her arms, and follows on her way<br />
Melissa sage, in whom she so confides,<br />
And thus, by fruitful field or forest gray,<br />
Her by forced journeys that enchantress guides;<br />
And studies to beguile their weary course<br />
Ever, as best she may, with sweet discourse:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>And as the fairest topic of all those<br />
Which might be grateful to the damsel’s ear,<br />
Her future offspring and Rogero’s chose<br />
(A race of demigods) in prince and peer.<br />
For as Melissa all the secrets knows<br />
Of the eternal gods who rule our sphere,<br />
The good enchantress can discover all<br />
Which should in many ages hence befall.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>“Oh! my best guide.” exclaimed the damsel bold<br />
To the weird-woman that to aid her came,<br />
“As thou hast many years before foretold<br />
Men who shall glorify my race and name,<br />
So now I pray thee, lady, to unfold<br />
The praise and virtues of some noble dame,<br />
If from my lineage any such shall rise.”<br />
To whom Melissa courteously replies:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>“Chaste dames of thee descended I survey,<br />
Mothers of those who wear imperial crown,<br />
And mighty kings; the column and the stay<br />
Of glorious realms and houses of renown.<br />
And as thy sons will shine in arms, so they<br />
Will no less fame deserve in female gown,<br />
With piety and sovereign prudence graced,<br />
And noble hearts, incomparably chaste.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>“And if at length, I should relate to thee<br />
The praise of all who from thy root ascend,<br />
Too long my tale would hold, nor do I see<br />
Whom I could pass, where all to fame pretend.<br />
But from a thousand I some two or three<br />
Will choose, because my tale may have an end.<br />
Why was not in the cave thy wish made known,<br />
Where I their shadows might as well have shown?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>“To hear of one of thy famed race prepare,<br />
Whom liberal studies and good works engage;<br />
Of whom, I know not well, if she more fair<br />
May be entitled, or more chaste and sage;<br />
The noble-minded Isabel, who, where<br />
It stands on Mincius’ bank, in other age<br />
Shall gild the town, of Ocnus’ mother hight,<br />
With her own glorious rays by day and night;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>“Where, with her worthiest consort she will strain,<br />
In honoured and in splendid rivalry,<br />
Which best shall prize the virtues’ goodly train,<br />
And widest ope the gates to courtesy.<br />
If he by Taro, and in Naples’ reign,<br />
(’Tis said), from Gauls delivered Italy,<br />
’Twill be replied. Penelope the chaste,<br />
As such, was not beneath Ulysses placed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>“Great things and many thus I sum in few<br />
Of this brave dame, and others leave behind:<br />
Which when I from the vulgar herd withdrew,<br />
Sage Merlin from the hollow stone divined.<br />
For I should leave old Typhis out of view,<br />
If on such sea I launched before the wind:<br />
And with this finish my prophetic strain,<br />
—All blessings on her head the skies will rain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>“With her shall be her sister Beatrice,<br />
Whose fortunes well shall with her name accord;<br />
Who, while she lives, not only shall not miss<br />
What good the heavens to those below afford,<br />
But make, with her, partaker of her bliss,<br />
First among wealthy dukes, her cherished lord;<br />
Who shall, when she from hence receives her call,<br />
Into the lowest depth of misery fall.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>“Viscontis’ serpents will be held in dread,<br />
And Moro and Sforza, while this dame shall be,<br />
From Hyperborean snows to billows red;<br />
From Ind to hills, which to a double sea<br />
Afford a passage; and, the lady dead,<br />
To the sore mischief of all Italy,<br />
Will with the Insubri into slavery fall;<br />
And men shall sovereign wisdom fortune call.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>“Other the same illustrious name will bear,<br />
And who will flourish many years before.<br />
Pannonia’s garland one of these shall wear.<br />
Another matron on the Ausonian shore,<br />
When she shall be released from earthly care,<br />
Men will among the blessed saints adore;<br />
With incense will approach the dame divine,<br />
And hang with votive images her shrine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>“The others I shall pass in silence by,<br />
For ’twere too much (as said before) to sound<br />
Their fame: though each might well deserve, that high<br />
Heroic trump should in her praise be wound.<br />
Hence the Biancas and Lucretias I<br />
And Constances and more reserve; who found,<br />
Or else repair, upon Italian land,<br />
Illustrious houses with supporting hand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>“Thy race, which shall all else in this excel,<br />
In the rare fortune of its women thrives;<br />
Nor of its daughters’ honour more I tell<br />
Than of the lofty virtue of its wives:<br />
And that thou may’st take note of this as well,<br />
Which Merlin said of thy descendents’ lives,<br />
(Haply that I the story might narrate)<br />
This I no little covet to relate.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>“Of good Richarda first shall be my strain,<br />
Mirror of chastity and fortitude,<br />
Who, young, remains a widow, in disdain<br />
Of fortune: (that which oft awaits the good)<br />
Exiles, and cheated of their father’s reign,<br />
She shall behold the children of her blood<br />
Wandering into the clutches of their foe;<br />
Yet find at last a quittance for her woe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>“Nor sprung from the ancient root of Aragon,<br />
I of the gorgeous queen will silent be;<br />
Than whom more prudent or more chaste is none,<br />
Renowned in Greek or Latin history;<br />
Nor who so fortunate a course will run,<br />
After that, by divine election, she<br />
Shall with the goodly race of princes swell,<br />
Alphonso, Hyppolite, and Isabel.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>“The prudent Eleanour is this: a spray<br />
Which will be grafted on thy happy tree.<br />
What of the fruitful stepchild shall I say,<br />
Who in succession next to her I see,<br />
Lucretia Borgia? who, from day to day,<br />
Shall wax in beauty, virtue, chastity,<br />
And fortune, that like youthful plant will shoot,<br />
Which into yielding soil has struck its root.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>“As tin by silver, brass by gold, as Corn-<br />
Poppy beside the deeply-crimsoning rose,<br />
Willow by laurel evergreen, as shorn<br />
Of light, stained glass by gem that richly glows,<br />
—So by this dame I honour yet unborn,<br />
Each hitherto distinguished matron shows;<br />
For beauty and for prudence claiming place,<br />
And all praise-worthy excellence and grace.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>“And above every other noble praise,<br />
Which shall distinguished her alive or dead,<br />
Is that by her shall be, through kingly ways,<br />
Her Hercules and other children led;<br />
Who thus the seeds of worth in early days,<br />
To bloom in council and in camp, will shed.<br />
For long wine’s savour lingers in the wood<br />
Of the new vessel, whether bad or good.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>“Nor the step-daughter of this noble dame,<br />
Will I, Renata, hight of France, forget,<br />
Of Louis born, twelfth monarch of his name,<br />
And Bretagne’s pride; all virtues ever yet<br />
Bestowed on woman, since the ruddy flame<br />
Has warmed, or water had the power to wet,<br />
Or overhead the circling heavens have rolled,<br />
United in Renata I behold.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>“ ’Twere long to tell of Alda de Sansogna,<br />
Or of Celano’s countess in this string,<br />
Or Blanche Maria, stiled of Catalonia;<br />
Or her, the daughter of Sicilia’s king,<br />
Or of the beauteous Lippa de Bologna,<br />
Or more, with whose renown the world shall ring,<br />
To speak whose separate praise with fitting lore,<br />
Were to attempt a sea without a shore.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>When of the larger portion of her seed<br />
The king enchantress at full ease had told,<br />
And oft and oft rehearsed, amid the rede,<br />
What arts Rogero to the wizard’s hold<br />
Had drawn, Melissa halted near the mead<br />
Where stood the mansion of Atlantes old,<br />
Nor would approach the magic dome more nigh,<br />
Lest her the false magician should espy.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>And yet again advised the martial maid,<br />
(Counsel she had a thousand times bestowed)<br />
Then left, Nor Bradamant through greenwood shade<br />
More than two miles in narrow path had rode,<br />
Before, by two fierce giants overlaid,<br />
She saw a knight, who like Rogero showed,<br />
So closely pressed, and labouring sore for breath,<br />
That he appeared well nigh reduced to death.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>When she beheld him in such perilous strait,<br />
Who of Rogero all the tokens wore,<br />
She quickly lost the faith she nourished late,<br />
Quickly her every fair design forbore.<br />
She weens Melissa bears Rogero hate,<br />
For some new injury unheard before:<br />
And with unheard of hate and wrong, her foe<br />
Would by her hand destroy who loves him so.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>She cried, “And is not this Rogero, who<br />
Aye present to my heart, is now to sight?<br />
If ’tis not him whom I agnize and view.<br />
Whom e’er shall I agnize or view aright?<br />
Why should I other’s judgment deem more true<br />
Than the belief that’s warranted by sight?<br />
Even without eyes, and by my heart alone,<br />
If he were near or distant, would be shown.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>While so the damsel thinks, a voice she hears,<br />
Which, like Rogero’s, seems for aid to cry;<br />
At the same time, the worsted knight appears<br />
To slack the bridle and the rowels ply:<br />
While at full speed the goaded courser clears<br />
His ground, pursued by either enemy.<br />
Nor paused the dame, in following them who sought<br />
His life, till to the enchanted palace brought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>Of which no sooner has she past the door,<br />
Than she is cheated by the common show.<br />
Each crooked way or straight her feet explore<br />
Within it and without, above, below;<br />
Nor rests she night or day, so strong the lore<br />
Of the enchanter, who has ordered so,<br />
She (though they still encounter and confer)<br />
Knows not Rogero, nor Rogero her.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>But leave we Bradamant, nor grieve, O ye<br />
Who hear, that she is prisoned by the spell,<br />
Since her in fitting time I shall set free,<br />
And good Rogero, from the dome as well,<br />
As taste is quickened by variety,<br />
So it appears that, in the things I tell,<br />
The wider here and there my story ranges,<br />
It will be found less tedious for its changes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>Meseems that I have many threads to clear<br />
In the great web I labour evermore;<br />
And therefore be ye not displeased to hear<br />
How, all dislodged, the squadrons of the Moor,<br />
Threatening the golden lines loud, appear<br />
In arms, the royal Agramant before:<br />
Who bids for a review his army post,<br />
Willing to know the numbers of his host.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>For besides horse and foot, in the campaign<br />
Sore thinned, whose numbers were to be supplied,<br />
Had many captains, and those good, of Spain,<br />
Of Libya, and of Aethiopia, died;<br />
And thus the nations, and the various train,<br />
Wandered without a ruler or a guide.<br />
To give to each its head and order due,<br />
The ample camp is mustered in review.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>To fill the squadrons ravaged by the sword,<br />
In those fierce battles and those conflicts dread,<br />
This to his Spain, to his Africa that lord,<br />
Sent to recruit, where well their files they fed;<br />
And next distributed the paynim horde<br />
Under their proper captains, ranged and led.<br />
I, with your leave, till other strain, delay<br />
The order of the muster to display.</td>
</tr>
