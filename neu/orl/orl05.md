<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>Lurcanio, by a false report abused,<br />
Deemed by Geneura’s fault his brother dead,<br />
Weening the faithless duke, whom she refused,<br />
Was taken by the damsel to her bed;<br />
And her before the king and peers accused:<br />

Strives with his brother in disguise. In season<br />
Rinaldo comes to venge the secret treason.</em></span></td>

<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>A<span class="small">MONG</span> all other animals who prey<br />
On earth, or who unite in friendly wise,<br />
Whether they mix in peace or moody fray,<br />
No male offends his mate. In safety hies<br />
The she bear, matched with hers, through forest gray:<br />
The lioness beside the lion lies:<br />
Wolves, male and female, live in loving cheer;<br />
Nor gentle heifer dreads the wilful steer.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>What Fury, what abominable Pest<br />
Such poison in the human heart has shed,<br />
That still ’twixt man and wife, with rage possessed,<br />
Injurious words and foul reproach are said?<br />
And blows and outrage base their peace molest,<br />
And bitter tears still wash the genial bed;<br />
Not only watered by the tearful flood,<br />
But often bathed by senseless ire with blood?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>Not simply a rank sinner, he appears<br />
To outrage nature, and his God to dare,<br />
Who his foul hand against a woman rears,<br />
Or of her head would harm a single hair.<br />
But who what drug the burning entrail sears,<br />
Or who for her would knife or noose prepare,<br />
No man appears to me, though such to sight<br />
He seem, but rather some infernal sprite.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>Such, and no other were those ruffians two,<br />
Whom good Rinaldo from the damsel scared,<br />
Conducted to these valleys out of view,<br />
That none might wot of her so foully snared.<br />
I ended where the damsel, fair of hue,<br />
To tell the occasion of her scathe prepared,<br />
To the good Paladin, who brought release;<br />
And in conclusion thus my story piece.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>“Of direr deed than ever yet was done,”<br />
The gentle dame began, “Sir cavalier,<br />
In Thebes, Mycene, Argos, or upon<br />
Other more savage soil, prepare to hear;<br />
And I believe, that if the circling sun<br />
To these our Scottish shores approach less near<br />
Than other land, ’tis that he would eschew<br />
A foul ferocious race that shocks his view.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>“All times have shown that man has still pursued<br />
With hair, in every clime, his natural foe;<br />
But to deal death to those who seek our good<br />
Does from too ill and foul a nature flow.<br />
Now, that the truth be better understood,<br />
I shall from first to last the occasion show,<br />
Why in my tender years, against all right,<br />
Those caitiffs would have done me foul despite.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>“ ’Tis fitting you should know, that in the spring<br />
Of life, I to the palace made resort;<br />
There served long time the daughter of the king,<br />
And grew with her in growth, well placed in court.<br />
When cruel love, my fortune envying,<br />
Willed I should be his follower and his sport;<br />
And made, beyond each Scottish lord and knight,<br />
Albany’s duke find favour in my sight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>“And for he seemed to cherish me above<br />
All mean; his love a love as ardent bred.<br />
We hear, indeed, and see, but do not prove<br />
Man’s faith, nor is his bosom’s purpose read.<br />
Believing still, and yielding to my love,<br />
I ceased not till I took him to my bed;<br />
Nor, of all chambers, in that evil hour,<br />
Marked I was in Geneura’s priviest bower.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>“Where, hoarded, she with careful privacy<br />
Preserved whatever she esteemed most rare;<br />
There many times she slept. A gallery<br />
From thence projected into the open air.<br />
Here oft I made my lover climb to me,<br />
And (what he was to mount) a hempen stair,<br />
When him I to my longing arms would call,<br />
From the projecting balcony let fall.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>“For here my passion I as often fed<br />
As good Geneura’s absence made me bold;<br />
Who with the varying season changed her bed,<br />
To shun the burning heat or pinching cold,<br />
And Albany, unseen and safely sped;<br />
For, fronting a dismantled street, and old,<br />
Was built that portion of the palace bright;<br />
Nor any went that way by day or night.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“So was for many days and months maintained<br />
By us, in secrecy, the amorous game;<br />
Still grew by love, and such new vigour gained,<br />
I in my inmost bosom felt the flame;<br />
And that he little loved, and deeply feigned<br />
Weened not, so was I blinded to my shame:<br />
Though, in a thousand certain signs betrayed,<br />
The faithless knight his base deceit bewrayed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“After some days, of fair Geneura he<br />
A suitor showed himself; I cannot say<br />
If this began before he sighed for me,<br />
Or, after, of this love he made assay:<br />
But judge, alas! with what supremacy<br />
He ruled my heart, how absolute his sway!<br />
Since this he owned, and thought no shame to move<br />
Me to assist him in his second love.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>“Unlike what he bore me, he said, indeed,<br />
That was not true which he for her displayed:<br />
But so pretending love, he hoped to speed,<br />
And celebrate due spousals with the maid.<br />
He with her royal sire might well succeed,<br />
Were she consenting to the boon he prayed;<br />
For after our good king, for wealth and birth<br />
In all the realm, was none of equal worth.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>“Me he persuades, if through my ministry<br />
He the king’s son-in-law elected were,<br />
For I must know he next the king would be<br />
Advanced as high, as subject could repair,<br />
The merit should be mine, and ever he<br />
So great a benefit in mind would bear;<br />
And he would cherish me above his bride,<br />
And more than every other dame beside.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>“I, who to please him was entirely bent,<br />
Who never could or would gainsay his will,<br />
Upon those days alone enjoy content,<br />
When I find means his wishes to fulfil:<br />
And snatch at all occasions which present<br />
A mode, his praise and merits to instil:<br />
And for my lover with all labour strain,<br />
And industry, Geneura’s love to gain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>“With all my heart, in furtherance of his suit,<br />
I wrought what could be done, God truly knows;<br />
But with Geneura this produced no fruit,<br />
Nor her to grace my duke could I dispose.<br />
For that another love had taken root<br />
In her, whose every fond affection flows<br />
Towards a gentle knight of courteous lore,<br />
Who sought our Scotland from a distant shore:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>“And with a brother, then right young, to stay<br />
In our king’s court, came out of Italy;<br />
And there of knightly arms made such assay,<br />
Was none in Britain more approved than he;<br />
Prized by the king, who (no ignoble pay),<br />
Rewarding him like his nobility,<br />
Bestowed upon the youth, with liberal hand,<br />
Burghs, baronies, and castles, woods and land.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>“Dear to the monarch, to the daughter still<br />
This lord was dearer, Ariodantes hight.<br />
Her with affection might his valour fill;<br />
But knowledge of his love brought more delight.<br />
Nor old Vesuvius, nor Sicilia’s hill,<br />
Nor Troy-town, ever, with a blaze so bright,<br />
Flamed, as with all his heart, the damsel learned,<br />
For love of her young Ariodantes burned.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>“The passion which she bore the lord, preferred<br />
And loved with perfect truth and all her heart,<br />
Was the occassion I was still unheard;<br />
Nor hopeful answer would she e’er impart:<br />
And still the more my lover’s suit I stirred,<br />
And to obtain his guerdon strove with art,<br />
Him she would censure still, and ever more<br />
Was strengthened in the hate she nursed before.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>“My wayward lover often I excite<br />
So vain and bootless an emprize to quit;<br />
Nor idly hope to turn her stedfast sprite,<br />
Too deeply with another passion smit;<br />
And make apparent to the Scottish knight,<br />
Ariodantes such a flame had lit<br />
In the young damsel’s breast, that seas in flood<br />
Would not have cooled one whit her boiling blood.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>“This Polinesso many times had heard<br />
From me (for such the Scottish baron’s name)<br />
Well warranted by sight as well as word,<br />
How ill his love was cherished by the dame.<br />
To see another to himself preferred<br />
Not only quenched the haughty warrior’s flame,<br />
But the fond love, which in his bosom burned<br />
Into despiteful rage and hatred turned.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>“Between Geneura and her faithful knight<br />
Such discord and ill will he schemed to shed,<br />
And put betwixt the pair such foul despite.<br />
No time should heal the quarrel he had bred;<br />
Bringing such scandal on that damsel bright,<br />
The stain should cleave to her, alive or dead:<br />
Nor, bent to wreck her on this fatal shelf,<br />
Counselled with me, or other but himself.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>“ ‘Dalinda mine,’ he said, his project brewed,<br />
(Dalinda is my name) ‘you needs must know,<br />
That from the root although the trunk be hewed,<br />
Successive suckers many times will grow.<br />
Thus my unhappy passion is renewed,<br />
Tenacious still of life, and buds; although<br />
Cut off by ill success, with new increase:<br />
Nor, till I compass my desire, will cease.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>“ ‘Nor hope of pleasure this so much has wrought,<br />
As that to compass my design would please;<br />
And, if not in effect, at least in thought<br />
To thrive, would interpose some little ease.<br />
Then every time your bower by me is sought,<br />
When in her bed Geneura slumbers, seize<br />
What she puts off, and be it still your care<br />
To dress yourself in all her daily wear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>“ ‘Dispose your locks and deck yourself as she<br />
Goes decked; and, as you can, with cunning heed,<br />
Imitate her; then to the gallery<br />
You, furnished with the corded stair, shall speed:<br />
I shall ascend it in the phantasy<br />
That you are she, of whom you wear the weed:<br />
And hope, that putting on myself this cheat,<br />
I in short time shall quench my amorous heat.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>“So said the knight; and I, who was distraught,<br />
And all beside myself, was not aware<br />
That the design, in which he help besought,<br />
Was manifestly but too foul a snare;<br />
And in Geneura’s clothes disguised, as taught,<br />
Let down (so oft I used) the corded stair.<br />
Nor I the traitor’s foul deceit perceived,<br />
Until the deadly mischief was achieved.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>“The duke, this while, to Ariodantes’ ears<br />
Had these, or other words like these, addressed;<br />
(For leagued in friendship were the cavaliers,<br />
Till, rivals, they pursued this common quest)<br />
‘I marvel, since you are of all my peers<br />
He, whom I must have honoured and caressed,<br />
And held in high regard, and cherished still,<br />
You should my benefits repay so ill.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>“ ‘I am assured you comprehend and know<br />
Mine and Geneura’s love, and old accord;<br />
And, in legitimate espousal, how<br />
I am about to claim her from my lord:<br />
Then why disturb my suit, and why bestow<br />
Your heart on her who offers no reward?<br />
By Heaven, I should respect your claim and place,<br />
Were your condition mine, and mine your case.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>“ ‘And I,’ cried Ariodantes, ‘marvel more’<br />
(In answer to the Scottish lord) ‘at you,<br />
Since I of her enamoured was, before<br />
That gentle damsel ever met your view;<br />
And know, you are assured how evermore<br />
We two have loved;—was never love more true—<br />
Are certain she alone would share my lot;<br />
And are as well assured she loves you not.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>“ ‘Why have not I from you the same respect,<br />
To which, for friendship past, you would pretend<br />
From me; and I should bear you in effect,<br />
If your hope stood more fair to gain its end?<br />
No less than you, to wed her I expect;<br />
And if your fortunes here my wealth transcend,<br />
As favoured of the king, as you, above<br />
You, am I happy in his daughter’s love.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“ ‘Of what a strange mistake,’ (to him replied<br />
The duke) ‘your foolish passion is the root!<br />
You think yourself beloved; I, on my side,<br />
Believe the same; this try we by the fruit.<br />
You of your own proceeding nothing hide,<br />
And I will tell the secrets of my suit:<br />
And let the man who proves least favoured, yield,<br />
Provide himself elsewhere, and quit the field.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>“ ‘I am prepared, if such your wish, to swear<br />
Nothing of what is told me to reveal;<br />
And will that you assure me, for your share,<br />
You shall what I recount as well conceal.’<br />
Uniting in the pact, the rival pair<br />
Their solemn vows upon the Bible seal:<br />
And when they had the mutual promise plighted,<br />
Ariodantes first his tale recited.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>“Then plainly, and by simple facts averred,<br />
How with Geneura stood his suit, avows;<br />
And how, engaged by writing and by word,<br />
She swore she would not be another’s spouse.<br />
How, if to him the Scottish king demurred,<br />
Virgin austerity she ever vows;<br />
And other bridal bond for aye eschewed,<br />
To pass her days in barren solitude.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“Then added, how he hoped by worth, which he<br />
Had more than once avouched, with knightly brand,<br />
And yet might vouch, to the prosperity<br />
And honour of the king, and of his land,<br />
To please so well that monarch, as to be<br />
By him accounted worthy of the hand<br />
Of his fair child, espoused with his consent:<br />
Since he in this her wishes would content.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>“Then so concludes—‘I stand upon this ground,<br />
Nor I intruder fear, encroaching nigh;<br />
Nor seek I more; ’tis here my hopes I bound;<br />
Nor, striving for Geneura’s love, would I<br />
Seek surer sign of it than what is found,<br />
By God allowed, in wedlock’s lawful tie;<br />
And other suit were hopeless, am I sure,<br />
So excellent she is, and passing pure.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“When Ariodantes had, with honest mind,<br />
Told what reward he hoped should quit his pain,<br />
False Polinesso, who before designed<br />
To make Geneura hateful to her swain,<br />
Began—‘Alas! you yet are far behind<br />
My hopes, and shall confess your own are vain<br />
And say, as I the root shall manifest<br />
Of my good fortune, I alone am blest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“ ‘With you Geneura feigns, nor pays nor prizes<br />
Your passion, which with hopes and words is fed;<br />
And, more than this, your foolish love despises:<br />
And this to me the damsel oft has said,<br />
Of hers I am assured; of no surmises,<br />
Vain, worthless words, or idle promise bred.<br />
And I to you the fact in trust reveal,<br />
Though this I should in better faith conceal.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“ ‘There passes not a month, but in that space<br />
Three nights, four, six, and often ten, the fair<br />
Receives me with that joy in her embrace,<br />
Which seems to second so the warmth we share.<br />
This you may witness, and shall judge the case;<br />
If empty hopes can with my bliss compare.<br />
Then since my happier fortune is above<br />
Your wishes, yield, and seek another love.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“ ‘This will I not believe,’ in answer cried<br />
Ariodantes, ‘well assured you lie,<br />
And that you have this string of falsehoods tied,<br />
To scare me from the dear emprize I try.<br />
But charge, so passing foul, you shall abide,<br />
And vouch what you have said in arms; for I<br />
Not only on your tale place no reliance;<br />
But as a traitor hurl you my defiance.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“To him rejoined the duke, ‘I ween ’twere ill<br />
To take the battle upon either part,<br />
Since surer mean our purpose may fulfill;<br />
And if it please, my proof I can impart.’<br />
Ariodantes trembled, and a chill<br />
Went through his inmost bones; and sick at heart,<br />
Had he in full believed his rival’s boast,<br />
Would on the spot have yielded up the ghost.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“With wounded heart, and faltering voice, pale face,<br />
And mouth of gall, he answered, ‘When I see<br />
Proofs of thy rare adventure, and the grace<br />
With which the fair Geneura honours thee,<br />
I promise to forego the fruitless chase<br />
Of one, to thee so kind, so cold to me.<br />
But think not that thy story shall avail,<br />
Unless my very eyes confirm the tale.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“ ‘To warn in due time shall be my care.’<br />
(Said Polinesso) and so went his way.<br />
Two nights were scarcely passed, ere his repair<br />
To the known bower was fixed for the assay.<br />
And, ready now to spring his secret snare,<br />
He sought his rival on the appointed day,<br />
And him to hide, the night ensuing, prayed<br />
I’ the street, which none their habitation made.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“And to the youth a station over-right<br />
The balcony, to which he clambered, shows.<br />
Ariodantes weened, this while, the knight<br />
Would him to seek that hidden place dispose,<br />
As one well suited to his fell despite,<br />
And, bent to take his life, this ambush chose,<br />
Under the false pretence to make him see<br />
What seemed a sheer impossibility.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“To go the peer resolved, but in such guise,<br />
He should not be with vantage overlaid;<br />
And should he be assaulted by surprise,<br />
He need not be by fear of death dismay’d.<br />
He had a noble brother, bold and wise,<br />
First of the court in arms; and on his aid,<br />
Lurcanio hight, relied with better heart<br />
Than if ten others fought upon his part.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“He called him to his side, and willed him take<br />
His arms; and to the place at evening led:<br />
Yet not his secret purpose would be break;<br />
Nor this to him, or other would have read:<br />
Him a stone’s throw removed he placed, and spake:<br />
‘—Come if thou hearest me cry,’ the warrior said;<br />
‘But as thou lovest me (whatsoe’er befall)<br />
Come not and move not, brother, till I call.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“ ‘Doubt not’ (the valiant brother said) ‘but go;’<br />
And thither went that baron silently,<br />
And hid within the lonely house, and low,<br />
Over against my secret gallery.<br />
On the other side approached the fraudful foe,<br />
So pleased to work Geneura’s infamy;<br />
And, while I nothing of the cheat divine,<br />
Beneath my bower renews the wonted sign.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“And I in costly robe, in which were set<br />
Fair stripes of gold upon a snowy ground,<br />
My tresses gathered in a golden net,<br />
Shaded with tassels of vermillion round,<br />
Mimicking fashions, which were only met<br />
In fair Geneura, at the accustomed sound,<br />
The gallery mount, constructed in such mode,<br />
As upon every side my person showed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“This while Lurcanio, either with a view<br />
To snares which might beset his brother’s feet,<br />
Or with the common passion to pursue,<br />
And play the spy on other, where the street<br />
Was darkest, and its deepest shadows threw,<br />
Followed him softly to his dim retreat:<br />
And not ten paces from the knight aloof,<br />
Bestowed himself beneath the self same roof.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“Suspecting nought, I seek the balcony,<br />
In the same habits which I mentioned, dressed;<br />
As more than once or twice (still happily)<br />
I did before; meanwhile the goodly vest<br />
Was in the moonlight clearly seen, and I,<br />
In aspect not unlike her, in the rest<br />
Resembling much Geneura’s shape and cheer,<br />
One visage well another might appear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“So much the more, that there was ample space<br />
Between the palace and the ruined row:<br />
Hence the two brothers, posted in that place,<br />
Were lightly cheated by the lying show.<br />
Now put yourself in his unhappy case,<br />
And figure what the wretched lover’s woe,<br />
When Polinesso climbed the stair, which I<br />
Cast down to him, and scaled the gallery.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“Arrived, my arms about his neck I throw,<br />
Weening that we unseen of others meet,<br />
And kiss his lips and face with loving show,<br />
As him I hitherto was wont to greet;<br />
And he assayed, with more than wonted glow,<br />
Me to caress, to mask his hollow cheat.<br />
Led to the shameful spectacle, aghast,<br />
That other, from afar, viewed all that passed,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“And fell into such fit of deep despair,<br />
He there resolved to die; and, to that end,<br />
Planted the pommel of his falchion bare<br />
I’ the ground, its point against his breast to bend.<br />
Lurcanio, who with marvel by that stair,<br />
Saw Polinesso to my bower ascend,<br />
But knew not who the wight, with ready speed<br />
Sprang forward, when he saw his brother’s deed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“And hindered him in that fell agony<br />
From turning his own hand against his breast.<br />
Had the good youth been later, or less nigh,<br />
To his assistance he had vainly pressed.<br />
Then, ‘Wretched brother, what insanity,’<br />
(He cried) ‘your better sense has dispossessed?<br />
Die for a woman! rather let her kind<br />
Be scattered like the mist before the wind!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>“ ‘Compass her death! ’tis well deserved; your own<br />
Reserve, as due to more illustrious fate.<br />
’Twas well to love, before her fraud was shown,<br />
But she, once loved, now more deserves your hate:<br />
Since, witnessed by your eyes, to you is known<br />
A wanton of what sort you worshipped late.<br />
Her fault before the Scottish king to attest,<br />
Reserve those arms you turn against your breast.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“Ariodantes, so surprised, forewent,<br />
Joined by his brother, the design in show;<br />
But resolute to die, in his intent<br />
Was little shaken: Rising thence to go,<br />
He bears away a heart not simply rent,<br />
But dead and withered with excess of woe:<br />
Yet better comfort to Lurcanio feigns,<br />
As if the rage were spent which fired his veins.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>“The morn ensuing, without further say<br />
To his good brother, or to man beside,<br />
He from the city took his reckless way<br />
With deadly desperation for his guide;<br />
Nor, save the duke and knight, for many a day<br />
Was there who knew what moved the youth to ride:<br />
And in the palace, touching this event,<br />
And in the realm, was various sentiment.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>“But eight days past or more, to Scotland’s court<br />
A traveller came, and to Geneura he<br />
Related tidings of disastrous sort;<br />
That Ariodantes perished in the sea:<br />
Drowned of his own free will was the report,<br />
No wind to blame for the calamity!<br />
Since from a rock, which over ocean hung,<br />
Into the raging waves he headlong sprung;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>“Who said, before he reached that frowning crest,<br />
To me, whom he encountered by the way,<br />
‘Come with me, that your tongue may manifest,<br />
And what betides me to Geneura say;<br />
And tell her, too, the occasion of the rest,<br />
Which you shall witness without more delay;<br />
In having seen too much, the occasion lies;<br />
Happy had I been born without these eyes!’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>“By chance, upon a promontory we<br />
Were standing, overright the Irish shore;<br />
When, speaking thus on that high headland, he<br />
Plunged from a rock amid the watery roar.<br />
I saw him leap, and left him in the sea;<br />
And, hurrying thence, to you the tidings bore.<br />
Geneura stood amazed, her colour fled,<br />
And, at the fearful tale, remained half dead.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>“O God! what said, what did she, when alone,<br />
She on her faithful pillow layed her head!<br />
She beat her bosom, and she tore her gown,<br />
And in despite her golden tresses shed<br />
Repeating often, in bewildered tone,<br />
The last sad words which Ariodantes said;—<br />
That the sole source of such despair, and such<br />
Disaster, was that he had seen too much.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>“Wide was the rumour scattered that the peer<br />
Had slain himself for grief; nor was the cry<br />
By courtly dame, or courtly cavalier,<br />
Or by the monarch, heard with tearless eye.<br />
But, above all the rest, his brother dear<br />
Was whelmed with sorrow of so deep a dye,<br />
That, bent to follow him, he well nigh turned<br />
His hand against himself, like him he mourned.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>“And many times repeating in his thought,<br />
It was Geneura who his brother slew,<br />
Who was to self-destruction moved by nought<br />
But her ill deed, which he was doomed to view,<br />
So on his mind the thirst of vengeance wrought,<br />
And so his grief his season overthrew;<br />
That he thought little, graced of each estate,<br />
To encounter king and people’s common hate;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>“And, when the throng was fullest in the hall,<br />
Stood up before the Scottish king, and said,<br />
‘Of having marred my brother’s wits withal,<br />
Sir king, and him to his destruction led,<br />
Your daughter only can I guilty call:<br />
For in his inmost soul such sorrow bred<br />
The having seen her little chastity,<br />
He loathed existence, and preferred to die.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>“ ‘He was her lover; and for his intent<br />
Was honest, this I seek not, I, to veil;<br />
And to deserve her by his valour meant<br />
Of thee, if faithful service might avail;<br />
But while he stood aloof, and dared but scent<br />
The blossoms, he beheld another scale,<br />
Scale the forbidden tree with happier boot,<br />
And bear away from him the wished-for fruit.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>“Then added, how into the gallery came<br />
Geneura, and how dropped the corded stair;<br />
And how into the chamber of the dame<br />
Had climbed a leman of that lady fair;<br />
Who, for disguise (he knew not hence his name),<br />
Had changed his habits, and concealed his hair;<br />
And, in conclusion, vowed that every word<br />
So said, he would avouch with lance and sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>“You may divine how grieves the sire, distraught<br />
With woe, when he the accusation hears:<br />
As well that what he never could have thought,<br />
He of his daughter learns with wondering ears,<br />
As that he knows, if succour be not brought<br />
By cavalier, that in her cause appears,<br />
Who may upon Lurcanio prove the lie,<br />
He cannot choose, but doom the maid to die.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>“I do not think our Scottish law to you<br />
Is yet unknown, which sentences to fire<br />
The miserable dame, or damsel, who<br />
Grants other than her wedded lord’s desire.<br />
She dies, unless a champion, good and true,<br />
Arm on her side before a month expire;<br />
And her against the accuser base maintain<br />
Unmeriting such death, and free from stain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>“The king has made proclaim by town and tower,<br />
(For he believes her wronged, his child to free)<br />
Her he shall have to wife, with ample dower,<br />
Who saves the royal maid from infamy.<br />
But each to the other looks, and to this hour<br />
No champion yet, ’tis said, appears: for he,<br />
Lurcanio, is esteemed so fierce in fight,<br />
It seems as he were feared of every knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>“And evil Fate has willed her brother dear,<br />
Zerbino, is not here the foe to face;<br />
Since many months has roved the cavalier,<br />
Proving his matchless worth with spear and mace;<br />
For if the valiant champion were more near,<br />
(Such is his courage) or in any place,<br />
Whither in time the news might be conveyed,<br />
He would not fail to bear his sister aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>“The king, mean time, who would the quest pursue,<br />
And by more certain proof than combat, try<br />
If the accuser’s tale be false or true,<br />
And she deserve, or merit not, to die,<br />
Arrests some ladies of her retinue,<br />
That, as he weens, the fact can verify.<br />
Whence I foresaw, that if I taken were,<br />
Too certain risque the duke and I must share.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>“That very night I from the palace flee,<br />
And to the duke repair, escaped from court;<br />
And, were I taken, make him plainly see<br />
How much it either’s safety would import:<br />
He praised, and bade me of good courage be,<br />
And, for his comfort, prayed me to resort<br />
To a strong castle which he held hard by;<br />
And gave me two to bear me company.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>“With what full proofs, sir stranger, you have heard,<br />
I of my love assured the Scottish peer;<br />
And clearly can discern, if so preferred,<br />
That lord was justly bound to hold me dear.<br />
Mark, in conclusion, what was my reward;<br />
The glorious meed of my great merit hear!<br />
And say if woman can expect to earn,<br />
However well she love, her love’s return.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>“For this perfidious, foul, ungrateful man,<br />
At length suspicious of my faith and zeal,<br />
And apprehending that his wily plan,<br />
In course of time, I haply might reveal,<br />
Feigned that meanwhile the monarch’s anger ran<br />
Too high, he would withdraw me, and conceal<br />
Within a fortress of his own, where I<br />
(Such was his real end) was doomed to die.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>“For secretly the duke enjoined the guide,<br />
Who with me through the gloomy forest went,<br />
The worthy guerdon of a faith so tried,<br />
To slay me; and had compassed his intent,<br />
But for your ready succour, when I cried.<br />
Behold! what wages love’s poor slaves content.”<br />
Thus to Rinaldo did Dalinda say,<br />
As they together still pursued their way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Above all other fortune, to the knight<br />
Was welcome to have found the gentle maid,<br />
Who the whole story of Geneura bright,<br />
And her unblemished innocence displayed;<br />
And, if he hoped, although accused with right,<br />
To furnish the afflicted damsel aid,<br />
Persuaded of the calumny’s disproof,<br />
He with more courage warred in her behoof.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>And for St. Andrew’s town, with eager speed,<br />
Where was the king with all his family,<br />
And where the single fight, in listed mead,<br />
Upon his daughter’s quarrel, was to be,<br />
The good Rinaldo pricked, nor spared his steed,<br />
Until, within an easy distance, he<br />
Now near the city, met a squire who brought<br />
More recent tidings than the damsel taught:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>That thither had repaired a stranger knight,<br />
To combat in Geneura’s quarrel bent,<br />
With ensigns strange, not known of living wight,<br />
Since ever close concealed the warrior went;<br />
Not, since he had been there, had bared to sight<br />
His visage, aye within his helmet pent:<br />
And that the very squire who with him came,<br />
Swore that he knew not what the stranger’s name.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>Not far they ride before the walls appear,<br />
And now before the gate their coursers stand.<br />
To advance the sad Dalinda was in fear,<br />
Yet followed, trusting in Rinaldo’s brand.<br />
The gate was shut, and to the porter near,<br />
‘What this implies’ Rinaldo makes demand:<br />
To him was said, the people, one and all,<br />
Were trooped to see a fight without the wall:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>Beyond the city, fought upon accord,<br />
Between Lurcanio and a stranger knight;<br />
Where, on a spacious meadow’s level sward,<br />
The pair already had begun the fight.<br />
The porter opened to Mount Alban’s lord,<br />
And straight behind the peer the portal hight.<br />
Rinaldo through the empty city rode,<br />
But in a hostel first the dame bestowed:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>And wills that she (he will not long delay<br />
To seek her there) till his return repose;<br />
And quickly to the lists pursued his way,<br />
Where the two made that fell exchange of blows,<br />
And strove and struggled yet in bloody fray.<br />
Lurcanio’s heart with vengeful hatred glows<br />
Against Geneura; while that other knight<br />
As well maintains the quarrel for her right.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>Six knights on foot within the palisade<br />
Stand covered with the corslet’s iron case;<br />
Beneath the Duke of Albany arrayed,<br />
Borne on a puissant steed of noble race:<br />
Who there, as lord high-constable obeyed,<br />
Was keeper of the field and of the place,<br />
And joyed Geneura’s peril to espy<br />
With swelling bosom and exulting eye.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>Rinaldo pierces through the parted swarm,<br />
(So wide is felt the good Bayardo’s sway,)<br />
And he who hears the courser come in storm,<br />
Halts not, in his desire to make him way:<br />
Above is seen Rinaldo’s lofty form,<br />
The flower of those who mix in martial fray.<br />
He stops his horse before the monarch’s chair,<br />
While all to hear the paladin repair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>“Dread sir,” to him the good Rinaldo said,<br />
“Let not the pair this combat longer ply;<br />
Since whichsoever of the two falls dead,<br />
Know, that you let him perish wrongfully:<br />
<em>This</em> thinks that he is right, and is misled,<br />
Vouches the false, and knows not ’tis a lie:<br />
Since that which brought his brother to his end,<br />
Moves him in causeless battle contend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>“<em>That</em>, in pure gentleness, with little care<br />
If what he here maintains be wrong or right,<br />
Because he would preserve a maid so fair,<br />
Perils his person in the furious fight.<br />
To injured innocence I safety bear,<br />
And to the evil man its opposite.<br />
But first, for love of God, the battle stay;<br />
Then list, sir king, to what I shall display.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>So moved the king the grave authority<br />
Of one who seemed so worthy, by his cheer,<br />
That he made sign the battle should not be<br />
Further continued then with sword or spear:<br />
To whom, together with his chivalry,<br />
And barons of the realm and others near<br />
Rinaldo all the treacherous plot displayed,<br />
Which Polinesso for Geneura layed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>Next that he there in arms would testify<br />
The truth of what he vouched, the warrior cried.<br />
False Polinesso, called, with troubled eye,<br />
Stood forth, but daringly the tale denied.<br />
To him the good Rinaldo in reply;<br />
“By deeds be now the doubtful quarrel tried.”<br />
The field was cleared, and, ready armed, the foes,<br />
Without more let, in deadly duel close.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>How was the hope to king and people dear,<br />
The proof might show Geneura innocent!<br />
All trust that God will make the treason clear,<br />
And show she was accused with foul intent:<br />
For Polinesso, greedy and severe,<br />
And proud was held, and false and fraudulent.<br />
So that none there, of all assembled, deemed<br />
It marvel, if the knight such fraud had schemed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>False Polinesso, with a mien distressed,<br />
A pallid cheek, and heart which thickly beat,<br />
At the third trumpet, laid his lance in rest;<br />
As well Rinaldo spurred the knight to meet,<br />
And levelled at his evil foeman’s breast,<br />
Eager to finish at a single heat.<br />
Nor counter to his wish was the event;<br />
Since through the warrior half his weapon went.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>Him, through his breast, impaled upon the spear,<br />
More than six yards beyond his horse he bore.<br />
With speed alighted Mount Albano’s peer,<br />
And, ere he rose, unlaced the helm he wore:<br />
But he for mercy prayed with humble cheer,<br />
Unfit to strive in joust or warfare more:<br />
And, before king and court, with faltering breath,<br />
Confessed the fraud which brought him to his death.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XC.</td>
<td>He brings not his confession to a close,<br />
And pangs of death the failing accents drown:<br />
The prince, who ended saw his daughter’s woes,<br />
Redeemed from death and scorn, her virtue shown,<br />
With more delight and rapture overflows,<br />
Than if he, having lost his kingly crown,<br />
Then saw it first upon his head replaced;<br />
So that he good Rinaldo singly graced.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCI.</td>
<td>And when, through his uplifted casque displaid,<br />
Features, well known before, the king descried,<br />
His thanks to God with lifted hands he paid,<br />
That he had deigned such succour to provide.<br />
That other cavalier, who bared his blade,<br />
Unknown of all, upon Geneura’s side,<br />
And thither came from far, his aid to impart,<br />
Looked upon all that passed, and stood apart.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCII.</td>
<td>Him the good king entreated to declare<br />
His name, or, at the least, his visage shew;<br />
That he might grace him with such guerdon fair,<br />
As to his good intent was justly due.<br />
The stranger, after long and earnest prayer,<br />
Lifted to covering casque, and bared to view<br />
What in the ensuing canto will appear,<br />
If you are fain the history to hear.</td>
