<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>Restored to sense, the beauteous Bradamant<br />
Finds sage Melissa in the vaulted tomb,<br />
And hears from her of many a famous plant<br />
And warrior, who shall issue from her womb.<br />
Next, to release Rogero from the haunt<br />
Of old Atlantes, learns how from the groom,<br />
Brunello hight, his virtuous ring to take;<br />
And thus the knight’s and others’ fetters break.</em></span></td>
</tr>

<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>Who will vouchsafe me voice that shall ascend<br />
As high as I would raise my noble theme?<br />
Who will afford befitting words, and lend<br />
Wings to my verse, to soar the pitch I scheme?<br />
Since fiercer fire for such illustrious end,<br />
Than what was wont, may well my song beseem.<br />
For this fair portion to my lord is due<br />
Which sings the sires from whom his lineage grew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>Than whose fair line, ’mid those by heavenly grace<br />
Chosen to minister this earth below,<br />
You see not, Phœbus, in your daily race,<br />
One that in peace or war doth fairer show;<br />
Nor lineage that hath longer kept its place;<br />
And still shall keep it, if the lights which glow<br />
Within me, but aright inspire my soul,<br />
While the blue heaven shall turn about the pole.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>But should I seek at full its worth to blaze,<br />
Not mine were needful, but that noble lyre<br />
Which sounded at your touch the thunderer’s praise,<br />
What time the giants sank in penal fire.<br />
Yet should you instruments, more fit to raise<br />
The votive work, bestow, as I desire,<br />
All labour and all thought will I combine,<br />
To shape and shadow forth the great design.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>Till when, this chisel may suffice to scale<br />
The stone, and give my lines a right direction;<br />
And haply future study may avail,<br />
To bring the stubborn labour to perfection.<br />
Return we now to him, to whom the mail<br />
Of hawberk, shield, and helm, were small protection:<br />
I speak of Pinabel the Maganzeze,<br />
Who hopes the damsel’s death, whose fall he sees.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>The wily traitor thought that damsel sweet<br />
Had perished on the darksome cavern’s floor,<br />
And with pale visages hurried his retreat<br />
From that, through him contaminated door.<br />
And, thence returning, clomb into his seat:<br />
Then, like one who a wicked spirit bore,<br />
To add another sin to evil deed,<br />
Bore off with him the warlike virgin’s steed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>Leave we sometime the wretch who, while he layed<br />
Snares for another, wrought his proper doom;<br />
And turn we to the damsel he betrayed,<br />
Who had nigh found at once her death and tomb.<br />
She, after rising from the rock, dismayed<br />
At her shrewd fall, and gazing through the gloom,<br />
Beheld and passed that inner door, which gave<br />
Entrance to other and more spacious cave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>For the first cavern in a second ended,<br />
Fashioned in form of church, and large and square;<br />
With roof by cunning architect extended<br />
On shafts of alabaster rich and rare.<br />
The flame of a clear-burning lamp ascended<br />
Before the central altar; and the glare,<br />
Illuminating all the space about,<br />
Shone through the gate, and lit the cave without.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>Touched with the sanctifying thoughts which wait<br />
On worthy spirit in a holy place,<br />
She prays with eager lips, and heart elate,<br />
To the Disposer of all earthly grace:<br />
And, kneeling, hears a secret wicket grate<br />
In the opposing wall; whence, face to face,<br />
A woman issuing forth, the maid addresses,<br />
Barefoot, ungirt, and with dishevelled tresses.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>“O generous Bradamant,” the matron cried,<br />
“Know thine arrival in this hallowed hold<br />
Was not unauthorized of heavenly guide:<br />
And the prophetic ghost of Merlin told,<br />
Thou to this cave shouldst come by path untried,<br />
Which covers the renowned magician’s mould.<br />
And here have I long time awaited thee,<br />
To tell what is the heavens’ pronounced decree.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>“This is the ancient memorable cave<br />
Which Merlin, that enchanter sage, did make:<br />
Thou may’st have heard how that magician brave<br />
Was cheated by the Lady of the Lake.<br />
Below, beneath the cavern, is the grave<br />
Which holds his bones; where, for that lady’s sake,<br />
His limbs (for such her will) the wizard spread.<br />
Living he laid him there, and lies there dead.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“Yet lives the spirit of immortal strain;<br />
Lodged in the enchanter’s corpse, till to the skies<br />
The trumpet call it, or to endless pain,<br />
As it with dove or raven’s wing shall rise.<br />
Yet lives the voice, and thou shalt hear how plain<br />
From its sepulchral case of marble cries:<br />
Since this has still the past and future taught<br />
To every wight that has its counsel sought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“Long days have passed since I from distant land<br />
My course did to this cemetery steer,<br />
That in the solemn mysteries I scanned,<br />
Merlin to me the truth should better clear;<br />
And having compassed the design I planned,<br />
A month beyond, for thee, have tarried here;<br />
Since Merlin, still with certain knowledge summing<br />
Events, prefixed this moment for thy coming.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>The daughter of Duke Aymon stood aghast,<br />
And silent listened to the speech; while she<br />
Knew not, sore marvelling at all that passed,<br />
If ’twere a dream or a reality.<br />
At length, with modest brow, and eyes down cast,<br />
Replied (like one that was all modesty),<br />
“And is this wrought for me? and have I merit<br />
Worthy the workings of prophetic spirit?”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>And full of joy the adventure strange pursues,<br />
Moving with ready haste behind the dame,<br />
Who brings her to the sepulchre which mews<br />
The bones and spirit, erst of Merlin’s name.<br />
The tomb, of hardest stone which masons use,<br />
Shone smooth and lucid, and as red as flame.<br />
So that although no sun-beam pierced the gloom,<br />
Its splendour lit the subterraneous room.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>Whether it be the native operation<br />
O certain stones, to shine like torch i’ the dark,<br />
Or whether force of spell or fumigation,<br />
(A guess that seems to come more near the mark)<br />
Or sign made under mystic constellation,<br />
The blaze that came from the sepulchral ark<br />
Discovered sculpture, colour, gems, and gilding,<br />
And whatsoever else adorned the building.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Scarcely had Bradamant above the sill<br />
Lifter her foot, and trod the secret cave,<br />
When the live spirit, in clear tones that thrill,<br />
Addressed the martial virgin from the grave;<br />
“May Fortune, chaste and noble maid, fulfil<br />
Thine every wish!” exclaimed the wizard brave.<br />
“Since from thy womb a princely race shall spring,<br />
Whose name through Italy and earth shall ring.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>“The noble blood derived from ancient Troy,<br />
Mingling in thee its two most glorious streams,<br />
Shall be the ornament, and flower, and joy<br />
Of every lineage on which Phœbus beams,<br />
Where genial stars lend warmth, or cold annoy,<br />
Where Indus, Tagus, Nile, or Danube gleams;<br />
And in thy progeny and long drawn line<br />
Shall marquises, counts, dukes and Cæsers shine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>“Captains and cavaliers shall spring from thee,<br />
Who both by knightly lance and prudent lore,<br />
Shall once again to widowed Italy<br />
Her ancient praise and fame in arms restore;<br />
And in her realms just lords shall seated be,<br />
(Such Numa and Augustus were of yore),<br />
Who with their government, benign and sage,<br />
Shall re-create on earth the golden age.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>“Then, that the will of Heaven be duly brought<br />
To a fair end through thee, in fitting date,<br />
Which from the first to bless thy love has wrought,<br />
And destined young Rogero for thy mate,<br />
Let nothing interpose to break that thought,<br />
But boldly tread the path perscribed by fate;<br />
Nor let aught stay thee till the thief be thrown<br />
By thy good lance, who keeps thee from thine own.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>Here Merlin ceased, that for the solemn feat<br />
Melissa might prepare with fitting spell,<br />
To show bold Bradamant, in aspect meet,<br />
The heirs who her illustrious race should swell.<br />
Hence many sprites she chose; but from what seat<br />
Evoked, I know not, or if called from hell;<br />
And gathered in one place (so bade the dame),<br />
In various garb and guise the shadows came.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>This done, into the church she called the maid,<br />
Where she had drawn a magic ring, as wide<br />
As might contain the damsel, prostrate laid;<br />
With the full measure of a palm beside.<br />
And on her head, lest spirit should invade,<br />
A pentacle for more assurance tied.<br />
So bade her hold her peace, and stand and look,<br />
Then read, and schooled the demons from her book.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>Lo! forth of that first cave what countless swarm<br />
Presses upon the circle’s sacred round,<br />
But, when they would the magic rampart storm,<br />
Finds the way barred as if by fosse or mound;<br />
Then back the rabble turns of various form;<br />
And when it thrice with bending march has wound<br />
About the circle, troops into the cave,<br />
Where stands that beauteous urn, the wizard’s grave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>“To tell at large the puissant acts and worth,<br />
And name of each who, figured in a sprite,<br />
Is present to our eyes before his birth,”<br />
Said sage Melissa to the damsel bright;<br />
“To tell the deeds which they shall act on earth,<br />
Were labour not to finish with the night.<br />
Hence I shall call few worthies of thy line,<br />
As time and fair occasion shall combine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>“See yonder first-born of thy noble breed,<br />
Who well reflects thy fair and joyous face;<br />
He, first of thine and of Rogero’s seed,<br />
Shall plant in Italy thy generous race.<br />
In him behold who shall distain the mead,<br />
And his good sword with blood of Pontier base;<br />
The mighty wrong chastised, and traitor’s guilt,<br />
By whom his princely father’s blood was spilt.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>“By him King Desiderius shall be pressed,<br />
The valiant leader of the Lombard horde:<br />
And of the fiefs of Calaon and Este;<br />
For this imperial Charles shall make him lord.<br />
Hubert, thy grandson, comes behind; the best<br />
Of Italy, with arms and belted sword:<br />
Who shall defend the church from barbarous foes,<br />
And more than once assure her safe repose.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>“Alberto next, unconquered captain, see,<br />
Whose trophies shall so many fanes array.<br />
Hugh, the bold son, is with the sire, and he<br />
Shall conquer Milan, and the snakes display.<br />
Azo, that next approaching form shall be,<br />
And, his good brother dead, the Insubri sway.<br />
Lo! Albertazo! by whose rede undone,<br />
See Berengarius banished, and his son.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>“With him shall the imperial Otho join<br />
In wedlock worthily his daughter fair.<br />
And lo! another Hugh! O noble line!<br />
O! sire succeeded by an equal heir!<br />
He, thwarting with just cause their ill design,<br />
Shall thrash the Romans’ pride who overbear;<br />
Shall from their hands the sovereign pontiff take,<br />
With the third Otho, and their leaguer break.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>“See Fulke, who to his brother will convey<br />
All his Italian birth-right, and command<br />
To take a mighty dukedom far away<br />
From his fair home, in Almayn’s northern land.<br />
There he the house of Saxony shall stay,<br />
And prop the ruin with his saving hand;<br />
This in his mother’s right he shall possess,<br />
And with his progeny maintain and bless.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>“More famed for courtesy than warlike deed,<br />
Azo the second, he who next repairs!<br />
Bertoldo and Albertazo are his seed:<br />
And, lo! the father walks between his heirs.<br />
By Parma’s walls I see the Germans bleed,<br />
Their second Henry quelled; such trophy bears<br />
The one renowned in story’s future page:<br />
The next shall wed Matilda, chaste and sage.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>“His virtues shall deserve so fair a flower,<br />
(And in his age, I wot, no common grace)<br />
To hold the half of Italy in dower,<br />
With that descendent of first Henry’s race.<br />
Rinaldo shall succeed him in his power,<br />
Pledge of Bertoldo’s wedded love, and chase<br />
Fierce Frederick Barbarossa’s hireling bands,<br />
Saving the church from his rapacious hands.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“Another Azo rules Verona’s town,<br />
With its fair fields; and two great chiefs this while<br />
(One wears the papal, one the imperial crown),<br />
The baron, Marquis of Ancona style.<br />
But to show all who rear the gonfalon<br />
Of the consistory, amid that file,<br />
Were task too long; as long to tell each deed<br />
Achieved for Rome by thy devoted seed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>“See Fulke and Obyson, more Azos, Hughs!<br />
Both Henrys!—mark the father and his boy.<br />
Two Guelphs: the first fair Umbria’s land subdues,<br />
And shall Spoleto’s ducal crown enjoy.<br />
Behold the princely phantom that ensues,<br />
Shall turn fair Italy’s long grief to joy;<br />
I speak of the fifth Azo of thy strain,<br />
By whom shall Ezelin be quelled and slain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>“Fierce Ezelin, that most inhuman lord,<br />
Who shall be deemed by men a child of hell.<br />
And work such evil, thinning with the sword<br />
Who in Ausonia’s wasted cities dwell;<br />
Rome shall no more her Anthony record,<br />
Her Marius, Sylla, Nero, Cajus fell.<br />
And this fifth Azo shall to scathe and shame<br />
Put Frederick, second Cæser of the name.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“He, with his better sceptre well contented,<br />
Shall rule the city, seated by the streams,<br />
Where Phœbus to his plaintive lyre lamented<br />
The son, ill-trusted with the father’s beams;<br />
Where Cygnus spread his pinions, and the scented<br />
Amber was wept, as fabling poet dreams.<br />
To him such honour shall the church decree;<br />
Fit guerdon of his works, and valour’s fee.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>“But does no laurel for his brother twine,<br />
Aldobrandino, who will carry cheer<br />
To Rome (when Otho, with the Ghibelline,<br />
Into the troubled capital strikes fear),<br />
And make the Umbri and Piceni sign<br />
Their shame, and sack the cities far and near;<br />
Then hopeless to relieve the sacred hold,<br />
Sue to the neighbouring Florentine for gold:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“And trust a noble brother to his hands,<br />
Boasting no dearer pledge, the pact to bind:<br />
And next, victorious o’er the German bands,<br />
Give his triumphant ensigns to the wind:<br />
To the afflicted church restore her lands,<br />
And take due vengeance of Celano’s kind.<br />
Then die, cut off in manhood’s early flower,<br />
Beneath the banners of the Papal power?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“He, dying, leaves his brother Azo heir<br />
Of Pesaro and fair Ancona’s reign,<br />
And all the cities which ’twixt Tronto are,<br />
And green Isauro’s stream, from mount to main;<br />
With other heritage, more rich and rare,<br />
Greatness of mind, and faith without a strain.<br />
All else is Fortune’s in this mortal state;<br />
But Virtue soars beyond her love and hate.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“In good Rinaldo equal worth shall shine,<br />
(Such is the promise of his early fire)<br />
If such a hope of thine exalted line.<br />
Dark Fate and Fortune wreck not in their ire.<br />
Alas! from Naples in this distant shrine,<br />
Naples, where he is hostage for his sire,<br />
His dirge is heard: A stripling of thy race,<br />
Young Obyson, shall fill his grandsire’s place.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“This lord to his dominion shall unite<br />
Gay Reggio, joined to Modena’s bold land.<br />
And his redoubted valour lend such light,<br />
The willing people call him to command.<br />
Sixth of the name, his Azo rears upright<br />
The church’s banner in his noble hand:<br />
Fair Adria’s fief to him in dower shall bring<br />
The child of second Charles, Sicilia’s king.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“Behold in yonder friendly group agreed.<br />
Many fair princes of illustrious name;<br />
Obyson, Albert famed for pious deed,<br />
Aldobrandino, Nicholas the lame.<br />
But we may pass them by, for better speed,<br />
Faenza conquered, and their feats and fame;<br />
With Adria (better held and surer gain)<br />
Which gives her title to the neighbouring main:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“And that fair town, whose produce is the rose,<br />
The rose which gives it name in Grecian speech:<br />
That, too, which fishy marshes round enclose,<br />
And Po’s two currents threat with double breach;<br />
Whose townsmen loath the lazy calm’s repose,<br />
And pray that stormy waves may lash the beach.<br />
I pass, mid towns and towers, a countless store,<br />
Argenta, Lugo, and a thousand more.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“See Nicholas, whom in his tender age,<br />
The willing people shall elect their lord;<br />
He who shall laugh to scorn the civil rage<br />
Of the rebellious Tideus and his horde;<br />
Whose infantine delight shall be to wage<br />
The mimic fight, and sweat with spear and sword:<br />
And through the discipline such nurture yields,<br />
Shall flourish as the flower of martial fields.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“By him rebellious plans are overthrown,<br />
And turned upon the rash contriver’s head;<br />
And so each stratagem of warfare blown,<br />
That vainly shall the cunning toils be spread.<br />
To the third Otho this too late is known,<br />
Of Parma and the pleasant Reggio dread;<br />
Who shall by him be spoiled in sudden strife,<br />
Of his possessions and his wretched life.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“And still the fair dominion shall increase,<br />
And without wrong its spreading bounds augment;<br />
Nor its glad subjects violate the peace,<br />
Unless provoked some outrage to resent,<br />
And hence its wealth and welfare shall not cease;<br />
And the Divine Disposer be content<br />
To let it flourish (such his heavenly love!)<br />
While the celestial spheres revolve above.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“Lo! Lionel! lo! Borse great and kind!<br />
First duke of thy fair race, his realm’s delight;<br />
Who reigns secure, and shall more triumphs find<br />
In peace, than warlike princes win in fight.<br />
Who struggling Fury’s hands shall tie behind<br />
Her back, and prison Mars, removed from sight.<br />
His fair endeavours bent to bless and stay<br />
The people, that his sovereign rule obey.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“Lo! Hercules, who may reproach his neighbour,<br />
With foot half burnt, and halting gait and slow,<br />
That at Budrio, with protecting sabre,<br />
He saved his troops from fatal overthrow;<br />
Not that, for guerdon of his glorious labour,<br />
He should distress and vex him as a foe;<br />
Chased into Barco. It were hard to say,<br />
If most he shine in peace or martial fray.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“Lucania, Puglia, and Calabria’s strand,<br />
Shall with the rumour of his prowess ring:<br />
Where he shall strive in duel, hand to hand,<br />
And gain the praise of Catalonia’s king.<br />
Him, with the wisest captains of the land<br />
His worth shall class; such fame his actions bring:<br />
And he the fief shall win like valiant knight,<br />
Which thirty years before was his of right.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“To him his grateful city owes a debt,<br />
The greatest subjects to their lord can owe;<br />
Not that he moves her from a marsh, to set<br />
Her stones, where Ceres’ fruitful treasures grow.<br />
Nor that he shall enlarge her bounds, nor yet<br />
That he shall fence her walls against the foe;<br />
Nor that he theatre and dome repairs,<br />
And beautifies her streets and goodly squares;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“Not that he keeps his lordship well defended<br />
From the winged lions’ claws and fierce attacks;<br />
Nor that, when Gallic ravage is extended,<br />
And the invader all Italia sacks,<br />
His happy state alone is unoffended;<br />
Unharassed, and ungalled by toll or tax.<br />
Not for these blessings I recount, and more<br />
His grateful realm shall Hercules adore;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“So much as that from him shall spring a pair<br />
Of brothers, leagued no less by love than blood;<br />
Who shall be all that Leda’s children were;<br />
The just Alphonso, Hippolite the good.<br />
And as each twin resigned the vital air<br />
His fellow to redeem from Stygian flood,<br />
So each of these would gladly spend his breath,<br />
And for his brother brave perpetual death.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“In these two princes’ excellent affection,<br />
Their happy lieges more assurance feel,<br />
Than if their noble town, for its protection,<br />
Were girded twice by Vulcan’s works of steel.<br />
And so Alphonso in his good direction,<br />
Justice, with knowledge and with love, shall deal,<br />
Astrea shall appear returned from heaven,<br />
To this low earth to varying seasons given.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“Well is it that his wisdom shines as bright<br />
As his good sire’s, nor is his valour less;<br />
Since here usurping Venice arms for fight,<br />
And her full troops his scanty numbers press,<br />
There she (I know not if more justly hight<br />
Mother or stepmother) brings new distress;<br />
But, if a mother, scarce to him more mild<br />
Than Progue or Medea to her child.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“This chief, what time soever he shall go<br />
Forth with his faithful crew, by night or day,<br />
By water or by land, will shame the foe,<br />
With memorable rout and disarray;<br />
And this too late Romagna’s sons shall know.<br />
Led against former friends in bloody fray,<br />
Who shall bedew the campaign with their blood,<br />
By Santern, Po, and Zaniolus’ flood.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>“This shall the Spaniard know, to his dismay,<br />
’Mid the same bounds, whom papal gold shall gain;<br />
Who shall from him Bastia win and slay,<br />
With cruel rage, her hapless Castellain,<br />
The city taken; but shall dearly pay;<br />
His crime, the town retrieved, and victor slain:<br />
Since in the rescued city not a groom<br />
Is left alive, to bear the news to Rome.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“ ’Tis he, who with his counsel and his lance,<br />
Shall win the honours of Romagna’s plain,<br />
And open to the chivalry of France<br />
The victory over Julius, leagued with Spain.<br />
Paunch-deep in human blood shall steeds advance<br />
In that fierce strife, and struggle through the slain,<br />
’Mid crowded fields, which scarce a grace supply,<br />
Where Greek, Italian, Frank, and Spaniard die.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>“Lo! who in priestly vesture clad, is crowned<br />
With purple hat, conferred in hallowed dome!<br />
’Tis he, the wise, the liberal, the renowned<br />
Hippolitus, great cardinal of Rome;<br />
Whose actions shall in every region sound,<br />
Where’er the honoured muse shall find a home:<br />
To whose glad era, by indulgent heaven,<br />
As to Augustus’ is a Maro given.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>“His deeds adorn his race, as from his car<br />
The glorious sun illumes the subject earth<br />
More than the silver moon or lesser star;<br />
So far all others he transcends in worth.<br />
I see this captain, ill bested for war,<br />
Go forth afflicted, and return in mirth:<br />
Backed by few foot, and fewer cavaliers,<br />
He homeward barks, and fifteen gallies steers.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>“Two Sigismonds, the first, the second, see;<br />
To these Alphonso’s five good sons succeed;<br />
Whose glories spread o’er seas and land shall be.<br />
The first shall wed a maid of France’s seed.<br />
This is the second Hercules; and <em>he</em>,<br />
(That you may know their every name and deed),<br />
Hippolitus; who with the light shall shine,<br />
Of his wise uncle, gilding all his line.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>“Francis the third comes next; the other two<br />
Alphonsos both;—but yet again I say,<br />
Thy line through all its branches to pursue,<br />
Fair virgin, would too long protract thy stay;<br />
And Phœbus, many times, to mortal view,<br />
Would quench and light again the lamp of day.<br />
Then, with thy leave, ’tis time the pageant cease,<br />
And I dismiss the shades and hold my peace.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>So with the lady’s leave the volume closed,<br />
Whose precepts to her will the spirits bent.<br />
And they, where Merlin’s ancient bones reposed,<br />
From the first cavern disappearing, went.<br />
Then Bradamant her eager lips unclosed,<br />
Since the divine enchantress gave consent;<br />
“And who,” she cried, “that pair of sorrowing mien,<br />
Alphonso and Hippolitus between?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>“Sighing, those youths advanced amid the show,<br />
Their brows with shame and sorrow overcast,<br />
With downward look, and gait subdued and slow:<br />
I saw the brothers shun them as they passed.”<br />
Melissa heard the dame with signs of woe,<br />
And thus, with streaming eyes, exclaim’d at last:<br />
“Ah! luckless youths, with vain illusions fed,<br />
Whither by wicked men’s bad counsel led!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>“O, worthy seed of Hercules the good,<br />
Let not their guilt beyond thy love prevail;<br />
Alas! the wretched pair are of thy blood,<br />
So many prevailing pity turn the scale!”<br />
And in a sad and softer tone pursued,<br />
“I will not further press the painful tale.<br />
Chew on fair fancy’s food: Nor deem unmeet<br />
I will not with a bitter chase the sweet.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>“Soon as to-morrow’s sun shall gild the skies<br />
With his first light, myself the way will show<br />
To where the wizard knight Rogero sties;<br />
And built with polished steel the ramparts glow:<br />
So long as through deep woods thy journey lies,<br />
Till, at the sea arrived, I shall bestow<br />
Such new instructions for the future way,<br />
That thou no more shalt need Melissa’s stay.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>All night the maid reposes in the cave,<br />
And the best part in talk with Merlin spends;<br />
While with persuasive voice the wizard grave<br />
To her Rogero’s honest love commends;<br />
Till from the vault goes forth that virgin brave,<br />
As through the sky the rising sun ascends,<br />
By path, long space obscure on either side,<br />
The weird woman still her faithful guide.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>They gain a hidden glen, which heights inclose,<br />
And mountains inaccessible to man:<br />
And they all day toil on, without repose,<br />
Where precipices frowned and torrents ran.<br />
And (what may some diversion interpose)<br />
Sweet subjects of discourse together scan,<br />
In conference, which best might make appear<br />
The rugged road less dismal and severe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>Of these the greater portion served to guide<br />
(Such the wise woman’s scope) the warlike dame;<br />
And teach by what device might be untied<br />
Rogero’s gyves, if stedfast were her flame.<br />
“If thou wert Mars himself, or Pallas,” cried<br />
The sage Melissa, “though with thee there came<br />
More than King Charles or Agramant command,<br />
Against the wizard foe thou could’st not stand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>“Besides that it is walled about with steel,<br />
And inexpugnable his tower, and high;<br />
Besides that his swift horse is taught to wheel,<br />
And caracol and gallop in mid sky,<br />
He bears a mortal shield of power to seal,<br />
As soon as ’tis exposed, the dazzled eye;<br />
And so invades each sense, the splendour shed,<br />
That he who sees the blaze remains as dead.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>“And lest to shut thine eyes, thou should’st suppose<br />
Might serve, contending with the wizard knight;<br />
How would’st thou know, when both in combat close,<br />
When he strikes home, or when eschews the fight?<br />
But to escape the blaze which blinds his foes,<br />
And render vain each necromantic sleight,<br />
Have here a speedy mean which cannot miss;<br />
Nor can the world afford a way but this.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>“King Agramant of Africa a ring,<br />
Thieved from an Indian queen by subtle guiles,<br />
Has to a baron of his following<br />
Consigned, who now precedes us by few miles;<br />
Brunello he. Who wears the gift shall bring<br />
To nought all sorceries and magic wiles.<br />
In thefts and cheats Brunello is as well<br />
Instructed, as the sage in charm and spell.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>“Brunello, he so practised and so sly<br />
As now I tell thee, by his king is sent,<br />
That he with aid of mother wit may try,<br />
And of this ring, well proved in like event,<br />
To take Rogero from the castle high;<br />
So has he boasted, by the wizard pent:<br />
And to his lord such promise did impart,<br />
Who has Rogero’s presence most at heart.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>“That his escape to thee alone may owe,<br />
Not to the king, the youthful cavalier,<br />
How to release Rogero from his foe<br />
And his enchanted cage, prepare to hear.<br />
Three days along the shingle shalt thou go,<br />
Beside the sea, whose waves will soon appear;<br />
Thee the third day shall to a hostel bring,<br />
Where he shall come who bears the virtuous ring.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>“That thou may’st recognise the man, in height<br />
Less than six palms, observe one at this inn<br />
Of black and curly hair, the dwarfish wight!<br />
Beard overgrown about the cheek and chin;<br />
With shaggy brow, swoln eyes, and cloudy sight,<br />
A nose close flattened, and a sallow skin;<br />
To this, that I may make my sketch complete,<br />
Succinctly clad, like courier, goes the cheat.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>“Thy conversation with this man shall turn<br />
Upon enchantment, spell, and mystic pact:<br />
And thou shalt, in thy talk, appear to yearn<br />
To prove the wizard’s strength, as is the fact.<br />
But, lady, let him not thy knowledge learn<br />
Of his good ring, which mars all magic act:<br />
He shall propose to bring thee as a guide<br />
To the tall castle, whither thou would’st ride.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>“Follow him close, and viewing (for a sign),<br />
Now near, the fortress of the enchanter hoar;<br />
Let no false pity there thy mind incline<br />
To stay the execution of my lore.<br />
Give him his death; but let him not divine<br />
Thy thought, nor grant him respite; for before<br />
Thine eyes, concealed by it, the caitiff slips<br />
If once he place the ring between his lips.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Discoursing thus, they came upon the sea<br />
Where Garonne near fair Bordeaux meets the tide;<br />
Here, fellow travellers no more to be,<br />
Some natural tears they drop and then divide.<br />
Duke Aymon’s child, who slumbers not till she<br />
Release her knight, holds on till even-tide:<br />
’Twas then the damsel at a hostel rested,<br />
Where Sir Brunello was already guested.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>The maid Brunello knows as soon as found<br />
(So was his image on her mind impressed),<br />
And asks him whence he came, and whither bound;<br />
And he replies and lies, as he is pressed.<br />
The dame, who is forewarned, and knows her ground,<br />
Feigns too as well as he, and lies her best:<br />
And changes sex and sect, and name and land,<br />
And her quick eye oft glances at his hand;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>Oft glances at his resless hand, in fear<br />
That he might undetected make some prize;<br />
Nor ever lets the knave approach too near,<br />
Well knowing his condition: In this guise<br />
The couple stand together, when they hear<br />
A sudden sound: but what that sound implies<br />
I, sir, shall tell hereafter with its cause;<br />
But first shall break my song with fitting pause.</td>
</tr>
