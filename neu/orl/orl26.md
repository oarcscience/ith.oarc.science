<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>Of mighty matters, sculptured in a font,<br />
Does Malagigi to his comrades tell:<br />
On them come Mandricardo and Rodomont,<br />
And forthwith battle follows fierce and fell.<br />
Discord goes scattering quarrel and affront<br />
Amid the crew: but whither, forced by spell,<br />
Fair Doralice upon her palfrey speeds,<br />
The Tartar king, and Sarzan, turn their steeds.</em></span></td>

<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>In former ages courteous ladies were,<br />
Who worshipt virtue, and not worldly gear.<br />
Women in this degenerate age are rare,<br />
To whom aught else but sordid gain is dear;<br />
But they who real goodness make their care,<br />
Nor with the avaricious many steer,<br />
In this frail life are worthy to be blest,<br />
—Held glorious and immortal when at rest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>Bradamant well would deathless praise inherit,<br />
Who nor in wealth nor empire took delight;<br />
But in Rogero’s worth, excelling spirit,<br />
In his unbounded gentlesse; and aright<br />
For this did good Duke Aymon’s daughter merit<br />
To be beloved of such a valorous knight;<br />
Who, what might be for miracles received,<br />
In future ages, for her sake achieved.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>He, with those two of Clermont, as whilere<br />
To you I in the former canto said,<br />
I say with Richardet and Aldigier,<br />
Was gone, to give the prisoned brethren aid:<br />
I told, as well how they a cavalier<br />
Of haughty look approaching had surveyed,<br />
Who bore that noble bird, by fiery birth<br />
Renewed, and ever single upon earth.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>When those three of that warrior were espied,<br />
Poised on the wing, as if about to smite,<br />
He fain by proof their prowess would have tried,<br />
And if their semblance tallied with their might.<br />
“Is there, among you, one,” the stranger cried,<br />
“Will prove upon me, which is best in fight,<br />
With lance or sword, till one to ground be cast,<br />
While in the sell his foe is seated fast?”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>“—I, at your choice,” said Aldigier, “were fain<br />
To flourish faulchion, or to tilt with spear;<br />
But this with feat, which, if you here remain,<br />
Yourself may witness, so would interfere,<br />
That for the present parley time with pain<br />
Suffices, and yet less for the career.<br />
Six hundred men, or more, we here attend,<br />
With whom we must to-day in arms contend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>“Two of our own to rescue from their foes,<br />
And free from chains, us Love and Pity sway.”<br />
He to that stranger next the reason shows<br />
Why thus in steel their bodies they array.<br />
“So just is the excuse which you oppose,”<br />
—He answered—“that I ill should this gainsay,<br />
And hold you surely for three cavaliers<br />
That seldom upon earth will find their peers.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>“With you a lance or two I would have crost<br />
To prove how great your prowess in the field;<br />
But, since ’tis shown me at another’s cost,<br />
Forego the joust, and to your reasons yield.<br />
Warmly I pray your leave against that host,<br />
To join with your good arms this helm and shield;<br />
And hope, if suffered of your band to be,<br />
No worthless comrade shall you find in me.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>Some one, meseems, may crave the stranger’s name,<br />
Who thus the champions on their road delayed,<br />
And so to partnership in arms laid claim<br />
With those three warriors, for the strife arrayed:<br />
SHE—style no more a man that martial dame—<br />
Marphisa was; that on Zerbino laid<br />
The task to bear about, against his will,<br />
Ribald Gabrina, prone to every ill.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>The two of Clermont and their bold compeer<br />
Gladly received her succour in their cause,<br />
Whom certes they believed a cavalier,<br />
And not a damsel, and not what she was.<br />
A banner was espied by Aldigier<br />
And shown the others, after little pause,<br />
Which by the wavering wind was blown about,<br />
And round about it ranged a numerous rout.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>And when, now nearer, the advancing crew<br />
Were better marked in Moorish habit stoled,<br />
For Saracens the stranger band they knew;<br />
And they upon two sorry jades behold,<br />
I’ the middle of that troop, the prisoners, who<br />
Were to the false Maganza to be sold.<br />
Marphisa cries, “Why is the feast delayed,<br />
When lo! the guests are here, for whom we stayed?”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>—“Not all,” Rogero said, “Of the array<br />
Invited, lacks as yet a numerous part:<br />
A solemn festival is held to-day,<br />
And we. to grace it more, use every art:<br />
Yet they can now but little more delay.”<br />
While thus they parley, they from other part<br />
Descry the treacherous Maganzese advance;<br />
So all was ready to begin the dance.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>They of Maganza from one quarter steer,<br />
And laden mules beneath their convoy go,<br />
Bearing vest, gold, and other costly gear.<br />
On the other side, mid faulchion, spear, and bow,<br />
Approached the captive two with doleful cheer,<br />
Who found themselves awaited by the foe;<br />
And false and impious Bertolagi heard,<br />
As with the Moorish captain he conferred.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>Nor Buovo’s nor Duke Aymon’s valiant son<br />
Can hold, when that false Maganzese they view;<br />
Against him both with rested lances run:<br />
He falls the victim of those furious two,<br />
Through belly and through pummel pierced by one,<br />
And by the other, in mid visage, through<br />
His bleeding cheeks: may like disastrous fate<br />
O’erwhelm all evil doers, soon or late!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>Marphisa with Rogero moved her horse<br />
At this, nor waited other trumpet-strain;<br />
Nor broke her lance in her impetuous course,<br />
Till in succession three had prest the plain.<br />
A mark well worthy fierce Rogero’s force,<br />
The paynim leader in a thought is slain;<br />
And with him, pierced by the same weapon, go<br />
Two others to the gloomy realms below.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>’Twas hence a foul mistake the assaulted made;<br />
It caused their utter loss, and ruined all:<br />
They of Maganza deemed themselves betrayed<br />
By the infidels, upon their leader’s fall:<br />
On the other side, so charged with hostile blade,<br />
The Moors those Maganzese assassins call;<br />
And, with fierce slaughter, either angry horde<br />
’Gan bend bow, and brandish lance and sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Rogero, charging this, or the other band,<br />
Slays ten or twenty, shifting his career;<br />
No fewer by the warlike damsel’s hand<br />
Are slaughtered and extinguished, there and here:<br />
As many men as feel the murderous brand<br />
Are from the saddle seen to disappear:<br />
Before it vanish cuirass, helms and shields,<br />
As the dry wood to fire in forest yields.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>If ever you remember to have viewed,<br />
Or heard,—what time the wasps divided are,<br />
And all the winged college is at feud,<br />
Mustering their swarms for mischief in mid air,—<br />
The greedy swallow swoop amid that brood,<br />
To mangle and devour, and kill, and tear,<br />
You must imagine so, on either part<br />
The bold Rogero and Marphisa dart.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>Not so Sir Richardet and Aldigier,<br />
Varied the dance between those squadrons twain;<br />
For, heedless of the Moors, each cavalier<br />
Had but an eye to false Maganza’s train.<br />
The brother of Rinaldo, Charles’s peer,<br />
Much courage added to much might and main;<br />
And these were now redoubled by the spite,<br />
Which against false Maganza warmed the knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>This cause made him who in his fury shared,<br />
Good Buovo’s bastard, seems a lion fell;<br />
He, without pause, each trusty helmet pared<br />
With his good blade, or crushed it like the shell<br />
Of brittle egg: and who would not have dared—<br />
Would not have shown a Hector’s worth as well,<br />
Having two such companions in the stower,<br />
Of warlike wights the very choice and flower?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>Marphisa, waging all the while the fight,<br />
On her companions often turned to gaze,<br />
And as she marked their rivalry in might,<br />
Admiring, upon all bestowed her praise;<br />
But when she on Rogero fixed her sight,<br />
Deemed him unparalleled; and in amaze,<br />
At times believed that Paladin was Mars,<br />
Who left his heaven to mix in mortal wars.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>She marvels at the champion’s horrid blows;<br />
She marvels how in vain they never fell.<br />
The iron, smit by Balisarda shows<br />
Like paper, not like stubborn plate and shell.<br />
To pieces helm and solid corslet goes,<br />
And men are severed, even to the sell;<br />
Whom into equal parts those strokes divide,<br />
Half dropt on this, and half on the other side.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>With the same downright stroke, he overbore<br />
The horse and rider, bleeding in the dust;<br />
The heads of others from their shoulders bore,<br />
And parted from the hips the bleeding bust.<br />
He often at a blow cleft five and more;<br />
And—but I doubt who hears me might distrust<br />
What of a seeming falsehood bears the impress—<br />
I would say more; but I parforce say less.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>Good Turpin, he who knows that he tells true,<br />
And leaves men to believe what they think right,<br />
Says of Rogero wondrous things, which you<br />
Hearing related, would as falsehoods slight.<br />
Thus, with Marphisa matched, that hostile crew<br />
Appears like ice, and she like burning light.<br />
Nor her Rogero with less marvel eyes,<br />
That she had marked his valour with surprise.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>As she had Mars in bold Rogero seen,<br />
Perhaps Bellona he had deemed the maid,<br />
If for a woman he had known that queen,<br />
Who seemed the contrary, in arms arrayed;<br />
And haply emulation had between<br />
The pair ensued, by whom with cruel blade<br />
Most deadly signs of prowess should be shown,<br />
Mid that vile herd, on sinew, flesh and bone.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>To rout each hostile squadron, filled with dread,<br />
Sufficed the soul and valour of the four;<br />
Nor better arms remained for them who fled<br />
Than the sharp goads which on their heels they wore.<br />
Happy was he with courser well bested!<br />
By trot or amble they set little store;<br />
And he who had no steed, here learned, dismayed,<br />
How wretched is the poor foot-soldier’s trade.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>The conqueror’s prize remained both field and prey;<br />
Nor was there footman left nor muleteer;<br />
The Moor took this, Maganza took that way;<br />
One leaves the prisoners, and one leaves the gear.<br />
With visage glad, and yet with heart more gay,<br />
The four united each captive cavalier;<br />
Nor were less diligent to free from chains<br />
The prisoned pages, and unload the wains.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>Besides good quantity of silver fine,<br />
Wrought into different vessels, with a store<br />
Of feminine array, of fair design,<br />
Embroidered round about with choicest lore,<br />
And suit of Flemish tapestry, framed to line<br />
Royal apartments, wrought with silk and ore—<br />
—They, ’mid more costly things in plenty spread—<br />
Discovered flasks of wine, and meat and bread.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>When now the conquering troop their temples bare,<br />
All see they have received a damsel’s aid,<br />
Known by her curling locks of golden hair,<br />
And delicate and beauteous face displayed:<br />
Her the knights honoured much, and to declare<br />
Her name, so well deserving glory, prayed;<br />
Nor she, that ever was of courteous mood<br />
Among her friends, their instances withstood.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>With viewing her they cannot sate their eyes,<br />
Who in the battle such had her espied,<br />
She speaks but with the Child, but him descries;<br />
None prizes, values none, ’twould seem, beside.<br />
Meanwhile that ready spread a banquet lies,<br />
To them is by the servants notified.<br />
This they had served about a neighbouring fountain,<br />
Screened from the sun by an o’ershadowing mountain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>This spring was one of those four fountains rare,<br />
Of those in France produced by Merlin’s sleight;<br />
Encompassed round about with marble fair,<br />
Shining and polished, and then milk more white.<br />
There in the stone choice figures chisseled were,<br />
By that magician’s godlike labour dight;<br />
Save voice was wanting, these you might have thought<br />
Were living and with nerve and spirit fraught.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>Here, to appearance, from the forest prest<br />
A cruel Beast and hideous to the eye,<br />
With teeth of wolf, an ass’s head and crest,<br />
A carcass with long famine lean and dry,<br />
And lion’s claws; a fox in all the rest:<br />
Which seemed to ravage France and Italy,<br />
And Spain and England’s desolated strands,<br />
Europe and Asia, and in fine all lands.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>The beast the low and those of proudest port<br />
Had slain or maimed throughout this earthly ball;<br />
Yea, fiercest seemed on those of noble sort,<br />
Sovereign and satrap, prince and peer, to fall;<br />
And made most havoc in the Roman court;<br />
For it had slaughtered Pope and Cardinal:<br />
Had filled St. Peter’s beauteous seat with scathe,<br />
And brought foul scandal on the HOLY FAITH.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>Whate’er she touches, wall or rampire steep,<br />
Goes to the ground’ where’er the monster wends,<br />
Each fortress opens; neither castle-keep,<br />
Nor city from her rage its wealth defends.<br />
Honours divine as well that Beast would reap,<br />
It seems (while the besotted rabble bends)<br />
And claim withal, as to its keeping given,<br />
The sacred keys which open Hell and Heaven.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>Approaching next, is seen a cavalier,<br />
His temples circled with imperial bay;<br />
Three youths with him in company appear,<br />
With golden lilies wrought in their array:<br />
A lion seems against that monster drear<br />
To issue, with the same device as they:<br />
The name of these are on the marble read,<br />
Some on their skirt, some written overhead.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>Of those who so against Beast advance,<br />
One to the hilt has in his life-blood dyed<br />
His faulchion, Francis styled the first of France;<br />
With Austrian Maximilian at his side:<br />
In one, who gores his gullet with the lance,<br />
The emperor Charles the fifth is signified:<br />
Henry the eighth of England is he hight,<br />
Who in the monster’s breast a dart has pight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>The TENTH, in writing, on his back displayed<br />
The Lion, who that Beast is seen to hold<br />
By both his ears, and him so well has bayed,<br />
That thither troop assistants manifold.<br />
’Twould seem the world all fear aside has laid;<br />
And, in amendment of their errors old,<br />
Thitherward nobles troop, but these are few;<br />
And so that hideous Beast those hunters slew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>In wonder stood long time that warlike train,<br />
Desirous, as the storied work they traced,<br />
To know by hands of whom that Beast was slain,<br />
Which had so many smiling lands defaced,<br />
The names unknown to them, though figured plain<br />
Upon the marble which that fountain cased:<br />
They one another prayed, if any guessed<br />
That story, he would tell it to the rest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>Vivian on Malagigi turned his eyes,<br />
Who listening stood this while, yet spake he nought.<br />
“With thee,” he cried, “to tell the meaning lies,<br />
Who are they, by whose darts and lances dies<br />
That shouldst by what I see in this be taught:<br />
The hideous monster, that to bay is brought?”<br />
—And Malagigi—“Hitherto their glory<br />
No author has consigned to living story.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“The chiefs whose names are graved upon the stone,<br />
Not yet have moved upon this worldly stage;<br />
But will within seven hundred years be known,<br />
To the great honour of a future age.<br />
What time king Arthur filled the British throne,<br />
This fountain Merlin made, enchanter sage;<br />
Who things to come upon the marble fair<br />
Made sculpture by a cunning artist’s care.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“This Beast, when weights and measures first were found,<br />
Came out of nether hell; when on the plain,<br />
Common before, men fixed the landmark’s bound,<br />
And fashioned written pacts with jealous pain;<br />
Yet walked not every where, at first, her round:<br />
Unvisited she left yet many a reign:<br />
Through diverse places in our time she wends;<br />
But the vile rabble and the crowd offends.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“From the beginning even to our day,<br />
Aye has that monster grown, and aye will grow;<br />
And till much time be past will grow alway:<br />
Was never mightier, nor worse cause of woe.<br />
That Python, oft the theme of ancient lay,<br />
So passing wonderful and fierce in show,<br />
Came not by half this loathsome monster nigh,<br />
In all its foulness and deformity.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“Dread desolation shall it make; nor place<br />
Will unpolluted or untainted be;<br />
And you in the mysterious sculptured trace<br />
But little of its foul iniquity.<br />
The world, when weary of imploring grace,<br />
Those worthy peers (whose names you sculptured see,<br />
And which shall blazing carbuncle outshine),<br />
To succour in its utmost need combine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“No one shall more that cruel beast molest<br />
Than Francis, who the realm of France will steer,<br />
Who justly shall be forward in this quest,<br />
Whom none shall go beyond, whom few shall peer<br />
Since he in splendour, as in all the rest,<br />
Wanting in worth, will many make appear<br />
Who whilom perfect seemed; so fade and yield<br />
All lesser glories to the sun revealed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“In the first year of his successful reign,<br />
The crown yet ill secure upon his front,<br />
He threads the Alps, and makes their labour vain,<br />
Who would against his arms maintain the Mount.<br />
Impelled by generous and by just disdain,<br />
The unavenged as yet is that affront,<br />
Which a French army suffered from their rage,<br />
Who poured from beast-cote, field, and pasturage:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“And thence shall into the rich Lombard plain<br />
Descend, with all the flower of France, and so<br />
Shall break the Switzer, that henceforth in vain<br />
Would he uplift his horn against the foe.<br />
To the sore scandal of the Church and Spain,<br />
And to the Florentine’s much scathe and woe,<br />
By him that famous castle shall be quelled,<br />
Which inexpugnable whilere was held.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“In quelling it his honoured faulchion, more<br />
Than other arms, availing shall be found;<br />
Which first that cruel Beast to death will gore,<br />
The foul destroyer of each country round:<br />
Parforce will every standard fly before<br />
That conquering faulchion, or be cast to ground:<br />
Nor, stormed by it, will rampart, fosse, or wall,<br />
Secure the city, they surround, from fall.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“Imbued with every generous quality,<br />
Which can in great commander be combined,<br />
—Prudence like his who won Thrasymenae<br />
And Trebbia’s field, with Caesar’s daring mind,<br />
And Alexander’s fortune, him I see;<br />
Without which all designs are mist and wind;<br />
Withal, so passing liberal, I in none<br />
Mark his example or his parragon.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>So Malagigi to his comrades said,<br />
And moved in them desire some name to hear<br />
Of others, who had laid that monster dead,<br />
Which to slay others had been used whilere.<br />
Among the first Bernardo’s name was read,<br />
Much vaunted in the writing of the Seer:<br />
Who said, “Through him as known as Bibbiena<br />
As her own neighbour Florence and Siena.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“More forward in this chase shall no one show<br />
Than Sigismond, than Lewis, and than John;<br />
Each to that hideous beast a cruel foe;<br />
One a Gonzaga, one of Arragon,<br />
And one a Salviati: with them go<br />
Francis Gonzaga and Frederick his son:<br />
Brother and son-in-law, their aid afford;<br />
One chief Ferrara’s, one Urbino’s lord.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“Of one of these the son, Sir Guidobald,<br />
Will not by sire, or other, distanced be:<br />
With Ottobon de Flisco, Sinibald<br />
Chases the Beast, both striving equally:<br />
Lewis de Gazolo its neck has galled<br />
With one of those keen darts, Apollo’s fee,<br />
Given with his bow, what time as well his glaive,<br />
The god of war, to gird that warrior, gave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“Two Hercules and two Hippolyti<br />
Of Este, a Hercules and Hippolyte<br />
Of the Gonzagas’ and the Medici,<br />
Hunt and fatigue the monster in his flight:<br />
Nor Julian lets his good son pass him by;<br />
Nor bold Ferrant his brother; nor less wight<br />
Is Andrew Doria; nor by any one<br />
Is Francis Sforza in the chase outdone.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“Of good Avalo’s glorious lineage bred,<br />
Two chiefs that mountain for their bearing show,<br />
Which, hiding him, from dragon-feet to head,<br />
The wicked Typheus seems to keep below.<br />
’Mid those combined, to lay the monster dead,<br />
Shall none more forward than this couple go:<br />
Him Francis of Pescara names the text;<br />
Alphonso, hight of Guasto, is the next.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“But where leave I Gonsalvo Ferrant, who<br />
Is held in such esteem, the pride of Spain?<br />
So praised by Malagigi, that him few<br />
Equal among the worthies of that train.<br />
William, surnamed of Monferrato, view<br />
’Mid those that have the hideous monster slain:<br />
But these are few compared with numbers round,<br />
Whom that despiteous Beast shall kill or wound.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>To converse gay the friends themselves addrest,<br />
And seemly pastimes, when their meal was done,<br />
Through the hot noontide, and fine carpets prest,<br />
’Mid shrubs, by which the limpid river run.<br />
Vivian and Malagigi, that the rest<br />
Might be more tranquil, watched with armour on;<br />
When unaccompanied they saw a dame,<br />
Who quickly towards their place of shelter came;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>Hippalca she; from whom was torn away<br />
Frontino, that good horse, by Rodomont:<br />
Him had she long pursued the former day,<br />
And now with prayer, now followed with affront.<br />
Which booting nought, she had retraced her way,<br />
To seek Rogero out in Agrismont;<br />
And, how I know not, heard upon her round,<br />
He here with Richardetto would be found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>And, for to her well known was that repair,<br />
Used by her often, she herself addrest<br />
Towards the fount, and in that quarter fair<br />
Found him, and in what manner, was exprest;<br />
But like embassadress, who—wise and ware—<br />
Better than was enjoined performs a hest,<br />
When Richardetto she beheld, made show<br />
As if she good Rogero did not know.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>She turned her wholly to Sir Richardet,<br />
As bound direct to him; and, on his side,<br />
He who well knew her, straight uprose and met,<br />
And asked that damsel whitherward she hied.<br />
Hippalca, with her eyes yet red and wet<br />
From her long weeping, sighing deeply, cried,<br />
But cried aloud, that young Rogero, near<br />
The warrior she addrest, her tale might hear:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>“I from Mount Alban with a courser sped;<br />
(So your good sister had commanded me)<br />
A horse much loved by her, and highly bred;<br />
Frontino is yclept that charger free;<br />
And him I more than thirty miles had led<br />
Towards Marseilles, where she designed to be<br />
Within few days; by her enjoined to wend<br />
Thither, and her arrival there attend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>“I in the sure belief pursued my course,<br />
Was none so stout of heart, if I should say<br />
How Sir Rinaldo’s sister owned the horse,<br />
He would presume to take that steed away.<br />
But vain was my design; for him parforce<br />
A Saracen took from me yesterday:<br />
Nor, when to him his master’s name I read,<br />
Will that bold robber render back the steed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>“Him I to-day and all the day before<br />
Have prayed, and prayer and menace proving vain,<br />
Aye cursing him and execrating sore,<br />
Have left at little distance; where, with pain,<br />
Both to his courser and himself, the Moor,<br />
As best he can, a combat does maintain<br />
Against a knight, who him so hard has prest,<br />
I trust my injury shall be redrest.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>At this Rogero, leaping on his feet,<br />
Who scarcely had endured the whole to hear,<br />
To Richardetto turned; and, as a meet<br />
Guerdon for his good deed, the cavalier<br />
Did, with beseechings infinite, entreat<br />
To let him singly with that damsel steer,<br />
Until she showed the paynim, who by force<br />
Had wrested from her hands that goodly horse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>Richardet (though it seems discourtesy<br />
To yield to other champion that emprize,<br />
Which by himself should terminated be)<br />
Yet with Rogero’s earnest suit complies;<br />
Who takes farewell of that good company,<br />
And with the damsel on her journey hies.<br />
And leaves those others, whom his feats confound,<br />
Not merely lost in wonder, but astoud.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>To him Hippalca said, when she apart<br />
Had drawn him to some distance from the rest,<br />
She was dispatched by her that in her heart<br />
Bore of his worth the image so imprest;<br />
—And added, without using farther art,<br />
All that her lady had to him addrest;<br />
And if she told another tale whilere,<br />
Of Richardetto she was then in fear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>She added how the author of that deed<br />
Had also said to her with mickle pride;<br />
“Because I know Rogero owns the steed,<br />
More willingly I take him from his guide.<br />
If he would repossess the courser, read<br />
To him what I have no desire to hide,<br />
I am that Rodomont, whose martial worth<br />
Scatters its splendour through this ample earth.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>Listening, the visage of the youthful knight<br />
Showed with what rage his heart was in a flame,<br />
As well as that the horse was his delight;<br />
As well upon account of whence it came;<br />
And also that ’twas reft in his despite;<br />
He sees dishonour will ensue and blame,<br />
Save he from Rodomont redeems the prey,<br />
And with a due revenge that wrong repay.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>With him, without repose, the damsel rides,<br />
Who with his foe would bring him front to front;<br />
And thither journies where the road divides,<br />
And one branch cuts the plain, one climbs the mount,<br />
And either pathway to that valley guides,<br />
Where she had newly left King Rodomont,<br />
The mountain track was short, but trod with pain;<br />
That other longer far, but smooth and plain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>Hippalca’s ardour to retrieve the prey,<br />
And upon Rodomont’s avenge the wrong,<br />
Incites that maid the mountain to assay;<br />
By which (as said) the journey was less long:<br />
While Mandricardo, Rodomont, and they<br />
Of whom I erst made mention in my song,<br />
That easier track across the level hold;<br />
And thus encounter not Rogero bold.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>Until King Agramant shall succoured be,<br />
Suspended is their quarrel (in what wise<br />
You know), and in the champions’ company<br />
Doralice, cause of all their discord, hies.<br />
Now hear the upshot of this history!<br />
Their way directly by that fountain lies,<br />
Beside whose margin are in pastime met<br />
Marphisa and Aldigier and Richardet.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>Marphisa had, at her companions’ prayer,<br />
Cloathed her in female ornaments and vest,<br />
Of those, which by Maganza’s traitour were<br />
Late to Lanfusa, in full trust, addrest;<br />
And, though the appearance of that maid was rare<br />
Without her corslet, casque and all the rest,<br />
—At their entreaty, these for once laid down—<br />
She deigned to seem a maid and donned the gown.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>As soon as Mandricardo saw her face,<br />
In trust that, could he win her in affray,<br />
He would that maid, in recompense and place<br />
Of Doralice, to Rodomont convey;<br />
As if Love trafficked in such contracts base,<br />
And lover could his lady change away,<br />
Nor yet with reason at the event be pained,<br />
If he in losing one another gained.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>Hence with a damsel to provide the peer,<br />
That he himself the other may retain;<br />
Deeming her worthy any cavalier,<br />
He would by force of arms the maid obtain;<br />
And, as if he could suddenly hold dear<br />
This maid as that, on him bestow the gain;<br />
And all of those, whom he about her spied,<br />
Forthwith to joust and single fight defied.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>Vivian and Malagigi (who were dight<br />
In arms, as guard and surety for the rest,)<br />
One and the other champion—prompt for fight,<br />
Rose lightly from the herbage which they prest,<br />
Deeming they had to joust with either knight;<br />
But Rodomont, who came not on this quest,<br />
No motion made as he a course would run;<br />
So that they had to tourney but with one.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>Sir Vivian is the first who moves his horse,<br />
With mighty heart, and lays his weapon low;<br />
And he, that Tartar king, renowned for force,<br />
With greater puissance meets the coming foe.<br />
His lance each warrior levels in the course<br />
Where he bests trusts to plant the furious blow.<br />
Vainly Sir Vivian’s spear the casque offends;<br />
Nor throws that paynim knight, nor even bends.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>That Tartar’s harder weapon makes the shield<br />
Of Vivian, at their onset, fly like grass;<br />
And, tumbling from his saddle on the field,<br />
Extends the champion amid flowers and grass.<br />
To run his chance Sir Malagigi, steeled,<br />
Did to his brother’s succour quickly pass;<br />
But (such that warrior’s hurry to be near)<br />
Rather accompanied, than venged the peer.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>The other of those brethren armed before<br />
His cousin, and had backed his courser wight;<br />
And, having first defied, encountered sore,<br />
Spurring with flowing rein, the stranger knight.<br />
Against the tempered helm that pagan wore<br />
Sounded the blow, an inch below the sight:<br />
Heaven-high the truncheon flew, in fragments broke,<br />
But the stout pagan winced not for the stroke.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>Him on the left side smote that paynim peer,<br />
And (for the blow was with huge force designed)<br />
Little his shield, and less his iron gear,<br />
Availed, which opened like the yielding rhind:<br />
The weapon pierced his shoulder; Aldigier<br />
Now right now left upon his horse inclined;<br />
Then him, ’mid grass and flowers, his comrades view,<br />
With arms of crimson, face of pallid, hue.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>Next Richardetto comes, and for the blow<br />
Intended, levels such a mighty lance,<br />
He showed himself, as he was wont to show,<br />
Worthy to be a paladin of France;<br />
And has stamped signs of this upon the foe.<br />
If he had warred on him with equal chance;<br />
But prostrate rolled, encumbered by his steed;<br />
Nor fell the courser through his lord’s misdeed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>When knight appeared not on the other side,<br />
Who should in joust the paynim king affront,<br />
He thought the damsel was his prize, and hied<br />
Thither, where she was seated by the fount.<br />
And—“Lady, you are mine,” the Tartar cried,<br />
“Save other champion in your succour mount;<br />
Nor can you make denial or excuse,<br />
Since such the right of war and common use.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>Marphisa raised her face with haughty cheer,<br />
And answered him: “Thy judgment wanders far;<br />
I will concede thy sentence would be clear,<br />
Concluding I am thine by right of war,<br />
If either were my lord or cavalier<br />
Of those, by thee unhorsed in bloody jar:<br />
Nor theirs am I, nor other’s, but my own,<br />
Who wins me, wins me from myself alone.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>“I too with lance and sword do doughty deed,<br />
And more than one good knight on earth have laid.<br />
—Give me,” she cried, “my armour and my steed.”<br />
And readily her squires that hest obeyed:<br />
Then in her waistcoat stood, of flowing weed<br />
Despoiled, with well-knit from and charms displayed;<br />
And in all points (such strength she shewed and grace)<br />
Resembled heavenly Mars, except her face.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>The damsel donned her sword, when armed all o’er,<br />
And on her courser leapt with nimble spring;<br />
And, right and left, she made him, thrice or more<br />
Poised on his haunches, turn in narrow ring.<br />
And, levelling the sturdy lance she bore,<br />
Defied, and next assailed, the Tartar king.<br />
So combating with Peleus’ son, of yore,<br />
Penthesilaea warred on Trojan shore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>Like brittle crystal, in that proud career,<br />
The weapons at the rest to pieces went;<br />
Yet neither of those warriors, ’twould appear,<br />
Backwards one inch at their encounter bent.<br />
Marphisa, who would willingly be clear<br />
What of a closer fight would be the event,<br />
For a new combat with the paynim lord,<br />
Wheeled, to attack that warrior with the sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>That Tartar cursed the elements and sky,<br />
When her he saw remaining in her sell;<br />
And she, who thought to make his buckler fly,<br />
Cursed heaven as loudly as that infidel.<br />
Already were their faulchions raised on high,<br />
Which on the enchanted arms like hammers fell:<br />
Enchanted arms both combatants enclose,<br />
Never more needed by those deadly foes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>So perfect are the champions’ plate and chain,<br />
They thrust or cut of spear or faulchion stay;<br />
So that the two the battle might maintain,<br />
Throughout this and throughout another day:<br />
But Rodomont leaps in between the twain,<br />
And taxes Mandricardo with delay;<br />
Crying, “If battle here is to be done,<br />
Finish we that which we to-day begun.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>“We made a truce, thou knowest, upon pact<br />
Of furnishing our baffled forces aid;<br />
Nor foe in joust or fight can be attacked<br />
By us with justice till this debt be paid.”<br />
Then to Marphisa he in reverent act<br />
Addressed himself, and of that courier said;<br />
And next recounted to the martial dame,<br />
How seeking aid for Agramant he came.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>Next prays not only with that Tartar knight<br />
She will abandon or defer the fray;<br />
But that, Troyano’s valiant son to right,<br />
She will, together with them, wend her way;<br />
By which her warlike fame a higher flight,<br />
More easily may, even to heaven, assay,<br />
Than in a quarrel of such paltry guise,<br />
Which offers hindrance to such fair emprize.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>Marphisa, who had evermore in thought<br />
To prove the paladins of Charles, and who<br />
To France was over land and ocean brought,<br />
From clime so distant, with no other view,<br />
Than by her own experience to be taught<br />
If their far-spread renown were false or true,<br />
Resolved together with the troop to speed,<br />
As soon as she had heard their monarch’s need.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>Meanwhile Rogero, with that guiding may,<br />
Had vainly by the rugged pathway sped;<br />
Who that king Rodomont another way<br />
Had taken, when he reached the mountain, read;<br />
And thinking, that he was not far away,<br />
And the road straight towards that fountain led,<br />
Trotting in haste behind the Sarzan hied,<br />
Where he new prints upon the path espied.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>Hippalca he to Mont Albano prayed,<br />
To wend, which distant one day’s journey lies;<br />
Because to seek anew that fountain-glade,<br />
Would be to wander in too wide a guise.<br />
And that she need not doubt withal, he said,<br />
But that he would retrieve the ravished prize.<br />
And, were she in Mount Alban—or where’er—<br />
Vowed she the tidings speedily should hear,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XC.</td>
<td>And gave the letter to that maid to bear,<br />
Which, writ by him, he in his bosom wore,<br />
And added many matters, with the prayer,<br />
She would excuse him by her friendly lore.<br />
Hippalca in her memory fixt, with care,<br />
The whole; took leave, and turned her horse once more:<br />
Nor ceased that faithful messenger to ride<br />
Till she Mount Alban reached at evening-tide.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCI.</td>
<td>Rogero followed fast the paynim knight,<br />
Tracked o’er the level by those footsteps new,<br />
But overtook him not, till he got sight,<br />
Beside the fount, of Mandricardo too.<br />
Already either had his promise plight,<br />
He nought unknown to his compeer would do,<br />
Till they had succour to that host conveyed,<br />
On which King Charles his yoke had nearly laid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCII.</td>
<td>Arrived, Rogero knew Frontino gay,<br />
And, through that courser, knew the knight astride;<br />
And on his lance with bending shoulder lay,<br />
And in fierce tone the African defied.<br />
Job was outdone by Rodomont that day,<br />
In that the king subdued his haughty pride,<br />
And the fell fight which he had ever used<br />
To seek with every instance, he refused.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCII.</td>
<td>The first day this and last, that e’er in fight<br />
King Rodomont refused his part to bear!<br />
But his desire appeared to him so right,<br />
In succour of his sovereign to repair;<br />
That if he had believed he clutched the knight<br />
Faster than nimble leopard gripes the hare,<br />
He not so far his purpose would forego,<br />
As on his prey to waste a passing blow.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIV.</td>
<td>Add, that he knows Rogero is the peer<br />
Who him for good Frontino now assails;<br />
—So famous, that no other cavalier<br />
Like him such eminence of glory scales;<br />
—The man, of whom he gladly would be clear,<br />
By proof, how much in battle he avails:<br />
Yet shuns the combat, proffered on his part;<br />
So much his monarch’s siege has he at heart.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCV.</td>
<td>Three hundred miles, a thousand, would he ride,<br />
—Were it not so—to purchase such affray;<br />
But he, if him Achilles had defied,<br />
Had done no otherwise than as I say;<br />
So deeply did the covering ashes hide<br />
That fire beneath, whose fury stifled lay:<br />
He told why he refused the strife; and prayed,<br />
As well Rogero the design to aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVI.</td>
<td>Adding that he, in doing so, would do<br />
What to his lord a faithful vassal owes;<br />
Still, when the siege was raised, might they renew<br />
And terminate their deadly strife by blows.<br />
To him Rogero cried, “The fight with you<br />
I freely will defer, till from his foes<br />
King Agramant be rescued by the sword;<br />
Provided first Frontino be restored.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVII.</td>
<td>“Would you that I delay to prove by deed,<br />
That you have acted in unworthy sort,<br />
—Nor did, like valiant man, to take my steed<br />
Thus from a woman—till we meet at court,<br />
Render me my Frontino back, or read,<br />
Upon no other ground, will I support<br />
That battle shall not be between us two;<br />
Nor will accord an hour of truce to you.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVIII.</td>
<td>While of that African he so demands<br />
Frontino, or him threats with instant fray;<br />
And either still the other’s claim withstands,<br />
Nor this the steed will grant, nor that delay;<br />
King Mandricardo stirs, on the other hand,<br />
Another strife; who sees that ensign gay<br />
Rogero on his shield was wont to wear,<br />
The bird which reigns o’er other fowls of air.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIX.</td>
<td>He bore on azure field that eagle white,<br />
The beauteous ensign of the Trojan throng:<br />
Such glorious bearing showed that youthful knight,<br />
Because he drew his line from Hector strong.<br />
But Mandricardo knew not of this right,<br />
Nor would endure—and deemed a crying wrong,<br />
That any other but himself should wield<br />
Famed Hector’s argent eagle on his shield.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">C.</td>
<td>King Mandricardo is like blazon wore<br />
The bird of Ide, which bore off Ganymede:<br />
How in the castle perilous of yore,<br />
He gained that noble ensign for his meed,<br />
—That enterprize I ween, with matter more,<br />
You bear in mind, and how, for his good deed,<br />
The fairy gave it him with all the gear,<br />
Erst given by Vulcan to the Trojan peer.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CI.</td>
<td>The Tartar and Rogero had before<br />
Engaged in battle, only on this quest,<br />
Divided by what accident, my lore<br />
Recites not, as already manifest:<br />
Nor had till now those knights encountered more:<br />
When Mandricardo sees that bird imprest<br />
On the Child’s shield, he shouts with threatening cry<br />
To young Rogero: “Take my proud defy!”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CII.</td>
<td>“Audacious man, mine ensign do’st thou wear,<br />
Nor this to-day for the first time, is said;<br />
And think’st thou, madman, I will thee forbear,<br />
Because for once to spare thee I was led?<br />
But since nor menace nor yet counsel are<br />
Of force to drive this folly from thy head,<br />
It shall appear how much it had been best<br />
For thee forthwith to have obeyed my hest.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIII.</td>
<td>“As fire, whereon dry, heated wood is strown,<br />
Roused by a little puff, at once ascends,<br />
So burns Rogero’s wrath, to fury blown,<br />
By the first word with which that king offends.<br />
“Thou thinkest,” he exclaims, “to bear me down,<br />
Because his knight as well with me contends:<br />
But learn that I can win in fighting field<br />
From him the horse, from thee good Hector’s shield.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIV.</td>
<td>“Yet once before—nor is it long ago—<br />
Twixt us in battle was this question tried:<br />
But I that day restrained the murderous blow,<br />
Because thou hadst no faulchion at thy side.<br />
These shall be deeds, that strife was but a show;<br />
And ill this argent bird shall thee betide;<br />
This is the ancient bearing of my line;<br />
Tis thou usurpest what by right is mine.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CV.</td>
<td>—“Say rather, thou usurpest mine from me”;<br />
Cried Mandricardo; and that faulchion drew,<br />
Which lately, underneath the greenwood tree,<br />
Orlando from his hand in fury threw.<br />
The Child, who could not aught but courteous be,<br />
(Such was his gentle nature) at the view<br />
Of Mandricardo, with his faulchion drawn,<br />
Let fall his ready lance upon the lawn;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVI.</td>
<td>And at the same time, strained his goodly sword;<br />
And better braced the covering shield he wore:<br />
But ’twixt those combatants leapt Argier’s lord,<br />
And quick Marphisa spurred the pair before;<br />
And one this foe, the other that implored,<br />
And both besought, that they would strive no more.<br />
King Rodomont complains the Tartar knight<br />
Has violated twice the compact plight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVII.</td>
<td>First, in belief he should Marphisa gain,<br />
He more than once had jousted for that fair;<br />
Now to bear off Rogero’s ensign fain,<br />
He for king Agramant shows little care.<br />
—“If thus” (said Rodomont) “you faith maintain,<br />
To finish our own combat better were,<br />
A cause of strife more fitting and more due<br />
Than either of the pleas maintained by you.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVIII.</td>
<td>“On this condition was the treaty plight,<br />
And the accord between us now in force;<br />
When I with thee shall have performed the fight,<br />
I next shall answer him about the horse:<br />
You then with him, if you survive, your right<br />
Shall to the shield maintain in warlike course.<br />
But I such work shall give you, I conceive,<br />
As will small labour for Rogero leave.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIX.</td>
<td>—“The bargain which thou hopest thou shalt not have,”<br />
(King Mandricardo answered Rodomont)<br />
“I will accord thee more than thou do’st crave,<br />
And trust to make thee sweat from feet to front.<br />
And to bestow on others, much shall save,<br />
As water never fails in plenteous font;<br />
And for Rogero and a thousand more,<br />
And all the world beside reserve a store.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CX.</td>
<td>Their fury waxed, and angrier words ensued,<br />
Now upon this and now upon that side.<br />
With Rodomont and with the Child at feud,<br />
Fierce Mandricardo both at once defied.<br />
Rogero, not endowed with suffering mood,<br />
Would hear no more of peace, but vengeance cried.<br />
Now here Marphisa hurried, and now there,<br />
But could not singly such an ill repair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXI.</td>
<td>As peasant, when a river saps its mounds,<br />
And seeking vent the oozing waters drop,<br />
Hastening to shut the stream within its bounds,<br />
And save his pastures and expected crop,<br />
Dams right and left; yet him the stream confounds:<br />
For, if he here the sinking ruin prop,<br />
There he beholds the rotten dyke give out,<br />
And from thick seams the restless water spout,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXII.</td>
<td>So, while the Tartar and Rogero rage,<br />
And Rodomont, in hurly-burly fray,<br />
For each of these would fiercest battle wage,<br />
And would outgo his fears in that assay,<br />
Marphisa seeks their fury to assuage,<br />
And strives, and time and trouble throws away;<br />
For as she makes one knight from strife retire,<br />
She sees the others re-engage with ire.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIII.</td>
<td>Marphisa, to appease the warriors bent,<br />
Exclaimed, “Sirs, listen to my better lore;<br />
A good remembrance ’tis, all argument<br />
To leave until we Agramant restore.<br />
If each is on his own design intent,<br />
With Mandricardo will I strive once more;<br />
And fain would see, according to his word,<br />
If he can conquer me with spear and sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIV.</td>
<td>“But if, to aid our sovereign, duty call,<br />
Him let us aid, nor civil discord breed.”<br />
—“To ground, through me, such project shall not fall,”<br />
Rogero said, “so he restore my steed.<br />
Let him resign that horse, or—once for all.<br />
I say again—to his defence take heed.<br />
I either here my parting breath will yield,<br />
Or on my courser will return afield.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXV.</td>
<td>—“Twere not so easy to obtain this quest<br />
As ’twere that other,” Rodomont replied;<br />
And thus pursued: “I unto thee protest,<br />
If any evil shall our king betide,<br />
Thine is the fault not mine; for I am prest<br />
To do whate’er is fitting, on my side.”<br />
Small heed to that protest Rogero paid,<br />
And stung by fury, griped his trenchant blade.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVI.</td>
<td>On Argier’s king he sprang, like savage boar,<br />
Encountering him with shoulder and with shield;<br />
And him disordered and distrest so sore,<br />
That with one stirrup’s loss, the monarch reeled.<br />
—“Rogero,” Mandricardo cried, “give o’er,<br />
Or else with me divide the battle-field”;<br />
And struck, this said, with worse than felon spite,<br />
Upon the morion of that youthful knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVII.</td>
<td>Even to his courser’s neck Rogero bends;<br />
Nor, when he would, himself can rear;<br />
Because the sword of Ulien’s son descends<br />
As well upon the youthful cavalier;<br />
And, but that adamant his face defends,<br />
Across the cheeks his tempered helm would sheer.<br />
The Child, in anguish, opens either hand;<br />
And this the bridle drops and that the brand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVIII.</td>
<td>Him o’er the field his courser bears away;<br />
On earth the faulchion lies, which he let go:<br />
Marphisa (with Rogero’s through that day,<br />
Comrade in arms) appeared like fire to glow,<br />
Enraged, that two one knight should overlay;<br />
And, as magnanimous and stout, for foe<br />
Singled King Mandricardo out, and sped,<br />
With all her might, stroke upon his head.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIX.</td>
<td>Rodomont o’er the plain pursues his man.<br />
—Another stroke, and he has lost the horse!<br />
But Richardetto drives, and Vivian,<br />
Between the Child and paynim in that course.<br />
This warrior at the king of Argier ran,<br />
And from Rogero severed him by force;<br />
That (it was Vivian) in Rogero’s hand,<br />
Now from the blow recovered, placed his brand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXX.</td>
<td>As soon as to himself the Child returns,<br />
And is by Vivian armed with sword again,<br />
To venge the injury that stripling burns,<br />
And runs at Rodomont with flowing rein,<br />
Like lion, whom a bull upon his horns<br />
Has lifted, though he feels this while no pain,<br />
So him his heat of blood, disdain, and ire,<br />
To venge that cruel outrage goad and fire.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXI.</td>
<td>Rogero storms upon the paynim’s crest;<br />
And, could that knight recover his own brand,<br />
Which by foul felony (as erst exprest)<br />
Was ravished from the youthful warrior’s hand,<br />
I well believe that the descending pest<br />
Rodomont’s iron casque will ill withstand;<br />
That casque which Babel’s king bade forge, who sought<br />
To war on Heaven in his presumptuous thought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXII.</td>
<td>Discord, believing nothing could ensue<br />
But stir, and strife, and combat on that head;<br />
And that there was no place, amid the crew,<br />
For truce or treaty, to her sister said,<br />
That she, her well-beloved monks to view,<br />
Might now again with her securely tread.<br />
Let them depart; and mark we where in front<br />
Rogero has sore wounded Rodomont.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIII.</td>
<td>Rogero’s blow was levelled with such spite,<br />
That this upon Frontino’s crupper made<br />
The helmet and the shell of iron smite,<br />
In which that Saracen his limbs arrayed;<br />
And he, three times or four, to left and right,<br />
—As if about to fall—head-foremost, swayed;<br />
And would have lost withal his trusty brand,<br />
But that the hilt was fastened to his hand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIV.</td>
<td>Marphisa has king Mandricardo prest<br />
Meanwhile, and makes him sweat breast, front, and face;<br />
And he Marphisa has as sore distrest:<br />
But such good plates each valiant bosom case,<br />
Impassable is either iron vest;<br />
And both have hitherto maintained their place.<br />
But, at a turn her martial courser made,<br />
Marphisa needed young Rogero’s aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXV.</td>
<td>Marphisa’s martial steed, in turning short,<br />
Where a firm footing that soft mead denied,<br />
On the moist surface slipt, and in such sort,<br />
That he fell, helpless, on his better side;<br />
And, as he rose in haste and lacked support,<br />
Athwart by furious Brigliador was plied;<br />
On which the paynim, little courteous, came;<br />
So that he fell anew beneath the dame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVI.</td>
<td>Rogero, when Marphisa on the ground<br />
He saw unhorsed, deferred no more his aid;<br />
Who for that deed had leisure; since, astound,<br />
Rodomont far away had been conveyed:<br />
He smote the morion which that Tartar crowned;<br />
And, cleft like stalk, his head on earth had laid,<br />
Had he his trusty Balisarda born,<br />
Or Mandricardo other helmet worn.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVII.</td>
<td>Rodomont, of his senses repossest,<br />
Turned round this while, and Richardetto spied;<br />
And recollecting how, when late distrest,<br />
He to Rogero succour had supplied,<br />
Quickly against that youthful warrior prest;<br />
Who an ill guerdon would from him abide,<br />
Did Malagigi not his malice thwart<br />
With other magic and with mickle art.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVIII.</td>
<td>Sage Malagigi versed in every sleight<br />
Which by the wisest wizard can be done;<br />
Although his book he has not, by whose might,<br />
He in his course can stop the passing sun;<br />
The conjuration recollects and rite,<br />
By which he tames the rebel fiends; and one<br />
Bids enter into Doralice’s steed,<br />
Whom he to fury stings and headlong speed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIX.</td>
<td>Into that gentle palfrey’s form, who bore<br />
The beauteous daughter of King Stordilane,<br />
Sir Vivian’s brother, simply by his lore,<br />
Made pass an angel of the dark domain;<br />
And the good horse, who never moved before,<br />
Except in due obedience to the rein,<br />
Now took a leap, possest by that ill sprite,<br />
Thirty feet long and sixteen feet in height.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXX.</td>
<td>It was a mighty leap, yet not so wide<br />
As to make any rider void the sell.<br />
Seeing herself so high in air, loud cried,<br />
(Yielding herself for dead) that bonnibel.<br />
Her palfrey, with the Daemon for his guide,<br />
After his leap, runs, goaded by the spell<br />
(The maid still screaming) such a furious course,<br />
An arrow had not reached the flying horse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXI.</td>
<td>At the first hearing of that voice, the son<br />
Of Ulien, on his part, the strife suspended;<br />
And thither, where the furious palfrey run,<br />
Swiftly in succour of the lady wended.<br />
No less was by the Tartar monarch done;<br />
Who neither Child nor damsel more offended;<br />
But without craving time, or truce, or peace,<br />
Pursued King Rodomont and Doralice.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXII.</td>
<td>Marphisa rose meanwhile, to fury stirred;<br />
And, with disdain all over in a glow,<br />
Thought to accomplish her revenge, and erred:<br />
For at too great a distance was the foe.<br />
Rogero, who beheld the war deferred,<br />
Rather like lion roared than sighed: well know<br />
Those two their coursers they should vainly gore,<br />
Following Frontino and good Brigliador.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIII.</td>
<td>Rogero will not halt till he renew<br />
And end the unfinished combat for the horse;<br />
Marphisa will not quit that Tartar, who<br />
Will to her satisfaction prove his force.<br />
To leave their quarrel in such guise the two<br />
Esteem foul scandal; as their better course,<br />
In chase of those offending knights to fare,<br />
Is the conclusion of that valiant pair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIV.</td>
<td>They in the paynim camp will find each foe,<br />
If them before they find not on their way;<br />
Whom thither bound, to raise the siege they know,<br />
Ere Charlemagne bring all beneath his sway.<br />
So thitherward the twain directly go<br />
Where these, they deem, will be their certain prey.<br />
Yet not so rudely thence Rogero broke,<br />
But that he first with his companion spoke.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXV.</td>
<td>Thither returns Rogero, where apart<br />
Is he, the brother of his lady fair;<br />
And vows himself his friend, with generous heart,<br />
In good or evil fortune, everywhere.<br />
Him he implores—and frames his speech with art—<br />
He his salutes will to his sister bear;<br />
And this so well, he moves by that request<br />
No doubt in him, nor any of the rest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVI.</td>
<td>Of Malagigi he and Viviane<br />
Next takes farewell and wounded Aldigier;<br />
Their services no less that kindly twain<br />
Proffer, as ever debtors to the peer.<br />
Marphisa to seek Paris is so fain,<br />
That parting she forgets her friends to cheer;<br />
But Malagigi and Vivian, in pursuit,<br />
Follow, and from afar that maid salute;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVII.</td>
<td>And so Sir Richardet as well: but low<br />
On earth lies Aldigier, and there must rest.<br />
The two first champions towards Paris go,<br />
And the two others next pursue that quest.<br />
In other canto, Sir, I hope to show<br />
Of wondrous and of superhuman gest,<br />
Wrought to the damage of the Christian king,<br />
By those two couples of whose worth I sing.</td>
