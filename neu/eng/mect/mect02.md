#### The Canterbury Tales

### The Miller's Prologue

<span id="an_3109"></span> Whan that the Knyght had thus his tale
ytoold,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3110">3110</span></span> In al the
route nas ther yong ne oold  
<span id="an_3111"></span> That he ne seyde it was a noble storie  
<span id="an_3112"></span> And worthy for to drawen to memorie,  
<span id="an_3113"></span> And namely the gentils everichon.  
<span id="an_3114"></span> Oure Hooste lough and swoor, "So moot I
gon,  
<span id="an_3115"></span> This gooth aright; unbokeled is the male.  
<span id="an_3116"></span> Lat se now who shal telle another tale;  
<span id="an_3117"></span> For trewely the game is wel bigonne.  
<span id="an_3118"></span> Now telleth ye, sir Monk, if that ye konne,  
<span id="an_3119"></span> Somwhat to quite with the Knyghtes tale."  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3120">3120</span></span> The
Millere, that for dronken was al pale,  
<span id="an_3121"></span> So that unnethe upon his hors he sat,  
<span id="an_3122"></span> He nolde avalen neither hood ne hat,  
<span id="an_3123"></span> Ne abyde no man for his curteisie,  
<span id="an_3124"></span> But in Pilates voys he gan to crie,  
<span id="an_3125"></span> And swoor, "By armes, and by blood and
bones,  
<span id="an_3126"></span> I kan a noble tale for the nones,  
<span id="an_3127"></span> With which I wol now quite the Knyghtes
tale."  
<span id="an_3128"></span> Oure Hooste saugh that he was dronke of
ale,  
<span id="an_3129"></span> And seyde, "Abyd, Robyn, my leeve brother;  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3130">3130</span></span> Som bettre
man shal telle us first another.  
<span id="an_3131"></span> Abyd, and lat us werken thriftily."  
<span id="an_3132"></span> "By Goddes soule," quod he, "that wol nat
I;  
<span id="an_3133"></span> For I wol speke or elles go my wey."  
<span id="an_3134"></span> Oure Hoost answerde, "Tel on, a devel wey!  
<span id="an_3135"></span> Thou art a fool; thy wit is overcome."  
<span id="an_3136"></span> "Now herkneth," quod the Millere, "alle and
some!  
<span id="an_3137"></span> But first I make a protestacioun  
<span id="an_3138"></span> That I am dronke; I knowe it by my soun.  
<span id="an_3139"></span> And therfore if that I mysspeke or seye,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3140">3140</span></span> Wyte it the
ale of Southwerk, I you preye.  
<span id="an_3141"></span> For I wol telle a legende and a lyf  
<span id="an_3142"></span> Bothe of a carpenter and of his wyf,  
<span id="an_3143"></span> How that a clerk hath set the wrightes
cappe."  
<span id="an_3144"></span> The Reve answerde and seyde, "Stynt thy
clappe!  
<span id="an_3145"></span> Lat be thy lewed dronken harlotrye.  
<span id="an_3146"></span> It is a synne and eek a greet folye  
<span id="an_3147"></span> To apeyren any man, or hym defame,  
<span id="an_3148"></span> And eek to bryngen wyves in swich fame.  
<span id="an_3149"></span> Thou mayst ynogh of othere thynges seyn."  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3150">3150</span></span> This dronke
Millere spak ful soone ageyn  
<span id="an_3151"></span> And seyde, "Leve brother Osewold,  
<span id="an_3152"></span> Who hath no wyf, he is no cokewold.  
<span id="an_3153"></span> But I sey nat therfore that thou art oon;  
<span id="an_3154"></span> Ther been ful goode wyves many oon,  
<span id="an_3155"></span> And evere a thousand goode ayeyns oon
badde.  
<span id="an_3156"></span> That knowestow wel thyself, but if thou
madde.  
<span id="an_3157"></span> Why artow angry with my tale now?  
<span id="an_3158"></span> I have a wyf, pardee, as wel as thow;  
<span id="an_3159"></span> Yet nolde I, for the oxen in my plogh,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3160">3160</span></span> Take upon
me moore than ynogh,  
<span id="an_3161"></span> As demen of myself that I were oon;  
<span id="an_3162"></span> I wol bileve wel that I am noon.  
<span id="an_3163"></span> An housbonde shal nat been inquisityf  
<span id="an_3164"></span> Of Goddes pryvetee, nor of his wyf.  
<span id="an_3165"></span> So he may fynde Goddes foyson there,  
<span id="an_3166"></span> Of the remenant nedeth nat enquere."  
<span id="an_3167"></span> What sholde I moore seyn, but this Millere  
<span id="an_3168"></span> He nolde his wordes for no man forbere,  
<span id="an_3169"></span> But tolde his cherles tale in his manere.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3170">3170</span></span> M'
athynketh that I shal reherce it heere.  
<span id="an_3171"></span> And therfore every gentil wight I preye,  
<span id="an_3172"></span> For Goddes love, demeth nat that I seye  
<span id="an_3173"></span> Of yvel entente, but for I moot reherce  
<span id="an_3174"></span> Hir tales alle, be they bettre or werse,  
<span id="an_3175"></span> Or elles falsen som of my mateere.  
<span id="an_3176"></span> And therfore, whoso list it nat yheere,  
<span id="an_3177"></span> Turne over the leef and chese another tale;  
<span id="an_3178"></span> For he shal fynde ynowe, grete and smale,  
<span id="an_3179"></span> Of storial thyng that toucheth gentillesse,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_3180">3180</span></span> And eek
moralitee and hoolynesse.  
<span id="an_3181"></span> Blameth nat me if that ye chese amys.  
<span id="an_3182"></span> The Millere is a cherl; ye knowe wel this.  
<span id="an_3183"></span> So was the Reve eek and othere mo,  
<span id="an_3184"></span> And harlotrie they tolden bothe two.  
<span id="an_3185"></span> Avyseth yow, and put me out of blame;  
<span id="an_3186"></span> And eek men shal nat maken ernest of game.
