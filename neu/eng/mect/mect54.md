#### Boece

### Book 2

<span id="an_1"></span> Aftir this sche stynte a lytel; and after that  
<span id="an_2"></span> sche hadde ygadrede by atempre stillenesse myn  
<span id="an_3"></span> attencioun, she seyde thus (as who so myghte  
<span id="an_4"></span> seyn thus: after thise thynges sche stynte a  
<span id="an_5"></span> litil, and whan sche aperceyved by atempre  
<span id="an_6"></span> stillenesse that I was ententyf to herkne
hire,  
<span id="an_7"></span> sche bygan to speke in this wyse): "If I,"
quod  
<span id="an_8"></span> sche, "have undirstonden and knowen outrely  
<span id="an_9"></span> the causes and the habyt of thy maladye,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> thow
languyssest and art desfeted for desir  
<span id="an_11"></span> and talent of thi rather fortune. Sche (that  
<span id="an_12"></span> ilke Fortune) oonly, that is chaunged, as  
<span id="an_13"></span> thow feynest, to the-ward, hath perverted the  
<span id="an_14"></span> cleernesse and the estat of thi corage. I  
<span id="an_15"></span> undirstonde the felefolde colours and
desceytes  
<span id="an_16"></span> of thilke merveylous monstre Fortune and how  
<span id="an_17"></span> sche useth ful flaterynge famylarite with hem  
<span id="an_18"></span> that sche enforceth to bygyle, so longe, til
that  
<span id="an_19"></span> sche confounde with unsuffrable sorwe  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> hem that sche
hath left in despeer unpurveied.  
<span id="an_21"></span> And yif thou remembrest wel the  
<span id="an_22"></span> kende, the maneris, and the desserte of
thilke  
<span id="an_23"></span> Fortune, thou shalt wel knowe that, as in
hir,  
<span id="an_24"></span> thow nevere ne haddest ne hast ylost any fair  
<span id="an_25"></span> thyng. But, as I trowe, I schal nat greetly  
<span id="an_26"></span> travailen to don the remembren on thise  
<span id="an_27"></span> thynges. For thow were wont to hurtlen and  
<span id="an_28"></span> despysen hir with manly woordes whan sche  
<span id="an_29"></span> was blaundyssching and present, and  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> pursuydest hir
with sentences that weren  
<span id="an_31"></span> drawen out of myn entre (that is to seyn,  
<span id="an_32"></span> of myn enformacioun). But no sodeyn mutacioun  
<span id="an_33"></span> ne bytideth noght withouten a manere  
<span id="an_34"></span> chaungynge of corages; and so is it byfallen  
<span id="an_35"></span> that thou art a litil departed fro the pees of
thi  
<span id="an_36"></span> thought.  
<span id="an_37"></span> "But now is tyme that thou drynke and ataste  
<span id="an_38"></span> some softe and delitable thynges, so that
whanne  
<span id="an_39"></span> thei ben entred withynne the, it mowe  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> maken wey to
strengere drynkes of medycines.  
<span id="an_41"></span> Com now forth, therfore, the  
<span id="an_42"></span> suasyoun of swetnesse rethorien, whiche that  
<span id="an_43"></span> goth oonly the righte wey while sche
forsaketh  
<span id="an_44"></span> nat myn estatutz. And with Rethorice com
forth  
<span id="an_45"></span> Musice, a damoysele of our hous, that syngeth  
<span id="an_46"></span> now lightere moedes or prolacions, now  
<span id="an_47"></span> hevyere. What eyleth the, man? What is it
that  
<span id="an_48"></span> hath cast the into moornynge and into
wepynge?  
<span id="an_49"></span> I trow that thou hast seyn some newe thyng  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> and unkouth.
Thou wenest that Fortune be  
<span id="an_51"></span> chaunged ayens the; but thow wenest  
<span id="an_52"></span> wrong (yif thou that wene): alway tho ben hir  
<span id="an_53"></span> maneres. Sche hath rather kept, as to
the-ward,  
<span id="an_54"></span> hir propre stablenesse in the chaungynge of  
<span id="an_55"></span> hirself. Ryght swiche was sche whan sche  
<span id="an_56"></span> flateryd the and desseyved the with unleful  
<span id="an_57"></span> lykynges of false welefulnesse. Thou hast now  
<span id="an_58"></span> knowen and ateynt the doutous or double
visage  
<span id="an_59"></span> of thilke blynde goddesse Fortune. Sche,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> that yit
covereth and wympleth hir to other  
<span id="an_61"></span> folk, hath schewyd hir every del to the. Yif  
<span id="an_62"></span> thou approvest here (and thynkest that sche
is  
<span id="an_63"></span> good), use hir maneris and pleyne the nat;
and  
<span id="an_64"></span> yif thou agrisest hir false trecherie, despise
and  
<span id="an_65"></span> cast awey hir that pleyeth so harmfully. For
sche,  
<span id="an_66"></span> that is now cause of so mochel sorwe to the,  
<span id="an_67"></span> sholde ben cause to the of pees and of joye.
Sche  
<span id="an_68"></span> hath forsaken the, forsothe, the whiche that  
<span id="an_69"></span> nevere man mai ben siker that sche ne schal  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> forsaken hym.
(Glose. But natheles some

<span id="an_72"></span> hath forsaken the, ne ther nys no man siker  
<span id="an_73"></span> that sche ne hath nat forsake.) Holdestow  
<span id="an_74"></span> thanne thilke welefulnesse precious to the,
that  
<span id="an_75"></span> schal passen? And is present Fortune
dereworth  
<span id="an_76"></span> to the, whiche that nys nat feithful for to
duelle,  
<span id="an_77"></span> and whan sche goth awey that sche bryngeth a  
<span id="an_78"></span> wyght in sorwe? For syn she may nat ben  
<span id="an_79"></span> withholden at a mannys wille, \[and\] sche  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_80">80</span></span> maketh hym a
wrecche whan sche departeth  
<span id="an_81"></span> fro hym, what other thyng is  
<span id="an_82"></span> flyttynge Fortune but a maner schewynge of  
<span id="an_83"></span> wrecchidnesse that is to comen? Ne it

<span id="an_84"></span> nat oonly to loken on thyng that is present

and  
<span id="an_86"></span> mesureth the ende of thynges. And the same  
<span id="an_87"></span> chaungynge from oon into another (that is to  
<span id="an_88"></span> seyn, fro adversite into prosperite) maketh

<span id="an_89"></span> the manaces of Fortune ne ben nat for to  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_90">90</span></span> dreden, ne the

<span id="an_92"></span> to suffren wyth evene wil in pacience al that
is  
<span id="an_93"></span> doon inwith the floor of Fortune (that is to
seyn,  
<span id="an_94"></span> in this world), syn thou hast oonys put thy
nekke  
<span id="an_95"></span> undir the yok of hir. For yif thow wilt writen
a  
<span id="an_96"></span> lawe of wendynge and of duellynge to Fortune,  
<span id="an_97"></span> whiche that thow hast chosen frely to ben thi  
<span id="an_98"></span> lady, artow nat wrongful in that, and makest  
<span id="an_99"></span> Fortune wroth and aspre by thyn  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_100">100</span></span> inpacience?
And yit thow mayst nat  
<span id="an_101"></span> chaungen hir. Yif thou committest and  
<span id="an_102"></span> betakest thi seyles to the wynd, thow schalt
ben  
<span id="an_103"></span> shoven, nat thider that thow woldest, but
whider  
<span id="an_104"></span> that the wynd schouveth the. Yif thow castest
thi  
<span id="an_105"></span> seedes in the feeldes, thou sholdest han in  
<span id="an_106"></span> mynde that the yeres ben amonges,
outherwhile  
<span id="an_107"></span> plentevous and outherwhile bareyne. Thow
hast  
<span id="an_108"></span> bytaken thiself to the governaunce of
Fortune  
<span id="an_109"></span> and forthi it byhoveth the to ben obeisaunt  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_110">110</span></span> to the
maneris of thi lady. Enforcestow the  
<span id="an_111"></span> to aresten or withholden the swyftnesse  
<span id="an_112"></span> and the sweighe of hir turnynge wheel? O
thow  
<span id="an_113"></span> fool of alle mortel foolis! Yif Fortune bygan
to  
<span id="an_114"></span> duelle stable, she cessede thanne to ben
Fortune.  
<span id="an_1"></span> "Whan Fortune with a proud ryght hand hath  
<span id="an_2"></span> turned hir chaungynge stowndes, sche fareth  
<span id="an_3"></span> lyke the maneres of the boylynge Eurippe.  
<span id="an_4"></span> (Glosa. Eurippe is an arm of the see that
ebbeth  
<span id="an_5"></span> and floweth, and somtyme the streem is on

<span id="an_7"></span> She, cruel Fortune, casteth adoun kynges that  
<span id="an_8"></span> whilom weren ydradd; and sche, desceyvable,  
<span id="an_9"></span> enhaunceth up the humble chere of hym  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> that is

<span id="an_11"></span> ne rekketh of wrecchide wepynges; and  
<span id="an_12"></span> she is so hard that sche leygheth and
scorneth  
<span id="an_13"></span> the wepynges of hem, the whiche sche hath  
<span id="an_14"></span> maked wepe with hir free wille. Thus sche  
<span id="an_15"></span> pleyeth, and thus sche prooeveth hir
strengthes,  
<span id="an_16"></span> and scheweth a greet wonder to alle hir
servauntz  
<span id="an_17"></span> yif that a wyght is seyn weleful and  
<span id="an_18"></span> overthrowe in an houre.  
<span id="an_1"></span> "Certes I wolde pleten with the a fewe  
<span id="an_2"></span> thynges, usynge the woordes of Fortune. Tak  
<span id="an_3"></span> hede now thyselve, yif that sche asketh ryght:  
<span id="an_4"></span> \`O thow man, wherfore makestow me gyltyf by  
<span id="an_5"></span> thyne every dayes pleynynges? What wrong  
<span id="an_6"></span> have I don the? What godes have I byreft the  
<span id="an_7"></span> that weren thyne? Stryf or pleet with me
byforn  
<span id="an_8"></span> what juge that thow wolt of the possessioun  
<span id="an_9"></span> of rychesses or of dignytees; and yif  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> thou maist
schewen me that ever any mortel  
<span id="an_11"></span> man hath resceyved ony of tho thynges  
<span id="an_12"></span> to ben hise in propre, thanne wil I graunte
freely  
<span id="an_13"></span> that thilke thynges weren thyne whiche that  
<span id="an_14"></span> thow axest.  
<span id="an_15"></span> "Whan that nature brought the foorth out of  
<span id="an_16"></span> thi modir wombe, I resceyved the nakid and  
<span id="an_17"></span> nedy of alle thynges, and I norissched the
with  
<span id="an_18"></span> my richesses, and was redy and ententyf
thurwe  
<span id="an_19"></span> my favour to sustene the -- and that maketh  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> the now
inpacient ayens me; and I  
<span id="an_21"></span> envyrounde the with al the habundaunce  
<span id="an_22"></span> and schynynge of alle goodes that ben in my  
<span id="an_23"></span> ryght. Now it liketh me to withdrawe myn  
<span id="an_24"></span> hand. Thow hast had grace as he that hath  
<span id="an_25"></span> used of foreyne goodes; thow hast no ryght to  
<span id="an_26"></span> pleyne the, as though thou haddest outrely  
<span id="an_27"></span> forlorn alle thy thynges. Why pleynestow  
<span id="an_28"></span> thanne? I have doon the no wrong. Richesses,  
<span id="an_29"></span> honours, and swiche othere thinges ben of  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> my right. My

<span id="an_31"></span> hir lady; they comen with me, and departen  
<span id="an_32"></span> whan I wende. I dar wel affermen hardely  
<span id="an_33"></span> that, yif tho thynges of whiche thow pleynest  
<span id="an_34"></span> that thou hast forlorn \[hem\] hadden ben  
<span id="an_35"></span> thyne, thow ne haddest nat lorn hem. Schal  
<span id="an_36"></span> I thanne, oonly, be defended to usen my
ryght?  
<span id="an_37"></span> "Certes it is leveful to the hevene to maken  
<span id="an_38"></span> clere dayes, and after that to coveren tho

<span id="an_39"></span> dayes with dirke nyghtes. The yeer hath  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> eek leve to

<span id="an_42"></span> fruyt, and to confownden hem somtyme with  
<span id="an_43"></span> reynes and with coldes. The see hath eek his  
<span id="an_44"></span> ryght to ben somtyme calm and blaundysschyng  
<span id="an_45"></span> with smothe watir, and somtyme to ben  
<span id="an_46"></span> horrible with wawes and with tempestes. But  
<span id="an_47"></span> the covetise of men, that mai nat be
stawnched  
<span id="an_48"></span> -- schal it bynde me to ben stedfast, syn
that  
<span id="an_49"></span> stidfastnesse is uncouth to my maneris?  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> Swiche is my
strengthe, and this pley  
<span id="an_51"></span> I pleye continuely. I torne the whirlynge  
<span id="an_52"></span> wheel with the turnynge sercle; I am glad to  
<span id="an_53"></span> chaungen the loweste to the heyeste, and the  
<span id="an_54"></span> heyeste to the loweste. Worth up yif thow  
<span id="an_55"></span> wolt, so it be by this lawe, that thow ne

<span id="an_56"></span> nat that I do the wroong, though thow

<span id="an_57"></span> adown whan the resoun of my pley axeth it.  
<span id="an_58"></span> Wystestow nat how Cresus, kyng of Lydyens,  
<span id="an_59"></span> of whiche kyng Cirus was ful sore agast a  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> lytil byforn --

<span id="an_63"></span> fro hevene that rescowyde hym? And is it out  
<span id="an_64"></span> of thy mynde how that Paulus, consul of Rome,  
<span id="an_65"></span> whan he had taken the kyng of Percyens, weep  
<span id="an_66"></span> pitously for the captivyte of the selve kyng?  
<span id="an_67"></span> What other thynge bywaylen the cryinges of  
<span id="an_68"></span> tragedyes but oonly the dedes of Fortune,
that  
<span id="an_69"></span> with an unwar strook overturneth the  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> realmes of

<span id="an_79"></span> of my richesses and prosperites)? And  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_80">80</span></span> what ek yif Y

<span id="an_86"></span> propre ryght.

<span id="an_17"></span> What brydles myghte withholden to  
<span id="an_18"></span> any certeyn ende the disordene covetise of  
<span id="an_19"></span> men, whan evere the rather that it fletith  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> in large

<span id="an_22"></span> qwakynge and dredful weneth hymselven  
<span id="an_23"></span> nedy, he ne lyveth nevermo ryche.  
<span id="an_1"></span> "Therfore, yif that Fortune spake with the  
<span id="an_2"></span> for hirself in this manere, forsothe thow ne  
<span id="an_3"></span> haddest noght what thou myghtest answere.  
<span id="an_4"></span> And yif thow hast any thyng wherwith thow  
<span id="an_5"></span> mayst rightfully defenden thi compleynte, it  
<span id="an_6"></span> behoveth the to schewen it, and I wol yyve  
<span id="an_7"></span> the space to tellen it."  
<span id="an_8"></span> "Serteynly," quod I thanne, "thise ben faire  
<span id="an_9"></span> thynges and enoynted with hony swetnesse  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> of Rethorik and

<span id="an_11"></span> whil thei ben herd thei ben delycious, but

<span id="an_13"></span> (this is to seyn, that wrecches felen the

<span id="an_14"></span> that thei suffren more grevously than the

<span id="an_15"></span> or the delites of thise wordes mowen gladen  
<span id="an_16"></span> or conforten hem). So that, whanne thise  
<span id="an_17"></span> thynges stynten for to soune in eris, the

<span id="an_18"></span> that es inset greveth the thought."  
<span id="an_19"></span> "Right so is it," quod sche. "For thise ne  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> ben yit none

<span id="an_23"></span> that tyme is, I schal moeve and ajuste swiche  
<span id="an_24"></span> thynges that percen hemselve depe. But

<span id="an_25"></span> that thow schalt noght wilne to leten thiself  
<span id="an_26"></span> a wrecche, hastow foryeten the nowmbre  
<span id="an_27"></span> and the maner of thi welefulnesse? I holde  
<span id="an_28"></span> me stille how that the sovereyn men of the  
<span id="an_29"></span> cite token the in cure and in kepynge,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> whan thow were
