#### Boece

### Book 1

<span id="an_1"></span> Allas! I wepynge, am constreyned to bygynnen  
<span id="an_2"></span> vers of sorwful matere, that whilom in
florysschyng  
<span id="an_3"></span> studie made delitable ditees. For  
<span id="an_4"></span> lo, rendynge muses of poetes enditen to me  
<span id="an_5"></span> thynges to ben writen, and drery vers of
wretchidnesse  
<span id="an_6"></span> weten my face with verray teres. At  
<span id="an_7"></span> the leeste, no drede ne myghte overcomen  
<span id="an_8"></span> tho muses, that thei ne were felawes, and
folwyden  
<span id="an_9"></span> my wey (that is to seyn, whan  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> I was exiled).
They that weren glorie of  
<span id="an_11"></span> my youthe, whilom weleful and grene,  
<span id="an_12"></span> conforten nowe the sorwful wyerdes of me,
olde  
<span id="an_13"></span> man. For eelde is comyn unwarly uppon me,  
<span id="an_14"></span> hasted by the harmes that Y have, and sorwe  
<span id="an_15"></span> hath comandid his age to ben in me. Heeris
hore  
<span id="an_16"></span> arn schad overtymeliche upon myn heved, and  
<span id="an_17"></span> the slakke skyn trembleth of myn emptid body.  
<span id="an_18"></span> Thilke deth of men is weleful that ne comyth  
<span id="an_19"></span> noght in yeeris that ben swete, but  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> cometh to
wrecches often yclepid. Allas,  
<span id="an_21"></span> allas! With how deef an ere deth, cruwel,  
<span id="an_22"></span> turneth awey fro wrecches and nayteth to  
<span id="an_23"></span> closen wepynge eien. Whil Fortune,
unfeithful,  
<span id="an_24"></span> favourede me with lyghte goodes, the sorwful  
<span id="an_25"></span> houre (that is to seyn, the deth) hadde
almoost  
<span id="an_26"></span> dreynt myn heved. But now, for Fortune  
<span id="an_27"></span> cloudy hath chaunged hir deceyvable  
<span id="an_28"></span> chere to meward, myn unpietous lif draweth  
<span id="an_29"></span> along unagreable duellynges in me. O ye,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> my frendes,
what or wherto avaunted ye  
<span id="an_31"></span> me to be weleful? For he that hath fallen
stood  
<span id="an_32"></span> noght in stedefast degre.  
<span id="an_1"></span> In the mene while that I, stille, recordede  
<span id="an_2"></span> these thynges with myself and merkid my weply  
<span id="an_3"></span> compleynte with office of poyntel, I saw,  
<span id="an_4"></span> stondynge aboven the heghte of myn heved, a  
<span id="an_5"></span> womman of ful greet reverence by semblaunt,  
<span id="an_6"></span> hir eien brennynge and cleer-seynge over the  
<span id="an_7"></span> comune myghte of men; with a lifly colour  
<span id="an_8"></span> and with swich vigour and strengthe that it ne  
<span id="an_9"></span> myghte nat ben emptid, al were it so  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> that sche was
ful of so greet age that men  
<span id="an_11"></span> ne wolden nat trowen in no manere that  
<span id="an_12"></span> sche were of our elde. The stature of hire
was  
<span id="an_13"></span> of a doutous jugement, for somtyme sche
constreyned  
<span id="an_14"></span> and schronk hirselven lik to the comune  
<span id="an_15"></span> mesure of men, and somtyme it semede  
<span id="an_16"></span> that sche touchede the hevene with the heghte  
<span id="an_17"></span> of here heved. And whan sche hef hir heved  
<span id="an_18"></span> heyere, sche percede the selve hevene so that  
<span id="an_19"></span> the sighte of men lokynge was in ydel.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> Hir clothes
weren makid of right delye  
<span id="an_21"></span> thredes and subtil craft of perdurable
matere;  
<span id="an_22"></span> the whiche clothes sche hadde woven with  
<span id="an_23"></span> hir owene handes, as I knew wel aftir by
hirselve  
<span id="an_24"></span> declarynge and schewynge to me. The  
<span id="an_25"></span> beaute \[of\] the whiche clothes a derknesse of
a  
<span id="an_26"></span> forleten and despised elde hadde duskid and  
<span id="an_27"></span> dirked, as it is wont to dirken besmokede  
<span id="an_28"></span> ymages. In the nethereste hem or bordure of  
<span id="an_29"></span> thise clothes, men redden ywoven in a  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> Grekissch P
(that signifieth the lif actif);  
<span id="an_31"></span> and aboven that lettre, in the heieste  
<span id="an_32"></span> bordure, a Grekyssh T (that signifieth the
lif  
<span id="an_33"></span> contemplatif). And bytwixen thise two lettres  
<span id="an_34"></span> ther were seyn degrees nobly ywrought in  
<span id="an_35"></span> manere of laddres, by whiche degrees men  
<span id="an_36"></span> myghten clymben fro the nethereste lettre to
the  
<span id="an_37"></span> uppereste. Natheles handes of some men hadden  
<span id="an_38"></span> korve that cloth by violence and by  
<span id="an_39"></span> strengthe, and everich man of hem hadde  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> boren awey
swiche peces as he myghte  
<span id="an_41"></span> geten. And forsothe this forseide womman bar  
<span id="an_42"></span> smale bokis in hir right hand, and in hir left
hand  
<span id="an_43"></span> sche bar a ceptre.  
<span id="an_44"></span> And whan she saughe thise poetical muses  
<span id="an_45"></span> aprochen aboute my bed and enditynge wordes  
<span id="an_46"></span> to my wepynges, sche was a litil amoeved, and  
<span id="an_47"></span> glowede with cruel eighen. "Who," quat sche,  
<span id="an_48"></span> "hath suffred aprochen to this sike man thise  
<span id="an_49"></span> comune strompettis of swich a place that  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> men clepen the
theatre? The whiche nat

<span id="an_52"></span> none remedies, but thei wolden fedyn and  
<span id="an_53"></span> noryssen hym with sweete venym. Forsothe  
<span id="an_54"></span> thise ben tho that with thornes and
prikkynges  
<span id="an_55"></span> of talentz or affeccions, whiche that ne bien  
<span id="an_56"></span> nothyng fructifyenge nor profitable,

<span id="an_57"></span> the corn plentyvous of fruytes of resoun. For  
<span id="an_58"></span> thei holden hertes of men in usage, but thei  
<span id="an_59"></span> delyvre noght folk fro maladye. But yif ye  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> muses hadden

unprofitable  
<span id="an_62"></span> man, as men ben wont to fynde  
<span id="an_63"></span> comonly among the peple, I wolde wene suffre  
<span id="an_64"></span> the lasse grevosly; forwhi, in swych an
unprofitable  
<span id="an_65"></span> man, myne ententes weren nothyng

<span id="an_67"></span> that hath ben noryssed in the studies or scoles
of  
<span id="an_68"></span> Eliaticis and Achademycis in Grece. But goth  
<span id="an_69"></span> now rather awey, ye mermaydenes, whiche  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> that ben swete

<span id="an_72"></span> by myne muses (that is to seyn, by noteful  
<span id="an_73"></span> sciences)."  
<span id="an_74"></span> And thus this companye of muses, iblamed,  
<span id="an_75"></span> casten wrothly the chere dounward to the
erthe,  
<span id="an_76"></span> and, schewynge by rednesse hir schame, thei  
<span id="an_77"></span> passeden sorwfully the thresschefold. And I,
of  
<span id="an_78"></span> whom the sighte, ploungid in teeres, was
dirked  
<span id="an_79"></span> so that Y ne myghte noght knowen what  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_80">80</span></span> that womman was
of so imperial auctorite,  
<span id="an_81"></span> I wax al abayssched and astoned, and caste  
<span id="an_82"></span> my syghte doun to the erthe, and bygan stille
for  
<span id="an_83"></span> to abide what sche woolde doon aftirward. Tho  
<span id="an_84"></span> com sche ner and sette her doun uppon the  
<span id="an_85"></span> uttereste corner of my bed; and sche,
byholdynge  
<span id="an_86"></span> my chere that was cast to the erthe  
<span id="an_87"></span> hevy and grevous of wepynge, compleynede  
<span id="an_88"></span> with thise wordis that I schal seyn the
perturbacion thought.  
<span id="an_1"></span> "Allas! How the thought of this man, dreynt  
<span id="an_2"></span> in overthrowynge depnesse, dulleth and
forleteth  
<span id="an_3"></span> his propre clernesse, myntynge to gon into  
<span id="an_4"></span> foreyne dirknesses as ofte as his anoyos  
<span id="an_5"></span> bysynes waxeth withoute mesure, that is  
<span id="an_6"></span> dryven with werldly wyndes. This man, that  
<span id="an_7"></span> whilom was fre, to whom the hevene was  
<span id="an_8"></span> opyn and knowen, and was wont to gon in  
<span id="an_9"></span> hevenliche pathes, and saughe the lyghtnesse  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> of the rede
sonne, and saughe the  
<span id="an_11"></span> sterres of the coolde mone, and whiche  
<span id="an_12"></span> sterre in hevene useth wandrynge recourses  
<span id="an_13"></span> iflyt by diverse speeris -- this man,
overcomere,  
<span id="an_14"></span> hadde comprehendid al this by nombre (of  
<span id="an_15"></span> acontynge in astronomye). And, over this, he  
<span id="an_16"></span> was wont to seken the causes whennes the
sounynge  
<span id="an_17"></span> wyndes moeven and bysien the smothe  
<span id="an_18"></span> watir of the see; and what spirit turneth the  
<span id="an_19"></span> stable hevene; and why the sterre ariseth  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> out of the rede
est, to fallen in the westrene  
<span id="an_21"></span> wawes; and what attemprith the lusty  
<span id="an_22"></span> houres of the firste somer sesoun, that
highteth  
<span id="an_23"></span> and apparaileth the erthe with rosene  
<span id="an_24"></span> floures; and who maketh that plentyvous  
<span id="an_25"></span> autumpne in fulle \[yere\] fletith with hevy  
<span id="an_26"></span> grapes. And eek this man was wont to tellen  
<span id="an_27"></span> the diverse causes of nature that weren
yhidd.  
<span id="an_28"></span> Allas! Now lyth he emptid of lyght of his  
<span id="an_29"></span> thoght, and his nekke is pressyd with hevy  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> cheynes, and
bereth his chere enclyned  
<span id="an_31"></span> adoun for the grete weyghte, and is
constreyned  
<span id="an_32"></span> to loken on the fool erthe!"  
<span id="an_1"></span> "But tyme is now," quod sche, "of medicyne  
<span id="an_2"></span> more than of compleynte." Forsothe thanne  
<span id="an_3"></span> sche, entendynge to meward with al the
lookynge  
<span id="an_4"></span> of hir eien, seyde: "Art nat thou he,"  
<span id="an_5"></span> quod sche, "that whilom, norissched with my  
<span id="an_6"></span> melk and fostred with myne metes, were escaped  
<span id="an_7"></span> and comyn to corage of a parfit man?  
<span id="an_8"></span> Certes I yaf the swiche armures that, yif thou  
<span id="an_9"></span> thiselve ne haddest first cast hem awey,  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> they schulden
han defended the in sekernesse  
<span id="an_11"></span> that mai nat ben overcomyn. Knowestow  
<span id="an_12"></span> me nat? Why arttow stille? Is it for  
<span id="an_13"></span> schame or for astonynge? It were me levere  
<span id="an_14"></span> that it were for schame, but it semeth me
that  
<span id="an_15"></span> astonynge hath oppresside the." And whan sche  
<span id="an_16"></span> say me nat oonly stille but withouten office  
<span id="an_17"></span> of tunge and al dowmbe, sche leyde hir hand  
<span id="an_18"></span> sooftly uppon my breest and seide: "Here nys  
<span id="an_19"></span> no peril," quod sche; "he is fallen into a  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> litargye,
whiche that is a comune seknesse  
<span id="an_21"></span> to hertes that been desceyved. He hath a  
<span id="an_22"></span> litil foryeten hymselve, but certes he schal  
<span id="an_23"></span> lightly remembren hymself yif so be that he  
<span id="an_24"></span> hath knowen me or now; and that he may so  
<span id="an_25"></span> doon, I will wipe a litil his eien that ben  
<span id="an_26"></span> dirked by the cloude of mortel thynges."
Thise  
<span id="an_27"></span> woordes seide sche, and with the lappe of hir  
<span id="an_28"></span> garnement yplited in a frownce sche dryede  
<span id="an_29"></span> myn eien, that weren fulle of the wawes of
wepynges.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Thus, whan that nyght was discussed and  
<span id="an_2"></span> chased awey, dirknesses forleten me, and to  
<span id="an_3"></span> myn eien repeyred ayen hir firste strengthe.  
<span id="an_4"></span> And ryght by ensaumple as the sonne is hydd  
<span id="an_5"></span> whan the sterres ben clustred (that is to
seyn,  
<span id="an_6"></span> whan sterres ben covered with cloudes) by  
<span id="an_7"></span> a swyft wynd that hyghte Chorus, and that  
<span id="an_8"></span> the firmament stant dirked with wete plowngy  
<span id="an_9"></span> cloudes; and that the sterres nat apeeren  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> upon hevene, so

<span id="an_11"></span> sprad upon erthe: yif thanne the wynde that  
<span id="an_12"></span> hyghte Boreas, isent out of the kaves of the  
<span id="an_13"></span> cuntre of Trace, betith this nyght (that is

<span id="an_14"></span> seyn, chaseth it awey) and discovereth the  
<span id="an_15"></span> closed day, thanne schyneth Phebus ischaken  
<span id="an_16"></span> with sodeyn light and smyteth with his beemes  
<span id="an_17"></span> in merveylynge eien.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Ryght so, and noon other wise, the cloudes  
<span id="an_2"></span> of sorwe dissolved and doon awey, I took
hevene,  
<span id="an_3"></span> and resceyved mynde to knowe the face  
<span id="an_4"></span> of my fisycien; so that \[whan\] \[that\] I
sette myne  
<span id="an_5"></span> eien on hir and fastned my lookynge, I byholde  
<span id="an_6"></span> my noryce, Philosophie, in whoos houses I  
<span id="an_7"></span> hadde conversed and hauntyd fro my youthe;  
<span id="an_8"></span> and I seide thus: "O thou maystresse of alle  
<span id="an_9"></span> vertues, descended from the sovereyne  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> sete, whi
arttow comen into this solitarie  
<span id="an_11"></span> place of myn exil? Artow comen for thou  
<span id="an_12"></span> art maad coupable with me of false blames?"  
<span id="an_13"></span> "O," quod sche, "my nory, schulde I forsake  
<span id="an_14"></span> the now, and schulde I nat parten with the by  
<span id="an_15"></span> comune travaile the charge that thow hast  
<span id="an_16"></span> suffred for envye of my name? Certes it nere
nat  
<span id="an_17"></span> leveful ne syttynge thyng to Philosophie to
leten  
<span id="an_18"></span> withouten companye the weye of hym that is  
<span id="an_19"></span> innocent. Schulde I thanne redowte my  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> blame and
agrysen as though ther were  
<span id="an_21"></span> byfallen a newe thyng? For trowestow that  
<span id="an_22"></span> Philosophie be now alderferst assailed in
periles  
<span id="an_23"></span> by folk of wykkide maneris? Have I noght  
<span id="an_24"></span> stryven with ful greet strif in old tyme, byfor
the  
<span id="an_25"></span> age of my Plato, ayens the foolhardynesse  
<span id="an_26"></span> of folye? And eek, the same Plato lyvynge,
his  
<span id="an_27"></span> mayster Socrates desserved victorie of
unryghtful  
<span id="an_28"></span> deth in my presence. The heritage of  
<span id="an_29"></span> the whiche Socrates (the heritage is to  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> seyn the
doctryne of the whiche Socrates  
<span id="an_31"></span> in his opinyoun of felicite, that I clepe  
<span id="an_32"></span> welefulnesse) whan that the peple of
Epycuriens  
<span id="an_33"></span> and Stoyciens and manye othere enforceden  
<span id="an_34"></span> hem to gon ravyssche everyche man for his
part  
<span id="an_35"></span> (that is to seyn, that everych of hem wolde  
<span id="an_36"></span> drawen to the deffense of his opinyoun the  
<span id="an_37"></span> wordes of Socrates), they as in partye of hir  
<span id="an_38"></span> preye todrowen me, cryinge and debatyng  
<span id="an_39"></span> ther-ayens, and korven and torente my  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> clothes that I
hadde woven with myn  
<span id="an_41"></span> handes; and with tho cloutes that thei  
<span id="an_42"></span> hadden arased out of my clothes thei wenten  
<span id="an_43"></span> awey wenynge that I hadde gon with hem every  
<span id="an_44"></span> del. In whiche Epycuriens and Stoyciens for
as  
<span id="an_45"></span> myche as ther semede some traces or steppes
of  
<span id="an_46"></span> myn abyte, the folie of men wenynge tho  
<span id="an_47"></span> Epycuryens and Stoyciens my familiers
pervertede  
<span id="an_48"></span> some thurw the errour of the wikkide  
<span id="an_49"></span> or unkunnynge multitude of hem.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> (This is to
seyn, that for they semeden  
<span id="an_51"></span> philosophres thei weren pursuyed to the  
<span id="an_52"></span> deth and slayn.)  
<span id="an_53"></span> "So yif thou ne hast noght knowen the  
<span id="an_54"></span> exilynge of Anaxogore, ne the empoisonynge of  
<span id="an_55"></span> Socrates, ne the turmentz of Zeno, for they  
<span id="an_56"></span> weren straungiers, yit myghtestow han knowen  
<span id="an_57"></span> the Senecciens and the Canyos and the
Soranas,  
<span id="an_58"></span> of whiche folk the renoun is neyther
over-oold  
<span id="an_59"></span> ne unsollempne. The whiche men nothyng  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> elles ne broght
hem to the deeth but oonly  
<span id="an_61"></span> for thei weren enformyd of myne maneris,  
<span id="an_62"></span> and semyde moost unlyk to the studies of  
<span id="an_63"></span> wykkid folk. And forthi thou oughtest noght
to  
<span id="an_64"></span> wondren thoughe that I, in the byttere see of
this  
<span id="an_65"></span> lif, be fordryven with tempestes blowynge  
<span id="an_66"></span> aboute, in the whiche this is my moste
purpoos,  
<span id="an_67"></span> that is to seyn to displesen to wikkide men.
Of  
<span id="an_68"></span> whiche schrewes al be the oost nevere so
greet,  
<span id="an_69"></span> it es to despise; for it nys nat governyd
with  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> no ledere (of
resoun), but it es ravyssched  
<span id="an_71"></span> oonly by fleetynge errour folyly and  
<span id="an_72"></span> lyghtly; and yif they somtyme, makynge an
oost  
<span id="an_73"></span> ayens us, assayle us as strengere, our ledere  
<span id="an_74"></span> draweth togidre his richesses into his tour,
and  
<span id="an_75"></span> they ben ententyf aboute sarpleris or
sachelis,
