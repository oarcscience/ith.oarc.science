#### A Treatise on the Astrolabe

### Part 1

<span id="an_1"></span> Lyte Lowys my sone, I aperceyve wel by
certeyne  
<span id="an_2"></span> evydences thyn abilite to lerne sciences  
<span id="an_3"></span> touching nombres and proporciouns; and as wel  
<span id="an_4"></span> considre I thy besy praier in special to lerne
the  
<span id="an_5"></span> tretys of the Astrelabie. Than for as moche as
a  
<span id="an_6"></span> philosofre saith, "he wrappith him in his
frend,  
<span id="an_7"></span> that condescendith to the rightfulle praiers of
his  
<span id="an_8"></span> frend," therfore have I yeven the a suffisant
Astrolabie  
<span id="an_9"></span> as for oure orizonte, compowned  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> after the
latitude of Oxenforde; upon  
<span id="an_11"></span> which, by mediacioun of this litel tretys, I  
<span id="an_12"></span> purpose to teche the a certein nombre of
conclusions  
<span id="an_13"></span> aperteynyng to the same instrument. I  
<span id="an_14"></span> seie a certein of conclusions, for thre causes.
The  
<span id="an_15"></span> first cause is this: truste wel that alle the
conclusions  
<span id="an_16"></span> that han be founde, or ellys possibly  
<span id="an_17"></span> might be founde in so noble an instrument as
is  
<span id="an_18"></span> an Astrelabie ben unknowe parfitly to eny
mortal  
<span id="an_19"></span> man in this regioun, as I suppose. Another  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> cause is this,
that sothly in any tretis of the  
<span id="an_21"></span> Astrelabie that I have seyn, there be somme  
<span id="an_22"></span> conclusions that wol not in alle thinges
parformen  
<span id="an_23"></span> her bihestes; and somme of hem ben to  
<span id="an_24"></span> harde to thy tendir age of ten yeer to
conceyve.  
<span id="an_25"></span> This tretis, divided in 5 parties, wol I
shewe  
<span id="an_26"></span> the under full light reules and naked wordes
in  
<span id="an_27"></span> Englissh, for Latyn canst thou yit but small,  
<span id="an_28"></span> my litel sone. But natheles suffise to the
these  
<span id="an_29"></span> trewe conclusions in Englissh as wel as
sufficith  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> to these noble
clerkes Grekes these  
<span id="an_31"></span> same conclusions in Grek; and to Arabiens  
<span id="an_32"></span> in Arabik, and to Jewes in Ebrew, and to  
<span id="an_33"></span> Latyn folk in Latyn; whiche Latyn folk had  
<span id="an_34"></span> hem first out of othere dyverse langages, and  
<span id="an_35"></span> writen hem in her owne tunge, that is to
seyn,  
<span id="an_36"></span> in Latyn. And God woot that in alle these  
<span id="an_37"></span> langages and in many moo han these
conclusions  
<span id="an_38"></span> ben suffisantly lerned and taught, and yit  
<span id="an_39"></span> by diverse reules; right as diverse pathes  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> leden diverse
folk the righte way to Rome.  
<span id="an_41"></span> Now wol I preie mekely every discret persone  
<span id="an_42"></span> that redith or herith this litel tretys to
have  
<span id="an_43"></span> my rude endityng for excusid, and my
superfluite  
<span id="an_44"></span> of wordes, for two causes. The firste cause  
<span id="an_45"></span> is for that curious endityng and hard
sentence  
<span id="an_46"></span> is ful hevy at onys for such a child to
lerne.  
<span id="an_47"></span> And the secunde cause is this, that sothly me  
<span id="an_48"></span> semith better to writen unto a child twyes a  
<span id="an_49"></span> god sentence, than he forgete it onys.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> And Lowys, yf
so be that I shewe the in  
<span id="an_51"></span> my lighte Englissh as trewe conclusions  
<span id="an_52"></span> touching this mater, and not oonly as trewe  
<span id="an_53"></span> but as many and as subtile conclusiouns, as  
<span id="an_54"></span> ben shewid in Latyn in eny commune tretys  
<span id="an_55"></span> of the Astrelabie, konne me the more thank.  
<span id="an_56"></span> And preie God save the king, that is lord of  
<span id="an_57"></span> this langage, and alle that him feith berith
and  
<span id="an_58"></span> obeieth, everich in his degre, the more and  
<span id="an_59"></span> the lasse. But considre wel that I ne usurpe  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> not to have
founden this werk of my labour  
<span id="an_61"></span> or of myn engyn. I n' am but a lewd
compilator  
<span id="an_62"></span> of the labour of olde astrologiens, and have
it  
<span id="an_63"></span> translatid in myn Englissh oonly for thy
doctrine.  
<span id="an_64"></span> And with this swerd shal I sleen envie.  
<span id="an_65"></span> Prima pars. -- The firste partie of this
tretys  
<span id="an_66"></span> shal reherse the figures and the membres of  
<span id="an_67"></span> thyn Astrelabie by cause that thou shalt have  
<span id="an_68"></span> the gretter knowing of thyn oune instrument.  
<span id="an_69"></span> Secunda pars. -- The secunde partie  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> shal techen the
worken the verrey practik  
<span id="an_71"></span> of the forseide conclusiouns, as ferforth and  
<span id="an_72"></span> as narwe as may be shewed in so small an  
<span id="an_73"></span> instrument portatif aboute. For wel woot  
<span id="an_74"></span> every astrologien that smallist fraccions ne  
<span id="an_75"></span> wol not be shewid in so small an instrument
as  
<span id="an_76"></span> in subtile tables calculed for a cause.  
<span id="an_77"></span> Tertia pars. -- The thirde partie shal
contene  
<span id="an_78"></span> diverse tables of longitudes and latitudes  
<span id="an_79"></span> of sterres fixe for the Astrelabie, and
tables  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_80">80</span></span> of the
declinacions of the sonne, and tables  
<span id="an_81"></span> of longitudes of citees and townes; and  
<span id="an_82"></span> tables as well for the governaunce of a clokke,
as  
<span id="an_83"></span> for to fynde the altitude meridian; and many
anothir  
<span id="an_84"></span> notable conclusioun after the kalenders  
<span id="an_85"></span> of the reverent clerkes, Frere J. Somer and  
<span id="an_86"></span> Frere N. Lenne.  
<span id="an_87"></span> Quarta pars. -- The fourthe partie shal ben  
<span id="an_88"></span> a theorike to declare the moevyng of the
celestiall  
<span id="an_89"></span> bodies with the causes. The whiche  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_90">90</span></span> fourthe partie
in speciall shal shewen a  
<span id="an_91"></span> table of the verrey moeving of the mone  
<span id="an_92"></span> from houre to houre every day and in every  
<span id="an_93"></span> signe after thyn almenak. Upon which table  
<span id="an_94"></span> there folewith a canoun suffisant to teche as  
<span id="an_95"></span> wel the manere of the worchynge of the same  
<span id="an_96"></span> conclusioun as to knowe in oure orizonte with  
<span id="an_97"></span> which degre of the zodiak that the mone
arisith  
<span id="an_98"></span> in any latitude, and the arisyng of any
planete  
<span id="an_99"></span> after his latitude fro the ecliptik lyne.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_100">100</span></span> Quinta pars.
-- The fifthe partie shal  
<span id="an_101"></span> be an introductorie, after the statutes of  
<span id="an_102"></span> oure doctours, in which thou maist lerne a
gret  
<span id="an_103"></span> part of the generall rewles of theorik in
astrologie.  
<span id="an_104"></span> In which fifthe partie shalt thou fynden  
<span id="an_105"></span> tables of equaciouns of houses after the
latitude of  
<span id="an_106"></span> Oxenforde; and tables of dignitees of
planetes,  
<span id="an_107"></span> and othere notefull thinges, yf God wol
vouche  
<span id="an_108"></span> saaf and his Moder the Maide, moo then I
behete.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Thyn Astrolabie hath a ring to putten on  
<span id="an_2"></span> the thombe of thi right hond in taking the  
<span id="an_3"></span> height of thinges. And tak kep, for from henes  
<span id="an_4"></span> forthward I wol clepen the heighte of any
thing  
<span id="an_5"></span> that is taken by the rewle "the altitude,"
withoute  
<span id="an_6"></span> moo wordes.  
<span id="an_1"></span> This ryng renneth in a maner toret fast  
<span id="an_2"></span> to the moder of thyn Astrelabie in so rowme a  
<span id="an_3"></span> space that it distourbith not the instrument
to  
<span id="an_4"></span> hangen after his right centre.  
<span id="an_1"></span> The moder of thin Astrelabye is thikkest  
<span id="an_2"></span> plate, perced with a large hool, that
resceiveth  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_in">in</span></span> hir wombe the
thynne plates compowned  
<span id="an_4"></span> for diverse clymates, and thy reet shapen in  
<span id="an_5"></span> manere of a nett or of a web of a loppe.  
<span id="an_1"></span> This moder is dividid on the bakhalf with  
<span id="an_2"></span> a lyne that cometh descending fro the ring  
<span id="an_3"></span> doun to the netherist bordure. The whiche  
<span id="an_4"></span> lyne, fro the forseide ring unto the centre of  
<span id="an_5"></span> the large hool amidde, is clepid the south
lyne,  
<span id="an_6"></span> or ellis the lyne meridional. And the
remenaunt  
<span id="an_7"></span> of this lyne doun to the bordure is  
<span id="an_8"></span> clepid the north lyne, or ellis the lyne of
midnyght.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Overthwart this forseide longe lyne ther  
<span id="an_2"></span> crossith him another lyne of the same lengthe  
<span id="an_3"></span> from eest to west. Of the whiche lyne, from  
<span id="an_4"></span> a litel cros (+) in the bordure unto the
centre  
<span id="an_5"></span> of the large hool, is clepid the est lyne, or  
<span id="an_6"></span> ellis the lyne orientale. And the remenaunt of  
<span id="an_7"></span> this lyne, fro the forseide centre unto the
bordure,  
<span id="an_8"></span> is clepid the west lyne, or ellis the lyne  
<span id="an_9"></span> occidentale. Now hast thou here the foure  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> quarters of
thin Astrolabie divided after the  
<span id="an_11"></span> foure principales plages or quarters of the
firmament.  
<span id="an_1"></span> The est syde of thyn Astrolabie is clepid  
<span id="an_2"></span> the right syde, and the west syde is clepid
the  
<span id="an_3"></span> left syde. Forget not thys, litel Lowys. Put  
<span id="an_4"></span> the ryng of thyn Astrolabie upon the thombe  
<span id="an_5"></span> of thi right hond, and than wol his right side  
<span id="an_6"></span> be toward thi lift side, and his left side wol
be  
<span id="an_7"></span> toward thy right side. Tak this rewle
generall,  
<span id="an_8"></span> as wel on the bak as on the wombe syde. Upon  
<span id="an_9"></span> the ende of this est lyne, as I first seide,
is  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> marked a litel
cros (+), where as evere  
<span id="an_11"></span> moo generaly is considerid the entring of  
<span id="an_12"></span> the first degre in which the sonne arisith.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Fro this litel cros (+) up to the ende  
<span id="an_2"></span> of the lyne meridionall, under the ryng, shalt  
<span id="an_3"></span> thou fynden the bordure divided with 90
degrees;  
<span id="an_4"></span> and by that same proporcioun is every  
<span id="an_5"></span> quarter of thin Astrolabie divided. Over the  
<span id="an_6"></span> whiche degrees there ben noumbres of augrym  
<span id="an_7"></span> that dividen thilke same degres fro 5 to 5, as  
<span id="an_8"></span> shewith by longe strikes bitwene. Of whiche  
<span id="an_9"></span> longe strikes the space bitwene contenith  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> a myle wey, and
every degre of the bordure  
<span id="an_11"></span> conteneth 4 minutes; this is to seien,  
<span id="an_12"></span> mynutes of an houre.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Under the compas of thilke degrees ben  
<span id="an_2"></span> writen the names of the 12 Signes: as  
<span id="an_3"></span> Aries, Taurus, Gemini, Cancer, Leo, Virgo,  
<span id="an_4"></span> Libra, Scorpio, Sagittarius, Capricornus,
Aquarius,  
<span id="an_5"></span> Piscis. And the nombres of the degrees of  
<span id="an_6"></span> thoo signes be writen in augrym above, and  
<span id="an_7"></span> with longe divisiouns fro 5 to 5, dyvidid fro  
<span id="an_8"></span> the tyme that the signe entrith unto the last  
<span id="an_9"></span> ende. But understond wel that these degres  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> of signes ben
everich of hem considred  
<span id="an_11"></span> of 60 mynutes, and every mynute of  
<span id="an_12"></span> 60 secundes, and so furth into smale
fraccions  
<span id="an_13"></span> infinite, as saith Alkabucius. And therfore  
<span id="an_14"></span> knowe wel that a degre of the bordure
contenith  
<span id="an_15"></span> 4 minutes, and a degre of a signe conteneth  
<span id="an_16"></span> 60 minutes, and have this in mynde.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Next this folewith the cercle of the daies,  
<span id="an_2"></span> that ben figured in manere of degres, that
contenen  
<span id="an_3"></span> in nombre 365, dividid also with longe  
<span id="an_4"></span> strikes fro 5 to 5, and the nombre in augrym  
<span id="an_5"></span> writen under that cercle.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Next the cercle of the daies folewith the  
<span id="an_2"></span> cercle of the names of the monthes, that is to  
<span id="an_3"></span> say, Januarius, Februarius, Marcius, Aprilis,  
<span id="an_4"></span> Maius, Junius, Julius, Augustus, September,  
<span id="an_5"></span> October, November, December. The names  
<span id="an_6"></span> of these monthes were clepid somme for  
<span id="an_7"></span> her propirtees and somme by statutes of  
<span id="an_8"></span> Arabiens, somme by othre lordes of Rome.  
<span id="an_9"></span> Eke of these monthes, as liked to Julius  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> Cesar and to
Cesar Augustus, somme were  
<span id="an_11"></span> compouned of diverse nombres of daies, as  
<span id="an_12"></span> Julie and August. Than hath Januarie 31
daies,  
<span id="an_13"></span> Februarie 28, March 31, Aprill 30, May 31,  
<span id="an_14"></span> Junius 30, Julius 31, Augustus 31, Septembre  
<span id="an_15"></span> 30, Octobre 31, Novembre 30, Decembre 31.  
<span id="an_16"></span> Natheles, all though that Julius Cesar toke 2  
<span id="an_17"></span> daies out of Feverer and putte hem in his  
<span id="an_18"></span> month of Juyll, and Augustus Cesar clepid the  
<span id="an_19"></span> month of August after his name and ordeined  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> it of 31 daies,
yit truste wel that the  
<span id="an_21"></span> sonne dwellith therfore nevere the more  
<span id="an_22"></span> ne lasse in oon signe than in another.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Than folewen the names of the holy  
<span id="an_2"></span> daies in the Kalender, and next hem the
lettres  
<span id="an_3"></span> of the A B C on whiche thei fallen.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Next the forseide cercle of the A B C,  
<span id="an_2"></span> under the cross lyne, is marked the skale in  
<span id="an_3"></span> manere of 2 squyres, or ellis in manere of
laddres,  
<span id="an_4"></span> that serveth by his 12 pointes and his  
<span id="an_5"></span> dyvisiouns of ful many a subtil conclusioun.  
<span id="an_6"></span> Of this forseide skale fro the cross lyne unto  
<span id="an_7"></span> the verrey angle is clepid Umbra Versa, and  
<span id="an_8"></span> the nethir partie is clepid Umbra Recta, or  
<span id="an_9"></span> ellis Umbra Extensa.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Than hast thou a brod reule, that hath  
<span id="an_2"></span> on either ende a square plate perced with
certein  
<span id="an_3"></span> holes, somme more and somme lasse, to  
<span id="an_4"></span> resceyve the stremes of the sonne by day, and  
<span id="an_5"></span> eke by mediacioun of thin eye to knowe the  
<span id="an_6"></span> altitude of sterres by night.  
<span id="an_1"></span> Than is there a large pyn in manere of  
<span id="an_2"></span> an extre, that goth thorugh the hole that halt  
<span id="an_3"></span> the tables of the clymates and the riet in the  
<span id="an_4"></span> wombe of the moder; thorugh which pyn ther  
<span id="an_5"></span> goth a litel wegge, which that is clepid the  
<span id="an_6"></span> hors, that streynith all these parties
to-hepe.  
<span id="an_7"></span> Thys forseide grete pyn in manere of an extre  
<span id="an_8"></span> is ymagyned to be the Pool Artik in thyn  
<span id="an_9"></span> Astralabie.  
<span id="an_1"></span> The wombe syde of thyn Astrelabie is  
<span id="an_2"></span> also divided with a longe cros in 4 quarters  
<span id="an_3"></span> from est to west, fro southe to northe, fro  
<span id="an_4"></span> right syde to left side, as is the bakside.  
<span id="an_1"></span> The bordure of which wombe side is  
<span id="an_2"></span> divided fro the point of the est lyne unto the  
<span id="an_3"></span> point of the south lyne under the ring, in 90  
<span id="an_4"></span> degrees; and by that same proporcioun is every  
<span id="an_5"></span> quarter divided, as is the bakside. That  
<span id="an_6"></span> amountith 360 degrees. And understond wel  
<span id="an_7"></span> that degres of this bordure ben aunswering and  
<span id="an_8"></span> consentrike to the degrees of the equinoxiall,  
<span id="an_9"></span> that is dividid in the same nombre as every  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> othir cercle is
in the highe hevene. This  
<span id="an_11"></span> same bordure is divided also with 23  
<span id="an_12"></span> lettres capitals and a small cross (+) above  
<span id="an_13"></span> the south lyne, that shewith the 24 houres  
<span id="an_14"></span> equals of the clokke. And, as I have seid, 5  
<span id="an_15"></span> of these degres maken a myle wey, and 3
mile-wei  
<span id="an_16"></span> maken an houre. And every degre of thys  
<span id="an_17"></span> bordure contenith 4 minutes, and every minute  
<span id="an_18"></span> 60 secundes. Now have I told the twyes.  
<span id="an_1"></span> The plate under the riet is discrived  
<span id="an_2"></span> with 3 cercles, of whiche the leest is  
<span id="an_3"></span> clepid the cercle of Cancre by cause that the  
<span id="an_4"></span> heved of Cancre turnith evermo consentrik  
<span id="an_5"></span> upon the same cercle. In this heved  
<span id="an_6"></span> of Cancer is the grettist declinacioun
northward  
<span id="an_7"></span> of the sonne, and therfore is he clepid  
<span id="an_8"></span> solsticium of somer; which declinacioun, after  
<span id="an_9"></span> Ptholome, is 23 degrees and 50 minutes as  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> wel in Cancer
as in Capricorn. This signe  
<span id="an_11"></span> of Cancer is clepid the tropik of somer, of  
<span id="an_12"></span> tropos, that is to seien "ageynward." For
than  
<span id="an_13"></span> beginneth the sonne to passen from us-ward.  
<span id="an_14"></span> The myddel cercle in wydnesse, of these 3,  
<span id="an_15"></span> is clepid the cercle equinoxiall, upon which  
<span id="an_16"></span> turnith evermo the hevedes of Aries and
Libra.  
<span id="an_17"></span> And understond wel that evermo thys cercle  
<span id="an_18"></span> equinoxiall turnith justly from verrey est to
verrey  
<span id="an_19"></span> west as I have shewed the in the speer  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> solide. This
same cercle is clepid also  
<span id="an_21"></span> Equator, that is the weyer of the day; for  
<span id="an_22"></span> whan the sonne is in the hevedes of Aries and  
<span id="an_23"></span> Libra, than ben the dayes and the nightes
