#### Boece

### Book 5

<span id="an_1"></span> Sche hadde seyd, and torned the cours of  
<span id="an_2"></span> hir resoun to some othere thingis to ben
treted  
<span id="an_3"></span> and to ben ispedd. Thanne seide I, "Certes  
<span id="an_4"></span> ryghtful is thin amonestynge and ful digne by  
<span id="an_5"></span> auctorite. But that thou seydest whilom that  
<span id="an_6"></span> the questioun of the devyne purveaunce is
enlaced  
<span id="an_7"></span> with many othere questiouns, I undirstande  
<span id="an_8"></span> wel and prove it by the same thing.  
<span id="an_9"></span> But I axe yif that thou wenest that hap be  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> anything in any
weys; and yif thou wenest  
<span id="an_11"></span> that hap be anything, what is it?"  
<span id="an_12"></span> Thanne quod sche, "I haste me to yelden  
<span id="an_13"></span> and assoilen to the the dette of my byheste,
and  
<span id="an_14"></span> to schewen and openen the the  
<span id="an_15"></span> wey, by whiche wey thou maist comen ayein to
thi contre. But  
<span id="an_16"></span> al be it so that the thingis whiche that thou
axest  
<span id="an_17"></span> ben ryght profitable to knowe, yit ben thei  
<span id="an_18"></span> divers somwhat fro the path of my purpos; and  
<span id="an_19"></span> it is to douten that thou ne be makid weery  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> by mysweyes, so
that thou ne maist nat  
<span id="an_21"></span> suffise to mesuren the ryghte weie."  
<span id="an_22"></span> "Ne doute the therof nothing," quod I; "for  
<span id="an_23"></span> for to knowen thilke thingis togidre, in the  
<span id="an_24"></span> whiche thinges I delite me gretly -- that
schal  
<span id="an_25"></span> ben to me in stede of reste, syn it nis nat
to  
<span id="an_26"></span> douten of the thingis folwynge, whan every
syde  
<span id="an_27"></span> of thi disputesoun schal han ben stedfast to
me  
<span id="an_28"></span> by undoutous feyth."  
<span id="an_29"></span> "Thanne," seide sche, "that manere wol  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> I don the," and
bygan to speken ryght thus:  
<span id="an_31"></span> "Certes," quod sche, "yif any wyght  
<span id="an_32"></span> diffynisse hap in this manere, that is to seyn
that  
<span id="an_33"></span> \`hap is bytydynge ibrought forth by
foolisshe  
<span id="an_34"></span> moevynge and by no knyttynge of causes,' I  
<span id="an_35"></span> conferme that hap nis ryght naught in no
wise;  
<span id="an_36"></span> and I deme al outrely that hap nis but an
idel  
<span id="an_37"></span> voys (as who seith, but an idel word),
withouten  
<span id="an_38"></span> any significacioun of thing summitted  
<span id="an_39"></span> to that voys. For what place myght ben  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> left or
duellynge to folie and to disordenaunce,  
<span id="an_41"></span> syn that God ledeth and  
<span id="an_42"></span> constreyneth alle thingis by ordre? For this  
<span id="an_43"></span> sentence is verray and soth, that \`no thing
hath  
<span id="an_44"></span> his beynge of naught,' to the whiche sentence  
<span id="an_45"></span> noon of thise oolde folk ne withseide nevere;
al  
<span id="an_46"></span> be it so that they ne undirstoden ne meneden
it  
<span id="an_47"></span> nat by God, prince and bygynnere of wirkynge,  
<span id="an_48"></span> but thei casten as a maner foundement of
subject  
<span id="an_49"></span> material (that is to seyn, of the nature of  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> alle resouns).
And yif that any thing is  
<span id="an_51"></span> woxen or comen of no causes, thanne schal  
<span id="an_52"></span> it seme that thilke thing is comen or woxen
of  
<span id="an_53"></span> nawght; but yif this ne mai nat ben don,
thanne  
<span id="an_54"></span> is it nat possible that hap be any swich thing
as  
<span id="an_55"></span> I have diffynysschid a litil herebyforn."  
<span id="an_56"></span> "How schal it thanne be?" quod I. "Nys ther  
<span id="an_57"></span> thanne nothing that by right may ben clepid  
<span id="an_58"></span> other hap or elles aventure of fortune; or is
ther  
<span id="an_59"></span> awght, al be it so that it is hidd fro the  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> peple, to
whiche thing thise wordes ben  
<span id="an_61"></span> covenable?"  
<span id="an_62"></span> "Myn Aristotle," quod sche, "in the book of  
<span id="an_63"></span> his Phisic diffynysseth this thing by schort  
<span id="an_64"></span> resoun, and nyghe to the sothe."  
<span id="an_65"></span> "In whiche manere?" quod I.  
<span id="an_66"></span> "As ofte, quod sche, "as men don any thing  
<span id="an_67"></span> for grace of any other thing, and another
thing  
<span id="an_68"></span> than thilke thing that men entenden to don  
<span id="an_69"></span> bytideth by some causes, it is clepid hap.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> Ryght as a man
dalf the erthe bycause of  
<span id="an_71"></span> tylyinge of the feld, and founde ther a  
<span id="an_72"></span> gobet of gold bydolven; thanne wenen folk  
<span id="an_73"></span> that it is byfalle by fortunous bytydynge.
But  
<span id="an_74"></span> forsothe it nis nat of naught, for it hath
his  
<span id="an_75"></span> propre causes, of whiche causes the cours
unforseyn  
<span id="an_76"></span> and unwar semeth to han makid hap.  
<span id="an_77"></span> For yif the tiliere of the feeld ne dulve nat
in the  
<span id="an_78"></span> erthe, and yif the hidere of the gold ne
hadde  
<span id="an_79"></span> hyd the gold in thilke place, the gold ne  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_80">80</span></span> hadde nat ben
founde. Thise ben thanne  
<span id="an_81"></span> the causes of the abregginge of fortuit hap,  
<span id="an_82"></span> the whiche abreggynge of fortuit hap cometh
of  
<span id="an_83"></span> causes encontrynge and flowynge togidere to  
<span id="an_84"></span> hemself, and nat by the entencioun of the
doere.  
<span id="an_85"></span> For neither the hidere of the gold ne the
delvere  
<span id="an_86"></span> of the feeld ne undirstoden nat that the gold  
<span id="an_87"></span> sholde han ben founde; but, as I seide, it
bytidde  
<span id="an_88"></span> and ran togidre that he dalf thare as that
oothir  
<span id="an_89"></span> had hid the gold. Now mai I thus diffinysshen  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_90">90</span></span> hap: hap is an
unwar betydinge  
<span id="an_91"></span> of causes assembled in thingis that ben  
<span id="an_92"></span> doon for som oothir thing; but thilke ordre,  
<span id="an_93"></span> procedinge by an uneschuable byndinge
togidre,  
<span id="an_94"></span> whiche that descendeth fro the welle of  
<span id="an_95"></span> purveaunce that ordeyneth alle thingis in hir  
<span id="an_96"></span> places and in hir tymes, makith that the
causes  
<span id="an_97"></span> rennen and assemblen togidre.  
<span id="an_1"></span> "Tigrys and Eufrates resolven and springen  
<span id="an_2"></span> of o welle in the cragges of the roche of the  
<span id="an_3"></span> contre of Achemenye, ther as the fleinge
bataile  
<span id="an_4"></span> ficcheth hir dartes retorned in the breestis  
<span id="an_5"></span> of hem that folwen hem. And sone aftir the  
<span id="an_6"></span> same ryverys, Tigris and Eufrates, unjoignen  
<span id="an_7"></span> and departen hir watres. And if thei comen
togidre  
<span id="an_8"></span> and ben assemblid and clepid togidre  
<span id="an_9"></span> into o cours, thanne moten thilke thingis  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> fleten togidre
whiche that the watir of  
<span id="an_11"></span> the entrechaungynge flood bryngeth. The  
<span id="an_12"></span> schippes and the stokkes araced with the
flood  
<span id="an_13"></span> moten assemblen; and the watris imedled  
<span id="an_14"></span> wrappeth or emplieth many fortunel happes  
<span id="an_15"></span> or maneris; the whiche wandrynge happes  
<span id="an_16"></span> natheles thilke enclynynge lowenesse of the  
<span id="an_17"></span> erthe and the flowinge ordre of the slydinge  
<span id="an_18"></span> watir governeth. Right so fortune, that
semeth  
<span id="an_19"></span> as it fletith with slakid or ungoverned  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> bridles, it
suffreth bridelis (that is to seyn,  
<span id="an_21"></span> to ben governed), and passeth by thilke  
<span id="an_22"></span> lawe (that is to seyn, by the devyne
ordenaunce)."  
<span id="an_1"></span> "This undirstonde I wel," quod I, "and I
accorde  
<span id="an_2"></span> me that it is ryght as thou seist. But I  
<span id="an_3"></span> axe yif ther be any liberte of fre wille in
this  
<span id="an_4"></span> ordre of causes that clyven thus togidre in  
<span id="an_5"></span> hemself, or elles I wolde witen yif that the  
<span id="an_6"></span> destinal cheyne constrenith the moevynges of  
<span id="an_7"></span> the corages of men."  
<span id="an_8"></span> "Yis," quod sche, "ther is liberte of fre wil,  
<span id="an_9"></span> ne ther ne was nevere no nature of resoun  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> that it ne
hadde liberte of fre wil. For every  
<span id="an_11"></span> thing that may naturely usen resoun,  
<span id="an_12"></span> it hath doom by whiche it discernith and
demeth  
<span id="an_13"></span> every thing; thanne knoweth it by itself  
<span id="an_14"></span> thinges that ben to fleen and thinges that
ben  
<span id="an_15"></span> to desiren. And thilke thing that any wight  
<span id="an_16"></span> demeth to ben desired, that axeth or desireth  
<span id="an_17"></span> he; and fleeth thilke thing that he troweth
be  
<span id="an_18"></span> to fleen. Wherfore in alle thingis that
resoun  
<span id="an_19"></span> is, in hem also is liberte of willynge and of  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> nillynge. But I
ne ordeyne nat (as who  
<span id="an_21"></span> seith, I ne graunte nat) that this liberte be  
<span id="an_22"></span> evenelyk in alle thinges. Forwhy in the
sovereynes  
<span id="an_23"></span> devynes substaunces (that is to seyn,  
<span id="an_24"></span> in spiritz) jugement is more cleer, and wil
nat  
<span id="an_25"></span> icorrumped, and myght redy to speden thinges  
<span id="an_26"></span> that ben desired. But the soules of men moten  
<span id="an_27"></span> nedes be more fre whan thei loken hem in the  
<span id="an_28"></span> speculacioun or lokynge of the devyne
thought;  
<span id="an_29"></span> and lasse fre whan thei slyden into the
bodyes;  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> and yit lasse
fre whan thei ben gadrid  
<span id="an_31"></span> togidre and comprehended in erthli  
<span id="an_32"></span> membres; but the laste servage is whan that  
<span id="an_33"></span> thei ben yeven to vices and han ifalle fro
the  
<span id="an_34"></span> possessioun of hir propre resoun. For aftir
that  
<span id="an_35"></span> thei han cast awey hir eyghen fro the lyght  
<span id="an_36"></span> of the sovereyn sothfastnesse to lowe thingis  
<span id="an_37"></span> and derke, anon thei derken by the cloude of  
<span id="an_38"></span> ignoraunce and ben troubled by felonous
talentz;  
<span id="an_39"></span> to the whiche talentz whan thei approchen  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> and assenten,
thei helpen and  
<span id="an_41"></span> encrecen the servage whiche thei han  
<span id="an_42"></span> joyned to hemself; and in this manere thei
ben  
<span id="an_43"></span> caytifs fro hir propre liberte. The whiche
thingis  
<span id="an_44"></span> natheles the lokynge of the devyne purveaunce  
<span id="an_45"></span> seth, that alle thingis byholdeth and seeth
fro  
<span id="an_46"></span> eterne, and ordeyneth hem everiche in here  
<span id="an_47"></span> merites as thei ben predestinat; and it is seid
in  
<span id="an_48"></span> Greke that \`alle thinges he seeth and alle
thinges  
<span id="an_49"></span> he herith.'  
<span id="an_1"></span> "Homer with the hony mouth (that is to seyn,  
<span id="an_2"></span> Homer with the swete ditees) singeth that the  
<span id="an_3"></span> sonne is cler by pure light; natheles yit ne  
<span id="an_4"></span> mai it nat, by the infirme light of his bemes,  
<span id="an_5"></span> breken or percen the inward entrayles of the  
<span id="an_6"></span> erthe or elles of the see. So ne seth nat God,  
<span id="an_7"></span> makere of the grete werld. To hym, that loketh  
<span id="an_8"></span> alle thinges from an hey, ne withstondeth  
<span id="an_9"></span> no thinges by hevynesse of erthe, ne the  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> nyght ne
withstondeth nat to hym by the  
<span id="an_11"></span> blake cloudes. Thilke God seeth in o strok  
<span id="an_12"></span> of thought alle thinges that ben, or weren,
or  
<span id="an_13"></span> schollen comen; and thilke God, for he loketh  
<span id="an_14"></span> and seeth alle thingis alone, thou maist seyn  
<span id="an_15"></span> that he is the verrai sonne."  
<span id="an_1"></span> Thanne seide I, "Now am I confowndide by  
<span id="an_2"></span> a more hard doute than I was."  
<span id="an_3"></span> "What doute is that?" quod sche, "for certes I  
<span id="an_4"></span> conjecte now by whiche thingis thou art
trubled."  
<span id="an_5"></span> "It semeth," quod I, "to repugnen and to  
<span id="an_6"></span> contrarien gretly, that God knoweth byforn
alle  
<span id="an_7"></span> thinges and that ther is any fredom of
liberte.  
<span id="an_8"></span> For yif so be that God loketh alle thinges  
<span id="an_9"></span> byforn, ne God ne mai nat ben desceyved  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_10">10</span></span> in no manere,
thanne moot it nedes ben that  
<span id="an_11"></span> alle thinges betyden the whiche that the  
<span id="an_12"></span> purveaunce of God hath seyn byforn to comen.  
<span id="an_13"></span> For whiche, yif that God knoweth byforn nat  
<span id="an_14"></span> oonly the werkes of men, but also hir
conseilles  
<span id="an_15"></span> and hir willes, thanne ne schal ther be no
liberte  
<span id="an_16"></span> of arbitrie; ne certes ther ne may be noon  
<span id="an_17"></span> othir dede, ne no wil, but thilke whiche that
the  
<span id="an_18"></span> devyne purveaunce, that ne mai nat ben
disseyved,  
<span id="an_19"></span> hath felid byforn. For yif that thei  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_20">20</span></span> myghten writhen
awey in othere manere  
<span id="an_21"></span> than thei ben purveyed, thanne ne sholde  
<span id="an_22"></span> ther be no stedefast prescience of thing to  
<span id="an_23"></span> comen, but rather an uncerteyn opynioun; the  
<span id="an_24"></span> whiche thing to trowen of God, I deme it
felonye  
<span id="an_25"></span> and unleveful.  
<span id="an_26"></span> "Ne I ne proeve nat thilke same resoun (as
who  
<span id="an_27"></span> seith, I ne allowe nat, or I ne preyse nat,
thilke  
<span id="an_28"></span> same resoun) by whiche that som men wenen  
<span id="an_29"></span> that thei mowe assoilen and unknytten the  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_30">30</span></span> knotte of this
questioun. For certes thei  
<span id="an_31"></span> seyn that thing nis nat to comen for that the  
<span id="an_32"></span> purveaunce of God hath seyn byforn that it is
to  
<span id="an_33"></span> comen, but rathir the contrarie; and that is
this:  
<span id="an_34"></span> that, for that the thing is to comen, that
therfore  
<span id="an_35"></span> ne mai it nat ben hidd fro the purveaunce of  
<span id="an_36"></span> God; and in this manere this necessite
slideth  
<span id="an_37"></span> ayein into the contrarie partie: ne it ne
byhoveth  
<span id="an_38"></span> nat nedes that thinges betiden that ben  
<span id="an_39"></span> ipurveied, but it byhoveth nedes that thinges  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_40">40</span></span> that ben to
comen ben ipurveied -- but as  
<span id="an_41"></span> it were Y travailed (as who seith, that  
<span id="an_42"></span> thilke answere procedith ryght as though men  
<span id="an_43"></span> travaileden or weren besy) to enqueren the  
<span id="an_44"></span> whiche thing is cause of the whiche thing, as  
<span id="an_45"></span> whethir the prescience is cause of the
necessite  
<span id="an_46"></span> of thinges to comen, or elles that the
necessite of  
<span id="an_47"></span> thinges to comen is cause of the purveaunce.
But  
<span id="an_48"></span> I ne enforce me nat now to schewen it, that  
<span id="an_49"></span> the bytidynge of thingis iwyst byforn is  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_50">50</span></span> necessarie, how
so or in what manere that  
<span id="an_51"></span> the ordre of causes hath itself; although  
<span id="an_52"></span> that it ne seme naught that the prescience
bringe  
<span id="an_53"></span> in necessite of bytydinge to thinges to
comen.  
<span id="an_54"></span> "For certes yif that any wyght sitteth, it
byhoveth  
<span id="an_55"></span> by necessite that the opynioun be soth of  
<span id="an_56"></span> hym that conjecteth that he sitteth. and  
<span id="an_57"></span> ayeinward also is it of the contrarie: yif
the  
<span id="an_58"></span> opinioun be soth of any wyght for that he
sitteth,  
<span id="an_59"></span> it byhoveth by necessite that he sitte.  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_60">60</span></span> Thanne is here
necessite in the toon and in  
<span id="an_61"></span> the tothir; for in the toon is necessite of  
<span id="an_62"></span> syttynge, and certes in the tothir is necessite
of  
<span id="an_63"></span> soth. But therfore ne sitteth nat a wyght for
that  
<span id="an_64"></span> the opynioun of the sittynge is soth, but the  
<span id="an_65"></span> opinioun is rather soth for that a wyght
sitteth  
<span id="an_66"></span> byforn. And thus, althoughe that the cause of
the  
<span id="an_67"></span> soth cometh of that other side (as who seith,  
<span id="an_68"></span> that althoughe the cause of soth cometh of
the  
<span id="an_69"></span> sittynge, and nat of the trewe opinioun),  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_70">70</span></span> algatis yit is
ther comune necessite in that  
<span id="an_71"></span> oon and in that othir. Thus scheweth it that  
<span id="an_72"></span> Y may make semblable skiles of the purveaunce  
<span id="an_73"></span> of God and of thingis to comen. For althoughe  
<span id="an_74"></span> that for that thingis ben to comen therfore
ben  
<span id="an_75"></span> thei purveied, and nat certes for thei be
purveied  
<span id="an_76"></span> therfore ne bytide thei nat; yit natheles
byhoveth  
<span id="an_77"></span> it by necessite that eyther the thinges to
comen  
<span id="an_78"></span> ben ipurveied of God, or elles that the
thinges  
<span id="an_79"></span> that ben ipurveyed of God betyden. And  
<span class="margnote"><span id="an_80">80</span></span> this thing
oonly suffiseth inow to destroien  
<span id="an_81"></span> the fredom of oure arbitre (that is to seyn,  
<span id="an_82"></span> of our fre wil).  
<span id="an_83"></span> "But certes now schewith it wel how fer fro  
<span id="an_84"></span> the sothe and how up-so-doun is this thing
that  
<span id="an_85"></span> we seyn, that the betydynge of temporel
thingis
