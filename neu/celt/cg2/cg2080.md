<span class="small">THIS poem was obtained in 1891 from Malcolm
Macmillan, crofter, Grimnis, Benbecula. Macmillan was then an old man.
He heard this and many other poems when a boy from old people who, when
evicted in Uist, emigrated to Prince Edward's Island, Nova Scotia, Cape
Breton, and other parts of the Canadian Dominion, and <span
id="page_163">p. 163</span> to Australia. These old people took great
quantities of traditional Gaelic lore with them to their new homes, some
of which still lingers among their descendants. Many original and
translated songs of the Highlands and Islands are sung among these
settlers, whose hearts still yearn towards their motherland.</span>

<td data-valign="top"><p>JOSEPH and Mary went<br />
To the numbering up,<br />
And the birds began chorusing<br />
In the woods of the turtle-doves.</p>
<p>The two were walking the way,<br />
Till they reached a thick wood,<br />
And in the wood there was fruit<br />
Which was as red as the rasp.</p>
<p>That was the time when she was great,<br />
That she was carrying the King of grace,<br />
And she took a desire for the fruit<br />
That was growing on the gracious slope.</p>
<p>Then spoke Mary to Joseph,<br />
In a voice low and sweet,<br />
'Give to me of the fruit, Joseph,<br />
That I may quench my desire.'</p>
<p>And Joseph spoke to Mary,<br />
And the hard pain in his breast,<br />
'I will give thee of the fruit, Mary,<br />
But who is the father of thy burthen?'</p></td>
