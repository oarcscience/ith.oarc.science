# The Discourses.

### Book III. Notes.

<span id="fn_28"></span>[3-1](dep060.htm#fr_28.md) 'The *pancratium*
consisted of boxing and wrestling, and was supposed to test all a man's
powers. The *pentathlon* included running, jumping, quoit-throwing,
spear-throwing, and wrestling.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_29"></span>[3-2](dep060.htm#fr_29.md) 'Young men of 18-20,
under military training.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_30"></span>[3-3](dep061.htm#fr_30.md) Cf. Book I, note 7.

<span id="fn_31"></span>[3-4](dep066.htm#fr_31.md) 'Such special
commissioners, sent to set in order the affairs of the "free cities",
date from Trajan's reign.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_32"></span>[3-5](dep066.htm#fr_32.md) These men are otherwise
unknown. Matheson suggests that they were freedmen who had risen to high
position.

<span id="fn_33"></span>[3-6](dep067.htm#fr_33.md) A technical Stoic term.

<span id="fn_34"></span>[3-7](dep068.htm#fr_34.md) I.e., a representative
of the town at Rome.

<span id="fn_35"></span>[3-8](dep068.htm#fr_35.md) A very valuable
material.

<span id="fn_36"></span>[3-9](dep072.htm#fr_36.md) A reference to the Stoic
doctrine, ultimately derived from Heraclitus, that the universe was
periodically consumed by fire.

<span id="fn_37"></span>[3-10](dep072.htm#fr_37.md) 'Trajan after his
conquest of Dacia.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_38"></span>[3-11](dep072.htm#fr_38.md) 'It is the drivel of
the sickly novice in philosophy.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_39"></span>[3-12](dep074.htm#fr_39.md) Cf. the *Manual*, chap.
29.

<span id="fn_40"></span>[3-13](dep079.htm#fr_40.md) Probably a slave.

<span id="fn_41"></span>[3-14](dep081.htm#fr_41.md) 'The Cynic whom
Epictetus describes is the ideal Stoic teacher, who is distinguished
from the ordinary Stoic by a more austere and isolated life.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_42"></span>[3-15](dep081.htm#fr_42.md) 'Used to get a smooth
skin.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_43"></span>[3-16](dep082.htm#fr_43.md) Cf. Xenophon,
*Memorabilia*, *init*.

<span id="fn_44"></span>[3-17](dep083.htm#fr_44.md) 'Probably refers to the
story that Nicocreon ordered Anaxarchus’ tongue to be cut out, whereupon
he bit it off himself and spat it in Nicocreon's face. Cf. Diogenes
Laertius, IX. 59.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_45"></span>[3-18](dep083.htm#fr_45.md) 'A saying attributed to
Solon, Anaxagoras, and Xenophon.' (Matheson)

<span id="fn_46"></span>[3-19](dep085.htm#fr_46.md) The name of a slave.
