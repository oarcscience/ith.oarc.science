#### EXCERPT XXIV.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### \[A HYMN OF THE GODS\]

<span class="small">(Text: Stob., *Phys*., v. 14, under the simple
heading: “Of Hermes”; G. p. 65 M. i. 45; W. i. 77. The same verses are
read in the appendix to the *Anthologia Palatina*, p. 768, n.
40.)</span>

<span class="smallcaps">Seven</span> Stars far varied in their course
revolved upon the \[wide\] Olympian plain; with them for ever will
Eternity <span id="fr_291"></span>[1](#fn_291) spin \[fate\] <span
id="fr_292"></span>[2](#fn_292):—Mēnē that shines by night, \[and\]
gloomy Kronos, \[and\] sweet Hēlios, and Paphiē who’s carried in the
shrine, <span id="fr_293"></span>[3](#fn_293) courageous Arēs,
fair-wingèd Hermēs, and Zeus the primal source <span
id="fr_294"></span>[4](#fn_294) from whom Nature doth come.

Now they themselves have had the race of

<span id="page_92">p. 92</span>

men entrusted to their care; so that in us there is a Mēnē, Zeus, an
Arēs, Paphiē, a Kronos, Hēlios and Hermēs.

Wherefore we are divided up \[so as\] to draw from the ætherial
spirit, <span id="fr_295"></span>[1](#fn_295) tears, laughter, anger,
birth, reason, sleep, desire.

Tears are Kronos, birth Zeus, reason \[is\] Hermēs, courage Mars, and
Mēnē sleep, in sooth, and Cytherēa desire, and Hēlios \[is\]
laughter—for ’tis because of him that justly every mortal thinking thing
doth laugh and the immortal world.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### COMMENT

This is the only known specimen of verses attributed to the Trismegistic
tradition. Liddell and Scott, however, under “νυκτιφανής,” do not
question this attribution, while Clement of Alexandria (*Strom*., vi. p.
633 \[this is a reference of Wachsmuth’s which I cannot verify\])
praises the “Hymns of the Gods” of Hermes. On the contrary, in *Anthol.
Palat*., p. 442, n. 491, the seventh verse is ascribed to Theon of
Alexandria.
