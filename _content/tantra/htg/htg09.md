### Footnotes

<span id="fn_204"></span>[43:1](htg09.htm#fr_204) From the Mahānirvāṇa
Tantra, Seventh Ullāsa, verses 12 *et seq*. This hymn to the primordial
Kālī contains a hundred of her names all beginning with "K." Thus Kālī,
Karālī, Kalyānī, Kalāvatī, Kamalā, Kalidarpaghni, Kaparddīśakripanvitā,
etc. *Kādi* is that which has "Ka" in the beginning. In the Tantrarāja,
Devī says to Śiva: "The syllable 'Ka' is in Thy form, and that Śakti
confers all *siddhis*" (see Lalitā Sahasranāma, where a number of the
following names occur).

<span id="fn_205"></span>[43:2](htg09.htm#fr_205) The *Māyābīja* (see
Fifth Ullāsa, verse 10).

<span id="fn_206"></span>[43:3](htg09.htm#fr_206) Kālī (see Fourth
Ullāsa, verses 30 *et seq*.). She is thus called *Kālakarshinī*.

<span id="fn_207"></span>[43:4](htg09.htm#fr_207) *Bīja* of Lakṣmī, Devī
of prosperity or beauty.

<span id="fn_208"></span>[43:5](htg09.htm#fr_208) Karālī.

<span id="fn_209"></span>[43:6](htg09.htm#fr_209) The Bījābhidāna says
*Ka* = Kālī, *Ra* = Brahmā, *Ī* = Mahāmāyā. The half circle of
*candrabindu* is the universal Mother, and the point is the destroyer of
misery.

<span id="fn_210"></span>[43:7](htg09.htm#fr_210) Kalyāṇī, or She who
bestows peace and happiness (see the Lalitā, verse 73). According to the
Padmapurāṇa, Devī is worshipped as Kalyāṇī in the Malaya mountains.

<span id="fn_211"></span>[44:1](htg09.htm#fr_211) Kalāvatī (see the
Lalitā, verse 74). The *Kālā*, or arts, are sixty-four in number. The
Śakti should always be *Kalāvatī*. Devī is also called *Kalāmālā*, or
garland of the arts. Kalāvatī may also mean possessed of all arts
complete.

<span id="fn_212"></span>[44:2](htg09.htm#fr_212) A name of Lakṣmī-Devī
is Kamala, for She is all Śaktis. In verse 73 of the Lalitā, Devī is
called Kāmakalārūpā, on which Bhāskararāya says that there are three
*bindus* and the *hārdakala*. The first *bindu* is called Kāma, and the
last Kalā; but according to the rule *pratyāhāra*, Kamalā includes all
four. Kālikā Purāṇa says, Devī, is alone indicated by Kāma.

<span id="fn_213"></span>[44:3](htg09.htm#fr_213) Kalidarpaghnī.

<span id="fn_214"></span>[44:4](htg09.htm#fr_214) Kapardīśakripanvitā.
*Kapardīśa* is a title of Śiva derived from his matted hair.

<span id="fn_215"></span>[44:5](htg09.htm#fr_215) Kālikā, because She
devours Śiva as Mahākāla (see Mahānirvāṇa Tantra, Fourth Ullāsa, verse
31).

<span id="fn_216"></span>[44:6](htg09.htm#fr_216) Kālamātā.

<span id="fn_217"></span>[44:7](htg09.htm#fr_217) Kālānalasamadyuti.

<span id="fn_218"></span>[44:8](htg09.htm#fr_218) Kapardinī. Spouse of
Śiva, called *Kapardi* from his matted hair (see Lalitā, verse 151). The
Viśva says that *Kaparda* means the matted hair of Śiva and the cowdung
cakes. When Śiva incarnated, as Mailāra, his spouse was decked with a
garland of cowdung-cakes.

<span id="fn_219"></span>[44:9](htg09.htm#fr_219) Karālāsyā.

<span id="fn_220"></span>[44:10](htg09.htm#fr_220) Karunāmritasāgarā
(see Lalitā, verse 73).

<span id="fn_221"></span>[45:1](htg09.htm#fr_221) Kripāmayī.

<span id="fn_222"></span>[45:2](htg09.htm#fr_222) Kripādhārā.

<span id="fn_223"></span>[45:3](htg09.htm#fr_223) Kripāpārā.

<span id="fn_224"></span>[45:4](htg09.htm#fr_224) Kripāgamā.

<span id="fn_225"></span>[45:5](htg09.htm#fr_225) Kṛṣānu. *Kṛṣānuretas*
is an epithet of Śiva whose male seed is fire.

<span id="fn_226"></span>[45:6](htg09.htm#fr_226) Kapilā.

<span id="fn_227"></span>[45:7](htg09.htm#fr_227) Kṛṣṇā.

<span id="fn_228"></span>[45:8](htg09.htm#fr_228) Kṛṣṇānandavivardhinī.
Kṛṣṇa is here the supreme Lord.

<span id="fn_229"></span>[45:9](htg09.htm#fr_229) Kālaratrī. The Lalitā,
verse 101, speaks of the Devī as attended by hosts of Śaktis, Kālarātrī,
and others--that is the twelve Śaktis from Kālarātrī to Tankārī, one in
each petal of the *anahāta padma*. The Varāha Purāṇa says that Raudrī,
who was born [from](errata.htm#5) darkness and went to the Blue Mountain
to perform penance (the Śakti causing destruction) is called Kālarātrī.

<span id="fn_230"></span>[45:10](htg09.htm#fr_230) Kāmarupā (*ibid*.,
verse 73), Kalika Purāṇa--says that Devī is called Kāma because She came
to the secret place in the Blue peak of the great mountain (Kāilāsa)
along with Śiva, for the sake of desire, and because She fulfils desires
and destroys and restores the body of Kāma.

<span id="fn_231"></span>[45:11](htg09.htm#fr_231) Kāmapāśavimocinī.

<span id="fn_232"></span>[45:12](htg09.htm#fr_232) Kādambinī. In the
heat of India the rain-cloud is welcome, and in some of Her forms She is
dark.

<span id="fn_233"></span>[46:1](htg09.htm#fr_233) Kalādhārā.

<span id="fn_234"></span>[46:2](htg09.htm#fr_234) Kalikalmaṣanāśinī (see
Lalitā, verse 113), Kūrma Purāṇa says that the repetition of the names
of Devī destroys the multitude of sins in the Kālī Age.

<span id="fn_235"></span>[46:3](htg09.htm#fr_235) Kumāripūjanapritā. The
*Kumārī pūjā*, or worship of virgins, is a *pūjā* common in Bengal. The
worship is by men, whereas the *sadhavapūjā*, or worship of married
women, is done by women. Or Kumārī may directly refer to the Devī
Herself. She is known as the unmarried Kumārī. A *sūtra* of the Śiva
Sūtras runs *Iccāśaktih Umā kumārī*. The energy of desire is Umā, the
unmarried. Bhāskararāya (Commentary, Lalitā, verses 25, 40) says: As
play She creates the universe, hence She is Kumārī, or She destroys
(*mārayate*) the ground (*ku*) of the great illusion. Hence She is
Kumārī; Kumārī is the enjoyer and not to be enjoyed, as She is one with
the *Yogi*, the enjoyer.

<span id="fn_236"></span>[46:4](htg09.htm#fr_236) Kumārī pūjakālayā, or
"who art the refuge of such worshippers."

<span id="fn_237"></span>[46:5](htg09.htm#fr_237) Kumāribhojanānanda.
The *Kumārīs* are feasted at the *Kumārī pūjā*.

<span id="fn_238"></span>[46:6](htg09.htm#fr_238) Kumārīrūpadhārinī. So
a very young marriageable girl is called Gauri.

<span id="fn_239"></span>[46:7](htg09.htm#fr_239) Kadambavanasanchārā
(see Hymn to Tripurasundari, *post*).

<span id="fn_240"></span>[46:8](htg09.htm#fr_240) Kadambapuṣpasantoṣā
(see Lalitā, verse 73).

<span id="fn_241"></span>[47:1](htg09.htm#fr_241) Kadambavanavāsinī (see
the Lalitā, verse 23). The palace of *Cintāmani* is surrounded by a
gallery of gems (*maṇimaṇḍapa*). Around this is the grove of Kadamba
trees, which in the Purāṇās are said to be seven yojanas in height in
the space between the walls of gold and silver. The Bhairavayāmala says
the abode of Bindu is the ocean of nectar. The five *yonis*--that is,
the five Śakti angles in the *śricakra*--are the divine trees. There is
the grove of *Nīpa* trees. Within that is the gallery of gems. Within
that is the palace of *Cintāmaṇi*.

<span id="fn_242"></span>[47:2](htg09.htm#fr_242) Kadambapuṣpamālinī.
The Lalitā, verse 8, speaks of Devī as decked above her ears with
clusters of Kadamba flowers.

<span id="fn_243"></span>[47:3](htg09.htm#fr_243) Kishorī.

<span id="fn_244"></span>[47:4](htg09.htm#fr_244) Kālakaṇṭhā. Devī is
also called Kālakaṇṭhī, or wife of Kālakaṇṭha, a name of Śiva whose
throat was coloured by the poison he swallowed at the churning of the
ocean. According to Devī purāṇa, Kālakaṇṭha was worshipped at Kālanjara.

<span id="fn_245"></span>[47:5](htg09.htm#fr_245) Kalanādaninādinī.
Water-birds which sing to one another at night--the male to the female
and the latter to the male--sitting on opposite banks of the river.
Their passionate devotion is often alluded to by the poets.

<span id="fn_246"></span>[47:6](htg09.htm#fr_246) Kādambaripānaratā.

<span id="fn_247"></span>[47:7](htg09.htm#fr_247) Kādambarīpriyā.
*Kādambarī* is mead. Bhāskararāya (Commentary, Lalitā), dealing with the
Tripurā Upanishad, which prescribes that mead, fish, flesh, and cooked
cereals should be offered to the Devatā, says that it enjoins those who
are allowed to take wine, flesh, etc., that they should do so after
first dedicating them to the Brahman, and minimize the habit by
gradation (see the same idea expressed in Manu v., 56, Bhāg. Pr. XI.,
5-11).

<span id="fn_248"></span>[48:1](htg09.htm#fr_248) Kapālapātraniratā.

<span id="fn_249"></span>[48:2](htg09.htm#fr_249) Kamkālamālyadhārini.

<span id="fn_250"></span>[48:3](htg09.htm#fr_250) Kamalāsanasantuṣṭā.

<span id="fn_251"></span>[48:4](htg09.htm#fr_251) Kamalāsanavāsinī.

<span id="fn_252"></span>[48:5](htg09.htm#fr_252) Kamalālayamadhyasthā.

<span id="fn_253"></span>[48:6](htg09.htm#fr_253) Kamalāmodamodinī.

<span id="fn_254"></span>[48:7](htg09.htm#fr_254) Kalahamsagatī. *Hamsa*
is variously translated goose, swan, flamingo. A swaying waddle like
that of a duck is admired. As the swans live in the celestial lake
called Mānasa, so She lives in the minds (*mānasa*) of Her devotees.

<span id="fn_255"></span>[48:8](htg09.htm#fr_255) Klaibyanāśinī.

<span id="fn_256"></span>[48:9](htg09.htm#fr_256) Kāmarūpinī (see also
*post*).

<span id="fn_257"></span>[48:10](htg09.htm#fr_257) Kāmarūpakritavāsā
(see next note).

<span id="fn_258"></span>[48:11](htg09.htm#fr_258) Kāmapīthavilāsinī.
Kāmarūpa, the great Tantrika centre in Assam, one of the *Mahāpīthas*,
where the genital organ of the Devī fell on the severance of Her dead
body by Viṣṇu after the *Dakṣa Yajna*. Kāmarupa is also one of the
*Ādibhuta*, which are in the *Mūlādhāra* and other tattvik centres (see
the Lalitā, verse 82).

<span id="fn_259"></span>[48:12](htg09.htm#fr_259) Kamanīyā.

<span id="fn_260"></span>[48:13](htg09.htm#fr_260) Kalpalatā. The
*Kalpa* tree is one of the celestial trees in the heaven of India, which
yielded whatever one desired. Woman is likened to a creeper (*latā*)
embracing and depending on her <span id="page_49">p. 49</span> husband
(see also the Lalitā, where the Devī is called *Bhaktimatkalpalatikā*,
the *kalpa* creeper of the devotee).

<span id="fn_261"></span>[49:1](htg09.htm#fr_261) Kamanīyavibhūṣanā, or
"who art the possessor of beautiful ornaments."

<span id="fn_262"></span>[49:2](htg09.htm#fr_262) Kamanīyagunārādhyā; or
it may mean that the Devī is to be worshipped by the worshipper with all
best and tender feeling.

<span id="fn_263"></span>[49:3](htg09.htm#fr_263) Komalāngī.

<span id="fn_264"></span>[49:4](htg09.htm#fr_264) Krishodarī. Literally,
small of belly (see Bhuvaneśvarī *stotra*, *apivarastanatating
tanuvrittamadhyām*, and the Lalitā, verse 79, *tanumadhyā*).

<span id="fn_265"></span>[49:5](htg09.htm#fr_265) Kāranāmritasantoshā.
*Kārana* is one of the technical terms given to the purified wine
consumed as an element of the Tāntrika pancatattva.

<span id="fn_266"></span>[49:6](htg09.htm#fr_266) Kāranānandasiddhidā.

<span id="fn_267"></span>[49:7](htg09.htm#fr_267) Kāranānandajapeshtā.

<span id="fn_268"></span>[49:8](htg09.htm#fr_268)
Kāranārchchanaharshitā.

<span id="fn_269"></span>[49:9](htg09.htm#fr_269) Kāranārnavasangmagnā.

<span id="fn_270"></span>[49:10](htg09.htm#fr_270) Kāranavratapālinī.
*Vratas* are ritual acts and devotional exercises which do not belong to
the obligatory (*nitya*) *karma*.

<span id="fn_271"></span>[50:1](htg09.htm#fr_271) Kastūrīsaurabhāmodā.
Musk is used in worship to scent *candan*, etc.

<span id="fn_272"></span>[50:2](htg09.htm#fr_272) Kastūrītilakojjvalā.
The *tilak* is the mark worn by sectarian Hindus on the forehead.

<span id="fn_273"></span>[50:3](htg09.htm#fr_273) Kastūrīpūjanaratā.

<span id="fn_274"></span>[50:4](htg09.htm#fr_274) Kastūrīpūjakapriyā.

<span id="fn_275"></span>[50:5](htg09.htm#fr_275) Kastūrīdāhajananī.

<span id="fn_276"></span>[50:6](htg09.htm#fr_276) Kastūrīmrigatoshinī.

<span id="fn_277"></span>[50:7](htg09.htm#fr_277) Kastūrībhojanapritā.

<span id="fn_278"></span>[50:8](htg09.htm#fr_278) Karpūrāmodamoditā.
Camphor is burnt in worship during *ārati*, and is used to scent the
*pādya*, betel leaf, etc., offered to the Devī.

<span id="fn_279"></span>[50:9](htg09.htm#fr_279) Karpūramālābharanā.
Balls of camphor are strung together in a garland.

<span id="fn_280"></span>[50:10](htg09.htm#fr_280) Karpūracandanākshitā.

<span id="fn_281"></span>[50:11](htg09.htm#fr_281) Karpūrakāranāhladā.

<span id="fn_282"></span>[50:12](htg09.htm#fr_282) Karpūrāmritapāyinī.

<span id="fn_283"></span>[51:1](htg09.htm#fr_283) Karpūrasāgarasnatā.

<span id="fn_284"></span>[51:2](htg09.htm#fr_284) Karpūrasāgarālayā.

<span id="fn_285"></span>[51:3](htg09.htm#fr_285) Kūrchchabījajapapritā.
*Hūm* is the *Kūrchhabīja*.

<span id="fn_286"></span>[51:4](htg09.htm#fr_286)
Kūrchchajapaparāyayanā--that is, She who mutters "*Hūm*" when conquering
the demons. The Devī then constantly uttered the *hūmkāra* with its
threatening, roaring sound.

<span id="fn_287"></span>[51:5](htg09.htm#fr_287) Kulīna. *Kula*,
according to the Tantra, means Śakti. *Akula* = Śiva. The union of
*Kula* with *Akula* is called Kaula, the essence common to both Śiva and
Śakti. Hence Devī is *Kaulinī* (see Lalitā, verse 37). Both *Kula* and
*Akula* are in the *Sahasrāra*. *Kulācāra* is the way of the *Kaula*
division of Tāntrik worshippers.

<span id="fn_288"></span>[51:6](htg09.htm#fr_288) Kaulikārādhyā. The
*Kaulikas* are followers of *Kulācāra* (see last note). So also in the
Lalitā, verse 17, the Devī is addressed as "adored by *Dakṣinas* and
*Adakṣinas*."

<span id="fn_289"></span>[51:7](htg09.htm#fr_289) Kaulikapriyakārīnī.

<span id="fn_290"></span>[51:8](htg09.htm#fr_290) Kulacārā, *v. ante*.

<span id="fn_291"></span>[51:9](htg09.htm#fr_291) Kantukinī.

<span id="fn_292"></span>[51:10](htg09.htm#fr_292) Kulamārgapradarshinī.

<span id="fn_293"></span>[51:11](htg09.htm#fr_293) Kaśīśvarī. Kāśī is
Benares, the sacred city of Śiva.

<span id="fn_294"></span>[51:12](htg09.htm#fr_294) Kartahartrī; and thus
the Lalitā speaks of the Devī as the moon-light which soothes those
burned by the triple fires of misery.

<span id="fn_295"></span>[52:1](htg09.htm#fr_295) Kāśīśavaradayinī
("Lord" is Śiva).

<span id="fn_296"></span>[52:2](htg09.htm#fr_296) Kāśīśvarakritāmodā.

<span id="fn_297"></span>[52:3](htg09.htm#fr_297) Kāśīśvaramanoramā.

<span id="fn_298"></span>[52:4](htg09.htm#fr_298) Kalamanjīracaranā.

<span id="fn_299"></span>[52:5](htg09.htm#fr_299) Kvanatkāncīvibhūṣanā.

<span id="fn_300"></span>[52:6](htg09.htm#fr_300) Kāncanādrikritāgarā
(the mountain Kāncana or Sumeru). Durvāsa says in his Lalitāstavaratna
(verses 2-4): "Let the gold mountain be victorious whose body is the
universe resounding with the music of celestial women living in the
golden bowers of creepers of the mountain peak. We salute the three
peaks which are the seats of Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Śiva, extending to the
four quarters of the earth. In their midst is another peak 400 yojanas
(a yojana is eight or nine miles) in height, making the place beauteous
with the golden rays of its flowers, and I worship it." The Lalitā
Sahasranāma (verse 22) also addresses Devī as dwelling on the middle
peak of Mount Sumeru.

<span id="fn_301"></span>[52:7](htg09.htm#fr_301) Kāncanācalakaumudi.

<span id="fn_302"></span>[52:8](htg09.htm#fr_302) Kāmabījajapānanda. The
*Kāma bīja* is "*klīm*." When the "*Ka*" and "*La*" are eliminated, the
remaining "*īm*" is called *kāmakalā*, which is in the *turīya* state.
In the Lalitā, verse 225, Devī is called creatrix of the *mantra klīm*
("*klīmkarī*"). *Klīmkāra* is *Śivakāma*, and She is His wife.

<span id="fn_303"></span>[52:9](htg09.htm#fr_303)
Kāmabījasvarūpinī--that is, She is "*klīm*" itself.

<span id="fn_304"></span>[53:1](htg09.htm#fr_304) Kumatighnī. Devī is
also (Lalitā, verse 78) *Sadācāra pravarttakā*, because She makes men
move to right action.

<span id="fn_305"></span>[53:2](htg09.htm#fr_305) Kulīnārtināśinī.

<span id="fn_306"></span>[53:3](htg09.htm#fr_306) Kulakāminī.

<span id="fn_307"></span>[53:4](htg09.htm#fr_307) *Krīm, Hrīm, Śrīm
mantravarṇena kālakanṭakaghātinī*.
