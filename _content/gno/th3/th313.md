#### EXCERPT XII.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### OF PROVIDENCE AND FATE

<span class="small">(Title from Patrizzi (p. 38); followed by: “From the
\[Sermons\] to Ammon.”</span>

<span class="small">Text: Stob., *Phys*., v. 20, under heading: “Of
Hermes from the \[Sermons\] to Ammon”; G. p. 70; M. i. 48, 49; W. i.
82.</span>

<span class="small">Ménard, Livre IV., No. ii. of “Fragments of the
Books of Hermes to Ammon,” p. 258.)</span>

<span class="smallcaps">All</span> things are born by Nature and by
Fate, and there is not a \[single\] space bereft of Providence.

Now Providence is the Self-perfect <span id="fr_196"></span>[1](#fn_196)
Reason.

And of this \[Reason\] there are two spontaneous powers,—Necessity and
Fate.

And Fate doth minister to Providence and to Necessity; while unto Fate
the Stars <span id="fr_197"></span>[2](#fn_197) do minister.

For Fate no one is able to escape, nor keep himself from their <span
id="fr_198"></span>[3](#fn_198) shrewd scrutiny. <span
id="fr_199"></span>[4](#fn_199)

For that the Stars are instruments of Fate; it is at its behest that
they effect all things for nature and for men. <span
id="fr_200"></span>[5](#fn_200)
