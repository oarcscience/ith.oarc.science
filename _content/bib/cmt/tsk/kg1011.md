Overview

[Kg1 11:1](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#001), Solomon's wives and
concubines; [Kg1 11:4](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#004), In his old age
they draw him to idolatry; [Kg1 11:9](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#009),
God threatens him; [Kg1 11:14](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#014),
Solomon's adversaries were Hadad, who was entertained in Egypt; [Kg1
11:23](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#023), Rezon, who reigned in Damascus;
[Kg1 11:26](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#026), and Jeroboam, to whom
Ahijah prophesied; [Kg1 11:41](../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#041),
Solomon's acts, reign, and death. Rehoboam succeeds him.

<span class="margnote"><span id="an_018">kg1 11:18</span></span>

Midian: Probably not the Midian east of the Red Sea, to which Moses fled
([Exo 2:15](../../../bib/kjv/exo002.htm#015), etc.) but the Midian east
of the Dead Sea, and south of Moab. These Midianites, whose daughters
seduced the Israelites to commit idolatry ([Num
22:4](../../../bib/kjv/num022.htm#004), [Num
22:7](../../../bib/kjv/num022.htm#007); [Num
25:15](../../../bib/kjv/num025.htm#015); [Num
31:2](../../../bib/kjv/num031.htm#002), etc.) were descendants of
Midian, son of Abraham ([Gen 25:2](../../../bib/kjv/gen025.htm#002)).
Their capital city was called Midian, and its remains were to be seen in
the time of Eusebius and Jerome. it was situated on the Arnon, south of
the city Ar, or Areopolis. [Gen 25:2](../../../bib/kjv/gen025.htm#002),
[Gen 25:4](../../../bib/kjv/gen025.htm#004); [Num
22:4](../../../bib/kjv/num022.htm#004), [Num
25:6](../../../bib/kjv/num025.htm#006), [Num
25:14](../../../bib/kjv/num025.htm#014), [Num
25:18](../../../bib/kjv/num025.htm#018)

Paran: Probably the city of Paran, or the district around it, situated
in the south of Idumea, and according to Eusebius, three days' journey
east from Elah or Elath, at the head of the eastern branch, or Elamitic
gulf of the Red Sea. [Gen 14:6](../../../bib/kjv/gen014.htm#006), [Gen
21:21](../../../bib/kjv/gen021.htm#021); [Num
10:12](../../../bib/kjv/num010.htm#012); [Deu

in Damascus: Damascus, called also Damesk, but generally El Sham, by the
Arabs, is situated in a delightful plain, well watered by the Barrada,
at the eastern foot of Antilibanus, being surrounded by the hills in the
form of a triumphal arch, 136 miles n of Jerusalem, 195 s of Antioch,
and 276 ssw of Diarbekir. It is a city of the highest antiquity, being
at least as ancient as the time of Abraham. it has been often captured,
and several times demolished, but has always risen to splendour and
dignity. The modern town is described by Maundrell as of a long,
straight figure, it ends pointing nearly ne and sw It is very slender in
the middle, but swells bigger at each end, especially at that to the ne
According to Niebuhr, the walls are something less than a league and
half in circumference; and the population is estimated at from 100,000
to 150,000.
