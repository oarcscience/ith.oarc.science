CHAPTER XXIII.

COUNTINGS-OUT.

OUNTINGS-OUT" form a curious item of folklore and seem to be common among different nations in a variety of forms. The following have been collected in Banffshire and Aberdeenshire. Those who wish to look more into this subject may be referred to a paper on the "Anglo-Cymric Score" in the Transactions of the Philological Society, London (volume for 1877-79, pp. 316-372), by Mr. Ellis, one of the vice-presidents of the society. "As I geed up the brandy hill
I met my father--he geed wull,
He hid jewels, he hid rings,
He'd a cat wi’ ten tails,
He'd a ship wi’ saiven sails,
He'd a haimmer dreeve nails.
Up Jack, doon Tam,
Blaw the bellows, aul’ man."

"Mr. Smith's a very good man,

Up t’ London, doon t’ France.

Tarry cedle, ye're oot."

"Endy tendy, ticker a been,

"Eentie, teentie, tippenny bun,
The cat geed oot to get some fun,

Eentie, teentie, tippenny bun."

"Mr. Mundie, foo’s yir wife
'Verra sick, an like t’ die.'
'Can she eat ony butcher meat?'

Fite puddin, black troot,
