<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>So far Orlando wends, he comes to where<br />
He of old Proteus’ hears the cruel use<br />
But feels such pity for Olympia fair,<br />
Wronged by Cymosco, who in prison mews<br />
Her plighted spouse, that ere he makes repair<br />
Further, he gives her hope to venge the abuse:<br />
He does so, and departs; and with his spouse<br />
Departs Bireno, to repeat his vows.</em></span></td>

<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>W<span class="small">HAT</span> cannot, when he has a heart possess’d<br />
This false and cruel traitor Love? since he<br />
Can banish from Orlando’s faithful breast<br />
Such tried allegiance and due loyalty?<br />
Wise, full of all regards, and of the blest<br />
And glorious church the champion wont to be,<br />
Now, little for himself or uncle, driven<br />
By a vain love, he cares, and less for heaven.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>But I excuse him well, rejoiced to know<br />
I have like partner in my vice: for still<br />
To seek my good I too am faint and slow,<br />
But sound and nimble in pursuit of ill.<br />
The count departs, disguised in sable show,<br />
Nor for so many friends, with froward will,<br />
Deserted cares; and comes where on the plain<br />
Are camped the hosts of Afric and of Spain;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>Rather uncamped: for, in less troops or more,<br />
Rains under shed and tree had driven the band.<br />
Here ten, there twenty, seven or eight, or four,<br />
Near or further off, Orlando scanned.<br />
Each sleeps, oppressed with toil and wearied sore;<br />
This stretched on earth, that propped upon his hand:<br />
They sleep, and many might the count have slain,<br />
Yet never bared his puissant Durindane.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>So generous is Orlando’s heart, he base<br />
Esteems it were to smite a sleeping foe.<br />
Now this he seeks, and now that other place;<br />
Yet cannot track his lady, high or low.<br />
If he finds any one in waking case,<br />
Sighing, to him he paints her form and show;<br />
Then prays him that for courtesy, he where<br />
The damsel is, will reach him to repair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>And when the day its shining light displayed,<br />
He wholly searched the Moorish army through.<br />
In that the gentle warrior was arrayed<br />
In Arab weeds, he this might safely do;<br />
And of his purpose came alike in aid<br />
That other tongues beside the French he knew;<br />
And in the African so well was read,<br />
He seemed in Tripoly one born and bred:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>He sojourns there three days, the camp to see;<br />
Still seeking nought beside: next up and down,<br />
Within, without, both burgh and city he<br />
Spies; nor surveys the realm of France alone;<br />
But fair Auvergne, and even Gascony<br />
Revisits, to its farthest little town.<br />
Roves from Provence to Brittany’s domain,<br />
And from the Picards to the bounds of Spain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>Between October and November’s moon,<br />
In that dull season when the leafy vest<br />
Is stript from trembling plant, whose limbs are shown<br />
Of all their mantling foliage dispossess’d<br />
And in close flights the swarming birds are flown,<br />
Orlando enters on his amorous quest:<br />
This he pursues the livelong winter through,<br />
Nor quits when gladsome spring returns anew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>As (such his wont) from land to land he goes,<br />
A river’s side he reaches on a day;<br />
Which to the neighbouring sea in quiet flows,<br />
Bretons and Normans parting on its way:<br />
But, swoln with mountain rain and melted snows,<br />
Then thundered, white with foam and flashing-spray:<br />
And with impetuous stream had overtopt<br />
Its brim, and burst the bridge, and passage stopt.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>The paladin this bank and the other eyed,<br />
Along the river’s channel, to explore,<br />
Since neither fish nor fowl, if from his side<br />
He could gain footing on the adverse shore;<br />
When, with a damsel in the poop, he spied<br />
A ready pinnace that towards him bore:<br />
She steered, as if she would approach the strand;<br />
But would not let her shallop make the land.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>Steered not to land; as haply with suspicion<br />
To take a lading, in her own despite.<br />
To her the good Orlando made petition<br />
To put him o’er the stream; and she: “No knight<br />
Passes this ferry, but upon condition<br />
He shall his faith and promise duly plight,<br />
That he will do a battle, at my prayer,<br />
Upon the justest quarrel and most fair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“So that if thou on that other shore to land<br />
Dost by my aid, Sir cavalier, desire,<br />
Promise me, ere the month which is at hand”<br />
(The damsel so pursued her speech) “expire,<br />
That thou wilt join the Hibernian monarch’s hand,<br />
Who forms a fair armada, in his ire,<br />
To sack Ebuda’s isle; of all compress’d<br />
By ocean’s circling waves, the cruellest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“Know, beyond Ireland, in the briny flood,<br />
An island, amid many others, lies;<br />
Ebuda is its name; whose people rude<br />
(Such is their law), in search of plunder hies;<br />
And all the women that it takes, for food<br />
To a voracious animal supplies;<br />
Which every day to shore for this does speed,<br />
And finds new wife or maid whereon to feed:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>“For of these merchant still and Corsair sell<br />
A large supply, and most of those most fair.<br />
Reckoning one slain a-day, you thus may well<br />
Compute what wives and maids have perished there.<br />
But if compassion in your bosom dwell,<br />
Nor you to Love an utter rebel are,<br />
Be you contented with this band to wend,<br />
United for such profitable end.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>To hear the whole Orlando scarce could bear,<br />
Ere to be first in that emprize he swore,<br />
As one who evil deed misliked to hear,<br />
And with impatience like relation bore:<br />
Hence first induced to think, and next to fear,<br />
Angelica is captive on that shore:<br />
Since he so long the missing maid pursues,<br />
Nor of the damsel yet can gather news.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>Breaking his every scheme, this phantasy<br />
The troubled cavalier did so confound,<br />
That will all speed to that fell island he<br />
Resolved to navigate; nor yet the round<br />
Of a new sun was buried in the sea,<br />
Ere he a vessel at St. Malo’s found;<br />
In which, embarking on his quest, the count<br />
Put forth, and cleared that night St. Michael’s Mount.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Breac and Landriglier past on the left hand,<br />
Orlando’s vessel skims the Breton shore;<br />
Then shapes her course towards the chalky strand,<br />
Whence England’s isle the name of Albion bore:<br />
But the south wind, which had her canvas fanned,<br />
Shifts to north-west, and freshening, blows so sore,<br />
The mariners are fain to strike all sail,<br />
And wear and scud before the boisterous gale.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>A distance traversed in four days, in one<br />
Backwards the ceaseless wind the frigate bore;<br />
The helmsman kept the sea, lest she should run<br />
Aground, and break like glass upon the shore.<br />
The wind upon the fifth day changed its tune,<br />
So loud and furious through the other four;<br />
And let, without more strife, the vessel gain<br />
A port, where Antwerp’s river met the main.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>As soon as harboured there in shattered plight,<br />
The weary mariners their frigate moor,<br />
Out of a city, seated on the right<br />
Of that fair stream, descends upon the shore,<br />
As his gray hairs may warrant him, a wight<br />
Stricken in years; who, full of courteous lore,<br />
Turns to the county, after greetings due,<br />
Reputing him the leader of that crew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>And prays him, on a damsel’s part, ‘that he<br />
To her would think not irksome to repair;<br />
Whom of unequalled affability<br />
And sweetness, he would find, as well as fair;<br />
Or otherwise would be content, that she<br />
Should to his bark resort, to seek him there,<br />
Nor prove less pliant than had been before<br />
All the knights errant, who had sought that shore:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>‘For hitherto, by land or sea conveyed,<br />
No cavalier had journeyed to that place<br />
That had refused to parlay with the maid,<br />
And give her counsel in a cruel case.’<br />
Orlando, hearing this, no more delayed,<br />
But issued from the bark with hurried pace,<br />
And, in all kind and courteous usage bred,<br />
His way directed where the ancient led.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>With him did Roland to the city go,<br />
And at the bottom of a palace-stair,<br />
Conducted by that elder, full of woe<br />
A lady found, if face may grief declare,<br />
And sable cloth, with which (a mournful show)<br />
Chamber, and hall, and gallery, furnished were;<br />
Who, after honourable welcome paid,<br />
Seated the paladin, and sadly said:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>“The daughter of the Count of Holland,” (cried<br />
The lady) “know in me, Sir cavalier.<br />
Though not his only offspring (for beside<br />
Myself two brothers were) to him so dear,<br />
That, for whatever favour I applied,<br />
I never met refusal from the peer.<br />
I living glady in this happy sort,<br />
A duke by chance was guested at our court;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>“The Duke of Zealand, meaning for Biscay;<br />
With purpose there to war upon the Moor;<br />
His youth and beauty, then in manhood’s May,<br />
And force of love, unfelt by me before,<br />
Made me, with little strife, his easy prey:<br />
Persuaded by his outward cheer yet more,<br />
I thought, and think, and still shall think, the peer<br />
Loved me, and loves me yet with heart sincere.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>“Those days, whenas the wind was contrary,<br />
(Which fair for me, if foul for others blew)<br />
To others forty seemed, an hour to me;<br />
So upon speedy wings the moments flew.<br />
This while, we oftentimes held colloquy,<br />
When, to be given with solemn right and due,<br />
I promised him, and he to me, his hand,<br />
On his return, in wedlock’s holy band.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>“Bireno hardly from our court was gone,<br />
For such the name my faithful lover bore,<br />
When Friesland’s king, whose realm is from our own<br />
No further than this stream from Ocean’s shore,<br />
Designing to bestow me on his son,<br />
Arbantes hight (the monarch had no more),<br />
To Holland sent the worthiest of his land,<br />
Me of the count, my father, to demand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>“I without power to falsify that vow,<br />
Which to my gentle lover I had plight;<br />
Nor though I had the power, would Love allow<br />
Me so to play the ingrate, if I might,<br />
(The treaty, well on foot, to overthrow,<br />
And nigh concluded) with afflicted sprite,<br />
Cried <em>to my father, I would rather shed<br />
My very life-blood, than in Friesland wed</em>.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>“My gracious father, he who took but pleasure<br />
In what pleased me, nor would my will constrain;<br />
Marking my grief, broke off the intended measure,<br />
To give me comfort and relieve my pain.<br />
At this proud Friesland’s sovereign such displeasure<br />
Conceived, and entertained such high disdain,<br />
He entered Holland, and the war began,<br />
In which my kin were slaughtered to a man.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>“Besides, that both his puissance and his might<br />
Are such, as in our age are matched of few,<br />
Such is in evil deeds his cunning sleight,<br />
He laughs to scorn what wit and force can do.<br />
Strange arms he bears, unknown to any wight,<br />
Save him, of the ancient nations or the new:<br />
A hollow iron, two yards long, whose small<br />
Channel he loads with powder and a ball</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>“He, where ’tis closed behind, in the iron round,<br />
Touches with fire a vent, discerned with pain;<br />
In guise that skilful surgeon tries his ground,<br />
Where need requires that he should breathe a vein.<br />
Whence flies the bullet with such deafening sound,<br />
That bolt and lightening from the hollow cane<br />
Appear to dart, and like the passing thunder,<br />
Burn what they smite, beat-down or rend asunder.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>“Twice broken, he our armies overthrew<br />
With this device, my gentle brethren slain;<br />
The first the shot in our first battle slew,<br />
Reaching his heart, through broken plate and chain;<br />
The other in the other onset, who<br />
Was flying from the fatal field in vain.<br />
The ball his shoulder from a distance tore<br />
Behind, and issued from his breast before.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“My father next, defending on a day<br />
The only fortress which he still possessed,<br />
The others taken which about it lay,<br />
Was sent alike to his eternal rest:<br />
Who going and returning, to purvey<br />
What lacked, as this or that occasion pressed,<br />
Was aimed at from afar, in privy wise,<br />
And by the traytour struck between the eyes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>“And I remaining, sire and brethren dead,<br />
The isle of Holland’s only heir, the king<br />
Of Friesland, who by the desire was led<br />
Of better there his power establishing,<br />
To me, and also to my people said,<br />
I peace and quiet to my state might bring,<br />
Would I (when I before would not accord)<br />
Now take his son Arbantes for my lord.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>“I, not so much for deadly hate I bear<br />
To him and all his kindred, by whose spite<br />
My sire and both my brothers slaughtered were,<br />
My country sacked and waste, as that the knight<br />
I would not wrong, to whom I fealty sware,<br />
And had my solemn word already plight<br />
That me to wedlock man should woo in vain,<br />
Till he to Holland should return from Spain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“For one ill-born, a hundred yet behind,<br />
Will bear (replied) to hazard all content,<br />
—Slain, burnt alive, to let them to the wind<br />
Scatter my ashes, rather than consent.—<br />
My people seek to move my stedfast mind,<br />
By prayer and by protest, from this intent;<br />
And threat to yield my city up and me,<br />
Lest all be lost through my obduracy.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>“When in my fixt and firm resolve they read,<br />
That prayer and protest are alike in vain;<br />
My town and me, with Friesland’s king agreed,<br />
Surrendered, as they vowed, my vassal train.<br />
Not doing by me any shameful deed,<br />
Me he assured of life and of domain,<br />
So I would soften my obdurate mood,<br />
And be to wed with his Arbantes wooed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“I who would have consented to forego<br />
My life to scape from him, reflection made,<br />
That, save I first avenged myself, all woe<br />
Endured, would be by this regret outweighed.<br />
—Long time I muse, and to my misery know,<br />
’Tis only simulation which can aid.<br />
Not simple willingness, I feign desire,<br />
To win his grace, and have him for my sire.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“ ’Mid many in my father’s service, I<br />
Select two brothers fitted for my view,<br />
Of valiant heart and great ability<br />
But more approved for truth, as followers, who<br />
Bred in my father’s court, from infancy<br />
Had with myself grown up; the brothers two<br />
So wholly bound to me, they would have thought<br />
My safety with their lives was cheaply bought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“To them I tell my project, and the pair<br />
Of brethren promise me their faithful aid:<br />
To Flanders this, a pinnace to prepare,<br />
I sent, and that with me in Holland stayed.<br />
Now, while both foreigners and natives were,<br />
Of Friesland’s kingdom, to our nuptials prayed,<br />
Bireno in Biscay (the tidings went)<br />
For Holland had equipt an armament.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“Since on the issue of the earliest fray,<br />
When in the rout one hapless brother fell,<br />
I had dispatched a courier to Biscay,<br />
Who the sad news should to Bireno tell:<br />
While he toils sore his squadron to array,<br />
Proud Friesland’s arms our wretched remnant quell.<br />
Bireno, who knew nought of this, had weighed,<br />
And with his barks put forth to bring us aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“These tidings told to Friesland’s monarch, he<br />
Confiding to his son the wedding’s care,<br />
To meet Bireno’s squadron puts to sea,<br />
And (so chance willed) burns, sinks, or routs them there,<br />
Leading him off into captivity;—<br />
But none to us as yet the tidings bear.<br />
This while I to the amorous youth am wed,<br />
Who, when the sun sought <em>his</em>, would seek <em>my</em> bed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“Behind the curtains, I had hid the tried<br />
And faithful follower, of whom I said,<br />
Who moved not till the bridegroom he descried,<br />
Yet waited not till he in bed was laid:<br />
But raised a hatchet, and so well applied<br />
Behind the stripling’s head the ponderous blade,<br />
Of speech and life it reft him; I, who note<br />
The deed, leap lightly up and cut his throat.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“As falls the bullock upon shamble-sill,<br />
Thus fell the ill-starred stripling, in despite<br />
Of king Cymosco, worst among the ill;<br />
So was the impious king of Friesland hight<br />
Who did my brothers and my father kill,<br />
And, in my state to found a better right;<br />
In wedlock wished to join me with his son,<br />
Haply to slay me when his end was won.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“Ere new disturbance interrupt the deed,<br />
Taking what costliest was and lightest weighed,<br />
Me my companion by a chord, with speed,<br />
Drops from a window, where with boat purveyed<br />
In Flanders (as related) for my need,<br />
His brother, watchful of our motions, stayed:<br />
We dip the oar, we loose the sail, and driven<br />
By both, escape, as was the will of Heaven.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“The daring feat achieved, I cannot say<br />
If Friesland’s king more sorrowed for his son,<br />
Or raged at me: he there arrived, the day<br />
Ensuing, where the dreadful deed was done,<br />
Proud he returned, both he and his array,<br />
Of the duke taken, and the victory won:<br />
And thought to feast and nuptials he was bound,<br />
But in his home all grief and darkness found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“His pity for his son, the hate he fed<br />
Towards me, torment the father day and night;<br />
But as lamenting will not raise the dead,<br />
And vengeance is a vent for smothered spite;<br />
That portion of his thoughts, which should have led<br />
The king, to ease by sighs his troubled sprite,<br />
Now willingly takes counsel with his hate,<br />
To seize me, and his vengeance satiate.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“All known or said to by my friends, or who<br />
Were friends of those that, chosen from my train,<br />
Had aided me the deadly deed to do,<br />
Their goods and chattels burnt, were doomed or slain:<br />
And he had killed Bireno, since he knew<br />
No other trouble could inflict such pain;<br />
But that he, saving him in malice, thought<br />
He had a net wherewith I might be caught.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“Yet him a cruel proposition made,<br />
Granting a year his purpose to complete;<br />
Condemned to privy death, till then delayed,<br />
Save in that time, through force or through deceit,<br />
He by his friends’ and kindred’s utmost aid,<br />
Doing or plotting, me from my retreat<br />
Conveyed into his prisons; so that he<br />
Can only saved by my destruction be.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“What for his safety could be done, behold,<br />
Short of my own destruction, had been tried.<br />
Six towns I had in Flanders: these I sold,<br />
And (great or small the produce set aside)<br />
A part of it, to wily persons told,<br />
That it to tempt his guards might be applied;<br />
The rest of it dispensed to move and arm<br />
Germans or English, to the miscreant’s harm.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“My agents, whether they their trust betrayed,<br />
Or that they could in truth perform no more,<br />
Me with vain words instead of help have paid,<br />
And scorn me, having drained my scanty store:<br />
And now the term is nigh expired, when aid,<br />
Whether of open force or treasured ore,<br />
No longer will arrive in time to save<br />
My cherished spouse from torture and the grave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“Through him, from me was my dominion rent;<br />
Through him, my father and my brethren slain;<br />
Through him, the little treasure left me, spent<br />
(What served alone existence to sustain)<br />
To rescue him, in cruel durance pent;<br />
Nor other means to succour him remain;<br />
Save I, to liberate him from prison, go<br />
And yield myself to such a cruel foe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“If nothing more be left me then to try,<br />
Nor other way for his escape appear,<br />
Than <em>his</em> with this <em>my</em> wretched life to buy,<br />
This life I gladly will lay down: one fear<br />
Alone molests me; and it is that I<br />
Can never my conditions make so clear,<br />
As to assure me, that with new deceit,<br />
Me, when his prey, the tyrant will not cheat.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“I fear, when I shall be in captive plight,<br />
And he has put all tortures upon me,<br />
He may not loose Bireno, and the knight<br />
Have not to thank me for his liberty:<br />
Like perjured king, and full of foul despite,<br />
Who with my murder will not satiate be;<br />
But by Bireno neither less nor more<br />
Will do, than he had done by me before.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“The occasion now that I confer with you,<br />
And tell my case to all who seek the land,<br />
Both lords and knights, is with the single view,<br />
That taking counsel of so large a band,<br />
Some one may indicate assurance due,<br />
That when before the cruel king I stand,<br />
No longer he Bireno shall detain;<br />
Nor, after I am killed, the duke be slain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>“Warrior to wend with me, I in my need,<br />
When I shall be to Friesland given, have prayed;<br />
But so he promise, that the exchange agreed<br />
Shall be between us in such manner made,<br />
That from his bonds Bireno shall be freed<br />
When I am to the monarch’s hands conveyed:<br />
Thus I, when I am slain, shall die content,<br />
Who to my spouse shall life by death have lent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“Not to this day have chanced upon a wight<br />
Who on his faith will give me warranty,<br />
That if the king refuse to loose the knight,<br />
When I am offered, from captivity,<br />
He will not suffer that in my despite<br />
(So feared those weapons!) I shall taken be.<br />
So feared those weapons, upon every hand!<br />
Which, howsoever thick, no plates withstand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>“Now, if as strong Herculean port and bold<br />
Appear to vouch, such worth to you belong;<br />
And you believe to give me or withhold<br />
Is in your power, should he intend me wrong;<br />
Be with me, when committed to his hold,<br />
Since I shall fear not, in your convoy strong,<br />
When you are with me, that my lord, though I<br />
Be after slain, shall by his order die.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>Here her discourse, wherewith were interposed<br />
Loud sobs, the lady ceased, and silent stood:<br />
Orlando, when her lips the damsel closed,<br />
Whose ready will ne’er halts in doing good,<br />
Briefly to her replies, as indisposed<br />
To idle speeches of his natural mood:<br />
But plights his solemn word, that better aid<br />
She should from him receive than that she prayed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>’Tis not his scheme to place her in the hand<br />
Of her foul foe, to have Bireno freed;<br />
He will save both the lovers, if his brand<br />
And wonted valour fail him not at need.<br />
Embarked that very day, they put from land<br />
With a clear sky and prosperous wind to speed.<br />
The county hastes in his impatient heat,<br />
Eager to reach that isle, the monster’s seat.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>Through the still deeps, on this or the other side,<br />
The skipper veered his canvas to the wind:<br />
This isle, and that of Zealand, they descried,<br />
One seen before, and one shut in behind.<br />
The third day, from the harboured vessel’s side,<br />
In Holland, Roland disembarks, not joined<br />
By the complaining dame; whom to descend<br />
He will not till she hear that tyrant’s end.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>Armed at all points, the county passed ashore,<br />
Borne on a horse ’twixt brown and black, the breed<br />
Of Denmark, but in Flanders nurtured, more<br />
Esteemed for weight and puissance than for speed:<br />
For when the paladin embarked before,<br />
In Brittany he left the gallant steed,<br />
His Brigliador; so nimble and so fair,<br />
That but Bayardo could with him compare.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>Orlando fares to Dordrecht, where he views<br />
A numerous squadron, which the gate maintain;<br />
As well, because suspicion still ensues<br />
On the foundation of a new domain;<br />
As that before they had received the news,<br />
That out of Zealand, backed with armed train,<br />
Was coming with a fleet of many sail,<br />
A cousin of the lord here pent in jail.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>One, good Orlando to the monarch’s ear<br />
Bade bear a message, ‘that an errant knight<br />
Oh him would prove himself, with sword and spear;<br />
But would lay down this pact before the fight:—<br />
That if the king unhorsed the cavalier,<br />
Her who Arbantes slew, he, as his right,<br />
Should have, that, at the cavalier’s command,<br />
Was ready for delivery to his hand;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>‘And willed the king should on his side agree,<br />
If him the knight in combat overbore,<br />
Forthwith released from his captivity,<br />
Bireno to full freedom to restore.’<br />
To him the footman does his embassy;<br />
But he, who knightly worth or courteous lore<br />
Had never known, directs his whole intent<br />
The count by treacherous fraud to circumvent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>He hopes as well, if he the warrior slay,<br />
To have the dame, whom, so aggrieved, he hates,<br />
If in the knight’s disposal, and the say<br />
Of that strange knight, the footman well relates.<br />
Hence thirty men dispatched by other way<br />
Than to the portal led, where Roland waits;<br />
Who with a long and privy circuit wind,<br />
And come upon the paladin behind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>He all this while had made his guard delay<br />
The knight with words, till horse and foot he spied<br />
Arrived, where he this ambuscade did lay;<br />
When from the gate he with as many hied:<br />
As is the practised hunter’s wonted way,<br />
To circle wood and beasts on every side:<br />
As nigh Volana, with his sweeping nets,<br />
The wary fisher fish and pool besets.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>’Tis thus the king bars every path which lies<br />
Free for the warrior’s flight, with armed train:<br />
He him alive, and in no other guise,<br />
Would have, and lightly hopes his end to gain;<br />
Nor for the earthly thunderbolt applies,<br />
That had so many and so many slain:<br />
Which here he deems would serve his purpose ill,<br />
Where he desires to take and not to kill.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>As wary fowler, bent on greater prey,<br />
Wisely preserves alive the game first caught,<br />
That by the call-bird and his cheating play,<br />
More may within the circling net be brought;<br />
Such cunning art Cymosco would assay:<br />
But Roland would not be so lightly bought;<br />
Like them by the first toil that springs betrayed;<br />
And quickly forced the circle which was made.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>Where he perceives the assailants thickest stand,<br />
He rests his lance, and sticks in his career<br />
First one and afterwards another, and<br />
Another, and another, who appear<br />
Of paste; till six he of the circling band<br />
Of foes impales upon a single spear;<br />
A seventh left out, who by the push is slain,<br />
Since the clogged weapon can no more contain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>No otherwise, upon the further shore<br />
Of fosse or of canal, the frogs we spy,<br />
By cautious archer, practised in his lore,<br />
Smote and transfixed the one the other nigh;<br />
Upon the shaft, until it hold no more,<br />
From barb to feathers full, allowed to lie.<br />
The heavy lance Orlando from him flung,<br />
And to close combat with his faulchion sprung.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>The lance now broke, his sword the warrior drew,<br />
That sword which never yet was drawn in vain,<br />
And still with cut or thrust some soldier slew;<br />
Now horse, now footman of the tyrant’s train.<br />
And, ever where he dealt a stroke, changed blue,<br />
Yellow, green, white and black, to crimson stain.<br />
Cymosco grieves, when most his need require,<br />
Not to have now his hollow cane and fire;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>And with loud voice and menacing command<br />
Bids these be brought, but ill his followers hear;<br />
For those who have found safety of his band,<br />
To issue from the city are in fear.<br />
He, when he sees them fly on either hand,<br />
Would fly as well from that dread cavalier;<br />
Makes for the gate, and would the drawbridge lift,<br />
But the pursuing county is too swift.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>The monarch turns his back, and leaves the knight<br />
Lord of the drawbridge and of either gate.<br />
Thanks to his swifter steed, the rest in flight<br />
He passes: good Orlando will not wait<br />
(Intent the felon, not his band, to smite)<br />
Upon the vulgar herd to wreck his hate.<br />
But his slow horse seems restive; while the king’s,<br />
More nimble, flies as if equipt with wings.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>From street to street, before the count he made;<br />
And vanished clean; but after little stay,<br />
Came with new arms, with tube and fire purveyed;<br />
Which, at his hest, this while his men convey.<br />
And posted at a corner, he waylaid:<br />
His foe, as hunter watches for his prey,<br />
In forest, with armed dogs and spear, attending<br />
The boar in fury from the hill descending,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>Who rends the branch and overthrows the stone;<br />
And wheresoe’er he turns his haughty front,<br />
Appears (so loud the deafening crash and groan)<br />
As if he were uprending wood and mount.<br />
Intent to make him his bold deed atone,<br />
Cymosco at the pass expects the count;<br />
As soon as he appears, with ready light<br />
Touches the hole, and fires upon the knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Behind, the weapon flames in lightning’s guise,<br />
And vents the thunder from before; the ground<br />
Shakes under foot and city wall; the skies<br />
The fearful echo all about rebound.<br />
The burning bolt with sudden fury flies,<br />
Not sparing aught which in its course is found.<br />
Hissing and whizzing through the skies it went;<br />
But smote not, to the assassin’s foul intent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>Whether it was his great desire to kill<br />
That baron, or his hurry made him fail,<br />
Or trembling heart, like leaf which flutters still,<br />
Made hand and arm together flinch and quail;<br />
Or that it was not the Creator’s will<br />
The church so soon her champion should bewail;<br />
The glancing stroke his courser’s belly tore,<br />
Outstretched on earth, from thence to rise no more.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>To earth fall horse and rider: this the knight<br />
Scarce touched; the other thundering pressed the plain:<br />
For the first rose so ready and so light,<br />
He from the fall seemed breath and force to gain.<br />
As African Antëus, in the fight,<br />
Rose from the sand with prouder might and main;<br />
So when Orlando touched the ground, to view<br />
He rose with doubled force and vigour new.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>He who has seen the thunder, from on high,<br />
Discharged by Jove with such a horrid sound,<br />
Descend where nitre, coal, and sulphur lie,<br />
Stored up for use in magazine profound,<br />
Which scarce has reached—but touched it, ere the sky<br />
Is in a flame, as well as burning ground,<br />
Firm walls are split, and solid marbles riven,<br />
And flying stones cast up as high as heaven;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>Let him imagine, when from earth he sprung,<br />
Such was the semblance of the cavalier;<br />
Who moved in mode to frighten Mars among<br />
The Gods, so fierce and horrid was his cheer.<br />
At this dismay’d, the King of Friesland stung<br />
His horse, and turned his rein, to fly the peer:<br />
But fierce Orlando was upon his foe<br />
Faster than arrow flies from bended bow:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>And, what before he could not, when possest<br />
Of his good courser, now afoot will do.<br />
His speed outgoes all thought in every breast,<br />
Exceeds all credence, save in those who view.<br />
The tyrant shortly joined, he on the crest<br />
Smote at his head so well, he cleft it through;<br />
And to the neck divided by the blow,<br />
Sent it, to shake its last on earth below.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>Lo! in the frighted city other sound<br />
Was heard to rise, and other crash of brands,<br />
From troop, who, thither in his guidance bound,<br />
Followed Bireno’s cousin from his lands:<br />
Who, since the unguarded gates he open found,<br />
Into the city’s heart had poured his bands;<br />
Where the bold paladin had struck such fear,<br />
He without let might scour it far and near.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>In rout the people fly, who cannot guess<br />
Who these may be, or what the foes demand:<br />
But, when this man and that by speech and dress<br />
As Zealand-men distinguishes the band,<br />
<em>Carte blanche</em> they proffer, and the chief address,<br />
Bidding him range them under his command;<br />
Against the Frieslanders to lend him aid,<br />
Who have their duke in loathsome prison stayed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>To Friesland’s king that people hatred bore<br />
With all his following; who their ancient lord<br />
Had put to death, and who by them yet more,<br />
As evil and rapacious, was abhorred.<br />
Orlando interposed with kindly lore,<br />
As friend of both, the parties to accord:<br />
By whom, so joined, no Frieslander was left<br />
But was of life or liberty bereft.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>They would not wait to seek the dungeon-key,<br />
But breaking-down the gate, their entrance made:<br />
Bireno to the count with courtesy<br />
And grateful thanks the service done repaid.<br />
Thence they, together with large company,<br />
Went where Olympia in her vessel stayed:<br />
For so was the expecting lady hight,<br />
To whom that island’s crown belonged of right.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>She who had thither good Orlando brought,<br />
Not hoping that he would have thriven so well;<br />
—Enough for her, if by her misery bought,<br />
Her spouse were rescued from the tyrant’s cell!—<br />
Her, full of love and loyal homage, sought<br />
The people one and all: ’Twere long to tell<br />
How she caressed Bireno, he the maid,—<br />
What thanks both lovers to the county paid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>The people, throned in her paternal reign,<br />
Replace the injured dame, and fealty swear:<br />
She on the duke, to whom in solid chain<br />
Love with eternal knot had linked the fair,<br />
The empire of herself and her domain<br />
Conferred: He, called away by other care,<br />
Left in the cousin’s guardian care this while<br />
His fortresses, and all the subject isle.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>Since he to visit Zealand’s duchy planned,<br />
His faithful consort in his company;<br />
And thence, upon the king of Friesland’s land,<br />
Would try his fortune (as he said), for he<br />
A pledge, he rated highly, had in hand,<br />
Which seemed of fair success the warranty,<br />
The daughter of the king; who here forsaken,<br />
With many others had been prisoner taken.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>To a younger brother, her, the duke pretends,<br />
To be conjoined in wedlock, he conveyed.<br />
The Roman senator thence parting wends<br />
Upon the very day Bireno weighed;<br />
But he to nothing else his hand extends<br />
Of all the many, many prizes made,<br />
Save to that engine, found amid the plunder,<br />
Which in all points I said resembled thunder.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>Not with intent, in his defence to bear<br />
What he had taken, of the prize possest;<br />
For he still held it an ungenerous care<br />
To go with vantage on whatever quest:<br />
But with design to cast the weapon where<br />
It never more should living wight molest;<br />
And, what was appertaining to it, all<br />
Bore off as well, the powder and the ball.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XC.</td>
<td>And thus, when of the tidesway he was clear,<br />
And in the deepest sea his bark descried,<br />
So that no longer distant signs appear<br />
Of either shore on this or the other side,<br />
He seized the tube, and said: “That cavalier<br />
May never vail through thee his knightly pride,<br />
Nor base be rated with a better foe,<br />
Down with thee to the darkest deep below!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCI.</td>
<td>“O loathed, O cursed piece of enginery,<br />
Cast in Tartarean bottom, by the hand<br />
Of Beelzebub, whose foul malignity<br />
The ruin of this world through thee has planned!<br />
To hell, from whence thou came, I render thee.”<br />
So said, he cast away the weapon; fanned<br />
Meanwhile, with flowing sheet, his frigate goes,<br />
By wind, which for the cruel island blows.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCII.</td>
<td>Such was the paladin’s desire to explore<br />
If in the place his missing lady were;<br />
Whom he prefers the united world before,<br />
Nor can an hour of life without her bear.<br />
He fears, if he set foot on Ireland’s shore,<br />
Some other chance may interrupt him there:<br />
So that he after have in vain to say,<br />
“Why hasted I no faster on my way?”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIII.</td>
<td>Nor he in England nor in Ireland port<br />
Will make, nor on the coast that’s opposite.<br />
But let him go, the naked archer’s sport,<br />
Sore smitten in the heart!—ere I indite<br />
Yet more of him, to Holland I resort,<br />
And you to hear me company invite.<br />
For well I wot that you as well as me<br />
’Twould grieve <em>that</em> bridal should without us be.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIV.</td>
<td>Sumptuous and fair the bridal there is made;<br />
But neither yet so sumptuous nor so fair<br />
As it will be in Zealand, it is said:<br />
But ’tis not my design you should repair<br />
Thither; since by new accidents delayed<br />
The feast will be, of which be it my care,<br />
In other strain, the tidings to report;<br />
If you to hear that other strain resort.</td>
