<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>A hermit parts, by means of hollow sprite,<br />
The two redoubted rivals’ dangerous play;<br />
Rinaldo goes where Love and Hope invite,<br />
But is dispatched by Charles another way:<br />
Bradamont, seeking her devoted knight,<br />
The good Rogero, nigh becomes the prey<br />
Of Pinabel, who drops the damsel brave<br />
Into the dungeon of a living grave.</em></span></td>

<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>I<span class="small">NJURIOUS</span> L<span class="small">OVE</span>, why still to mar accord<br />
Between desires has been thy favourite feat?<br />
Why does it please thee so, perfidious lord,<br />
Two hearts should with a different measure beat?<br />
Thou wilt not let me take the certain ford,<br />
Dragging me where the stream is deep and fleet.<br />
Her I abandon who my love desires,<br />
While she who hates, respect and love inspires.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>Thou to Rinaldo show’st the damsel fair,<br />
While he seems hideous to that gentle dame;<br />
And he, who when the lady’s pride and care,<br />
Paid back with deepest hate her amorous flame,<br />
Now pines, himself, the victim of despair,<br />
Scorned in his turn, and his reward the same.<br />
By the changed damsel in such sort abhorred,<br />
She would choose death before that hated lord.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>He to the Pagan cries: “Forego thy theft,<br />
And down, false felon, from that pilfer’d steed;<br />
I am not wont to let my own be reft.<br />
And he who seeks it dearly pays the deed.<br />
More—I shall take from thee yon lovely weft;<br />
To leave thee such a prize were foul misdeed;<br />
And horse and maid, whose worth outstrips belief,<br />
Were ill, methinks, relinquished to a thief.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>“Thou liest,” the haughty Saracen retorts,<br />
As proud, and burning with as fierce a flame,<br />
“A thief thyself, if Fame the truth reports:<br />
But let good deeds decide our dubious claim,<br />
With whom the steed or damsel fair assorts:<br />
Best proved by valiant deeds: though, for the dame,<br />
That nothing is so precious, I with thee<br />
(Search the wide world throughout) may well agree.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>As two fierce dogs will sometimes stand at gaze,<br />
Whom hate or other springs of strife inspire,<br />
And grind their teeth, while each his foe surveys<br />
With sidelong glance and eyes more red than fire,<br />
Then either falls to bites, and hoarsely bays,<br />
While their stiff bristles stand on end with ire:<br />
So from reproach and menace to the sword<br />
Pass Sacripant and Clermont’s angry lord.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>Thus kindling into wrath the knights engage:<br />
One is on foot, the other on his horse:<br />
Small gain to this; for inexperienced page<br />
Would better rein his charger in the course.<br />
For such Bayardo’s sense, he will not wage<br />
War with his master, or put out his force.<br />
For voice, nor hand, nor manage, will he stir,<br />
Rebellious to the rein or goading spur.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>He, when the king would urge him, takes the rest,<br />
Or, when he curbs him, runs in giddy rings;<br />
And drops his head beneath his spreading chest,<br />
And plays his spine, and runs an-end and flings.<br />
And now the furious Saracen distressed,<br />
Sees ’tis no time to tame the beast, and springs,<br />
With one hand on the pummel, to the ground;<br />
Clear of the restless courser at a bound.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>As soon as Sacripant, with well-timed leap,<br />
Is from the fury of Bayardo freed,<br />
You may believe the battle does not sleep<br />
Between those champions, matched in heart and deed.<br />
Their sounding blades such changeful measure keep,<br />
The hammer-strokes of Vulcan with less speed<br />
Descend in that dim cavern, where he heats,<br />
And Jove’s red thunders on his anvil beats.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>Sometimes they lunge, then feign the thrust and parry:<br />
Deep masters of the desperate game they play;<br />
Or rise upon the furious stroke, and carry<br />
Their swords aloft, or stoop and stand at bay.<br />
Again they close, again exhausted tarry;<br />
Now hide, now show themselves, and now give way,<br />
And where one knight an inch of ground has granted,<br />
His foeman’s foot upon that inch is planted.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>When, lo! Rinaldo, now impatient grown,<br />
Strikes full at Sacripant with lifted blade;<br />
And he puts forth his buckler made of bone,<br />
And well with strong and stubborn steel inlaid:<br />
Though passing thick, Fusberta cleaves it: groan<br />
Greenwood, and covert close, and sunny glade.<br />
The paynim’s arm rings senseless with the blow,<br />
And steel and bone, like ice, in shivers go.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>When the fair damsel saw, with timid eye,<br />
Such ruin follow from the faulchion’s sway,<br />
She, like the criminal, whose doom is nigh,<br />
Changed her fair countenance through sore dismay,<br />
And deemed that little time was left to fly<br />
If she would not be that Rinaldo’s prey,<br />
Rinaldo loathed by her as much, as he<br />
Doats on the scornful damsel miserably.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>So turned her horse into the gloomy chase,<br />
And drove him through rough path and tangled ally,<br />
And oftentimes bent back her bloodless face,<br />
And saw Rinaldo from each thicket sally.<br />
Nor flying long had urged the frantic race,<br />
Before she met a hermit in a valley.<br />
Devotion in his aspect was expressed,<br />
And his long beard descended on his breast.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>Wasted he was as much by fasts as age,<br />
And on an ass was mounted, slow and sure;<br />
His visage warranted that never sage<br />
Had conscience more precise or passing pure.<br />
Though in his arteries time had stilled the rage<br />
Of blood, and spake him feeble and demure,<br />
At sight of the delighted damsel, he<br />
Was inly stirred for very charity.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>The lady prayed that kindly friar, that he<br />
Would straight conduct her to some haven near,<br />
For that she from the land of France might flee,<br />
And never more of loathed Rinaldo hear.<br />
The hermit, who was skilled in sorcery,<br />
Ceased not to soothe the gentle damsel’s fear.<br />
And with the promise of deliverance, shook<br />
His pocket, and drew forth a secret book.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>This opened, quick and mighty marvel wrought;<br />
For not a leaf is finished by the sage,<br />
Before a spirit, by his bidding brought,<br />
Waits his command in likeness of a page:<br />
He, by the magic writ constrained and taught,<br />
Hastes where the warriors face to face engage,<br />
In the cool shade—but not in cool disport—<br />
And steps between, and stops their battle short.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>“In courtesy,” he cried, “let either show<br />
What his foe’s death to either can avail,<br />
And what the guerdon conquest will bestow<br />
On him who in the battle shall prevail,<br />
If Roland, though he has not struck a blow,<br />
Or snapt in fight a single link of mail,<br />
To Paris-town conveys the damsel gay,<br />
Who has engaged you in this bitter fray.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>“Within an easy mile I saw the peer<br />
Pricking to Paris with that lady bright;<br />
Riding, in merry mood, with laugh and jeer,<br />
And mocking at your fierce and fruitless fight.<br />
Sure it were better, while they yet are near,<br />
To follow peer and damsel in their flight:<br />
For should he once in Paris place his prize<br />
The lady never more shall meet your eyes.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>You might have seen those angry cavaliers<br />
Change at the demon’s tale for rage and shame;<br />
And curse themselves as wanting eyes and ears,<br />
To let their rival cheat them of the dame.<br />
Towards his horse the good Rinaldo steers,<br />
Breathing forth piteous sighs which seem of flame;<br />
And, if he joins Orlando—ere they part—<br />
Swears in his fury he will have his heart.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>So, passing where the prompt Bayardo stood,<br />
Leaps on his back, and leaves, as swift as wind,<br />
Without farewell, his rival in the wood;<br />
Much less invites him to a seat behind.<br />
The goaded charger, in his heat of blood,<br />
Forces whate’er his eager course confined,<br />
Ditch, river, tangled thorn, or marble block;<br />
He swims the river, and he clears the rock.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>Let it not, sir, sound strangely in your ear<br />
Rinaldo took the steed thus readily,<br />
So long and vainly followed far and near;<br />
For he, endued with reasoning faculty,<br />
Had not in vice lured on the following peer,<br />
But fled before his cherished lord, that he<br />
Might guide him whither went the gentle dame,<br />
For whom, as he had heard, he nursed a flame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>For when Angelica, in random dread,<br />
From the pavilion winged her rapid flight,<br />
Bayardo marked the damsel as she fled,<br />
His saddle lightened of Mount Alban’s knight;<br />
Who then on foot an equal combat sped,<br />
Matched with a baron of no meaner might;<br />
And chased the maid by woods, and floods, and strands,<br />
In hopes to place her in the warrior’s hands.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>And, with desire to bring him to the maid,<br />
Gallopped before him still with rampant play;<br />
But would not let his master mount, afraid<br />
That he might make him take another way.<br />
So luring on Rinaldo through the shade,<br />
Twice brought him to his unexpected prey;<br />
Twice foiled in his endeavour: once by bold<br />
Ferrau; then Sacripant, as lately told.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>Now good Bayardo had believed the tiding<br />
Of that fair damsel, which produced the accord;<br />
And in the devil’s cunning tale confiding,<br />
Renewed his wonted service to his lord.<br />
Behold Rinaldo then in fury riding,<br />
And pushing still his courser Paris-ward!<br />
Though he fly fast, the champion’s wishes go<br />
Faster; and wind itself had seemed too slow.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>At night Rinaldo rests his steed, with pain<br />
To meet Anglante’s lord he burned so sore;<br />
And lent such credit to the tidings vain<br />
Of the false courier of that wizard hoar:<br />
And that day and the next, with flowing rein,<br />
Rode, till the royal city rose before<br />
His eyes; where Charlemagne had taken post,<br />
With the sad remnant of his broken host.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>He, for he fears the Afric king’s pursuit,<br />
And sap and siege, upon his vassals calls<br />
To gather in fresh victual, and recruit<br />
And cleanse their ditches, and repair their walls.<br />
And what may best annoy the foes, and suit<br />
For safety, without more delay forestalls;<br />
And plans an embassy to England, thence<br />
To gather fresher forces for defence.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>For he is bent again to try the fate<br />
Of arms in tented field, though lately shamed;<br />
And send Rinaldo to the neighbouring state<br />
Of Britain, which was after England named.<br />
Ill liked the Paladin to cross the strait;<br />
Not that the people or the land he blamed,<br />
But that King Charles was sudden; nor a day<br />
Would grant the valiant envoy for delay.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>Rinaldo never executed thing<br />
Less willingly, prevented in his quest<br />
Of that fair visage he was following,<br />
Whose charms his heart had ravished from his breast.<br />
Yet, in obediance to the christian king,<br />
Prepared himself to do the royal hest.<br />
To Calais the good envoy wends with speed,<br />
And the same day embarks himself and steed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>And there, in scorn of cautious pilot’s skill<br />
(Such his impatience to regain his home),<br />
Launched on the doubtful sea, which boded ill,<br />
And rolled its heavy billows, white with foam.<br />
The wind, enraged that he opposed his will,<br />
Stirred up the waves; and, ’mid the gathering gloom,<br />
So the loud storm and tempest’s fury grew,<br />
That topmast-high the flashing waters flew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>The watchful mariners, in wary sort,<br />
Haul down the mainsail, and attempt to wear;<br />
And would put back in panic to the port,<br />
Whence, in ill hour, they loosed with little care.<br />
—“Not so,” exclaims the wind, and stops them short,<br />
“So poor a penance will not pay the dare.”<br />
And when they fain would veer, with fiercer roar<br />
Pelts back their reeling prow and blusters more.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>Starboard and larboard bears the fitful gale,<br />
And never for a thought its ire assuages;<br />
While the strained vessel drives with humble sail<br />
Before the billows, as the tempest rages.<br />
But I, who still pursue a varying tale,<br />
Must leave awhile the Paladin, who wages<br />
A weary warfare with the wind and flood;<br />
To follow a fair virgin of his blood.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>I speak of that famed damsel, by whose spear<br />
O’erthrown, King Sacripant on earth was flung;<br />
The worthy sister of the valiant peer,<br />
From Beatrix and good Duke Aymon sprung.<br />
By daring deeds and puissance no less dear<br />
To Charlemagne and France: Since proved among<br />
The first, her prowess, tried by many a test,<br />
Equal to good Rinaldo’s shone confessed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>A cavalier was suitor to the dame,<br />
Who out of Afric passed with Agramant;<br />
Rogero was his valiant father’s name,<br />
His mother was the child of Agolant.<br />
And she, who not of bear or lion came,<br />
Disdained not on the Child her love to plant,<br />
Though cruel Fortune, ill their wishes meeting,<br />
Had granted to the pair a single greeting.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>Alone thenceforth she sought her lover (he<br />
Was named of him to whom he owed his birth),<br />
And roved as safe as if in company<br />
Of thousands, trusting in her single worth.<br />
She having made the king of Circassy<br />
Salute the visage of old mother earth,<br />
Traversed a wood, and that wood past, a mountain;<br />
And stopt at length beside a lovely fountain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>Through a delicious mead the fountain-rill,<br />
By ancient trees o’ershaded, glides away;<br />
And him whose ear its pleasing murmurs fill,<br />
Invites to drink, and on its banks to stay;<br />
On the left side a cultivated hill<br />
Excludes the fervors of the middle day.<br />
As first the damsel thither turns her eyes,<br />
A youthful cavalier she seated spies;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>A cavalier, who underneath the shade,<br />
Seems lost, as in a melancholy dream;<br />
And on the bank, which gaudy flowers displayed,<br />
Reposing, overhangs the crystal stream.<br />
His horse beneath a spreading beech is laid,<br />
And from a bough the shield and helmet gleam.<br />
While his moist eyes, and sad and downcast air,<br />
Speak him the broken victim of despair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>Urged by the passion lodged in every breast,<br />
A restless curiosity to know<br />
Of others’ cares, the gentle maid addressed<br />
The knight, and sought the occasion of his woe.<br />
And he to her his secret grief confessed,<br />
Won by her gentle speech and courteous show,<br />
And by that gallant bearing, which at sight,<br />
Prepared who saw her for nimble knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“Fair sir, a band of horse and foot,” he said,<br />
“I brought to Charlemagne; and thither pressed,<br />
Where he an ambush for Marsilius spread,<br />
Descending from the Pyrenean crest;<br />
And in my company a damsel led,<br />
Whose charms with fervid love had fired my breast.<br />
When, as we journey by Rhone’s current, I<br />
A rider on a winged courser spy.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“The robber, whether he were man or shade,<br />
Or goblin damned to everlasting woe,<br />
As soon as he beheld my dear-loved maid,<br />
Like falcon, who, descending, aims its blow,<br />
Sank in a thought and rose; and soaring, laid<br />
Hands on his prize, and snatched her from below.<br />
So quick the rape, that all appeared a dream,<br />
Until I heard in air the damsel’s scream.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“The ravening kite so swoops and plunders, when<br />
Hovering above the shelterd yard, she spies<br />
A helpless chicken near unwatchful hen,<br />
Who vainly dins the thief with after cries.<br />
I cannot reach the mountain-robber’s den,<br />
Compassed with cliffs, or follow one who flies.<br />
Besides, way-foundered is my weary steed,<br />
Who ’mid these rocks has wasted wind and speed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“But I, like one who from his bleeding side<br />
Would liefer far have seen his heart out-torn,<br />
Left my good squadrons masterless, to ride<br />
Along the cliffs, and passes least forlorn;<br />
And took the way (love served me for a guide)<br />
Where it appeared the ruthless thief had born,<br />
Ascending to his den, the lovely prey,<br />
What time he snatched my hope and peace away.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“Six days I rode, from morn to setting sun,<br />
By horrid cliff, by bottom dark and drear;<br />
And giddy precipice, where path was none,<br />
Nor sign, nor vestiges of man were near.<br />
At last a dark and barren vale I won,<br />
Where caverned mountains and rude cliffs appear;<br />
Where in the middle rose a rugged block,<br />
With a fair castle planted on the rock.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“From far it shone like flame, and seemed not dight<br />
Of marble or of brick; and in my eye<br />
More wonderful the work, more fair to sight<br />
The walls appeared, as I approached more nigh.<br />
I, after, learned that it was built by sprite<br />
Whom potent fumes had raised and sorcery:<br />
Who on this rock its towers of steel did fix,<br />
Case-hardened in the stream and fire of Styx.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“Each polished turret shines with such a ray<br />
That it defies the mouldering rust and rain:<br />
The robber scours the country night and day,<br />
And after harbours in this sure domain.<br />
Nothing is safe which he would bear away;<br />
Pursued with curses and with threats in vain.<br />
There (fruitless every hope to foil his art)<br />
The felon keeps my love, oh! say my heart.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“Alas! what more is left me but to eye<br />
Her prison on that cliff’s aërial crest?<br />
Like the she-fox, who hears her offspring cry,<br />
Standing beneath the ravening eagle’s nest;<br />
And since she has not wings to rise and fly,<br />
Runs round the rugged rock with hopeless quest.<br />
So inaccessible the wild dominion<br />
To whatsoever has not plume and pinion.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“While I so lingered where those rocks aspire,<br />
I saw a dwarf guide two of goodly strain;<br />
Whose coming added hope to my desire<br />
(Alas! desire and hope alike were vain)<br />
Both barons bold, and fearful in their ire:<br />
The one Gradasso, King of Sericane,<br />
The next, of youthful vigour, was a knight,<br />
Prized in the Moorish court, Rogero hight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“The dwarf exclaimed, ‘These champions will assay<br />
Their force with him who dwells on yonder steep,<br />
And by such strange and unattempted way<br />
Spurs the winged courser from his mountain-keep.’<br />
And I to the approaching warriors say,<br />
‘Pity, fair sirs, the cruel loss I weep,<br />
And, as I trust, yon daring spoiler slain,<br />
Give my lost lady to my arms again.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“Then how my love was ravished I make known,<br />
Vouching with bitter tears my deep distress.<br />
They proffer aid, and down the path of stone<br />
Which winds about the craggy mountain, press.<br />
While I, upon the summit left alone,<br />
Look on, and pray to God for their success.<br />
Beneath the wily wizard’s castle strong<br />
Extends a little plain, two bow-shots long.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“Arrived beneath the craggy keep, the two<br />
Contend which warrior shall begin the fight.<br />
When, whether the first lot Gradasso drew,<br />
Or young Rogero held the honor light,<br />
The King of Sericane his bugle blew,<br />
And the rock rang and fortress on the height;<br />
And, lo! apparelled for the fearful course,<br />
The cavalier upon his winged horse!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“Upwards, by little and by little, springs<br />
The winged courser, as the pilgrim crane<br />
Finds not at first his balance and his wings,<br />
Running and scarcely rising from the plain;<br />
But when the flock is launched and scattered, flings<br />
His pinions to the wind, and soars amain.<br />
So straight the necromancer’s upward flight,<br />
The eagle scarce attempts so bold a height.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“When it seems fit, he wheels his courser round,<br />
Who shuts his wings, and falling from the sky,<br />
Shoots like a well trained falcon to the ground,<br />
Who sees the quarry, duck or pigeon, fly:<br />
So, through the parting air, with whizzing sound,<br />
With rested lance, he darted from on high;<br />
And while Gradasso scarcely marks the foe<br />
He hears him swooping near, and feels the blow.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“The wizard on Gradasso breaks his spear,<br />
He wounds the empty air, with fury vain.<br />
This in the feathered monster breeds no fear;<br />
Who to a distance shifts, and swoops again.<br />
While that encounter made the Alfana rear,<br />
Thrown back upon her haunches, on the plain.<br />
The Alfana that the Indian monarch rode,<br />
The fairest was that ever man bestrode.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“Up to the starry sphere with swift ascent<br />
The wizard soars, then pounces from the sky,<br />
And strikes the young Rogero, who, intent<br />
Upon Gradasso, deems no danger nigh.<br />
Beneath the wizard’s blow the warrior bent,<br />
Which made some deal his generous courser ply;<br />
And when to smite the shifting foe he turned,<br />
Him in the sky, and out of reach discerned.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“His blows Rogero, now Gradasso, bruise<br />
On forehead, bosom, back, or flanks, between;<br />
While he the warrior’s empty blows eschews,<br />
Shifting so quickly that he scarce is seen.<br />
Now this, now that, the wizard seems to choose,<br />
The monster makes such spacious rings and clean,<br />
While the enchanter so deceives the knights,<br />
They view him not, and know not whence he smites.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>“Between the two on earth and him o’ the sky,<br />
Until that hour the warfare lasted there,<br />
Which, spreading wide its veil of dusky dye,<br />
Throughout the world, discolours all things fair.<br />
What I beheld, I say; I add not, I,<br />
A tittle to the tale; yet scarcely dare<br />
To tell to other what I stood and saw;<br />
So strange it seems, so passing Nature’s law.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“Well covered in a goodly silken case,<br />
He, the celestial warrior, bore his shield;<br />
But why delayed the mantle to displace<br />
I know not, and its lucid orb concealed.<br />
Since this no sooner blazes in his face,<br />
Than his foe tumbles dazzled on the field;<br />
And while he, like a lifeless body, lies,<br />
Becomes the necromancer’s helpless prize.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>“Like carbuncle, the magic buckler blazed,<br />
No glare was ever seen which shone so bright:<br />
Nor could the warriors choose but fall, amazed<br />
And blinded by the clear and dazzling light.<br />
I, too, that from a distant mountain gazed,<br />
Fell senseless; and when I regained my sight,<br />
After long time, saw neither knights nor page,<br />
Nor aught beside a dark and empty stage.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>“This while the fell enchanter, I supposed,<br />
Dragged both the warriors to his prison-cell;<br />
And by strange virtue of the shield disclosed,<br />
I from my hope and they from freedom fell:<br />
And thus I to the turrets, which enclosed<br />
My heart, departing, bade a last farewell.<br />
Now sum my griefs, and say if love combine<br />
Other distress or grief to match with mine.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>The knight relapsed into his first disease,<br />
After his melancholy tale was done.<br />
This was Count Pinabel, the Maganzese,<br />
Anselmo d’ Altaripa’s faithless son.<br />
He, where the blood ran foul through all degrees,<br />
Disdained to be the only virtuous one;<br />
Nor played a simple part among the base,<br />
Passing in vice the villains of his race.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>With aspect changing still, the beauteous dame<br />
Hears what the mournful Maganzese narrates;<br />
And, at first mention of Rogero’s name,<br />
Her radiant face with eager joy dilates.<br />
But, full of pity, kindles into flame<br />
As Pinabel his cruel durance states.<br />
Nor finds she, though twice told, the story stale;<br />
But makes him oft repeat and piece his tale.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>And, after, when she deemed that all was clear,<br />
Cried to the knight, “Repose upon my say.<br />
To thee may my arrival well be dear,<br />
And thou as fortunate account this day.<br />
Straight wend me to the keep, sir cavalier,<br />
Which holds a jewel of so rich a ray:<br />
Nor shalt thou grudge thy labour and thy care,<br />
If envious Fortune do but play me fair.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>The knight replied, “Then nought to me remains<br />
But that I yonder mountain-passes show;<br />
And sure ’tis little loss to lose my pains,<br />
Where every thing is lost I prize below.<br />
But you would climb yon cliffs, and for your gains<br />
Will find a prison-house, and be it so!<br />
Whate’er betide you, blame yourself alone;<br />
You go forewarned to meet a fate foreshown.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>So said, the cavalier remounts his horse,<br />
And serves the gallant damsel as a guide;<br />
Who is prepared Rogero’s gaol to force,<br />
Or to be slain, or in his prison stied.<br />
When lo! a messenger, in furious course,<br />
Called to the dame to stay, and rode and cried.<br />
This was the post who told Circassa’s lord<br />
What valiant hand had stretched him on the sward.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>The courier, who so plied his restless heel,<br />
News of Narbonne and of Montpelier bore:<br />
How both had raised the standard of Castile,<br />
All Acquamorta siding with the Moor;<br />
And how Marseilles’ disheartened men appeal<br />
To her, who should protect her straightened shore;<br />
And how, through him, her citizens demand<br />
Counsel and comfort at their captain’s hand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>This goodly town, with many miles of plain,<br />
Which lie ’twixt Var and Rhone, upon the sea,<br />
To her was given by royal Charlemagne:<br />
Such trust he placed in her fidelity.<br />
Still wont with wonder on the tented plain<br />
The prowess of that valiant maid to see.<br />
And now the panting courier, as I said,<br />
Rode from Marseilles to ask the lady’s aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>Whether or not she should the call obey,<br />
The youthful damsel doubts some little space;<br />
Strong in one balance Fame and Duty weigh,<br />
But softer thoughts both Fame and Duty chase:<br />
And she, at length, resolved the emprize to assay,<br />
And free Rogero from the enchanted place:<br />
Or, should her valour in the adventure fail,<br />
Would with the cherished lover share his jail.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>And did with such excuse that post appay,<br />
He was contented on her will to wait:<br />
Then turned the bridle to resume her way<br />
With Pinabel, who seemed no whit elate.<br />
Since of that line he knows the damsel gay,<br />
Held in such open and such secret hate;<br />
And future trouble to himself foresees,<br />
Were he detected as a Maganzese.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>For ’twixt Maganza’s and old Clermont’s line<br />
There was an ancient and a deadly feud:<br />
And oft to blows the rival houses came,<br />
And oft in civil blood their hands embrued.<br />
And hence some treason to this gentle dame<br />
In his foul heart, the wicked County brewed;<br />
Or, as the first occasion served, would stray<br />
Out of the road, and leave her by the way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>And so the traitor’s troubled fancy rack<br />
Fear, doubt, and his own native, rancorous mood,<br />
That unawares he issued from the track,<br />
And found himself within a gloomy wood:<br />
Where a rough mountain reared its shaggy back,<br />
Whose stony peak above the forest stood;<br />
The daughter of Dodona’s duke behind,<br />
Dogging his footsteps through the thicket blind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>He, when he saw himself within the brake,<br />
Thought to abandon his unweeting foe;<br />
And to the dame—“ ’Twere better that we make<br />
For shelter ere the gathering darkness grow;<br />
And, yonder mountain past, (save I mistake)<br />
A tower is seated in the vale below.<br />
Do you expect me then, while from the peak<br />
I measure the remembered place I seek.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>So said, he pushed his courser up the height<br />
Of that lone mountain; in his evil mind<br />
Revolving, as he went, some scheme or sleight<br />
To rid him of the gentle dame behind.<br />
When lo! a rocky cavern met his sight,<br />
Amid those precipices dark and blind:<br />
Its sides descended thirty yards and more,<br />
Worked smooth, and at the bottom was a door.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>A void was at the bottom, where a wide<br />
Portal conducted to an inner room:<br />
From thence a light shone out on every side,<br />
As of a torch illumining the gloom.<br />
Fair Bradamant pursued her faithless guide,<br />
Suspended there, and pondering on her doom:<br />
And came upon the felon where he stood,<br />
Fearing lest she might lose him in the wood.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>When her approach the County’s first intent<br />
Made vain, the wily traitor sought to mend<br />
His toils, and some new stratagem invent<br />
To rid her thence, or bring her to her end.<br />
And so to meet the approaching lady went,<br />
And showed the cave, and prayed her to ascend;<br />
And said that in its bottom he had seen<br />
A gentle damsel of bewitching mien.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>Who, by her lovely semblance and rich vest,<br />
Appeared a lady of no mean degree;<br />
But melancholy, weeping, and distressed,<br />
As one who pined there in captivity;<br />
And that when he towards the entrance pressed,<br />
To learn who that unhappy maid might be,<br />
One on the melancholy damsel flew,<br />
And her within that inner cavern drew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>The beauteous Bradamant, who was more bold<br />
Than wary, gave a ready ear; and, bent<br />
To help the maid, imprisoned in that hold,<br />
Sought but the means to try the deep descent.<br />
Then, looking round, descried an elm-tree old,<br />
Which furnished present means for her intent;<br />
And from the tree, with boughs and foliage stored,<br />
Lopt a long branch, and shaped it with her sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>The severed end she to the count commended,<br />
Then, grasping it, hung down that entrance steep.<br />
With her feet foremost, by her arms suspended:<br />
When asking if she had the skill to leap,<br />
The traitor, with a laugh, his hands extended.<br />
And plunged his helpless prey into the deep.<br />
“And thus,” exclaimed the ruffian, “might I speed<br />
With thee each sucker of thy cursed seed!”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>But not, as was the will of Pinabel,<br />
Such cruel lot fair Bradamant assayed;<br />
For striking on the bottom of the cell,<br />
The stout elm-bough so long her weight upstayed,<br />
That, though it split and splintered where it fell,<br />
It broked her fall, and saved the gentle maid.<br />
Some while astounded there the lady lay,<br />
As the ensuing canto will display.</td>
</tr>
