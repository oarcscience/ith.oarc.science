<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>After long search for good Rogero made,<br />
Him Leon finds, and yields to him his prize:<br />
Informed of all—already with that maid<br />
He wives; already in her bosom lies:<br />
When thither he that Sarza’s sceptre swayed<br />
To infect such bliss with impious venom hies,<br />
But falls in combat; and, blaspheming loud,<br />
To Acheron descends his spirit proud.</em></span></td>

<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>I, if my chart deceives me not, shall now<br />
In little time behold the neighbouring shore;<br />
So hope withal to pay my promised vow<br />
To one, so long my guide through that wide roar<br />
Of waters, where I feared, with troubled brow,<br />
To scathe my bark or wander evermore.<br />
But now, methinks—yea, now I see the land;<br />
I see the friendly port its arms expand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>A burst of joy, like thunder to my ear,<br />
Rumbles along the sea and rends the sky.<br />
I chiming bells, I shrilling trumpets hear,<br />
Confounded with the people’s cheerful cry;<br />
And now their forms, that swarm on either pier<br />
Of the thick-crowded harbour, I descry.<br />
All seem rejoiced my task is smoothly done,<br />
And I so long a course have safely run.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>What beauteous dames and sage, here welcome me!<br />
With them what cavaliers the shore adorn!<br />
What friends! to whom I owe eternity<br />
Of thanks for their delight at my return.<br />
Mamma, Ginevra, with the rest I see,<br />
Correggio’s seed, on the harbour’s furthest horn.<br />
Veronica de Gambara is here,<br />
To Phœbus and the Aonian choir so dear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>With Julia, a new Ginevra is in sight,<br />
Another offset from the selfsame tree;<br />
Hippolita Sforza, and Trivultia bright,<br />
Bred in the sacred cavern, I with thee<br />
Emilia Pia, and thee, Margherite,<br />
Angela Borgia, Graziosa, see,<br />
And fair Richarda d’Este, Lo! the twain,<br />
Blanche and Diana, with their sister train!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>Beauteous, but wiser and more chaste than fair,<br />
I Barbara Turca, linked with Laura, know:<br />
Nor beams the sun upon a better pair<br />
’Twixt Ind and where the Moorish waters flow.<br />
Behold Ginevra! that rich gem and rare<br />
Which gilds the house of Malatesta so,<br />
That never worthier or more honoured thing<br />
Adorned the dome of Keysar or of king.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>If she had dwelt in Rimini of yore,<br />
What time, from conquered Gaul returning home,<br />
Julius stood fearing on the river-shore,<br />
To ford the stream and make a foe of Rome,<br />
He every banner would have bowed before<br />
That dame, discharged his trophies, and such doom,<br />
Such pact would have received as liked her best;<br />
And haply ne’er had Freedom been opprest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>The consort of my lord of Bozzolo<br />
Behold! the mother, sisters, cousinhood;<br />
Them of Torello, Bentivoglio,<br />
Pallavigini’s and Visconti’s brood!<br />
Lo! she to whom all living dames forego<br />
The palm, and all of Grecian, Latin blood,<br />
Or barbarous, all that ever were, whose name<br />
For grace and beauty most is noised by Fame;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>Julia Gonzaga, she that wheresoe’er<br />
She moves, where’er she turns her lucid eyes,<br />
Not only is in charms without a peer,<br />
But seems a goddess lighted from the skies:<br />
With her is paired her brother’s wife, who ne’er<br />
Swerved from her plighted faith—aye good and wise—<br />
Because ill Fortune bore her long despite;<br />
Lo! Arragonian Anna, Vasto’s light!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>Anne gentle, courteous, and as sage as fair,<br />
Temple of Love and Truth and Chastity:<br />
With her, her sister dims all beauty, where<br />
Her radiance shines. Lo! one that hath set free<br />
Her conquering lord from Orcus’ dark repair,<br />
And him in spite of death and destiny<br />
(Beyond all modern instance) raised on high,<br />
To shine with endless glory in the sky.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>My ladies of Ferrara, those of gay<br />
Urbino’s court are here; and I descry<br />
Mantua’s dames, and all that fair array<br />
Which Lombardy and Tuscan town supply.<br />
The cavalier amid that band, whom they<br />
So honour, unless dazzled is mine eye<br />
By those fair faces, is the shining light<br />
Of his Arezzo, and Accolti hight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>Adorned with scarlet hat, and scarlet pall,<br />
His nephew Benedict, lo! there I see;<br />
With him Campeggio and Mantua’s cardinal;<br />
Glory and light of the consistory;<br />
And (if I dote not) mark how one and all<br />
In face and gesture show such mighty glee<br />
At my return, no easy task ’twould seem<br />
So vast an obligation to redeem.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>With them Lactantius is, Claude Ptolemy,<br />
Trissino, Pansa, and Capilupi mine,<br />
Latino Giovenal, it seems to me;<br />
Sasso, and Molza, and Florian hight Montine;<br />
With him, by whom through shorter pathway we<br />
Are led to the Ascraean font divine,<br />
Julio Camillo; and meseems that I<br />
Berna, and Sanga, and Flaminio spy.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>Lo! Alexander of Farnese, and O<br />
Learned company that follows in his train!<br />
Phaedro, Cappella, Maddalen’, Portio,<br />
Surnamed the Bolognese, the Volterrane.<br />
Blosio, Pierio, Vida, famed for flow<br />
Of lofty eloquence of exhaustless vein;<br />
Mussuro, Lascari, and Navagero,<br />
And Andrew Maro, and the monk Severo.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>Lo! two more Alexanders! of the tree<br />
Of the Orologi one, and one Guarino:<br />
Mario d’ Olvito, and of royalty<br />
That scourge, divine Pietro Aretino.<br />
I two Girolamos amid them see,<br />
Of Veritade and the Cittadino;<br />
See the Mainardo, the Leoniceno,<br />
Panizzato, Celio, and Teocreno.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>Bernardo Capel, Peter Bembo here<br />
I see, through whom our pure, sweet idiom rose,<br />
And who, of vulgar usage winnowed clear,<br />
Its genuine form in his example shows.<br />
Behold an Obyson, that in his rear<br />
Admires the pains which he so well bestows.<br />
I Fracastoro, Bevezzano note,<br />
And Tryphon Gabriel, Tasso more remote.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Upon me Nicholas Tiepoli<br />
And Nicholas Ammanio fix their eyes;<br />
With Anthony Fulgoso, who to spy<br />
My boat near land shows pleasure and surprise.<br />
There, from those dames apart, my Valery<br />
Stands with Barignan, haply to devise<br />
With him how, evermore by woman harmed,<br />
By her he shall not evermore be charmed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>Of high and superhuman genius, tied<br />
By love and blood, lo! Pico and Pio true;<br />
He that approaches at the kinsmen’s side,<br />
—So honoured by the best—I never knew;<br />
But, if by certain tokens signified,<br />
He is the man I so desire to view,<br />
That Sannazaro, who persuades the nine<br />
To leave their fountain for the foaming brine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>Diligent, faithful secretary, lo!<br />
The learned Pistophilus, mine Angiar here,<br />
And the Acciajuoli their joint pleasure show<br />
That for my bark there is no further fear.<br />
There I my kinsman Malaguzzo know;<br />
And mighty hope from Adoardo hear,<br />
That these my nest-notes shall by friendly wind<br />
Be blown from Calpe’s rock to furthest Ind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>Joys Victor Fausto; Tancred joys to view<br />
My sail; and with them joy a hundred more.<br />
Women and men I see, a mingled crew,<br />
At my return rejoicing, crowd the shore.<br />
Then, since the wind blows fair, nor much to do<br />
Remains, let me my course delay no more;<br />
And turning to Melissa, in what way<br />
She rescued good Rogero let me say.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>Much bent was this Melissa (as I know<br />
I many times have said to you whilere)<br />
That Bradamant in wedlock should bestow<br />
Her hand upon the youthful cavalier;<br />
And so at heart had either’s weal and woe,<br />
That she from hour to hour of them would hear:<br />
Hence ever on that quest she spirits sent,<br />
One still returning as the other went.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>A prey to deep and stubborn grief, reclined<br />
Mid gloomy shades Rogero they descried;<br />
Firm not to swallow food of any kind,<br />
Nor from that purpose to be turned aside;<br />
And so to die of hunger he designed:<br />
But weird Melissa speedy aid supplied;<br />
Who took a road, from home forth issuing, where<br />
She met the Grecian emperor’s youthful heir;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>Leo that, one by one, dispatched his train<br />
Of followers, far and wide, through every bourn,<br />
And afterwards, in person went in vain,<br />
To find the warrior of the unicorn.<br />
The wise enchantress, that will sell and rein,<br />
Had on that day equipt a demon, borne<br />
By him, in likeness of a hackney horse,<br />
Constantine’s son encountered in her course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>“If such as your ingenuous mien” (she cried<br />
To Leo) “is your soul’s nobility,<br />
And corresponding with your fair outside<br />
Your inward goodness and your courtesy,<br />
Some help, some comfort, sir, for one provide<br />
In whom the best of living knights we see;<br />
Who, save ye help and comfort quickly lend,<br />
Is little distant from his latter end.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>“The best of knights will die of all, who don,<br />
Or e’er donned sword and buckler, the most fair<br />
And gentle of all warriors that are gone,<br />
Or who throughout the world yet living are,<br />
And simply for a courteous deed, if none<br />
Shall comfort to the youthful sufferer bear.<br />
Then come, sir, for the love of Heaven, and try<br />
If any counsel succour may supply.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>It suddenly came into Leo’s mind<br />
The knight of whom she parlayed was that same,<br />
Whom throughout all the land he sought to find,<br />
And seeking whom, he now in person came.<br />
So that obeying her that would persuade<br />
Such pious work, he spurred behind the dame;<br />
Who thither led (nor tedious was the way)<br />
Where nigh reduced to death the stripling lay.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>They found Rogero fasting from all food<br />
For three long days, so broken down; with pain<br />
The knight could but upon his feet have stood,<br />
To fall, albeit unpushed, to ground again.<br />
With helm on head, and with his faulchion good<br />
Begirt, he lay reclined in plate and chain.<br />
A pillow of his buckler had he made,<br />
Where the white unicorn was seen pourtraid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>There thinking what an injury he had done<br />
To his lady love—how ingrate, how untrue<br />
To her had been—not simple grief alone<br />
O’erwhelmed him, to such height his fury grew,<br />
He bit his hands and lips; while pouring down<br />
His cheeks, the tears unceasing ran, and through<br />
The passion that so wrapt his troubled sprite,<br />
Nor Leo nor Melissa heard the knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>Nor therefore interrupts he his lament,<br />
Nor checks his sighs, nor checks his trickling tears.<br />
Young Leo halts, to hear his speech intent;<br />
Lights from his courser, and towards him steers:<br />
He knows that of the sorrows which torment<br />
Love is the cause; but yet from nought appears<br />
Who is the person that such grief hath bred;<br />
For by Rogero this remains unsaid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>Approaching nearer and yet nearer, now<br />
He fronts the weeping warrior, face to face,<br />
Greets with a brother’s love, and stooping low,<br />
His neck encircles with a fast embrace.<br />
By the lamenting Child I know not how<br />
Is liked his sudden presence in that place;<br />
Who fears annoy or trouble at his hand;<br />
And lest he should his wish for death withstand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>Him with the sweetest words young Leo plied,<br />
And with the warmest love that he could show,<br />
“Let it not irk thee,” to the Child he cried,<br />
“To tell the cause from whence thy sorrows flow;<br />
For few such desperate evils man betide,<br />
But that there is deliverance from his woe,<br />
So that the cause be known; nor he bereft<br />
Of hope should ever be, so life be left.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“Much grieve I thou wouldst hide thyself from me,<br />
That known me for thy faithful friend and true;<br />
Not only now I am so bound to thee,<br />
That I the knot can never more undo;<br />
But even from the beginning, when to be<br />
Thy deadly foeman I had reason due.<br />
Hope then that I will succour thee with pelf,<br />
With friends, with following, and with life itself.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>“Nor shun to me thy sorrow to explain,<br />
And I beseech thee leave to me to try<br />
If wealth avail to free thee from thy pain,<br />
Art, cunning, open force, or flattery,<br />
If my assistance is employed in vain,<br />
The last relief remains to thee to die:<br />
But be content awhile this deed to shun<br />
Till all that thou canst do shall first be done.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>He said; and with such forceful prayer appealed;<br />
So gently and benignly soothed his moan;<br />
That good Rogero could not choose but yield,<br />
Whose heart was not of iron or of stone;<br />
Who deemed, unless he now his lips unsealed,<br />
He should a foul discourteous deed have done.<br />
He fain would have replied, but made assay<br />
Yet twice or thrice, ere words could find their way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“My lord, when known for what I am (and me<br />
Now shalt thou know),” he made at last reply,<br />
“I wot thou, like myself, content wilt be,<br />
And haply more content, that I should die.<br />
Know me for him so hated once by thee;<br />
Rogero who repaid that hate am I;<br />
And now ’tis many days since with intent<br />
Of putting thee to death from court I went.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>“Because I would not see my promised bride<br />
Borne off by thee; in that Duke Aymon’s love<br />
And favour was engaged upon thy side.<br />
But, for man purposes, and God above<br />
Disposes, thy great courtesy, well tried<br />
In a sore need, my fixt resolve did move.<br />
Nor only I renounced the hate I bore,<br />
But purposed to be thine for evermore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“What time I as Rogero was unknown,<br />
Thou madest suit I would obtain for thee<br />
The Lady Bradamant; which was all one<br />
As to demand my heart and soul from me.<br />
Whether thy wish I rather than mine own<br />
Sought to content, thou hast been made to see.<br />
Thine is the lady; her in peace possess;<br />
Far more than mine I prize thy happiness.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“Content thee, that deprived of her, as well<br />
I should myself of worthless life deprive;<br />
For better I without a soul could dwell<br />
Than without Bradamant remain alive.<br />
And never while these veins with life-blood swell<br />
Canst thou with her legitimately wive:<br />
For vows erewhile have been between us said;<br />
Nor she at once can with two husbands wed.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>So filled is gentle Leo with amaze<br />
When he the stranger for Rogero knows,<br />
With lips and brow unmoved, with stedfast gaze<br />
And rooted feet, he like a statue shows;<br />
Like statue more than man, which votaries raise<br />
In churches, for acquittance of their vows.<br />
He deems that courtesy of so high a strain<br />
Was never done nor will be done again;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>And that he him doth for Rogero know<br />
Not only that goodwill he bore whilere<br />
Abates not, but augments his kindness so,<br />
That no less grieves the Grecian cavalier<br />
Than good Rogero for Rogero’s woe.<br />
For this, as well as that he will appear<br />
Deservedly an emperor’s son—although<br />
In other things outdone—he will not be<br />
Defeated in the race of courtesy;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>And says, “That day my host was overthrown,<br />
Rogero, by thy wond’rous valour, though<br />
I had thee at despite, if I had known<br />
Thou was Rogero, as I know it now,<br />
So me thy virtue would have made thine own,<br />
As then it made me, knowing not my foe;<br />
So hatred from my bosom would have chased,<br />
And with my present love have straight replaced.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“That I Rogero hated, ere I knew<br />
Thou was Rogero, will I not deny.<br />
But think not that I further would pursue<br />
The hatred that I bore thee; and had I,<br />
When thee I from thy darksome dungeon drew,<br />
Descried the truth, as this I now descry,<br />
Such treatment shouldst thou then have had, as thou<br />
Shalt have from me, to thine advantage, now;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“And if I willingly had done so then,<br />
When not, as I am now, obliged to thee;<br />
How much more gladly should I now; and when,<br />
Not doing so, I should with reason be<br />
Deemed most ungrateful amid ingrate men;<br />
Since thou foregoest thine every good for me!<br />
But I to thee restore thy gift, and, more<br />
Glady than I received it, this restore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“The damsel more to thee than me is due;<br />
And though for her deserts I hold her dear,<br />
If that fair prize some happier mortal drew,<br />
I think not I my vital thread should shear:<br />
Nor would I by thy death be free to woo:<br />
That from the hallowed bands of wedlock clear<br />
Wherein the lady hath to thee been tied,<br />
I might possess her as my lawful bride.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“Not only Bradamant would I forego,<br />
But whatsoe’er I in the world possess;<br />
And rather forfeit life than ever know<br />
That grief, through me, should such a knight oppress.<br />
To me is thy distrust great cause of woe,<br />
That since thou couldst dispose of me no less<br />
Than of thyself, thou—rather than apply<br />
To me for succour—wouldst of sorrow die.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>These words he spake, and more to that intent,<br />
Too tedious in these verses to recite;<br />
Refuting evermore such argument<br />
As might be used in answer by the knight:<br />
Who said, at last, “I yield, and am content<br />
To live; but how can I ever requite<br />
The obligation, which by me is owed<br />
To thee that twice hast life on me bestowed?”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>Melissa generous wine and goodly cheer<br />
Thither bade carry, in a thought obeyed;<br />
And comforted the mourning cavalier,<br />
Who would have sunk without her friendly aid.<br />
Meanwhile the sound of steeds Frontino’s ear<br />
Had reached, and thither had he quickly made:<br />
Him Leo’s squires at his commandment caught,<br />
And saddled, and to good Rogero brought;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>Who, though by Leo helped, with much ado<br />
And labour sore the gentle courser scaled.<br />
So wasted was the vigour which some few<br />
Short days before, in fighting field, availed<br />
To overthrow a banded host, and do<br />
The deeds he did, in cheating armour mailed.<br />
Departing thence, ere they had measured more<br />
Than half a league, they reached an abbey hoar:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>Wherein what of that day was yet unworn<br />
They past, the morrow, and succeeding day;<br />
Until the warrior of the unicorn<br />
His vigour had recruited by the stay.<br />
He, Leo, and Melissa then return<br />
To Charles’s royal residence; where lay<br />
An embassy, arrived the eve before,<br />
Which from the Bulgars’ land a message bore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>Since they that had for king proclaimed the knight<br />
Besought Rogero thither to repair<br />
Through these their envoys deeming they would light<br />
On him in Charles’s court, where they should swear<br />
Fidelity, and yield to him his right;<br />
And he from them the crown receive and wear.<br />
Rogero’s squire who served this band to steer<br />
Has published tidings of the cavalier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>He of the fight has told which at Belgrade<br />
Erewhile Rogero for the Bulgars won;<br />
How Leo and his sire were overlaid,<br />
And all their army slaughtered and undone;<br />
Wherefore the Bulgars him their king had made;<br />
Their royal line excluding from the throne:<br />
Then how Ungiardo took the warrior brave,<br />
And him to cruel Theodora gave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>He speaks with that of certain news, which say<br />
How good Rogero’s jailer was found dead,<br />
The prison broke and prisoner away:<br />
Of what became of him was nothing said.<br />
—Towards the city by a secret way<br />
(Nor was his visage seen) Rogero sped.<br />
He, on the following morning, and his friend,<br />
Leo, to Charles’s court together wend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>To Charles’ court he wends; the bird he bore<br />
Of gold with its two heads—of crimson hue<br />
Its field—and that same vest and ensigns wore,<br />
As was erewhile devised between the two;<br />
And such as in the listed fight before<br />
His bruised and battered armour was in shew.<br />
So that they quickly knew the cavalier<br />
From him that strove with Bradamant whilere.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>In royal ornaments and costly gown,<br />
Unarmed, beside him doth young Leo fare.<br />
A worthy following and of high renown<br />
Before, behind him, and about him are.<br />
He bowed to Charlemagne, who from his throne<br />
Had risen to do honour to the pair:<br />
Then holding still Rogero by the hand,<br />
So spake, while all that warrior closely scanned.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>“Behold the champion good, that did maintain<br />
From dawn till fall of day the furious fight;<br />
And since by Bradamant nor taken, slain,<br />
Nor forced beyond the barriers was the knight,<br />
He is assured his victory is plain,<br />
Dread sir, if he your edict reads aright;<br />
And he hath won the lady for his wife:<br />
So comes to claim the guerdon of the strife.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“Besides that by your edict’s tenor none<br />
But him can to the damsel lift his eyes,<br />
—Is she deserved by deeds of valour done,<br />
What other is so worthy of the prize?<br />
—Should she by him that loves her best be won,<br />
None passes him, nor with the warrior vies;<br />
And he is here to fight against all foes<br />
That would in arms his right in her oppose.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>King Charlemagne and all his peerage stand<br />
Amazed, who well believed the Grecian peer<br />
With Bradamant had striven with lifted brand<br />
In fight, and not that unknown cavalier.<br />
Marphisa, thither borne amid the band,<br />
That crowded round the royal chair to hear,<br />
Hardly till Leo made an ending staid;<br />
Then prest before the listening troop, and said:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>“Since here Rogero is not, to contest<br />
The bride’s possession with the stranger knight,<br />
Lest he, as undefended, be opprest,<br />
And forfeit so without dispute his right,<br />
On his behalf I undertake this quest,<br />
—His sister I—against whatever wight<br />
Shall here assert a claim to Bradamant,<br />
Or more desert than good Rogero vaunt.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>She spake this with such anger and disdain,<br />
Many surmised amid the assistant crew,<br />
That, without waiting leave from Charlemagne,<br />
What she had threatened she forthwith would do.<br />
No longer Leo deemed it time to feign;<br />
And from Rogero’s head the helm withdrew;<br />
And to Marphisa, “For himself to speak,<br />
Behold him here and ready!” cried the Greek.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>As looked old Aegeus at the accursed board,<br />
Seeing it was his son to whom—so willed<br />
His wicked consort—that Athenian lord<br />
Had given the juice from deadly drugs distilled;<br />
Whom he, if he had recognized his sword<br />
Though but a little later, would have killed;<br />
So looked Marphisa when, disclosed to view,<br />
She in the stranger knight Rogero knew;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>And ran forthwith to clip the cavalier;<br />
Nor could unclasp her arms: with loving show<br />
Charlemagne, Roland, and Rinaldo, here<br />
And there, fix friendly kisses on his brow.<br />
Nor him Sir Dudon, nor Sir Olivier,<br />
Nor King Sobrino can caress enow:<br />
Nor paladin nor peer, amid the crew,<br />
Wearies of welcoming that warrior true.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>Leo, who well can play the spokesman, now<br />
That warlike band hath ceased to clip the knight,<br />
Tells before Charles and all that audience, how<br />
Rogero’s daring, how Rogero’s might,<br />
—Albeit to his good squadron’s scathe and woe—<br />
Which at Belgrade he witnessed in that fight,<br />
So moved him that they overweighed all harms<br />
Inflicted on him by the warrior’s arms.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>So that to her Rogero being brought,<br />
Who would all havoc of the youth have made,<br />
He setting all his family at nought,<br />
Had out of durance vile the knight conveyed;<br />
And how Rogero, that the rescue wrought<br />
By Leo might be worthily repaid,<br />
Did that high courtesy; which can by none,<br />
That ever were or e’er will be, outdone;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>And he from point to point continuing, said<br />
That which Rogero had for him achieved;<br />
And after, how by sorrow sore bested,<br />
In that to leave his cherished wife he grieved,<br />
He had resolved to die, and, almost dead,<br />
Was only by his timely aid relieved;<br />
And this he told so movingly, no eye<br />
Remained, amid those martial many, dry.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>So efficaciously he after prayed<br />
To the obstinate Duke Aymon, not alone<br />
The stubborn sire of Bradamant he swayed,<br />
And to forego his settled purpose won;<br />
But that proud lord in person did persuade<br />
To beg Rogero’s pardon, and his son<br />
And son-in-law to be beseech the knight;<br />
And thus to him his Bradamant was plight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>To her, where, of her feeble life in doubt,<br />
She in a secret chamber made lament,<br />
Through many a messenger, with joyful shout<br />
And mickle haste, the happy tidings went.<br />
Hence the warm blood, that stagnated about<br />
Her heart, by her first sorrow thither sent,<br />
Ebbed at this notice in so full a tide,<br />
Well nigh for sudden joy the damsel died.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>Of all her vigour is she so foregone,<br />
She cannot on her feeble feet rely:<br />
Yet what her force must needs to you be known,<br />
And what the damsel’s magnanimity.<br />
None doomed to prison, wheel or halter, none<br />
Condemned some other evil death to die,<br />
About whose brows the sable band is tied,<br />
Rejoices more to hear his pardon cried.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>Joys Clermont’s, joys Mongrana’s noble house,<br />
Those kindred branches that fresh know to view.<br />
With equal grief Count Anselm overflows,<br />
Gan, Falcon, Gini and Ginami’s crew:<br />
Yet they meanwhile beneath contented brows<br />
Conceal the dark and envious thoughts they brew.<br />
As the fox waits the motions of the hare,<br />
They wait their time for vengeance, and forbear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>Besides that oftentimes before the rage<br />
Of Roland and Rinaldo on them fell,<br />
Though they were calmed by Charles’s counsel sage,<br />
And common danger from the infidel,<br />
They had new cause for grief in Bertolage<br />
Slain by their foemen and Sir Pinnabel:<br />
But they concealed their hatred, and endured<br />
Those griefs, as of the matter ill assured.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>Those envoys of the Bulgars that had made<br />
For Charles’s court (as hath erewhile been shown),<br />
Hoping to find the knight, whose shield pourtrayed<br />
The unicorn, elected to their throne,<br />
Bless the good fortune which their hope repayed,<br />
Seeing that valiant warrior, and fall down<br />
Before his feet, and him in humble speech<br />
Again to seek their Bulgary beseech;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>Where kept for him in Adrianople are<br />
The sceptre and the crown, his royal due:<br />
But let him succour to his kingdom bear;<br />
For—to their further scathe—advices shew<br />
Constantine doth a mighty host prepare,<br />
And thitherward in person moves anew;<br />
And they—of their elected king possest—<br />
Hope the Greek empire from his hands to wrest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>He accepts the realm, by their entreaties won;<br />
And, to afford them aid against their foes,<br />
Will went to Bulgary when three months are done;<br />
Save Fortune otherwise of him dispose.<br />
When this is heard by that Greek emperor’s son,<br />
He bids Rogero on his faith repose;<br />
For since by him the Bulgar’s realm is swayed,<br />
Peace between them and Constantine is made;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>Nor needeth he depart in haste, to guide<br />
His Bulgar bands against the Grecian foe;<br />
For all that he had conquered far and wide,<br />
He will persuade his father to forego.<br />
None of the virtues, in Rogero spied,<br />
Moved Bradamant’s ambitious mother so,<br />
Or so to endear her son-in-law availed,<br />
As hearing now that son a sovereign hailed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>The rich and royal nuptials they prepare<br />
As well befits him, by whose care ’tis done,<br />
’Tis done by Charles; and with such cost and care<br />
As if ’twere for a daughter of his own.<br />
For such the merits of the damsel are,<br />
And such had all her martial kindred shown,<br />
Charles would not think he should exceed due measure<br />
If spent for her was half his kingdom’s treasure.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>He a free court bids cry; whither his way<br />
Securely every one that wills may wend;<br />
And offers open lists till the ninth day<br />
To whosoever would in arms contend;<br />
And bids build bowers afield, and interlay<br />
Green boughs therein, and flowers and foliage blend;<br />
And make those bowers so gay with silk and gold,<br />
No fairer place this ample world doth hold.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Guested within fair Paris cannot be<br />
The countless foreign bands that thither fare;<br />
Who, rich and poor, of high and low degree,<br />
And Greeks and Latins and Barbarians are.<br />
There is no end of lord and embassy<br />
That thither from all ends of earth repair;<br />
All lodged conveniently, to their content,<br />
Beneath pavilion, booth, and bower and tent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>The weird Melissa against the coming night<br />
With singular and matchless ornament<br />
Had for that pair the nuptial chamber dight;<br />
Whereon long time before she had been bent:<br />
Long time before desirous of the rite<br />
Had been that dame, presageful of the event;<br />
Presageful of futurity, she knew<br />
What goodly fruit should from their stems ensue.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>She had prepared the genial, fruitful bed,<br />
Under a broad pavilion; one more rich,<br />
Adorned, and jocund, never overhead<br />
(Did this for peace or war its master pitch)<br />
Was in the world, before or after, spread;<br />
And this from Thracian strand had borne the witch.<br />
The costly prize from Constantine she bore,<br />
Who for disport was tented on that shore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>She with young Leo’s leave, or rather so<br />
The Grecian’s admiration to obtain,<br />
And a rare token of that art to show,<br />
Which on Hell’s mighty dragon puts the rein,<br />
And at her pleasure rules that impious foe<br />
Of Heaven, together with his evil train,<br />
Bade demons the pavilion through mid air<br />
To Paris from Constantinople bear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>From Constantine that lay therein, who swayed<br />
The Grecian empire’s sceptre, at mid-day<br />
This with its cordage, shaft whereby ’twas stayed,<br />
And all within and out, she bore away;<br />
And of the costly tent, through air conveyed,<br />
For young Rogero made a lodging gay.<br />
The bridal ended, this her demon crew<br />
Thither, from whence ’twas brought, conveyed anew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>Two thousand tedious years were nigh complete,<br />
Since this fair work was fashioned by the lore<br />
Of Trojan maid, warmed with prophetic heat;<br />
Who, ’mid long labour and ’mid vigil sore,<br />
With her own fingers all the storied sheet<br />
Of the pavilion had embroidered o’er;<br />
Cassandra hight; that maid to Hector brave<br />
(Her brother he) this costly present gave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>The curtiest cavalier, the kindliest shoot<br />
That ever from her brother’s stock should grow<br />
(Albeit she knew far distant from its root,<br />
With many a branch between, should be that bough)<br />
In silk and gold upon the gorgeous suit<br />
Of hangings had she wrought in goodly show.<br />
Much prized that gift, while living, Priam’s son,<br />
For its rare work and her by whom ’twas done.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>But when by treachery perished Priam’s heir,<br />
And Greeks the Trojans scathed in cruel sort,<br />
When her gates opened by false Sinon were,<br />
And direr ill was done than tales report,<br />
This plunder fell to Menelaus’ share,<br />
Wherewith to Egypt’s land he made resort;<br />
There left it to King Proteus, Egypt’s lord,<br />
In ransom for his prisoned wife restored;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>She Helen hight: her Menelaus to free,<br />
To Proteus the pavilion gave away;<br />
Which, passing through the line of Ptolemy,<br />
To Cleopatra fell; from her in fray<br />
Agrippa’s band on the Leucadian sea<br />
Bore off the treasure, amid other prey.<br />
Augustus and Tiberius heired the loom,<br />
Kept till the time of Constantine in Rome:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>That Constantine, whom thou shall ever rue<br />
Fair Italy, while the heavens above are rolled.<br />
Constantine to Byzantium, when he grew<br />
Weary of Tyber, bore the tent of old.<br />
Melissa from his namesake this withdrew,<br />
Its pole of ivory and its cord of gold,<br />
And all its cloth with beauteous figures fraught;<br />
Fairer Apelles’ pencil never wrought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>Here the three Graces in gay vesture gowned<br />
Assisted the delivery of a queen.<br />
Not in four ages in this earthly round<br />
Was ever born a boy so fair of mien.<br />
Jove, Venus, Mars, and Mercury renowned<br />
For fluent speech, about the child are seen:<br />
Him have they strewed, and stew with heaven’s perfume,<br />
Ambrosial odours and aetherial bloom.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>Hippolytus a little label said,<br />
Inscribed upon the baby’s swaddling clothes.<br />
By the hand him Fortune leads in age more staid;<br />
And Valour as a guide before him goes.<br />
An unknown band in sweeping vest arraid,<br />
With long descending locks, the tapestry shows,<br />
Deputed by Corvinus to desire<br />
The tender infant from his princely sire.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>He reverently parts from Hercules’ side,<br />
From her, his lady mother, Eleanor;<br />
And to the Danube wends; where far and wide<br />
They meet the boy, and as a god adore.<br />
The prudent king of Hungary is descried,<br />
Who does due honour to his ripened lore,<br />
In yet unripe, yea, raw and tender years,<br />
And ranks the stripling above all his peers.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>One is there that in his green age and new<br />
Places Strigonia’s crozier in his hand.<br />
Him ever at Corvinus’ side we view;<br />
Whether he doth in court or camp command,<br />
Whether against the Turk, or German crew<br />
The puissant monarch leads his martial band,<br />
Watchful Hippolytus is at his side,<br />
And gathers virtue from his generous guide.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>There is it seen, how he his blooming age<br />
Divides mid arts and wholesome discipline:<br />
The secret spirit of the ancient page<br />
There Fuscus well instructs him to divine:<br />
“This must thou shun, that follow”—seems the sage<br />
To say—“if thou immortally wouldst shine.”<br />
Fashioned withal with so much skill and care<br />
By her who wrought that work, their gestures were.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XC.</td>
<td>A cardinal he next is seen, though young<br />
In years, at council in the Vatican;<br />
Where for deep wisdom graced by eloquent tongue,<br />
With wonder him the assembled conclave scan.<br />
“What will he be”—they seem to say among<br />
Themselves—“when he is ripened into man?<br />
Oh! if on him St. Peter’s mantle fall,<br />
What a blest aera! what a happy call!”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCI.</td>
<td>That brave youth’s liberal pastimes are designed<br />
In other place; on Alpine mountain hoar<br />
Here he affronts the bear of rugged kind;<br />
And there in rushy bottom bays the boar:<br />
Now on his jennet he outgoes the wind,<br />
And drives some goat or gallant hind before;<br />
Which falls o’ertaken on the dusty plain,<br />
By his descending faulchion cleft in twain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCII.</td>
<td>He is descried, amid a fair array<br />
Of poets and philosophers elsewhere<br />
This pricks for him the wandering planets’ way;<br />
These earth, these heaven for his instruction square.<br />
Some chant sad elegies, some verses gay<br />
Lays lyric or heroic; singers there<br />
He with rich music hears; nor moves a pace<br />
But what in every step is sovereign grace.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIII.</td>
<td>The first part of the storied walls pourtraied<br />
That noble prince’s gentle infancy.<br />
Cassandra all beside had overlaid<br />
With fears of justice, prudence, modesty,<br />
Valour, and that fifty virtue, which hath made<br />
With those fair sisters closest amity;<br />
I speak of her that gives and that bestows.<br />
With all these virtues gilt, the stripling glows.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIV.</td>
<td>In this part is the princely youth espied<br />
With that unhappy duke, the Insubri’s head;<br />
In peace they sit in council at his side,<br />
Together armed, the serpent-banner spread.<br />
The youth by one unchanging faith is tied<br />
To him for ever, well or ill bested;<br />
His followers still in flight before the foe,<br />
His guide in peril, his support in woe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCV.</td>
<td>Him in another quarter you descry,<br />
For his Ferrara and her duke in fear,<br />
Who by strange proofs doth sift, and certify<br />
To his just brother, vouched by tokens clear,<br />
The close device of that ill treachery,<br />
Hatched by those kinsmen whom he held most dear;<br />
Hence justly he becomes that title’s heir,<br />
Which Rome yet free bade righteous Tully bear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVI.</td>
<td>Elsewhere in martial panoply he shone,<br />
Hasting to help the church with lifted blade;<br />
With scanty and tumultuous levy gone<br />
Against well-ordered host in arms arraid:<br />
And lo! the coming of that chief alone<br />
Affords the priestly band such present aid,<br />
Extinguished are the fires before they spread.<br />
He came, he saw, he conquered, may be said.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVII.</td>
<td>Elsewhere he stands upon his native strand,<br />
Fighting against the mightiest armament,<br />
That whensoever against Argive land,<br />
Or Turkish, from Venetian harbour went;<br />
Scatters and overthrows the hostile band,<br />
And—spoil and prisoners to his brother sent—<br />
Nothing reserves save that unfading bay;<br />
The only prize he cannot give away.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVIII.</td>
<td>Upon those figures gazed the courtly crew,<br />
But read no meaning in the storied wall:<br />
Because there was not any one to shew<br />
That these were things hereafter to befall.<br />
Those fair and quaintly fashioned forms they view<br />
With pleasure, and peruse the scrolls withal:<br />
But Bradamant, to whom the whole was known,<br />
By wise Melissa taught, rejoiced alone.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIX.</td>
<td>Though not instructed in that history<br />
Like gentle Bradamant, the affianced knight<br />
Remembers how amid his progeny<br />
Atlantes often praised this Hippolyte.<br />
—Who faithfully could verse such courtesy,<br />
As Charlemagne vouchsafed to every wight?<br />
With various games that solemn feast was cheered,<br />
And charged with viands aye the board appeared.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">C.</td>
<td>Who is a valiant knight, is here descried;<br />
For daily broke a thousand lances lay:<br />
Singly to combat or in troops they ride;<br />
On horseback or afoot, they mix in fray.<br />
Worthiest of all Rogero is espied,<br />
Who always conquers, jousting night and day;<br />
And so, in wrestling, dance, and every deed,<br />
Still from its rivals bears away the meed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CI.</td>
<td>On the last day, when at their festive cheer<br />
Was seated solemnly the assembled band,<br />
Where at Charles’ left was placed the wedded peer,<br />
And Bradamant upon his better hand,<br />
Across the fields an armed cavalier,<br />
Of semblance haughty, and of stature grand,<br />
Was seen to ride towards the royal table;<br />
Himself and courser wholly clothed in sable.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CII.</td>
<td>The King of Argier he; that for the scorn<br />
Received from her, when on the bridge he fell,<br />
Never to clothe himself in arms had sworn,<br />
Nor draw the faulchion nor bestride the sell,<br />
Till he had like an anchoret outworn<br />
A year and month and day in lowly cell.<br />
So to chastise themselves for such like crimes<br />
Were cavaliers accustomed in those times.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIII.</td>
<td>Albeit of Charles and Agramant the Moor<br />
Had heard the several fortunes while away,<br />
Not to foreswear himself, he armed no more<br />
Than if in nought concerned in that affray:<br />
But when the year and month were wholly o’er,<br />
And wholly past was the succeeding day,<br />
With other courser, harness, sword, and lance,<br />
The king betook him to the court of France.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIV.</td>
<td>He neither lighted from his horse, nor bowed<br />
His head; and, without sign of reverence due,<br />
His scorn for Charlemagne by gestures showed,<br />
And the high presence of so fair a crew.<br />
Astound and full of wonder stood the crowd,<br />
Such license in that haughty man to view.<br />
All leave their meat, all leave their talk, to hear<br />
The purpose of the stranger cavalier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CV.</td>
<td>To Charles and to Rogero opposite,<br />
With a loud voice, and in proud accent, “I<br />
Am Rodomont of Sarza,” said the knight,<br />
“Who thee, Rogero, to the field defy;<br />
And here, before the sun withdraws his light,<br />
Will prove on thee thine infidelity;<br />
And that thou, as a traitor to thy lord,<br />
Deserv’st not any honour at this board.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVI.</td>
<td>“Albeit thy felony be plain and clear,<br />
Which thou, as christened, canst not disavow;<br />
Nathless to make it yet more plain appear,<br />
This will I prove upon thee; and, if thou<br />
Canst find a knight to combat for thee here,<br />
Him will accept;—if one be not enow—<br />
Will four, nay six accept; and will maintain<br />
My words against them all in listed plain.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVII.</td>
<td>Rogero, with the leave of Pepin’s son,<br />
Uprose at that appeal, and thus replied:<br />
That he—nor he alone—but every one,<br />
Who thus impeached him as a traitor, lied;<br />
That so he by his king had ever done,<br />
Him none could justly blame; and on his side,<br />
He was prepared in listed field to shew<br />
He evermore by him had done his due.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVIII.</td>
<td>He can defend himself; nor need he crave<br />
Another warrior’s help that course to run;<br />
And ’tis his hope to show him he would have<br />
Enough, perhaps would have too much, of one.<br />
Thither Orlando and Rinaldo, brave<br />
Olivier, and his white and sable son,<br />
Thither good Dudon and Marphisa wend;<br />
Who fain with that fierce paynim will contend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIX.</td>
<td>They tell Rogero that, as newly wed<br />
The combat he in person should refuse.<br />
“Take ye no further pains,” the warrior said,<br />
“For such would be for me a foul excuse.”<br />
The Tartar’s arms were brought, which cut the thread<br />
Of more delay and of all further truce:<br />
With spurs Orlando deck’d the youthful lord,<br />
King Charlemagne begirt him with the sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CX.</td>
<td>Marphisa and Bradamant in corslet case<br />
His breast, and clothe him in his other gear.<br />
Astolpho led his horse of noble race:<br />
Sir Dudon held his stirrup: far and near<br />
Rinaldo and Namus made the mob give place,<br />
Assisted by the Marquis Olivier.<br />
All from the crowded lists they drive with speed,<br />
Evermore kept in order for such need.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXI.</td>
<td>The pale-faced dames and damsels troop, in guise<br />
Of pigeons round the lists, a timid show;<br />
When, homeward bound, from fruitful field they rise,<br />
Scared by wide-sweeping winds, which loudly blow,<br />
Mid flash and clap; and when the sable skies<br />
Threat hail and rain, the harvest’s waste and woe:<br />
A timid troop, they for Rogero fear,<br />
Ill matched they deem with that fierce cavalier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXII.</td>
<td>So him deemed all the rabble; and so most<br />
Of those bold cavalier and barons thought;<br />
In that they had not yet the memory lost<br />
Of what that paynim had in Paris wrought,<br />
When singly fire and sword the warrior tost,<br />
And much of that fair town to ruin brought;<br />
Whose signs remained, and yet will long remain:<br />
Nor ever greater havoc plagued that reign.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIII.</td>
<td>Bradamant’s heart above those others’ beat:<br />
Not that she deemed the Saracen in might,<br />
Or valour which in the heart-core hath its seat,<br />
Was of more prowess than the youthful knight;<br />
Nor (what oft gives success in martial feat<br />
That with the paynim was the better right.<br />
Yet cannot she her some ill misgivings quell.<br />
But upon those that love such fear sits well.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIV.</td>
<td>Oh! in her fear for him, how willingly<br />
She battle for Rogero would have done!<br />
If lifeless on the listed field to lie<br />
Surer than sure,—in fight with Ulien’s son.<br />
More than one death would she consent to die,<br />
If she withal could suffer more than one,<br />
Rather than she in that unhappy strife<br />
Would see her cherished consort risk his life.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXV.</td>
<td>But prayer availed not on the damsel’s part<br />
To make Rogero leave to her the quest:<br />
She then with mournful face and beating heart<br />
Stood by to view that pair to fight addrest.<br />
From right and left the peer and paynim start,<br />
And at each other run with lance in rest.<br />
The spears seem ice, as they in shivers fly.<br />
The fragments birds, that mount through middle sky.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVI.</td>
<td>Rodomont’s lance which smote in the career<br />
Upon mid-shield, yet harmed it little; so<br />
Perfect was famous Hector’s iron gear,<br />
Hardened by Vulcan’s hand, and safe from blow.<br />
As well against the shield his levelled spear<br />
Rogero guides, and that good buckler—though<br />
Well steeled within and out, with bone between,<br />
And nigh a palm in thickness—pierces clean;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVII.</td>
<td>And—but his lance resists not that fierce shock,<br />
And at the first assault its splinters fly,<br />
And bits and fragments of the shivered stock<br />
Seem fledged with feathers they ascend so high;<br />
Were his arms hewn from adamantine rock,<br />
The spear would pierce the paynim’s panoply;<br />
And end that battle: but it breaks withal,<br />
And on their croups both staggering coursers fall.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVIII.</td>
<td>With bridle and with spur the martial pair<br />
Raise their proud horses nimbly from the ground;<br />
And having broke their spears, with faulchions bare<br />
Return, to bandy fierce and cruel wound.<br />
Wheeling with wondrous mastery, here and there,<br />
The bold and ready coursers in a round,<br />
The warriors with their biting swords begin<br />
To try where either’s armour is most thin.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIX.</td>
<td>Rodomont had not that hard dragon-hide<br />
Which heretofore had cased the warrior’s breast;<br />
Nor Nimrod’s trenchant sword was at his side;<br />
Nor the accustomed helm his temples prest.<br />
For on that bridge which spanned the narrow tide,<br />
A loser to Dordona’s lady, vest<br />
And arms suspended from the votive stone<br />
He left; as I, meseems, erewhile have shown.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXX.</td>
<td>Clad was the king in other goodly mail;<br />
Yet not like that first panoply secure:<br />
But neither this, nor that, nor harder scale<br />
Could Balisarda’s deadly dint endure;<br />
Against which neither workmanship avail,<br />
Enchantment, temper, nor prime steel and pure.<br />
So here so there Rogero plied his sword,<br />
He more than once the paynim’s armour bored.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXI.</td>
<td>When Rodomont beholds in that fierce close<br />
His widely crimsoned arms, nor can restrain<br />
The greater portion of those griding blows<br />
From biting to the quick, through plate and chain,<br />
He with more fury, with more rage o’erflows,<br />
Than in mid winter the tempestrous main<br />
Flings down his shield, and with both hands outright<br />
Lays at Rogero’s helm with all his might.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXII.</td>
<td>With that excessive force, wherewith the gin,<br />
Erected in two barges upon Po,<br />
And raised by men and wheels, with deafening din<br />
Descends upon the sharpened piles below,<br />
With all his might he smote the paladin<br />
With either hand; was never direr blow:<br />
Him the charmed helmet helped, or—such its force—<br />
The stroke would have divided man and horse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIII.</td>
<td>As if about to fall, the youthful lord<br />
Twice nodded, opening legs and arms; anew<br />
Rodomont smote, in that he would afford<br />
His foe no time his spirits to renew:<br />
Then threatened other stroke; but that fine sword<br />
Bore not such hammering, and in shivers flew;<br />
And the bold Saracen, bereft of brand<br />
Was in the combat left with unarmed hand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIV.</td>
<td>But not for this doth Rodomont refrain:<br />
He swoops upon the Child, unheeding aught:<br />
So sore astounded is Rogero’s brain;<br />
So wholly overclouded is his thought.<br />
But him the paynim well awakes again,<br />
Whom by the neck he with strong arm has caught,<br />
And gripes and grapples with such mighty force,<br />
He falls on earth, pulled headlong from his horse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXV.</td>
<td>Yet leaps from earth as nimbly, moved by spleen<br />
Far less than shame; for on his gentle bride<br />
He turned his eyes, and that fair face serene<br />
Now troubled the disdainful warrior spied.<br />
She in sore doubt her champion’s fall had seen;<br />
And well nigh at that sight the lady died.<br />
Rogero, quickly to revenge the affront,<br />
Clutches his sword and faces Rodomont.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVI.</td>
<td>He at Rogero rode, who that rude shock<br />
Shunned warily, retiring from his ground,<br />
And, as he past, the paynim’s bridle took<br />
With his left had, and turned his courser round;<br />
While with his right he at his rider struck,<br />
Whom he in belly, flank and breast would wound;<br />
And twice sore anguish felt the monarch, gored<br />
In flank and thigh, by good Rogero’s sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVII.</td>
<td>Rodomont, grasping still in that close fight<br />
The hilt and pommel of his broken blade,<br />
Layed at Rogero’s helmet with such might,<br />
That him another stroke might have dismaid:<br />
But good Rogero, who should win of right,<br />
Seizing his arm, the king so rudely swayed,<br />
Bringing his left his better hand to speed,<br />
That he pulled down the paynim from his steed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVIII.</td>
<td>Through force or skill, so fell the Moorish lord,<br />
He stood his match, I rather ought to say<br />
Fell on his feet; because Rogero’s sword<br />
Gave him, ’twas deemed, advantage in the fray.<br />
Rogero stands aloof, with wary ward<br />
As fain to keep the paynim king at bay.<br />
For the wise champion will not let a wight<br />
So talk and bulky close with him in fight;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIX.</td>
<td>Rogero flank and thigh dyed red beheld,<br />
And other wounds; and hoped he would have failed<br />
By little and by little, as it welled;<br />
So that he finally should have prevailed.<br />
His hilt and pommel in his fist yet held<br />
The paynim, which with all his might he scaled<br />
At young Rogero; whom he smote so sore,<br />
The stripling never was so stunned before.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXX.</td>

He, tripping still and staggering to and fro,<br />
Scarce kept himself from falling to the ground.<br />
Rodomont fain would close upon his foe;<br />
But his foot fails him, weakened by the wound,<br />

And on his kneepan fell the paynim knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXI.</td>

Hammered, and held the Saracen so close,<br />
To ground he bore that champion with his hand.<br />
But he so stirred himself, again he rose:<br />
He gripes Rogero so, fast locked they stand.<br />
Seconding their huge vigour by address,<br />
They circle one another, shake, and press.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXII.</td>

Weakened the vigour of the Moorish king:<br />
Rogero had address; had mickle lore;<br />
Was greatly practised in the wrestlers’ ring:<br />
He marked his vantage, nor from strife forbore;<br />
And, where he saw the blood most freely spring,<br />
And where most wounded was the warrior, prest<br />
The paynim with his feet, his arms, and breast.</td>
</tr>

<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIII.</td>

Towards him, and now pushes from him; now<br />
Raises from earth, and on his chest suspends;<br />
Whirls here and there and grapples; and to throw<br />

To keep his vantage taxing strength and thought.</td>

Against his left flank shoved his breast, and sore<br />
Strained him with all his strength engirdled round.<br />
At once he past his better leg before<br />
Rodomont’s knees and pushed, and from the ground<br />
Uplifted high in air the Moorish lord;<br />
Then hurled him down head foremost on the sward.</td>
</tr>

In unexpected ruin whelm the train<br />
