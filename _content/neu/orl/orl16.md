<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>Gryphon finds traitorous Origilla nigh<br />

Slaughtered the Saracens and Christians lie<br />
By thousands and by thousands heaped this while;<br />
And if the Moor outside of Paris die,<br />

Such slaughter deals, that greater ill than this<br />
Never before has been exprest, I wiss.</em></span></td>

<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>Love’s penalties are manifold and dread:<br />
Of which I have endured the greater part,<br />
And, to my cost, in these so well am read,<br />
That I can speak of them as ’twere my art.<br />
Hence if I say, or if I ever said,<br />
(Did speech or living page my thoughts impart)<br />
“One ill is grievous and another light.”<br />
Yield me belief, and deem my judgment right.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>I say, I said, and, while I live, will say,<br />
“He, who is fettered by a worthy chain,<br />
Though his desire his lady should gainsay,<br />
And, every way averse, his suit disdain;<br />
Though Love deprive him of all praised pay,<br />
After long time and trouble spent in vain,<br />
He, if his heart be placed well worthily,<br />
Needs not lament though he should waste and die.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>Let him lament, who plays a slavish part,<br />
Whom two bright eyes and lovely tresses please:<br />
Beneath which beauties lurks a wanton heart<br />
With little that is pure, and much of lees.<br />
The wretch would fly; but bears in him a dart,<br />
Like wounded stag, whichever way he flees;<br />
Dares not confess, yet cannot quench, his flame,<br />
And of himself and worthless love has shame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>The youthful Gryphon finds him in this case,<br />
Who sees the error which he cannot right;<br />
He sees how vilely he his heart does place<br />
On faithless Origille, his vain delight:<br />
Yet evil use doth sovereign reason chase,<br />
And free will is subdued by appetite.<br />
Though a foul mind the lady’s actions speak,<br />
Her, wheresoe’er she is, must Gryphon seek.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>Resuming the fair history, I say,<br />
Out of the city he in secret rode;<br />
Nor to his brother would his plan bewray,<br />
Who oft on him had vain reproof bestowed:<br />
But to the left t’wards Ramah shaped his way,<br />
By the most level and most easy road.<br />
Him six days’ journey to Damascus brought,<br />
Whence, setting out anew, he Antioch sought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>He nigh Damascus met the lover, who<br />
Perfidious Origilla’s heart possest,<br />
And matched in evil customs were the two,<br />
Like stalk and flower: for that in either’s breast<br />
Was lodged a fickle heart; the dame untrue,<br />
And he a traitor whom she loved the best.<br />
While both the lovers hid their nature base,<br />
To others’ cost, beneath a courteous face.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>As I relate to you, the cavalier<br />
Came on huge courser, trapped with mickle pride;<br />
With faithless Origille, in gorgeous gear,<br />
With gold embroidered, and with azure dyed.<br />
Two ready knaves, who serve the warrior, rear<br />
The knightly helm and buckler at his side;<br />
As one who with fair pomp and semblance went<br />
Towards Damascus, to a tournament.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>Damascus’ king a splendid festival<br />
Had in these days bid solemnly proclaim;<br />
And with what pomp they could, upon his call,<br />
Thither, in shining arms, the champions came.<br />
At Gryphon’s sight the harlot’s spirits fall,<br />
Who fears that he will work her scathe and shame;<br />
And knows her lover has not force and breath<br />
To save her from Sir Gryphon, threatening death;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>But like most cunning and audacious quean,<br />
Although she quakes from head to foot with fear,<br />
Her voice so strengthens, and so shapes her mien,<br />
That in her face no signs of dread appear,<br />
Having already made her leman ween<br />
The trick devised, she feigns a joyous cheer,<br />
Towards Sir Gryphon goes, and for long space<br />
Hangs on his neck, fast-locked in her embrace.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>She, after suiting with much suavity<br />
The action to the word, sore weeping, cried:<br />
“Dear lord, is this the guerdon due to me,<br />
For love and worship? that I should abide<br />
Alone one live long year, deprived of thee,<br />
—A second near—and, yet upon thy side<br />
No grief?—and had I borne for thee to stay,<br />
I know not if I should have seen that day.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“When I from Nicosia thee expected<br />
(When thou wast journeying to the plenar court)<br />
To cheer me,—left with fever sore infected,<br />
And in the dread of death,—I heard report<br />
That thou wast gone to Syria; and dejected<br />
By that ill tiding, suffered in such sort,<br />
I, all unable to pursue thy quest,<br />
Had nigh with this right hand transfixt my breast.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“But fortune, by her double bounty, shows<br />
She guards me more than thou: me to convey<br />
She sent my brother here, who with me goes,<br />
My honour safe in his protecting stay;<br />
And this encounter with thee now bestows,<br />
Which I above all other blessings weigh,<br />
And in good time; for hadst thou longer stayed,<br />
My lord, I should have died of hope delayed.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>The wicked woman, full of subtlety<br />
(Worse than a fox in crafty hardihood)<br />
Pursues, and so well shapes her history,<br />
She wholly throws the blame on Gryphon good;<br />
Makes him believe that other not to be<br />
Her kin alone, but of her flesh and blood,<br />
Got by one father;—and so puts upon<br />
The knight, that he less credits Luke and John.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>Nor he the fraud of her, more false than fair,<br />
Only forbore with just reproach to pay;<br />
Nor only did the threatened stranger spare,<br />
Who was the lover of that lady gay;<br />
But deemed to excuse himself sufficient were,<br />
Turning some portion of the blame away;<br />
And as the real brother she profest,<br />
Unceasingly the lady’s knight carest;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>And to Damascus, with the cavalier<br />
Returned, who to Sir Gryphon made report,<br />
That Syria’s wealthy king, with sumptuous cheer,<br />
Within that place would hold a splendid court;<br />
And who, baptized or infidel, appear<br />
There at his tourney (of whatever sort),<br />
Within the city and without, assures<br />
From wrong, for all the time the feast endures.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Yet I of Origilla’s treachery<br />
Shall not so steadfastly pursue the lore,<br />
Who, famed not for one single perfidy,<br />
Thousands and thousands had betrayed before,<br />
But that I will return again to see<br />
Two hundred thousand wretched men or more<br />
Burnt by the raging wild-fire, where they spread,<br />
About the walls of Paris, scathe and dread.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>I left you where king Agramant prepared<br />
To storm a gate, and to the assault was gone:<br />
This he had hoped to find without a guard;<br />
And work elsewhere to bar the way was none.<br />
For there, in person, Charles kept watch and ward<br />
With many, practised warriors every one;<br />
Two Angelines, two Guidos, Angelier,<br />
Avino, Avolio, Otho, and Berlinghier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>One and the other host its worth, before<br />
Charles and king Agramant, desire to show,<br />
Where praise, where riches are, they think, in store<br />
For those that do their duty on the foe.<br />
But such were not the atchievements of the Moor<br />
As to repair the loss; for, to his woe,<br />
Full many a Saracen the champaign prest;<br />
Whose folly was a beacon to the rest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>The frequent darts a storm of hail appear,<br />
Which from the city-wall the Christians fling;<br />
The deafening clamours put the heavens in fear,<br />
Which, from our part, and from that other, ring.<br />
But Charles and Agramant must wait; for here<br />
I of the Mars of Africa will sing,<br />
King Rodomont, that fierce and fearful man,<br />
That through the middle of the city ran.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>I know not, sir, if you the adventure dread<br />
Of that so daring Moor to mind recall,<br />
The leader, who had left his people dead,<br />
Between the second work and outer wall;<br />
Upon those limbs the ravening fire so fed,<br />
Was never sight more sad!—I told withal,<br />
How vaulting o’er that hindrance at a bound,<br />
He cleared the moat which girt the city round.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>When he was known the thickening crowd among,<br />
By the strange arms he wore and scaly hide,<br />
There, where the aged sires and feebler throng.<br />
Listened to each new tale on every side;<br />
Heaven-high groan, moan, and lamentation rung,<br />
And loud they beat their lifted palms and cried:<br />
While those who had the strength to fly aloof,<br />
Sought safety not from house or temple’s roof.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>But this the cruel sword concedes to few,<br />
So brandished by that Saracen robust;<br />
And here, with half a leg dissevered, flew<br />
A foot, there head divided from the bust:<br />
This cleft across, and that behold him hew,<br />
From head to hips, so strong the blow and just.<br />
While, of the thousands wounded by the Moor,<br />
Is none that shows an honest scar before.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>What by weak herd, in fields of Hircany,<br />
The tiger does, or Indian Ganges near,<br />
Or wolf, by lamb or kid, on heights which lie<br />
On Typheus’ back, the cruel cavalier<br />
Now executes on those, I will not, I<br />
Call phalanxes or squadrons, but a mere<br />
Rabble, that I should term a race forlorn,<br />
Who but deserved to die ere they were born.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>Of all he cuts, and thrusts, and maims, and bleeds,<br />
There is not one who looks him in the face.<br />
Throughout that street, which in a straight line leads<br />
Up to St. Michael’s bridge, so thronged a space,<br />
Rodomont, terrible and fearful, speeds,<br />
Whirling his bloody brand, nor grants he grace,<br />
In his career, to servant or to lord;<br />
And saint and sinner feel alike the sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>Religion cannot for the priest bespeak<br />
Mercy, nor innocence avail the child:<br />
Nor gently beaming eyes, nor vermeil cheek,<br />
Protect the blooming dame or damsel mild.<br />
Age smites its breast and flies: while bent to wreak<br />
Vengeance, the Saracen, with gore defiled,<br />
Shows not his valour more than cruel rage,<br />
Heedless alike of order, sex, and age.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>Nor the impious king alone with human blood,<br />
—Lord of the impious he—his hand distains,<br />
But even on walls so sorely vents his mood,<br />
He fires fair houses, and polluted fanes.<br />
The houses almost all were made of wood,<br />
Then (as ’tis told) and this, by what remains,<br />
May be believed; for yet in Paris we<br />
Six out of ten no better builded see.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>Though flames demolish all things far and wide,<br />
This ill appears his furious hate to slake:<br />
Where’er the paynim has his hands applied,<br />
He tumbles down a roof at every shake.<br />
My lord, believe, you never yet espied<br />
Bombard in Padua, of so large a make,<br />
That it could rend from wall of battered town<br />
What, at a single pull, the king plucked down.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>While the accursed man, amid the rout,<br />
So warred with fire and sword, if at his post,<br />
King Agramant had prest it from without,<br />
The ample city had that day been lost.<br />
But he was hindered by the warrior stout,<br />
Who came from England with the advancing host,<br />
Composed of English and of Scotch allied,<br />
With Silence and the Angel for their guide.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>It was God’s will, that while through town and tower<br />
The furious Rodomont such ruin spread,<br />
Thither arrived Rinaldo, Clermont’s flower.<br />
Three leagues above, he o’er the river’s bed<br />
Had cast a bridge; from whence his English power<br />
To the left-hand by crooked ways he led;<br />
That, meaning to assail the barbarous foes,<br />
The stream no obstacle might interpose.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>Rinaldo had, with Edward, sent a force,<br />
Six thousand strong, of archer infantry,<br />
And sped, with Ariman, two thousand horse<br />
Of lightest sort; and foot and cavalry<br />
Sought Paris by those roads, which have their course<br />
Directly to, and from, the Picard sea;<br />
That by St. Martin’s and St. Denys’ gate,<br />
They might convey the aid the burghers wait.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>Rinaldo sent with these the baggage train<br />
And carriages, with which his troops were stored;<br />
And fetching, with the forces that remain,<br />
A compass, he the upper way explored.<br />
He bridge, and boat, and means to pass the Seine,<br />
Had with him; for it here was ill to ford.<br />
He past his army, broke the bridges down,<br />
And rank’d in line the bands of either crown.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>But having first the peers and captains wheeled<br />
About him in a ring, the cavalier<br />
Mounted the bank which overtopt the field,<br />
So much, that all might plainly see and hear;<br />
And cried, “My lords, you should thanksgiving yield,<br />
With lifted hands, to God, who brought you here;<br />
Through whom, o’er every nation, you may gain<br />
Eternal glory, bought with little pain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>“Two princes, by your means, will rescued be,<br />
If you relieve those city gates from siege;<br />
Him, your own king, whom you from slavery<br />
And death to save, a subject’s vows oblige;<br />
And a famed emperor, of more majesty<br />
Than ever yet in court was served by liege,<br />
And with them other kings, and dukes, and peers,<br />
And lords of other lands, and cavaliers.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“So that one city saving, not alone<br />
Will the Parisians bless your helping hand,<br />
Who, sadder than for sorrows of their own,<br />
Timid, afflicted, and disheartened stand;<br />
And their unhappy wives and children moan,<br />
Which share in the same peril, and the band<br />
Or virgins, dedicate to heavenly spouse,<br />
Lest this day frustrate see their holy vows;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>—“I say, this city saved from deadly wound,<br />
Not only will Parisians hold you dear;<br />
But habitants of all the countries round:<br />
Nor speak I only of the nations near;<br />
For city there is none on Christian ground.<br />
But what has citizens beleaguered here;<br />
So that to you, for vanquishing the foe,<br />
More lands than France will obligation owe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“If him the ancients with a crown endued,<br />
Who saved one citizen by worthy deed,<br />
For rescuing such a countless multitude,<br />
What recompense shall be your worthy meed?<br />
But if, from jealousy or sloth, so good<br />
And holy, enterprise should ill succeed,<br />
Believe me, only while these walls endure,<br />
Is Italy or Almayn’s realm secure;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“Or any other part, where men adore<br />
Him, who for us upon the cross was hung;<br />
Nor think that distance saves you from the Moor,<br />
Nor deem your island strong, the waves among.<br />
For if, from far Gibraltar’s straits of yore,<br />
And old Alcides’ pillars, sailed the throng,<br />
To bear off plunder from your sea-girt strands,<br />
What will they do when they possess our lands?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“And, if in this fair enterprise arrayed,<br />
No gain, no glory served you as a guide,<br />
A common debt enjoins you mutual aid,<br />
Militant here upon one Church’s side.<br />
Moreover, let not any be afraid,<br />
Our broken foemen will the assault abide;<br />
Who seem to me ill-taught in warlike art,<br />
A feeble rabble without arms or heart.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>Such reasons, and yet better for, that need<br />
Might good Rinaldo in his speech infer;<br />
And with quick phrase and voice, to valiant deed<br />
The high-minded barons and bold army stir;<br />
And this was but to goad a willing steed<br />
(As the old proverb says) who lacks no spur.<br />
He moved the squadrons, having closed his speech,<br />
Softly, beneath their separate banners, each.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>He, without clamour, without any noise.<br />
So moves his triple host, their flags below.<br />
Zerbino, marching by the stream, enjoys<br />
The honour first to assail the barbarous foe;<br />
The paladin the Irishmen employs<br />
More inland, with a wider wheel to go.<br />
Thus England’s horse and foot, the two between,<br />
Led by the Duke of Lancaster, are seen.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>The paladin rode on, along the shore,<br />
When he had put the warriors in their way,<br />
And, passing by their squadrons, pricked before<br />
Valiant Zerbino and his whole array,<br />
Until he reached the quarters of the Moor,<br />
Where Oran’s king, and king Sobrino lay;<br />
Who, half-a-mile removed from those of Spain,<br />
Posted upon that side, observed the plain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>With such a faithful escort fortified<br />
And sure, the Christians who had thither wound,<br />
With Silence and the Angel for their guide,<br />
No longer could stand mute or keep their ground:<br />
But hearing now the foe, with shouts defied<br />
Their host, and made the shrilling trumpets sound;<br />
And with loud clamours, which Heaven’s concave fill,<br />
Sent through the paynim’s bones a deadly chill.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>Rinaldo spurs before the troops combined<br />
His foaming courser, and his weapon rests;<br />
And a full bow-shot leaves the Scots behind:<br />
So all delay the impatient peer molests.<br />
As oftentimes an eddying gust of winds<br />
Issues, ere yet the horrid storm infests,<br />
So sallying swiftly from the following herd,<br />
Rinaldo forth upon Bayardo spurred.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>As the aspect of the paladin of France,<br />
The wavering Moorish files betray their fear;<br />
And, trembling in their hands, is seen the lance,<br />
Their thighs and stirrups quivering, like the spear.<br />
King Pulian only marks the knight’s advance,<br />
Knowing Rinaldo not, unchanged in cheer;<br />
Nor thinking such a cruel shock to meet,<br />
Gallops against him on his courser fleet.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>He stoops upon the weapon which he strains,<br />
Whole and collected for the martial game:<br />
Then to his horse abandoning the reins,<br />
And goading with both spurs the courser, came.<br />
Upon the other side no valour feigns,<br />
But shows, by doings, what he is in name;<br />
—With what rare grace and matchless art he wars,<br />
The son of Aymon, rather son of Mars.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>Well-matched in skill, they aimed their cruel blows,<br />
With lances at each other’s heads addrest;<br />
Ill matched, in arms and valour, were the foes,<br />
For this past on, and that the champaigne prest.<br />
More certain proof of worth, when warriors close,<br />
There needs than knightly lance, well placed in rest;<br />
But Fortune even more than Valour needs,<br />
Which ill, without her saving succour, speeds.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>With the good spear new levelled in his fist,<br />
At Oran’s king behold Rinaldo dart.<br />
Of bulk, and bone, and sinew, to resist<br />
The monarch was, but ill supplied with heart.<br />
And his might pass for a fair stroke in list,<br />
Though planted in the buckler’s nether part.<br />
Let those excuse it who refuse to admire,<br />
Since the good paladin could reach no higher.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>Nor did the buckler so the weapon stay,<br />
Though made of palm within, and steel without,<br />
But that it pierced the paunch, and made a way<br />
To let that mean and ill matched spirit out.<br />
The courser, who had deemed that all the day<br />
He must so huge a burden bear about,<br />
Thanked in his heart the warrior, who well met,<br />
Had thus preserved him from so sore a sweat.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>Rinaldo, having broke his rested spear,<br />
So wheels his horse, he seems equipt with wings;<br />
Who, turning swiftly with the cavalier,<br />
Amid the closest crowd, impetuous springs.<br />
Composed of brittle glass the arms appear<br />
Where Sir Rinaldo red Fusberta swings.<br />
Nor tempered steel is there, nor corslet thick,<br />
Which keeps the sword from biting to the quick.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>Yet few the tempered plates or iron pins<br />
With which encounters that descending brand;<br />
But targets, some of oak and some of skins,<br />
And quilted vest and turban’s twisted band.<br />
Lightly such drapery good Rinaldo thins,<br />
And cleaves, and bores, and shears, on either hand;<br />
Nor better from his sword escapes the swarm,<br />
Than grass from sweeping scythe, or grain from storm.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>The foremost squadron had been put to flight,<br />
When thither the vanguard Zerbino led.<br />
Forth pricking from the following crowd, in sight<br />
Appeared, with levelled lance, their youthful head:<br />
With no less fury those who trooped to fight<br />
Beneath his banner, to the combat sped;<br />
Like lions, like so many wolves, who leap<br />
In fury to the assault of goat or sheep.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>Both spurred their coursers on, with rested lance,<br />
When either warrior to his foe was near;<br />
And that short interval, that small expanse,<br />
Of plain, between, was seen to disappear.<br />
Was never witnessed yet a stranger dance!<br />
For the Scots only ply the murderous spear;<br />
Only the scattered paynims slaughtered lie,<br />
As if conducted thither but to die.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>It seemed as if each coward paynim grew<br />
More cold than ice, each Scot more fierce than flame.<br />
The Moors believed that with Rinaldo’s thew<br />
And muscle fortified, each Christian came.<br />
Sobrino quickly moved his ordered crew,<br />
Nor stayed till herald should his call proclaim:<br />
Better were they than those which went before,<br />
For captain, armour, and for martial lore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>Less worthless men of Africa were they,<br />
Though ill had they been deemed of much avail.<br />
Ill harnessed, and worse trained to martial fray,<br />
Forthwith King Dardinel, the foe to assail,<br />
Moved up his host, himself in helmet gay,<br />
And sheathing all his limbs in plate and mail.<br />
The fourth division I believe was best,<br />
Which, under Isolier, to battle prest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>Thraso, this while, the valiant Duke of Mar,<br />
Glad in the tumult, for the cavaliers<br />
Who muster in his train, uplifts the bar,<br />
And to the lists of fame his following chears,<br />
When Isolier, with horsemen of Navarre,<br />
Entered in that fierce fray he sees and hears.<br />
Next Ariodantes moved his chivalry,<br />
Who was of late made Duke of Albany.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>The deep sonorous trumpet’s bellowing,<br />
And sound of drum, and barbarous instrument,<br />
Combined with twang of bow, and whiz of sling,<br />
Wheel and machine, and stone from engine sent,<br />
And (what more loud than these appeared to ring)<br />
Tumult, and shriek, and groan, and loud lament,<br />
Composed a direr whole than what offends<br />
The neighbouring tribes where deafening Nile descends.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>The arrows’ double shower the ample sky<br />
With wide-extended shade is seen to shrowd;<br />
Breath, smoke of sweat and dust ascend on high,<br />
And seem to stamp in air a murky cloud.<br />
By turns each host gives way, and you might spy,<br />
Now chasing, now in flight, the self-same crowd;<br />
And here some wight, beside his foeman slain,<br />
Or little distant, prostrate on the plain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>When, harassed with fatigue, a wearied crew<br />
Withdraw, fresh files their fellows reinforce:<br />
Men, here and there, the wasted ranks renew;<br />
Here march supplies of foot, and there of horse:<br />
Her mantle green for robe of crimson hue<br />
Earth shifts, ensanguined where the warriors course:<br />
And there were azure flowers and yellow sprung,<br />
Now slaughtered men lie stretched their steeds among.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>Zerbino was more wonders seen to do<br />
Than ever stripling of his age, he strowed<br />
The ground with heaps of dead, and overthrew<br />
The paynim numbers which about him flowed.<br />
The valiant Ariodantes to his new-<br />
Entrusted squadron mighty prowess showed;<br />
Filling with dread and wonder, near and far,<br />
The squadrons of Castile and of Navarre.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>Chelindo and Mosco (bastards were the twain<br />
Of Calabrun, late king of Arragon),<br />
And one esteemed among the valiant train,<br />
Calamidor, of Barcellona’s town,<br />
Leaving their standards, in the hope to gain,<br />
By young Zerbino’s death, a glorious force,<br />
And wounded in his flanks the prince’s horse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>Pierced by three lances lay the courser strong,<br />
But bold Zerbino quickly rose anew;<br />
And, eager to avenge his charger’s wrong,<br />
The assailants, where he sees them, will pursue.<br />
Zerbino at Mosco first, that overhung<br />
Him, in the hope to make him prisoner, flew,<br />
And pierced him in the flank; who from his sell,<br />
Pallid and cold, upon the champaign fell.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>When him so killed, as ’twere by stealthy blow,<br />
Chelindo viewed, to avenge his brother slain,<br />
He charged, intent the prince to overthrow;<br />
But he seized fast his courser by the rein,<br />
And, thence to rise not, laid the charger low,<br />
Destined no more to feed on hay or grain;<br />
For at one stroke, so matchless was his force,<br />
Zerbino cleft the rider and his horse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>When that fell blow Calamidor espied,<br />
He turned the bridle short to speed away,<br />
But him with downright cut Zerbino plied<br />
Behind, and cried withal, “Stay, traitor, stay.”<br />
Nor from its aim the sword-stroke wandered wide,<br />
Though from the mark it went somedeal astray;<br />
The falchion missed the rider as he fled,<br />
But reached the horse’s croup, and stretched him dead,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>He quits the horse, and thence for safety crawls;<br />
But he with little boot escapes his foe;<br />
For him Duke Thraso’s horse o’erturns and mawls,<br />
Opprest the ponderous courser’s weight below.<br />
Where the huge crowd upon Zerbino falls,<br />
Ariodantes and Lurcanio go;<br />
And with them many a cavalier and count,<br />
Who do their best Zerbino to remount.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>Then Artalico and Margano knew<br />
The force of Ariodantes’ circling brand:<br />
While Casimir and Enearco rue<br />
More deeply yet the puissance of his hand.<br />
Smote by the knight, escaped the former two;<br />
The others were left dead upon the strand.<br />
Lurcanio shows what are his force and breath;<br />
Who charges, smites, o’erturns, and puts to death.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>Sir, think not that more inland on the plain<br />
The warfare is less mortal than along<br />
The stream, nor that the troops behind remain<br />
Which to the duke of Lancaster belong.<br />
He valiantly assailed the flags of Spain,<br />
And long in even scale the battle hung.<br />
For Horse and Foot, and Captains of those bands,<br />
On either side, could deftly ply their hands.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>Forward Sir Oldrad pricks and Fieramont;<br />
This Glocester’s duke, and York’s the other knight;<br />
With them conjoined is Richard, Warwick’s count,<br />
And the bold duke of Clarence, Henry hight.<br />
These Follicon and Matalista front,<br />
And Baricond, with all they lead to fight.<br />
Almeria this, and that Granada guides,<br />
And o’er Marjorca Baricond presides.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>Well matched awhile the Christian and the Moor<br />
Appeared, without advantage in the fray.<br />
Not this, now that gave ground, like corn before<br />
The light and fickle breeze which blows in May:<br />
Or as the sea which ripples on the shore,<br />
Still comes and goes, nor keeps one certain way,<br />
When hollow Fortune thus had sported long,<br />
She proved disastrous to the paynim throng.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>The duke of Glocester Matalista bold<br />
Assailed this while, and hurtled from his sell;<br />
Fieramont Follicon o’erturned and rolled,<br />
In the right shoulder smit, on earth as well.<br />
The advancing English either paynim hold,<br />
And bear their prisoners off to dungeon cell.<br />
This while, Sir Baricond is, in the strife,<br />
By Clarence’s bold duke deprived of life.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>Hence ’tis among the Moors amazement all,<br />
While hence the Christians take such heart and pride,<br />
The bands do nought but quit their ground and fall,<br />
And break their order on the Paynim side,<br />
What time the Christian troops come on, and gall<br />
Their flying rants, which nowhere will abide:<br />
And had not one arrived to aid their host.<br />
The Paynim camp had on that side been lost.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>But Ferrau, who till this time ever nigh<br />
Marsilius, scarce had quitted him that day,<br />
When half destroyed he marked his chivalry,<br />
And saw that baffled banner born away,<br />
Pricked his good courser forth, in time to spy,<br />
(Where mid those squadrons hottest waxed the fray)<br />
With his head severed in a griesly wound,<br />
Olympio de la Serra fall to ground:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>A stripling he, who such sweet musick vented,<br />
Accorded to the horned lyre’s soft tone;<br />
That at the dulcet melody relented<br />
The hearer’s heart, though harder than a stone.<br />
Happy! if, with such excellence contented,<br />
He had pursued so fair a fame alone,<br />
And loathed shield, quiver, helmet, sword and lance;<br />
Destined by these to die a youth in France.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>When bold French beheld his cruel plight,<br />
For whom he love and much esteem profest,<br />
He felt more pity at the doleful sight<br />
Than, ’mid those thousands slain, for all the rest.<br />
And smote the foe who slew him with such might,<br />
That he his helm divided from the crest;<br />
Cut front, eyes, visage, and mid bosom through,<br />
And cast him down amid the slaughtered crew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>Nor stops he here, nor leaves a corslet whole,<br />
Nor helm unbroken, where his sword is plied,<br />
Of this the front or cheek, of that the poll,<br />
The arm of other foe his strokes divide;<br />
And he, of these divorcing body and soul,<br />
Restores the wavering battle on that side;<br />
Whence the disheartened and ignoble throng<br />
Are scattered wide, and broke, and driven along.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Into the medley pricks King Agramant,<br />
Desirous there his bloody course to run;<br />
With him King Baliverzo, Farurant,<br />
Soridan, Bambirago, Prusion;<br />
And next so many more of little vaunt,<br />
Whose blood will form a lake ere day be done,<br />
That I could count each leaf with greater ease<br />
When autumn of their mantle strips the trees.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>Agramant from the wall a numerous band<br />
Of horse and foot withdraws, and sends the array<br />
Beneath the king of Fez, with a command<br />
Behind the Moorish tents to make his way,<br />
And those of Ireland in their march withstand,<br />
Whom he sees hurrying with what haste they may,<br />
And with wide wheel and spacious compass wind,<br />
To fall upon the paynim camp behind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>The king of Fez upon this service prest;<br />
For all delay might sore his work impede.<br />
This while King Agramant unites the rest,<br />
And parts the troops who to the battle speed.<br />
He sought himself the river, where he guessed<br />
The Moorish host might most his presence need;<br />
And, from that quarter, had a courier prayed,<br />
By King Sobrino sent, the monarch’s aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>He more than half his camp behind him led,<br />
In one deep phalanx. At the mighty sound<br />
Alone, the Scotsmen trembled, and in dread<br />
Abandoned honour, order, and their ground:<br />
Lurcanio, Ariodantes, and their head,<br />
Zerbino, there alone the torrent bound;<br />
And haply he, who was afoot, had died,<br />
But that in time his need Rinaldo spied.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>Elsewhere the paladin was making fly<br />
A hundred banners: while the cavalier<br />
So chased the quailing Saracens, the cry<br />
Of young Zerbino’s peril smote the ear;<br />
For, single and afoot, his chivalry<br />
Amid the Africans had left the peer.<br />
Rinaldo turned about and took his way<br />
Where he beheld the Scots in disarray.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>He plants his courser, where their squadrons yield<br />
To the fierce paynims, and exclaims: “Where go<br />
Your bands, and why so basely quit the field,<br />
Yielding so vilely to so vile a foe?<br />
Behold the promised trophies, spear and shield,<br />
Spoils which your loaded churches ought to show!<br />
What praise! what glory! that alone, and reft<br />
Of his good horse, your monarch’s son is left!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>He from a squire receives a lance, and spies<br />
King Prusion little distant, sovereign<br />
Of the Alvaracchiae, and against him hies;<br />
Whom he unhorses, dead upon the plain.<br />
So Agricalt, so Bambirago dies;<br />
And next sore wounded is Sir Soridane;<br />
Who had been slain as well amid the throng,<br />
If good Rinaldo’s lance had proved more strong.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>That weapon broken, he Fusberta rears,<br />
And smites Sir Serpentine, him of the star.<br />
Though charmed from mischief are the cavalier’s<br />
Good arms, he falls astounded by the jar,<br />
And thus Rinaldo round Zerbino clears<br />
The field so widely, where those champions war,<br />
That without more dispute he takes a horse<br />
Of those, who masterless, at random, course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>That he in time remounted it was well,<br />
Who haply would not, if he more delayed:<br />
For Agramant at once, and Dardinel,<br />
Sobrino, and Balastro thither made;<br />
But he, who had in time regained the sell,<br />
Wheeled, here and there his horse, with brandished blade,<br />
Dispatching into hell the mixt array,<br />
That how men live above their ghosts might say.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>The good Rinaldo, who to overthrow<br />
The strongest of the foeman covets still,<br />
At Agramant directs a deadly blow,<br />
—Who seems too passing-proud, and greater ill<br />
Works there, than thousand others of the foe—<br />
And spurs his horse, the Moorish chief to spill.<br />
He smote the monarch, broadside charged the steed,<br />
And man and horse reversed upon the mead.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>What time, without, in such destructive frays<br />
Hate, Rage, and Fury, all offend by turns,<br />
In Paris Rodomont the people slays,<br />
And costly house, and holy temple burns:<br />
While Charles elsewhere anther duty stays,<br />
Who nothing hears of this, nor aught discerns.<br />
He, in the town, receives the British band,<br />
Which Edward and Sir Ariman command.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>To him a squire approached, who pale with dread,<br />
Scarce drew his breath, and cried: “Oh, well away!<br />
Alas! alas!” (and thus he often said,<br />
Ere he could utter aught beside) “To-day,<br />
To-day, sire, is the Roman empire sped,<br />
And Christ to the heathen makes his flock a prey.<br />
A fiend from air to-day has dropt, that none<br />
Henceforth may in this city make their won.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>“Satan (in sooth, it can no other be)<br />
Destroys and ruins the unhappy town.<br />
Turn, and the curling wreaths of vapour see,<br />
From the red flames which wander up and down;<br />
List to those groans, and be they warrantry<br />
Of the sad news thy servant now makes known!<br />
One the fair city wastes with sword and fire,<br />
Before whose vengeful fury all retire.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>Even such as he, who hears the tumult wide,<br />
And clatter of church-bells, ere he espy<br />
The raging fire, concealed from none beside<br />
Himself, to him most dangerous, and most nigh;<br />
Such was King Charles; who heard, and then descried<br />
The new disaster with his very eye.<br />
Hence he the choicest of his meiny steers<br />
Thither, where he the cry and tumult hears.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>With many peers and chiefs, who worthiest are,<br />
Summoned about him, Charlemagne is gone:<br />
He bids direct his standards to the square<br />
Whither the paynim had repaired; hears groan<br />
And tumult, spies the horrid tokens there<br />
Of cruelty, sees human members strown.<br />
—No more—Let him return another time,<br />
Who willingly will listen to this rhyme.</td>
</tr>
