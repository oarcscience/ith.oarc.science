<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>By good Rogero and those paynims three<br />
Defeated, Charlemagne to Paris flies.<br />
Already all, throughout their chivalry,<br />
Are mad with spite and hatred; jars arise,<br />
And strife; and means to still their enmity<br />
Their sovereign is unable to devise.<br />
From him departs the monarch of Argier,<br />
Who is rejected of his lady dear.</em></span></td>
</tr>

<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>A woman for the most part reasons best<br />
Upon a sudden motion, and untaught;<br />
For with that special grace the sex is blest,<br />
’Mid those so many gifts, wherewith ’tis fraught;<br />
But man, of a less nimble wit possest,<br />
Is ill at counsel, save, with sober thought,<br />
He ruminates thereon, content to spend<br />
Care, time and trouble to mature his end.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>That seemed good counsel, but was ill indeed<br />
Of Malagigi’s, as before was said;<br />
Albeit he so rescued in his need<br />
His cousin Richardet, with odds o’erlaid,<br />
When from the paynim monarchs him he freed<br />
By ready demon, who his hest obeyed;<br />
For sure he never deemed they should be borne,<br />
Where they would work the Christian army scorn.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>Had he some little prize for counsel stayed,<br />
(We with the same success may well suppose)<br />
He to his cousin might have furnished aid,<br />
Yet brought not on the Christian host their foes:<br />
That evil sprite he might as well have made,<br />
Him, who embodied in the palfrey goes,<br />
Eastward or west, so far that lady bear,<br />
That France should hear no further of the pair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>So the two lovers, following her who flies,<br />
To other place than Paris might be brought:<br />
But this calamity was a surprise<br />
On Malagigi, through his little thought;<br />
And fiendish malice, banished from the skies,<br />
Which ever blood and fire and ravage sought,<br />
Guided them by that way to Charles’ disaster;<br />
Left to his choice by him, the wizard master.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>The wayward fiend who makes that palfrey ramp<br />
Bears off the frighted Doralice amain;<br />
Nor river nor yet yawning ditch, or swamp,<br />
Wood, rock, or rugged cliff, the steed restrain;<br />
Till, traversing the French and English camp,<br />
And other squadrons of the mingled train,<br />
Beneath the holy flag of Christ arraid,<br />
He to Granada’s king the fair conveyed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>The Sarzan and the Tartar the first day<br />
That royal damsel a long while pursue;<br />
Because her distant form they yet survey;<br />
But finally they lose that lady’s view;<br />
When, like a lyme-dog, whom the hunters lay<br />
On hare or roebuck’s trail, the valiant two<br />
Follow upon her track, nor halt, till told<br />
That she is harboured in her father’s hold.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>Guard thyself, Charles: for, lo! against thee blown<br />
Is such a storm, that I no refuge see:<br />
Nor these redoubted monarchs come alone,<br />
But those of Sericane and Circassy;<br />
While Fortune, who would probe thee to the bone,<br />
Has taken those two shining stars from thee,<br />
Who kept thee by their wisdom and their light;<br />
And thou remainest blind and wrapt in night.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>’Tis of the valiant cousins I would speak:<br />
Of these, Orlando of his wit bereft,<br />
Naked, in sun or shower, by plain or peak,<br />
Wanders about the world, a helpless weft;<br />
And he, in wisdom little less to seek,<br />
Rinaldo, in thy peril thee has left;<br />
And, for in Paris-town she is not found,<br />
In search of his Angelica is bound.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>A cunning, old enchanter him deceived,<br />
As in the outlet of my tale was said:<br />
Deluded by a phantom, he believed<br />
Angelica was with Orlando fled;<br />
And hence with jealousy, at heart, aggrieved<br />
(Lover ne’er suffered worse) to Paris sped;<br />
Whence he, as soon as he appeared at court,<br />
By chance, was named to Britain to resort.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>Now, the field won, wherein with mickle fame<br />
He drove King Agramant his works behind,<br />
To Paris yet again the warrior came,<br />
Searched convent, tower, and house, and, save confined<br />
’Twixt solid walls or columns be the dame,<br />
Her will the restless lover surely find:<br />
Nor her nor yet Orlando he descries,<br />
So forth in the desire to seek them hies.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>Her to Anglantes or to Brava brought,<br />
He deemed the Count enjoyed in mirth and play;<br />
And vainly, here and there, that damsel sought,<br />
Nor here nor there, descried the long-sought prey.<br />
To Paris he repaired again, in thought<br />
The paladin returning to waylay;<br />
Because he deemed he could not rove at large<br />
Without that Town, but on some special charge.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>Within he takes a day or two’s repose;<br />
And, when he finds Orlando comes not there,<br />
Again to Brava and Anglantes goes<br />
Inquiring tidings of the royal fair;<br />
Nor, whether morning dawns or noontide glows,<br />
—Nor night nor day—his weary steed does spare;<br />
Nor once—but twice a hundred times—has run<br />
The selfsame course, by light of moon or sun.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>But the ancient foe, deluded by whose say,<br />
To the forbidden fruit Eve raised her hand,<br />
Turned his wan eyes on Charlemagne one day,<br />
When he the good Rinaldo absent scanned;<br />
And seeing what foul rout and disarray<br />
Might at that time be given to Charles’s band,<br />
Of all the Saracens the choice and flower<br />
Marshalled in arms against the Christian power.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>King Sacripant and King Gradasso (who<br />
Whilere companionship in war had made,<br />
When from Atlantes’ palace fled the two)<br />
Together to unite their arms, in aid<br />
Of royal Agramant’s beleaguered crew,<br />
And where through unknown lands the warriors hied,<br />
Made smooth the way, and served them as a guide.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>Thither another fiend that ruthless foe<br />
Bade Rodomont and Mandricardo bear<br />
Through ways, by which his comrade was not slow<br />
With the affrighted Doralice to fare:<br />
A third, lest they their enterprize forego,<br />
Rogero and Marphisa has in care:<br />
But their conductor journeys not so fast;<br />
And hence that martial pair arrives the last.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Later by half an hour, against their foes,<br />
So matched, Rogero and Marphisa speed;<br />
Because the sable angel, who his blows<br />
Aimed at the bands that held the Christian creed,<br />
Provided, that the contest which arose<br />
About that horse, his work should not impede;<br />
Which had again been kindled, had the twain,<br />
Rodomont and Rogero, met again.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>The first four ride until themselves they find<br />
Where the besiegers and besieged they view;<br />
And see the banners shaking in the wind,<br />
And the cantonments of those armies two.<br />
Here they short counsel took, and next opined,<br />
In spite of Charlemagne’s beleaguering crew,<br />
To carry speedy succour to their liege,<br />
And rescue royal Agramant from siege.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>Where thickest camped lay Charles’s host, they spurred,<br />
Closing their files against the Christian foe.<br />
“Afric and Spain!” is the assailants’ word,<br />
Whom at all points the Franks for paynims know.<br />
—“To arms, to arms!” throughout their camp is heard:<br />
But first is felt the Moorish sabre’s blow:<br />
Even on the rear-guard falls the vengeful stroke,<br />
Not charged alone, but routed, beat and broke.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>The Christian host throughout is overthrown,<br />
And how they know not, in tumultuous wise;<br />
And that it is a wonted insult done<br />
By Switzer or by Gascon, some surmise;<br />
But—since the reason is to most unknown—<br />
Each several nation to its standard flies,<br />
This to the drum, that to the trumpet’s sound,<br />
And shriek and shout from earth to heaven redound.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>All armed is Charlemagne, except his head,<br />
And, girt with paladins, his faithful stay,<br />
Arrived demanding what alarm has bred<br />
Disorder in his host and disarray;<br />
And stopt with menace this or that who fled,<br />
And many fugitives, upon their way,<br />
Some with maimed face, breast, arm, or hand, espied,<br />
And some with head or throat with life-blood dyed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>Advancing, he on earth saw many more,<br />
Or rather in a lake of crimson laid,<br />
Horribly weltering in their own dark gore,<br />
Beyond the leech’s and magician’s aid;<br />
And busts dissevered from the heads they bore,<br />
And legs and arms—a cruel show—surveyed;<br />
And, from the first cantonments to the last,<br />
Saw slaughtered men on all sides as he past.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>Where the small band advances in such wise,<br />
Deserving well eternal praise to gain,<br />
Vouching their deeds, a long-drawn furrow lies,<br />
A signal record of their might and main.<br />
His army’s cruel slaughter, with surprise,<br />
Anger and rage, is viewed by Charlemagne.<br />
So he whose shattered walls have felt its force,<br />
Throughout his mansion tracks the lightning’s course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>Not to the ramparts of the paynim crew<br />
Of Agramant as yet had pierced this aid,<br />
When, on the further side, these other two,<br />
Rogero and Marphisa, thither made.<br />
When, once or twice, that worthy pair a view<br />
Have taken of the ground, and have surveyed<br />
The readiest way assistance to afford,<br />
They swiftly move in succour of their lord.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>As when we spark to loaded mine apply,<br />
Through the long furrow, filled with sable grain,<br />
So fast the furious wildfire darts, that eye<br />
Pursues the progress of the flash with pain;<br />
And as dire ruin follows, and from high,<br />
The loosened rock and solid bastion rain,<br />
So bold Rogero and Marphisa rush<br />
To battle, so the Christian squadrons crush.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>Front and askance, the assailants smote, and low<br />
On earth heads, arms, and severed shoulders lay,<br />
Where’er the Christian squadrons were too slow<br />
To free the path and break their close array.<br />
Whoe’er has seen the passing tempest blow,<br />
And of the hill or valley, in its way,<br />
One portion ravage and another leave,<br />
May so their course amid that host conceive.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>Many who had escaped by quick retreat,<br />
Rodomont and those other furious three,<br />
Thank God that he had given them legs and feet,<br />
Wherewith to fly from that calamity;<br />
And from the Child and damsel new defeat<br />
Encounter, while with endlong course they flee:<br />
As man, no matter if he stands or run,<br />
Seeks vainly his predestined doom to shun.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>Who ’scape one peril, into other fly,<br />
And pay the penalty of flesh and blood;<br />
So, by the teeth of dog, is wont to die<br />
The fox, together with her infant brood,<br />
By one who dwells her ancient cavern nigh<br />
Unearthed, and with a thousand blows pursued;<br />
When from some unsuspected place, that foe<br />
Has filled with fire and smoke the den below.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>Marphisa and the Child, of danger clear,<br />
Enter the paynim ramparts; and, with eyes<br />
Upturned, the Saracens, with humble cheer,<br />
Thank Heaven for the success of that emprize:<br />
The paladins no longer are their fear;<br />
The meanest Moor a hundred Franks defies;<br />
And ’tis resolved, without repose, again<br />
To drench with Christian blood the thirsty plain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>At once a formidable larum rose;<br />
Horns, drums, and shrilling clarions filled the skies;<br />
And the wind ruffles, as it comes and goes,<br />
Banner and gonfalon of various dyes.<br />
The Germans and the warlike Bretons close;<br />
Ranged on the other part, in martial wise,<br />
Italians, English, French, were seen, and through<br />
Those armies furious war blazed forth anew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>The force of the redoubted Rodomont,<br />
And that of Agrican’s infuriate son,<br />
That of Rogero, valiant’s copious font,<br />
Gradasso’s, so renowned for trophies won,<br />
The martial maid, Marphisa’s fearless front,<br />
And might of Sacripant, excelled by none,<br />
Made Charles upon Saint John and Denys call,<br />
And fly for shelter to his Paris wall.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>Of fierce Marphisa and her bold allies<br />
The unconquered daring and the wondrous might,<br />
Sir, was not of a nature—of a guise—<br />
To be conceived, much less described aright:<br />
The number slaughtered hence may you surmise!<br />
What cruel blow King Charles sustained in fight!<br />
Add to these warriors of illustrious name,<br />
More than one Moor, with Ferrau, known to Fame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>Many through reckless haste were drowned in Seine,<br />
For all too narrow was the bridge’s floor,<br />
An wished, like Icarus, for wings in vain,<br />
Having grim death behind them and before,<br />
Save Oliver, and Ogier hight the Dane,<br />
The paladins are prisoners to the Moor:<br />
Wounded beneath his better shoulder fled<br />
The first, that other with a broken head.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>And. like Orlando and Duke Aymon’s son,<br />
Had faithful Brandimart thrown up the game,<br />
Charles had from Paris into exile gone,<br />
If he had scaped alive so fierce a flame.<br />
Brandimart does his best, and when ’tis done,<br />
Yields to the storm: Thus Fortune, fickle dame,<br />
Now smiles upon the paynim monarch, who<br />
Besieges royal Charlemagne anew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>From earth beneath the widow’s outcry swells,<br />
Mingled with elder’s and with orphan’s prayer,<br />
Into the pure serene, where Michael dwells,<br />
Rising above this dim and troubled air;<br />
And to the blest archangel loudly tells,<br />
How the devouring wolf and raven tear<br />
His faithful English, French, and German train,<br />
Whose slaughtered bodies overspread the plain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>Red blushed the blessed angel, who believed<br />
He ill obedience to his lord had paid;<br />
And, in his anger, deemed himself deceived<br />
By the perfidious Discord and betrayed:<br />
He his Creator’s order had received<br />
To stir the Moors to strife, nor had obeyed;<br />
Had rather in their eyes who marked the event,<br />
Appeared throughout to thwart his high intent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>As servant faithful to his lord, and more<br />
In love than memory strong, who finds that he<br />
Has that forgotten which at his heart-core,<br />
As precious as his life and soul should be,<br />
Hastes to repair his error, nor before<br />
He mend that fault, again his lord will see,<br />
So not to God St. Michael will ascend<br />
Until he has achieved his holy end.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>Again he to that monastery flew,<br />
Where whilom he had Discord seen; and there<br />
Seated in chapter sees her, while anew<br />
Their yearly officers elected are,<br />
She taking huge delight those friers to view,<br />
That at each other hurled their books of prayer.<br />
His hand within her locks the archangel twists,<br />
And deals her endless scathe with feet and fists.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>On her he next a cross’s handle broke;<br />
Wherewith her back, and arms, and head he plies:<br />
His mercy with loud voice the wretch bespoke,<br />
And hugged that angel’s knees with suppliant cries.<br />
Michael suspends not the avenging stroke<br />
Till hunted to the Moorish camp she flies,<br />
Then thus: “Believe worse vengeance yet in store,<br />
If I beyond these lines behold thee more.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>Albeit in back and arms all over shent<br />
Was Discord by that angel, in her fear<br />
Of suffering yet again such chastisement,<br />
Such horrid fury and such blows severe,<br />
She speedily to take her bellows went,<br />
And, adding food to what she lit whilere,<br />
And setting other ready piles afire,<br />
Kindled in many hearts a blaze of ire;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>And good Rogero (she inflames them so)<br />
With Rodomont and Mandricardo fares<br />
To Agramant; and all (since now the foe<br />
The paynims pressed no more, the vantage theirs)<br />
To him the seed of their dissensions show,<br />
And what the bitter produce which it bears:<br />
Then to the judgment of the king refer<br />
Who first in listed field his claim should stir.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>As well Marphisa to Troyano’s son,<br />
Relates her case, and will conclude the fray<br />
Which with the Tartar king she had begun,<br />
Because by him provoked to that assay;<br />
Nor will she yield her place to any one,<br />
No, not a single hour, yet less a day;<br />
But with loud instances maintains her right<br />
With Mandricardo first to wage the fight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>To have the first possession of the field<br />
No less renowned king Rodomont contended,<br />
Which he, the African array to shield,<br />
Had interrupted and till now suspended.<br />
Rogero to King Agramant appealed,<br />
As having borne too long, though sore offended,<br />
That Rodomont form him detained his horse,<br />
Nor yet would meet him first in martial course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>The Tartar king, for more perplexity,<br />
Denied on any ground Rogero’s right<br />
The bearer of the white-winged bird to be;<br />
And was so passing wood with wrath and spite,<br />
That, if to this those others would agree,<br />
He would at once those several quarrels fight;<br />
And so those others would as well have done,<br />
If Agramant’s consent they could have won.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>King Agramant, with prayer and kingly word,<br />
Had willingly appeased that jarring crew;<br />
But since the foes were deaf to all accord,<br />
Nor would assent to peace or truce anew,<br />
Considered how at least he might afford<br />
The field of each of them in order due;<br />
And, as the best resolve, at last decreed,<br />
Each should by lot possess the listed mead.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>Four lots the monarch bade prepare, which done,<br />
This “Rodomont and Mandricardo” said;<br />
“Rogero and Mandricardo” were in one;<br />
In one, “Rogero and Rodomont” were read;<br />
That “Mandricardo and Marphisa” run:<br />
Next, as the fickle goddess, Fortune, led,<br />
The lots are drawn, and in the first appear<br />
The Tartar king and sovereign of Argier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>Rogero and Mandricardo for that play<br />
Were next; Rogero and Rodomont were third;<br />
Marphisa’s lot and Mardricardo’s lay<br />
At bottom; whence the dame was deeply stirred;<br />
Nor young Rogero seems a whit more gay:<br />
Who knows the prowess of those two preferred<br />
Will nothing in the listed combat leave<br />
For him or for Marphisa to achieve.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>There lies a place, of Paris little wide,<br />
Covering a mile or somewhat less, and round;<br />
Like ancient theatre, on every side,<br />
Encompast by a tall and solid mound;<br />
With castle whilom was it fortified,<br />
Which sword and fire had levelled with the ground.<br />
The Parmesan like circle does survey,<br />
Whenever he to Borgo wends his way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>In this place is prepared the listed mead,<br />
Which palisades of little height inclose;<br />
A square, of just proportions for that need,<br />
With two capacious gates, as usage goes.<br />
The day on which to combat have agreed<br />
Those valiant knights, who will not balk their foes,<br />
Beside the palisades, to left and right,<br />
Facing each entrance, are pavilions pight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>In that, which looks towards the western sun,<br />
Is lodged the giant monarch of Argier;<br />
And him assist his serpent-hide to don<br />
Bold Ferrau and Circassia’s cavalier.<br />
Gradasso and the puissant Falsiron,<br />
In that which fronts the morning hemisphere,<br />
Clothe with their hands, in Trojan plate and chain,<br />
The good successor of King Agricane.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>High on a throne of ample state appeared<br />
Agramant and Marsilius; next in place<br />
Were Stordilane and all the chiefs, revered<br />
Throughout the squadrons of the paynim race.<br />
Happy was he who found himself upreared<br />
On mound or tree, above that level space.<br />
Great was the throng, and round the palisade<br />
On every side the eddying people swayed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>Were seated with the Queen of fair Castille<br />
Queens, princesses, and dames of noble strain,<br />
From Arragon, Granada, and Seville,<br />
And Atlas’ columns; and amid the train<br />
Assembled to behold that fierce appeal,<br />
Was placed the daughter of King Stordilane:<br />
Two costly vests—one red, one green—she wore;<br />
But ill the first was dyed, and faded sore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>In dress succinct Marphisa sate; in plight<br />
Such as beseemed a warrior and a maid:<br />
Thermodoon haply witnessed Hippolyte<br />
And her fair squadron in like garb arrayed.<br />
Afield already, in his livery dight,<br />
Agramant’s herald made proclaim, and said<br />
It was forbid to all men, far and wide,<br />
In act or word, with either part to side.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>The frequent crowd expects the double foe;<br />
And often, in impatience, they complain,<br />
And call those famous cavaliers too slow:<br />
When from the Tartar’s tent an angry strain<br />
Is heard, and cries which multiply; sir, know<br />
It was the martial king of Sericane,<br />
And puissant Tartar, who that question stirred,<br />
And made the mighty tumult which has heard.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>Sericane’s monarch, having with his hand<br />
Equipt the king of Tartary all o’er,<br />
Approached to gird him with that sovereign brand,<br />
With which Orlando went adorned of yore.<br />
When Durindana on the hilt he scanned,<br />
Graved with the quartering that Almontes wore;<br />
Which from that wretched man, beside a font,<br />
Youthful Orlando reft in Aspramont.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>He, seeing this, agnised it for the blade<br />
So famous, which Anglantes’ warrior bore,<br />
For which he had the fairest fleet arrayed<br />
Which ever put to sea from eastern shore;<br />
And had Castille’s rich kingdom overlaid,<br />
And conquered fruitful France some years before;<br />
But cannot now imagine how that sword<br />
Is in possession of the Tartar lord;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>And asks had he by force or treaty won,<br />
And when and where and how, that faulchion bright;<br />
And Mandricardo said that he had done<br />
Fierce battle for that sword with Brava’s knight;<br />
Who feigned himself of sober sense foregone,<br />
Hoping that so he should conceal his fright:<br />
—“For I on him would ceaseless war have made,”<br />
(He added) “while he kept the goodly blade.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>Saying the Count, in yielding to his foe<br />
That sword, the Beavers’ known device had tried;<br />
Who. followed closely by the hunter, know<br />
Their fell pursuer covers nought beside.<br />
Ere he had heard him out,—“Nor I forego<br />
That sword to thee nor any one,” (replied<br />
Gradasso, fierce,) “well earned by me, at cost<br />
Of treasure, and of pain, and people lost.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>“Some other faulchion for thyself purvey;<br />
This will I have; nor deem my reasons new;<br />
Whether Orlando wise or foolish stray,<br />
I make it mine where’er it meets my view.<br />
With none to witness, thou, beside the way<br />
Usurped that sword; I claim it as my due:<br />
For this my scimeter shall reasons yield,<br />
And we will try the cause in listed field.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>“Prepare to win the sword before thou rear<br />
That goodly blade against King Rodomont.<br />
To win his arms is use of cavalier,<br />
Before his foe in duel he affront.”<br />
—“No sweeter music ever soothes my ear”<br />
(Replied the Tartar, as he raised his front)<br />
“Than voice which champions me to martial field;<br />
But see that his consent the Sarzan yield.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>“Be thou the first; and, next on listed ground<br />
Let Sarza’s valiant lord the question try;<br />
Nor doubt but I in readiness be found<br />
To thee and every other to reply.”<br />
“—Thou shalt not so the ordered lots confound,<br />
Or break our compact (was Rogero’s cry):<br />
Either, first Rodomont shall take the field,<br />
Or shall to me his right of battle yield.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>“It that be true Gradasso has averred,<br />
That knight should win the arms he would assay,<br />
Thou hast no title to my white-winged bird,<br />
Save this from me thou first shalt bear away.<br />
But since, forsooth, whilere I said the word,<br />
I will not what I once pronounced unsay,<br />
That mine shall be the second battle, so<br />
That Argier’s monarch first affront his foe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>“I will confuse the order of the field,<br />
Throughout, if partially confused by thee;<br />
Abandon will I not my blazoned shield,<br />
Unless thou combat for it now with me.”<br />
—“Were one and the other Mars, for battle steeled,<br />
(Replies enraged, the king of Tartary)<br />
“Nor one nor the other’s might should make me waive<br />
My title to that shield and goodly glaive”;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>And over mastered by his choler, flies<br />
With a clenched fist at him of Sericane,<br />
And smites him with his right-hand in such wise,<br />
As makes him quit his hold of Durindane.<br />
Gradasso bold was taken by surprise,<br />
Not deeming him so furious and insane;<br />
And, while he looked not to the Tartar lord,<br />
Found himself robbed of good Orlando’s sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>Fury and scorn Gradasso’s visage heats,<br />
Which seems to flash with fire, at that disgrace;<br />
And with more rage and pain his bosom beats,<br />
In that ’twas offered in such public place.<br />
To draw his scimeter, the king retreats,<br />
Intent upon revenge, some little space.<br />
So Mandricardo on himself relies<br />
Rogero he to fight, as well defies.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>“Come on in arms against me, both combined,<br />
And be King Rodomont the third!” (he said)<br />
“Come Spain and Afric and all human kind;<br />
Ne’er will I turn.” And he, at nought dismaid,<br />
So saying, in his fury, sawed the wind<br />
About him, with Almontes’ noble blade,<br />
Embraced his shield, and, full of choler, stood<br />
Against Gradasso and Rogero good.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>“Leave me the care,” the fierce Gradasso cried,<br />
“The phrensy of this madman to subdue.”<br />
—“Not so, by Heaven!” Rogero wroth replied,<br />
“For I this field claim justly as my due.”<br />
—“Stand back!” and “stand thou back!” on either side<br />
They shout; yet neither of the twain withdrew.<br />
And thus among those three began a feud;<br />
And thence some strange result would have ensued,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>If many had not interposed, and sought<br />
With little wit their fury to restrain;<br />
Who had well-nigh too dear the experience bought<br />
Of saving others at their proper pain;<br />
Nor to accord the world had ever brought<br />
Those knights, but that the worthy king of Spain<br />
Came thither with renowned Troyano’s heir;<br />
Awed by whose sovereign presence all forbear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>Agramant those contending warriors made<br />
The cause of their so burning strife display;<br />
Next earnestly bestirred himself, and prayed<br />
Gradasso that he would, in courteous way,<br />
Concede the Trojan Hector’s goodly blade<br />
To Mandricardo, solely for that day,<br />
Until the cruel fight was at an end,<br />
Wherein he should with Rodomont contend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>While royal Agramant would peace restore,<br />
And now with this and now with that conferred,<br />
From the other tent, between the Sarzan Moor<br />
And Sacripant, another strife was heard.<br />
Valiant King Sacripant (as said before)<br />
To equip Sir Rodomont himself bestirred,<br />
And he and Ferrau had that champion drest<br />
In his forefather Nimrod’s iron vest;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>And there had they arrived, where with his spume<br />
The horse was making his rich bridle white:<br />
I of the good Frontino speak, for whom<br />
Rogero urged with yet unfelt despite.<br />
King Sacripant, who plays the part of groom,<br />
And has to bring afield the Sarzan knight,<br />
Marks narrowly the courser’s gear and shoes,<br />
And sell and furniture throughout reviews;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>And as his points and nimble parts, more near,<br />
He, in this view, observes with better heed,<br />
The youthful king, beyond all doubt, is clear<br />
He sees his Frontilatte in that steed,<br />
Him he of old had held so passing dear,<br />
Whilom of such debates the fruitful seed;<br />
And for whose loss, whilere he was so woe,<br />
He evermore on foot resolved to go.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>This from beneath him had Brunello borne<br />
Before Albracca, on the very day<br />
Angelica’s rare ring, and Roland’s horn,<br />
And Balisarda he conveyed away,<br />
With fierce Marphisa’s blade,—and on return<br />
To Afric—to Rogero, from his prey,<br />
Gave Balisarda and the courser, who<br />
Was by the Child Frontino named anew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>Assured ’twas no mistake, Circassia’s chief<br />
Turned him about to Rodomont, and cried:<br />
“Reft from me in Albracca, by a thief,<br />
This horse is mine; which might be certified<br />
By them whose words would warrant well belief:<br />
But as my witnesses are distant wide,<br />
If it be questioned, I will make it plain,<br />
And will, with sword in hand, the truth maintain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>“Yet am I well contented, for that we<br />
Have for these some few days together gone,<br />
To lend him for to-day; since well I see,<br />
That not without him could the fight be done;<br />
But on condition, that the courser be<br />
Acknowledged mine, and furnished as a loan:<br />
Otherwise hope not for that horse, save first<br />
Me, on this quarrel, thou in combat worst.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>The furious king of Argier, that in pride<br />
Surpassed all knights that ever girt the sword,<br />
Whose paragon, for heart and prowess tried,<br />
Meseems no ancient histories record,<br />
Cried: “Sacripant, if any one beside<br />
Thyself, to me should utter such a word,<br />
He should deem quickly, from its bitter fruit,<br />
He from his birth would better have been mute.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>“But, for that fellowship in which we went,<br />
(As thou hast said) together, I to show<br />
Such patience and forbearance am content,<br />
As warning thee, thy purpose to forego,<br />
Until thou shalt have witnessed the event<br />
Of strife between me and my Tartar foe:<br />
When him I such example hope to make,<br />
That thou shalt humbly say, ‘The courser take.’ “</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>Fierce and enraged, replied Circassia’s peer,<br />
“To play the churl with thee is courteous deed,<br />
But I to thee repeat more plain and clear,<br />
Thou ill wouldst aught design against that steed,<br />
For, while I an avenging sabre rear,<br />
This I prohibit thee, and, should it need,<br />
And every better means of battle fail,<br />
With thee for this would battle, tooth and nail.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>They from dispute proceed to ribaldry,<br />
From words to blows; and through their mickle ire,<br />
Fierce battle was inflamed, and blazed more high<br />
Than ever lightly-kindled straw took fire.<br />
King Rodomont is steeled in panoply;<br />
Sacripant neither plate nor mail attire:<br />
Yet so in fence is skilled that nimble lord,<br />
He seems all over sheltered by his sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>No greater were the daring and the might<br />
(Though infinite) which Rodomont displaid<br />
Than the precaution and the nimble sleight<br />
Which the Circassian summoned to his aid:<br />
No mill-wheel ever turns with swifter flight<br />
The circling stone by which the grain is brayed,<br />
Than Sacripant at need moves foot or hand,<br />
And shifts now here, now there his restless stand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>But Serpentine and Ferrau interfere:<br />
They with drawn swords the twain asunder bore;<br />
With them Grandonio was and Isolier,<br />
And many other leaders of the Moor,<br />
This was the tumult which was heard whilere<br />
In the other tent, what time they laboured sore,<br />
Rogero vainly to a peace to bring<br />
With Tartary’s and Sericana’s king.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>This while some voice to Agramant the news<br />
Reports aright, that Ulien’s might seed,<br />
With Sacripant, Circassia’s king, pursues<br />
A fierce and furious quarrel for the steed.<br />
Agramant, whom so many jars confuse,<br />
Exclaims to King Marsilius: “Take thou heed<br />
That no worse evil mid these knights betide,<br />
While for this new disorder I provide.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>Rodomont reined his anger, and retired<br />
Some deal, at his approaching sovereign’s view;<br />
Nor less respect in Sacripant inspired<br />
The Moorish monarch; of the furious two,<br />
He with grave voice and royal mien inquired<br />
What cause of strife such deadly discord blew;<br />
And having searched their quarrel to the root,<br />
Would fain accord them; but with little fruit.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>Circassia’s monarch would not, on his side,<br />
Longer his horse to Argier’s lord allow,<br />
Save humbly Rodomont to him applied,<br />
That steed for this occasion to bestow.<br />
To him Sir Rodomont, with wonted pride,<br />
Returned for answer: “Neither Heaven nor thou<br />
Shall make me recognize as gift or loan<br />
What I with this good hand can make mine own.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>
<td>The king bade Sacripant explain his right,<br />
And how that horse was taken from him sought;<br />
And this from first to last Circassia’s knight<br />
Rehearsed, and reddened as the tale he taught,<br />
Relating to the king the robber’s sleight;<br />
Who had surprised him overwhelmed with thought,<br />
Upon four spears his courser’s saddle stayed,<br />
And from beneath the naked horse conveyed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXV.</td>
<td>Marphisa, whom these cries, mid others, bring,<br />
When of the robbery of the horse advised,<br />
In visage is disturbed, remembering<br />
How on that day her faulchion was surprised;<br />
And when that courser (which equipt with wing<br />
Appeared when flying her) she recognized;<br />
And recognized as well—at first unknown—<br />
The valiant king who filled Circassia’s throne.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVI.</td>
<td>The others who stood round her, wont to hear<br />
Brunello often boast of the deceit,<br />
’Gan turn towards that wretch, and made appear<br />
By open signs they knew him for the Cheat.<br />
Marphisa who the subtle knave whilere<br />
Suspected as the author of that feat,<br />
Now questions this, now that, who all accord<br />
In saying ’twas Brunello stole her sword;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVII.</td>
<td>Who, well deserving as a fitting pain<br />
To dangle from the gallows-tree in air,<br />
By Agramant the crown of Tingitane<br />
(An ill example) was preferred to wear.<br />
This fires anew Marphisa’s old disdain,<br />
Nor she from instant vengeance will forbear,<br />
For this, as well as other shame and scorn<br />
She on her road had from that caitiff born.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXVIII.</td>
<td>A squire laced on her helmet, at her hest;<br />
She wore the remnant of her armour sheen;<br />
Nor without martial cuirass on her breast,<br />
Find I, that she ten times was ever seen,<br />
Even from the day when first that iron vest<br />
Braced on her limbs the passing-valiant queen:<br />
With helm on head, where, mid the highest rows,<br />
Brunello sits among the first, she goes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIX.</td>
<td>Him by mid breast Marphisa griped amain,<br />
And lifted up the losel from the ground;<br />
As is rapacious eagle wont to strain<br />
The pullet, in her talons circled round;<br />
And bore him where the sons of King Troyane<br />
Heard the two knights their jarring claims propound.<br />
He who perceives himself in evil hands,<br />
Aye weeps, and mercy of that maid demands.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XC.</td>
<td>Above the universal noise and shout,<br />
Which rose nigh equally on either side,<br />
Brunello, who from all the crowd about<br />
For pity now, and now for succour, cried,<br />
So loud was heard, that of that ample rout<br />
He gathered round himself the pressing tide.<br />
Arrived before the Moorish army’s head,<br />
To him with haughty mien Marphisa said:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCI.</td>
<td>“This thief (said she), thy vassal, will I slay,<br />
And with this hand of mine will knot the cord<br />
About his neck; because the very day<br />
He stole this courser, he purloined my sword.<br />
But is there any one who deems I say<br />
Amiss, let him stand forth and speak the word;<br />
For I on him will prove, before thine eyes,<br />
I have done right, and who gainsays me, lies.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCII.</td>
<td>“But because haply some one may pretend<br />
I have till such a time of strife delayed<br />
My vengeance, when such famous knights contend,<br />
For three days shall the wretch’s doom be stayed;<br />
In the mean time let him who would defend<br />
That caitiff, come himself, or send him aid.<br />
For afterwards, if none the deed prevent,<br />
His carcass shall a thousand birds content.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIII.</td>
<td>“I hence to yonder tower, which distant nigh<br />
Three leagues, o’erlooks a little copse, repair,<br />
But with one varlet in my company,<br />
And with one waiting-maid; if any dare<br />
Rescue the thief, let him come thither; I<br />
Wait the approach of his defenders there.”<br />
Thus she; and thither quickly wends her ways<br />
Whither was said, nor any answer stays.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIV.</td>
<td>Held on the pommel grappled by his hair,<br />
Brunello on Marphisa’s courser lies:<br />
The caitiff weeps, and shrieking in despair,<br />
On all in whom he hopes, for succour cries.<br />
In such confusion is Troyano’s heir,<br />
He sees no way through these perplexities;<br />
And, that Marphisa thence Brunello bore<br />
In such a guise, yet grieved the monarch more.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCV.</td>
<td>Not that he loved the losel or esteemed,<br />
Rather to him some time had borne despite;<br />
And often had to hand the caitiff schemed,<br />
Since he had forfeited the ring of might.<br />
But here his honour touched the monarch deemed,<br />
So that his visage reddened at the slight:<br />
He would, in person, follow her at speed,<br />
And to his utmost power avenge the deed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVI.</td>
<td>But the wise king, Sobrino, who was by,<br />
Him from the quest endeavoured to dissuade,<br />
And that with his exalted majesty<br />
Such enterprize were ill assorted said:<br />
Although firm hope, nay full security,<br />
He had to overcome that martial maid,<br />
If he with pain subdued a woman, shame,<br />
Rather than honour, would pursue his name.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVII.</td>
<td>Small profit and much peril would succeed<br />
From any fight he should with her maintain,<br />
(And he advised him) as the better deed,<br />
To leave that wretched caitiff to his pain;<br />
And albeit but a simple nod should need<br />
To free him, from that nod he should refrain.<br />
In that the monarch would do ill to force<br />
Even-handed Justice from her destined course.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCVIII.</td>
<td>“Thou to the fierce Marphisa may’st apply<br />
To leave his trial (he pursued) to thee,<br />
With promise, her in this to satisfy<br />
And to suspend him from the gallows-tree:<br />
And even should the maid thy prayer deny,<br />
Let her in every wish contented be:<br />
And rather than that she desert thy side,<br />
Let her hang him and every thief beside.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIX.</td>
<td>Right willingly King Agramant gave way<br />
To King Sobrino’s counsel sage and staid;<br />
And let renowned Marphisa wend her way,<br />
Nor scathed he, nor let scathe, that martial maid,<br />
Neither endured that any her should pray;<br />
And heaven knows with what courage he obeyed<br />
That wise advice, to calm such ruder strife<br />
And quarrel, as throughout his camp were rife.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">C.</td>
<td>At this mad Discord laughed, no more in fear<br />
That any truce or treaty should ensue;<br />
And scowered the place of combat there and here,<br />
Nor could stand still, for pleasure at the view.<br />
Pride gamboled and rejoiced with her compeer,<br />
And on the fire fresh food and fuel threw,<br />
And shouted so that Michael in the sky<br />
Knew the glad sign of conquest in that cry.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CI.</td>
<td>Paris-town rocked, and turbid ran the flood<br />
Of Seine at that loud voice, that horrid roar;<br />
And, so it echo rang in Arden’s wood,<br />
Beasts left their caverns in that forest hoar.<br />
Alp and Cevenne’s mountain-solitude,<br />
And Blois, and Arles, and Rouen’s distant shore,<br />
Rhine, Rhone, and Saone, and Garonne, heard the pest;<br />
Scared mothers hugged their children to their breast.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CII.</td>
<td>Five have set up their rest, resolved to be<br />
The first their different quarrels to conclude:<br />
And tangled so is one with other plea,<br />
That ill Apollo’s self could judge the feud.<br />
To unravel that first cause of enmity<br />
The king began—the strife which had ensued,<br />
Because of beauteous Doralice, between<br />
The king of Scythia and her Algerine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIII.</td>
<td>King Agramant oft moved, between the pair,<br />
Now here now there, to bring them to accord;<br />
Now there now here, admonishing that pair,<br />
Like faithful brother and like righteous lord:<br />
But when he found that neither would forbear,<br />
Deaf and rebellious to his royal word,<br />
Nor would consent that lady to forego,<br />
The cause of strife, in favour of his foe,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIV.</td>
<td>As his best lore, at length the monarch said,<br />
And to obey his sentence both were fain;<br />
That he who was by her preferred, should wed<br />
The beauteous daughter of King Stordilane:<br />
And that what was established on his head<br />
Should not be changed, to either’s loss or gain.<br />
The compromise was liked on either side,<br />
Since either hoped she would for him decide.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CV.</td>
<td>The mighty king of Sarza, who long space<br />
Before the Tartar, had loved Doralice,<br />
(Who had preferred that sovereign to such grace<br />
As modest lady may, nor do amiss)<br />
Believed, when she past sentence on the case,<br />
She must pronounce what would ensure his bliss.<br />
Nor thus alone King Rodomont conceived,<br />
But all the Moorish host with him believed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVI.</td>
<td>All know what exploits wrought by him had been<br />
For her in joust and war; they all unsound<br />
And weak King Mandricardo’s judgment ween;<br />
But he, who oft was with her on their round,<br />
And oftener private with the youthful queen,<br />
What time the tell-tale sun was under ground,<br />
He, knowing well how sure he was to speed,<br />
Laughed at the silly rabble’s idle creed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVII.</td>
<td>They, after, ratify the king’s award,<br />
Between his hands, and next the suitors twain<br />
Before that damsel go, that on the sward<br />
Fixing her downcast eyes, in modest vein,<br />
Avows her preference of the Tartar lord;<br />
At which sore wondering stand the paynim train;<br />
And Rodomont remains so sore astound,<br />
He cannot raise his visage from the ground.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVIII.</td>
<td>But wonted anger chasing shame which dyed<br />
The Sarzan’s face all over, he arraigned<br />
The damsel’s sentence, of the faulchion, tied<br />
About his manly waist, the handle strained,<br />
And in the king’s and others’ hearing cried:<br />
“By this the question shall be lost or gained;<br />
And not by faithless woman’s fickle thought,<br />
Which thither still inclines, where least it ought.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIX.</td>
<td>Kind Mandricardo on his feet once more,<br />
Exclaims, “And be it as it pleases thee.”<br />
So that ere yet the vessel made the shore<br />
Unploughed remained a mighty space of sea;<br />
But that this king reproved the Sarzan sore,<br />
Ruling that to appeal upon that plea<br />
No more with Mandricardo could avail,<br />
And made the moody Sarzan strike his sail.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CX.</td>
<td>Branded with double scorn, before those peers,<br />
By noble Agramant, whose sovereign sway<br />
He, as in loyal duty bound, reveres,<br />
And by his lady on the selfsame day,<br />
There will no more the monarch of Algiers<br />
Abide, but of his band—a large array—<br />
Two serjeants only for his service takes,<br />
And with that pair the paynim camp forsakes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXI.</td>
<td>As the afflicted bull who has foregone<br />
His heifer, nor can longer warfare wage,<br />
Seeks out the greenwood-holt and stream most lone,<br />
Or sands at distance from his pasturage;<br />
There ceases not, in sun or shade to moan;<br />
Yet not for that exhales his amorous rage:<br />
So parts, constrained his lady to forego,<br />
The king of Argier, overwhelmed with woe.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXII.</td>
<td>Rogero moved, his courser to regain,<br />
And had already donned his warlike gear,<br />
Then recollecting, that on listed plain<br />
At Mandricardo he must couch the spear,<br />
Followed not Rodomont, but turned his rein,<br />
To end his quarrel with the Tartar, ere<br />
He met in combat Sericana’s lord<br />
Within close barriers, for Orlando’s sword.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIII.</td>
<td>To have Frontino ravished in his sight,<br />
And be unable to forbid the deed,<br />
He sorely grieves; but, when he shall that fight<br />
Have done, resolves he will regain the steed;<br />
But Sacripant, whom, like the youthful knight,<br />
No quarrels in the Moor’s pursuit impede,<br />
And who was unengaged in other quest,<br />
Upon the Sarzan’s footsteps quickly prest;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIV.</td>
<td>And would have quickly joined him that was gone,<br />
But for the chance of an adventure rare;<br />
Which him detained until the day was done,<br />
And made him lose the track of Ulien’s heir:<br />
A woman who had fallen into the Saone,<br />
And who without his help had perished there,<br />
The warrior drowning in that water found,<br />
And stemmed the stream and dragged the dame aground.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXV.</td>
<td>When afterwards he would remount the sell,<br />
From him his restless charger broke astray,<br />
Who fled before his lord till evening fell,<br />
Nor lightly did the king that courser stay.<br />
At last he caught him; but no more could spell<br />
Where he had wandered from the beaten way:<br />
Two hundred miles he roved, ’twixt hill and plain,<br />
Ere he came up with Rodomont again.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVI.</td>
<td>How he by Sacripant was overtaken,<br />
And fought by him, to his discomfit sore,<br />
And how he lost his courser, how was taken,<br />
I say not now, who have to say before,<br />
With what disdain and with what anger shaken,<br />
Against his liege and love, the Sarzan Moor<br />
Forth from the Saracen cantonments sped,<br />
And what he of the one and other said.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVII.</td>
<td>Wherever that afflicted paynim goes,<br />
He fills the kindling air with sighs that burn;<br />
And Echo oft, for pity of his woes,<br />
With him from hollow rock is heard to mourn:<br />
“O female mind! how lightly ebbs and flows<br />
Your fickle mood,” (he cries,) “aye prone to turn!<br />
Object most opposite to kindly faith!<br />
Lost, wretched man, who trusts you to his scathe!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVIII.</td>
<td>“Neither my love nor length of servitude,<br />
Though by a thousand proofs to you made clear,<br />
Had power even so to fix your faithless mood,<br />
That you at least so lightly should not veer:<br />
Nor am I quitted, because less endued<br />
With worth than Mandricardo I appear;<br />
Nor for your conduct cause can I declare,<br />
Save this alone, that you a woman are.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIX.</td>
<td>“I think that nature and an angry God<br />
Produced thee to the world, thou wicked sex,<br />
To be to man a plague, a chastening rod;<br />
Happy, wert thou not present to perplex.<br />
So serpent creeps along the grassy sod;<br />
So bear and ravening wolf the forest vex;<br />
Wasp, fly, and gad-fly buzz in liquid air,<br />
And the rich grain lies tangled with the tare.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXX.</td>
<td>“Why has not bounteous Nature willed that man<br />
Should be produced without the aid of thee,<br />
As we the pippin, pear, and service can<br />
Engraft by art on one another’s tree?<br />
But she directs not all by certain plan;<br />
Rather, upon a nearer view, I see,<br />
In naming her, she ill can act aright,<br />
Since Nature is herself a female hight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXI.</td>
<td>“Yet be not therefore proud and full of scorn<br />
Women, because man issues from your seed;<br />
For roses also blossom on the thorn,<br />
And the fair lily springs from loathsome weed.<br />
Despiteous, proud, importunate, and lorn<br />
Of love, of faith, of counsel, rash in deed,<br />
With that, ungrateful, cruel and perverse,<br />
And born to be the world’s eternal curse!”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXII.</td>
<td>These plaints and countless others to the wind<br />
Poured forth the paynim knight, to fury stirred;<br />
Now easing in low tone his troubled mind,<br />
And now in sounds which were at distance heard,<br />
In shame and in reproach of womankind;<br />
Yet certes he from sober reason erred:<br />
For we may deem a hundred good abound,<br />
Where one or two perchance are evil found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIII.</td>
<td>Though none for whom I hitherto have sighed<br />
—Of those so many—have kept faith with me,<br />
All with ingratitude, or falsehood dyed<br />
I deem not, I accuse my destiny.<br />
Many there are, and have been more beside<br />
Unmeriting reproach: but if there be,<br />
’Mid hundreds, one or two of evil way,<br />
My fortune wills that I should be their prey.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIV.</td>
<td>Yet will I make such search before I die,<br />
Rather before my hair shall wax more white,<br />
That haply on some future day, even I<br />
Shall say, “That one has kept her promise plight.”<br />
And should not the event my trust belie,<br />
(Nor am I hopeless) I with all my might<br />
Will with unwearied pain her praise rehearse<br />
With pen and ink and voice, in prose and verse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXV.</td>
<td>The Saracen, whom rage no less profound<br />
Against his sovereign lord than lady swayed,<br />
And who of reason thus o’erpast the bound,<br />
And ill of one and of the other said,<br />
Would fain behold that monarch’s kingdom drowned<br />
With such a tempest, with such scathe o’erlaid,<br />
As should in Africk every house aggrieve,<br />
Nor one stone standing on another leave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVI.</td>
<td>And would that from his realm, in want and woe,<br />
King Agramant a mendicant should wend;<br />
That through his means the monarch, brought thus low,<br />
His fathers’ ancient seat might reascend:<br />
And thus he might the fruit of fealty show,<br />
And make his sovereign see, a real friend<br />
Was aye to be preferred in wrong or right,<br />
Although the world against him should unite;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVII.</td>
<td>And thus the Saracen pours forth his moan,<br />
With rage against his liege and love possest;<br />
And on his way is by long journeys gone,<br />
Giving himself and courser little rest.<br />
The following day or next, upon the Saone<br />
He finds himself, who has his course addrest<br />
Towards the coast of Provence, with design<br />
To his African domain to cross the brine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVIII.</td>
<td>From bank to bank the stream was covered o’er<br />
With boat of little burden, which conveyed,<br />
For the supply of the invading Moor,<br />
Victual, from many places round purveyed:<br />
Since even from Paris to the pleasant shore<br />
Of Acquamorta, all his rule obeyed;<br />
And—fronting Spain—whate’er of level land<br />
Was seen, extending on the better hand.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIX.</td>
<td>The victual, disembarked from loaded barge,<br />
Was laid on sumpter-horse or ready wain;<br />
And sent, with escort to protect the charge,<br />
Where barges could not come; about the plain,<br />
Fat herds were feeding on the double marge,<br />
Brought thither from the march of either reign;<br />
And, by the river-side, at close of day,<br />
In different homesteads lodged, the drovers lay.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXX.</td>
<td>The king of Argier (for the dusky air<br />
Of night began upon the world to close)<br />
Here listened to a village-landlord’s prayer,<br />
That in his inn besought him to repose.<br />
—His courser stalled—the board with plenteous fare<br />
Is heaped, and Corsic wine and Grecian flows;<br />
For, in all else a Moor, the Sarzan drank<br />
Of the forbidden vintage like a Frank.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXI.</td>
<td>To warlike Rodomont, with goodly cheer<br />
And kindlier mien, the landlord honour paid;<br />
For he the port of an illustrious peer<br />
In his guest’s lofty presence saw pourtrayed.<br />
But, sore beside himself, the cavalier<br />
Had scarce his heart within him, which had strayed<br />
To her—whilere his own—in his despite;<br />
Nor word escaped the melancholy knight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXII.</td>
<td>Mine host, most diligent in his vocation<br />
Of all the trade who throughout France were known,<br />
(In that he had, ’mid strange and hostile nation,<br />
And every chance of warfare, kept his own)<br />
—Prompt to assist him in his occupation,<br />
Some of his kin had called; whereof was none<br />
Who dared before the warrior speak of aught,<br />
Seeing that paynim mute and lost in thought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIII.</td>
<td>From thought to thought the Sarzan’s fancy flies,<br />
Himself removed from thence a mighty space,<br />
Who sits so bent, and with such downcast eyes,<br />
He never once looks any in the face.<br />
Next, after silence long, and many sighs,<br />
As if deep slumber had but then given place,<br />
His spirits he recalls, his eyelids raises,<br />
And on the family and landlord gazes.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIV.</td>
<td>Then silence broke, and with a milder air,<br />
And visage somewhat less disturbed, applied<br />
To him, the host, and those by-standers there,<br />
To know if any to a wife were tied;<br />
And landlord and attendants,—that all were,<br />
To Sarza’s moody cavalier replied:<br />
He asked what each conceited of his spouse,<br />
And if he deemed her faithful to her vows.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXV.</td>
<td>Except mine host, those others were agreed<br />
That chaste and good their consorts they believed.<br />
—“Think each man as he will, but well I read,”<br />
(The landlord said,) “You fondly are deceived:<br />
Your rash replies to one conclusion lead,<br />
That you are all of common sense bereaved;<br />
And so too must believe this noble knight,<br />
Unless he would persuade us black is white.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVI.</td>
<td>“Because, as single is that precious bird<br />
The phoenix, and on earth there is but one,<br />
So, in this ample world, it is averred,<br />
One only can a woman’s treason shun.<br />
Each hopes alike to be that wight preferred,<br />
The victor who that single palm has won.<br />
—How is it possible that what can fall<br />
To one alone, should be the lot of all?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVII.</td>
<td>“Erewhile I made the same mistake as you,<br />
And that more dames than one were virtuous thought,<br />
Until a gentleman of Venice, who,<br />
For my good fortune, to this inn was brought,<br />
My ignorance by his examples true<br />
So ably schooled, he better wisdom taught.<br />
Valerio was the name that stranger bore;<br />
A name I shall remember evermore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVIII.</td>
<td>“Of wives and mistresses the treachery<br />
Was known to him, with all their cunning lore.<br />
He, both from old and modern history,<br />
And from his own, was ready with such store,<br />
As plainly showed that none to modesty<br />
Could make pretension, whether rich or poor;<br />
And that, if one appeared of purer strain,<br />
’Twas that she better hid her wanton vein.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIX.</td>
<td>“He of his many tales, among the rest,<br />
(Whereof a third is from my memory gone)<br />
So well one story in my head imprest,<br />
It could not be more firmly graved in stone:<br />
And what I thought and think, would be professed<br />
For that ill sex, I ween by every one<br />
Who heard; and, Sir—if pleased to lend an ear—<br />
To their confusion yon that tale shall hear.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXL.</td>
<td>“What could’st thou offer which could better please<br />
At present” (made reply the paynim knight)<br />
“Than sample, chosen from thine histories,<br />
Which hits the opinion that I hold, aright?<br />
That I may hear thee speak with better ease<br />
Sit so, that I may have thee in my sight.”<br />
But in the following canto I unfold<br />
What to King Rodomont the landlord told.</td>
</tr>
