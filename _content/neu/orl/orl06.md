<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>Ariodantes has, a worthy meed,<br />
With his loved bride, the fief of Albany.<br />
Meantime Rogero, on the flying steed,<br />
Arrives in false Alcina’s empery:<br />
There from a myrtle-tree her every deed,<br />
A human myrtle hears, and treachery,<br />
And thence would go; but they who first withdrew<br />
Him from one strife, engage him in a new.</em></span></td>

<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>W<span class="small">RETCHED</span> that evil man who lives in trust<br />
His secret sin is safe in his possession!<br />
Since, if nought else, the air, the very dust<br />
In which the crime is buried, makes confession,<br />
And oftentimes his guilt compels the unjust,<br />
Though sometime unarraigned in worldly session,<br />
To be his own accuser, and bewray,<br />
So God has willed, deeds hidden from the day.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>The unhappy Polinesso hopes had nursed,<br />
Wholly his secret treason to conceal.<br />
By taking off Dalinda, who was versed<br />
In this, and only could the fact reveal;<br />
And adding thus a second to his first<br />
Offence, but hurried on the dread appeal,<br />
Which haply he had stunned, at least deferred;<br />
But he to self-destruction blindly spurred.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>And forfeited estate, and life, and love<br />
Of friends at once, and honour, which was more.<br />
The cavalier unknown, I said above,<br />
Long of the king and court entreated sore,<br />
At length the covering helmet did remove,<br />
And showed a visage often seen before,<br />
The cherished face of Ariodantes true,<br />
Of late lamented weeping Scotland through;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>Ariodantes, whom with tearful eye<br />
His brother and Geneura wept as dead,<br />
And king, and people, and nobility:<br />
Such light his goodness and his valour shed.<br />
The pilgrim therefore might appear to lie<br />
In what he of the missing warrior said.<br />
Yet was it true that from a headland, he<br />
Had seen him plunge into the foaming sea.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>But, as it oft befalls despairing wight,<br />
Who grisly Death desires till he appear;<br />
But loathes what he had sought, on nearer sight;<br />
So painful seems the cruel pass and drear.<br />
Thus, in the sea engulphed, the wretched knight,<br />
Repentant of his deed, was touched with fear;<br />
And, matchless both for spirit and for hand,<br />
Beat back the billows, and returned to land.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>And, now despising, as of folly bred,<br />
The fond desire which did to death impell,<br />
Thence, soaked and dripping wet, his way did tread,<br />
And halted at a hermit’s humble cell:<br />
And housed within the holy father’s shed,<br />
There secretly awhile designed to dwell;<br />
Till to his ears by rumour should be voiced,<br />
If his Geneura sorrowed or rejoiced.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>At first he heard that, through excess of woe,<br />
The miserable damsel well-nigh died:<br />
For so abroad the doleful tidings go,<br />
’Twas talked of in the island, far and wide:<br />
Far other proof than that deceitful show,<br />
Which to his cruel grief he thought he spied!<br />
And next against the fair Geneura heard<br />
Lurcanio to her sire his charge preferred:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>Nor for his brother felt less enmity<br />
Than was the love he lately bore the maid;<br />
For he too foul, and full of cruelty,<br />
Esteemed the deed, although for him essayed;<br />
And, hearing after, in her jeopardy,<br />
That none appeared to lend the damsel aid,<br />
Because so puissant was Lurcanio’s might,<br />
All dreaded an encounter with the knight,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>And that who well the youthful champion knew,<br />
Believed he was so wary and discreet,<br />
That, had what he related been untrue,<br />
He never would have risqued so rash a feat,<br />
—For this the greater part the fight eschew,<br />
Fearing in wrongful cause the knight to meet—<br />
Ariodantes (long his doubts are weighed)<br />
Will meet his brother in Geneura’s aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>“Alas! (he said) I cannot bear to see<br />
Thus by my cause the royal damsel die;<br />
My death too bitter and too dread would be,<br />
Did I, before my own, her death descry:<br />
For still my lady, my divinity<br />
She is;—the light and comfort of my eye.<br />
Her, right or wrong, I cannot choose but shield,<br />
And for her safety perish in the field.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“I know I choose the wrong, and be it so!<br />
And in the cause shall die: nor this would move;<br />
But that, alas! my death, as well I know,<br />
Will such a lovely dame’s destruction prove,<br />
To death I with one only comfort go,<br />
That, if her Polinesso bears her love,<br />
To her will manifestly be displayed,<br />
That hitherto he moves not in her aid.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“And me, so wronged by her, the maid shall view<br />
Encounter death in her defence; and he,<br />
My brother, who such flames of discord blew,<br />
Shall pay the debt of vengeance due to me.<br />
For well I ween to make Lurcanio rue<br />
(Informed of the event) his cruelty,<br />
Who will have thought to venge me with his brand,<br />
And will have slain me with his very hand.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>He, having this concluded in his thought,<br />
Made new provision of arms, steed, and shield;<br />
Black was the vest and buckler which he bought,<br />
Where green and yellow striped the sable field:<br />
By hazard found, with him a squire he brought,<br />
A stranger in that country; and, concealed<br />
(As is already told) the unhappy knight,<br />
Against his brother came, prepared for fight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>And yielding to his natural inclination,<br />
And at the suit of all his court beside,<br />
And mostly at Rinaldo’s instigation,<br />
Assigned the youth the damsel as his bride.<br />
Albany’s duchy, now in sequestration,<br />
Late Polinesso’s, who in duel died,<br />
Could not be forfeited in happier hour;<br />
Since this the monarch made his daughter’s dower.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>Rinaldo for Dalinda mercy won;<br />
Who from her fault’s due punishment went free.<br />
She, satiate of the world, (and this to shun,<br />
The damsel so had vowed) to God will flee:<br />
And hence, in Denmark’s land, to live a nun,<br />
Straight from her native Scotland sailed the sea.<br />
But it is time Rogero to pursue,<br />
Who on his courser posts the welkin through.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>Although Rogero is of constant mind,<br />
Not from his cheek the wonted hues depart.<br />
I ween that faster than a leaf i’ the wind<br />
Fluttered within his breast the stripling’s heart.<br />
All Europe’s region he had left behind<br />
In his swift course; and, issuing in that part,<br />
Passed by a mighty space, the southern sound<br />
Where great Alcides fixed the sailor’s bound.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>That hippogryph, huge fowl, and strange to sight,<br />
Bears off the warrior with such rapid wing,<br />
He would have distanced, in his airy flight,<br />
The thunder bearing bird of Æther’s king:<br />
Nor other living creature soars such height,<br />
Him in his mighty swiftness equalling.<br />
I scarce believe that bolt, or lightning flies,<br />
Or darts more swiftly from the parted skies.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>When the huge bird his pinions long had plied,<br />
In a straight line, without one stoop or bend,<br />
He, tired of air, with sweeping wheel and wide,<br />
Began upon an island to descend;<br />
Like that fair region, whither, long unspied<br />
Of him, her wayward mood did long offend,<br />
Whilom in vain, through strange and secret sluice,<br />
Passed under sea the Virgin Arethuse.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>A more delightful place, wherever hurled<br />
Through the whole air, Rogero had not found:<br />
And, had he ranged the universal world,<br />
Would not have seen a lovelier in his round,<br />
Than that, where, wheeling wide, the courser furled<br />
His spreading wings, and lighted on the ground,<br />
’Mid cultivated plain, delicious hill,<br />
Moist meadow, shady bank, and crystal rill.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>Small thickets, with the scented laurel gay,<br />
Cedar, and orange, full of fruit and flower,<br />
Myrtle and palm, with interwoven spray,<br />
Pleached in mixed modes, all lovely, form a bower;<br />
And, breaking with their shade the scorching ray,<br />
Make a cool shelter from the noontide hour.<br />
And nightingales among those branches wing<br />
Their flight, and safely amorous descants sing.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>Amid red roses and white lilies there,<br />
Which the soft breezes freshen as they fly,<br />
Secure the cony haunts, and timid hare,<br />
And stag, with branching forehead broad and high.<br />
These, fearless of the hunter’s dart or snare,<br />
Feed at their ease, or ruminating lie;<br />
While, swarming in those wilds, from tuft or steep<br />
Dun deer or nimble goat, disporting, leap.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>When the hyppogryph above the island hung,<br />
And had approached so nigh that landscape fair,<br />
That, if his rider from the saddle sprung,<br />
He might the leap with little danger dare,<br />
Rogero lit the grass and flowers among,<br />
But held him, lest he should remount the air;<br />
And to a myrtle, nigh the rolling brine,<br />
Made fast, between a bay-tree and a pine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>And there, close-by where rose a bubbling fount,<br />
Begirt the fertile palm and cedar-tree,<br />
He drops the shield, the helmet from his front<br />
Uplifts, and, either hand from gauntlet free,<br />
Now turning to the beach, and now the mount,<br />
Catches the gales which blow from hill or sea,<br />
And, with a joyous murmur, lightly stir<br />
The lofty top of beech, or feathery fir:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>And, now, to bathe his burning lips he strains;<br />
Now dabbles in the crystal wave, to chase<br />
The scorching heat which rages in his veins,<br />
Caught from the heavy corslet’s burning case.<br />
Nor is it marvel if the burden pains;<br />
No ramble his in square or market-place!<br />
Three thousand miles, without repose, he went,<br />
And still, at speed, in ponderous armour pent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>Meanwhile the courser by the myrtle’s side,<br />
Whom he left stabled in the cool retreat,<br />
Started at something in the wood descried,<br />
Scared by I know not what; and in his heat<br />
So made the myrtle shake where he was tied,<br />
He brought a shower of leaves about his feet;<br />
He made the myrtle shake and foliage fall,<br />
But, struggling, could not loose himself withal.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>As in a stick to feed the chimney rent,<br />
Where scanty pith ill fills the narrow sheath,<br />
The vapour, in its little channel pent,<br />
Struggles, tormented by the fire beneath;<br />
And, till its prisoned fury find a vent,<br />
Is heard to hiss and bubble, sing and seethe:<br />
So the offended myrtle inly pined,<br />
Groaned, murmured, and at last unclosed its rind:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>And hence a clear, intelligible speech<br />
Thus issued, with a melancholy sound;<br />
“If, as thy cheer and gentle presence teach,<br />
Thou courteous art and good, his reign unbound,<br />
Release me from this monster, I beseech:<br />
Griefs of my own inflict sufficient wound:<br />
Nor need I, compassed with such ills about,<br />
Other new pain to plague me from without.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>At the first sound, Rogero turns to see<br />
Whence came the voice, and, in unused surprise,<br />
Stands, when he finds it issues from the tree;<br />
And swiftly to remove the courser hies.<br />
Then, with a face suffused with crimson, he<br />
In answer to the groaning myrtle, cries;<br />
“Pardon! and, whatsoe’er thou art, be good,<br />
Spirit of man, or goddess of the wood!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>“Unweeting of the wonderous prodigy<br />
Of spirit, pent beneath the knotty rind,<br />
To your fair leaf and living body I<br />
Have done this scathe and outrage undesigned.<br />
But not the less for that, to me reply,<br />
What art thou, who, in rugged case confined,<br />
Dost live and speak? And so may never hail<br />
From angry heaven your gentle boughs assail!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“And if I now or ever the despite<br />
I did thee can repair, or aid impart,<br />
I, by that lady dear, my promise plight,<br />
Who in her keeping has my better part,<br />
To strive with word and deed, till thou requite<br />
The service done with praise and grateful heart.”<br />
Rogero said; and, as he closed his suit,<br />
That gentle myrtle shook from top to root.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>Next drops were seen to stand upon the bark,<br />
As juice is sweated by the sapling-spray,<br />
New-severed, when it yields to flame and spark,<br />
Sometime in vain kept back and held at bay.<br />
And next the voice began: “My story dark,<br />
Forced by thy courteous deed, I shall display;—<br />
What once I was—by whom, through magic lore,<br />
Changed to a myrtle on the pleasant shore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>“A peer of France, Astolpho was my name,<br />
Whilom a paladin, sore feared in fight;<br />
Cousin I was to two of boundless fame,<br />
Orlando and Rinaldo. I by right<br />
Looked to all England’s crown; my lawful claim<br />
After my royal father, Otho hight.<br />
More dames than one my beauty served to warm,<br />
And in conclusion wrought my single harm.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“Returning from those isles, whose eastern side<br />
The billows of the Indian ocean beat,<br />
Where good Rinaldo and more knights beside<br />
With me were pent in dark and hollow seat,<br />
Thence, rescued by illustrious Brava’s pride,<br />
Whose prowess freed us from that dark retreat,<br />
Westward I fared along the sandy shores,<br />
On which the stormy north his fury pours.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>“Pursuing thus our rugged journey, we<br />
Came (such our evil doom) upon the strand,<br />
Where stood a mansion seated by the sea:<br />
Puissant Alcina owned the house and land.<br />
We found her, where, without her dwelling, she<br />
Had taken on the beach her lonely stand;<br />
And though nor hook nor sweeping net she bore,<br />
What fish she willed, at pleasure drew to shore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“Thither swift dolphins gambol, inly stirred,<br />
And open-mouthed the cumbrous tunnies leap;<br />
Thither the seal or porpus’ wallowing herd<br />
Troop at her bidding, roused from lazy sleep;<br />
Raven-fish, salmon, salpouth, at her word,<br />
And mullet hurry through the briny deep,<br />
With monstrous backs above the water, sail<br />
Ork, physeter, sea-serpent, shark, and whale.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“There we behold a mighty whale, of size<br />
The hugest yet in any water seen:<br />
More than eleven paces, to our eyes,<br />
His back appears above the surface green:<br />
And (for still firm and motionless he lies,<br />
And such the distance his two ends between)<br />
We all are cheated by the floating pile,<br />
And idly take the monster for an isle.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“Alcina made the ready fish obey<br />
By simple words and by mere magic lore:<br />
Born with Morgana—but I cannot say<br />
If at one birth, or after or before.<br />
As soon as seen, my aspect pleased the fay;<br />
Who showed it in the countenance she wore:<br />
Then wrought with art, and compassed her intent,<br />
To part me from the friends with whom I went.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“She came towards us with a cheerful face,<br />
With graceful gestures, and a courteous air,<br />
And said: ‘So you my lodging please to grace,<br />
Sir cavalier, and will with me repair,<br />
You shall behold the wonders of my chace,<br />
And note the different sorts of fish I snare;<br />
Shaggy or smooth, or clad in scales of light,<br />
And more in number than the stars of night:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“ ‘And would you hear a mermaid sing so sweet,<br />
That the rude sea grows civil at her song,<br />
Wont at this hour her music to repeat,<br />
(With that she showed the monster huge and long<br />
—I said it seemed an island—as her seat)<br />
Pass with me where she sings the shoals among.’<br />
I, that was always wilful, at her wish,<br />
I now lament my rashness, climb the fish.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“To Dudon and Rinaldo’s signal blind,<br />
I go, who warn me to misdoubt the fay.<br />
With laughing face Alcina mounts behind,<br />
Leaving the other two beside the bay.<br />
The obedient fish performs the task assigned,<br />
And through the yielding water works his way.<br />
Repentant of my deed, I curse the snare,<br />
Too far from land my folly to repair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“To aid me swam Mount Alban’s cavalier,<br />
And was nigh drowned amid the waves that rise;<br />
For a south-wind sprang up that, far and near,<br />
Covered with sudden darkness seas and skies.<br />
I know not after what befel the peer:<br />
This while Alcina to console me tries,<br />
And all that day, and night which followed, me<br />
Detained upon that monster in mid-sea,</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“Till to this isle we drifted with the morn,<br />
Of which Alcina keeps a mighty share;<br />
By that usurper from a sister torn,<br />
Who was her father’s universal heir:<br />
For that she only was in wedlock born,<br />
And for those other two false sisters were<br />
(So well-instructed in the story, said<br />
One who rehearsed the tale) in incest bred.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“As these are practised in iniquity,<br />
And full of every vice and evil art;<br />
So she, who ever lives in chastity,<br />
Wisely on better things has set her heart.<br />
Hence, leagued against her, in conspiracy,<br />
Those others are, to drive her from her part:<br />
And more than once their armies have o’errun<br />
Her realm, and towns above a hundred won.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“Nor at this hour a single span of ground<br />
Would Logistilla (such her name) command,<br />
But that a mountain here, and there a sound,<br />
Protects the remnant from the invading band.<br />
’Tis thus the mountain and the river bound<br />
England, and part it from the Scottish land.<br />
Yet will the sisters give their foe no rest,<br />
Till of her scanty remnant dispossest.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“Because in wickedness and vice were bred<br />
The pair, as chaste and good they loath the dame.<br />
But, to return to what I lately said,<br />
And to relate how I a plant became;<br />
Me, full of love, the kind Alcina fed<br />
With full delights; nor I a weaker flame<br />
For her, within my burning heart did bear,<br />
Beholding her so courteous and so fair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>“Clasped in her dainty limbs, and lapt in pleasure,<br />
I weened that I each separate good had won,<br />
Which to mankind is dealt in different measure,<br />
Little or more to some, and much to none.<br />
I evermore contemplated my treasure,<br />
Nor France nor aught beside I thought upon:<br />
In her my every fancy, every hope<br />
Centered and ended as their common scope.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“By her I was as much beloved, or more;<br />
Nor did Alcina now for other care;<br />
She left her every lover; for before,<br />
Others, in truth, the fairy’s love did share:<br />
I was her close adviser evermore;<br />
And served by her, where they commanded were.<br />
With me she counselled, and to me referred;<br />
Nor, night nor day, to other spake a word.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“Why touch my wounds, to aggravate my ill,<br />
And that, alas! without the hope of cure?<br />
Why thus the good possessed remember still,<br />
Amid the cruel penance I endure?<br />
When kindest I believed Alcina’s will,<br />
And fondly deemed my happiness secure,<br />
From me the heart she gave, the fay withdrew,<br />
And yielded all her soul to love more new.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>“Late I discerned her light and fickle bent,<br />
Still loving and unloving at a heat:<br />
Two months, I reigned not more, no sooner spent,<br />
Than a new paramour assumed my seat;<br />
And me, with scorn, she doomed to banishment,<br />
From her fair grace cast out. ’Tis then I weet<br />
I share a thousand lovers’ fate, whom she<br />
Had to like pass reduced, all wrongfully.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>“And these, because they should not scatter bruits,<br />
Roaming the world, of her lascivious ways,<br />
She, up and down the fruitful soil, transmutes<br />
To olive, palm, or cedar, firs or bays.<br />
These, as you see me changed, Alcina roots;<br />
While this transformed into a monster strays;<br />
Another melts into a liquid rill;<br />
As suits that haughty fairy’s wanton will.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>“Thou, too, that to this fatal isle art led<br />
By way unwonted and till now unknown,<br />
That some possessor of the fairy’s bed,<br />
May be for thee transformed to wave or stone,<br />
Thou shalt, with more than mortal pleasures fed,<br />
Have from Alcina seigniory and throne;<br />
But shalt be sure to join the common flock,<br />
Transformed to beast or fountain, plant or rock.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>“I willingly to thee this truth impart,<br />
Not that I hope with profit to advise:<br />
Yet ’twill be better, that informed, in part,<br />
Of her false ways, she harm not by surprise.<br />
Perhaps, as faces differ, and in art<br />
And wit of man an equal difference lies,<br />
Thou may’st some remedy perchance apply<br />
To the ill, which thousand others could not fly.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>The good Rogero, who from Fame had learned<br />
That he was cousin to the dame he wooed,<br />
Lamented much the sad Astolpho, turned<br />
From his true form, to barren plant and rude:<br />
And for her love, for whom so sore he burned,<br />
Would gladly serve the stripling if he cou’d:<br />
But, witless how to give the wished relief,<br />
Might but console the unhappy warrior’s grief.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>As best he could, he strove to soothe his pain;<br />
Then asked him, if to Logistil’s retreat<br />
Were passage, whether over hill or plain;<br />
That he might so eschew Alcina’s seat.<br />
—‘There was a way’, the myrtle said again,<br />
—‘But rough with stones, and rugged to the feet—<br />
If he, some little further to the right,<br />
Would scale the Alpine mountain’s very height:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>‘But that he must not think he shall pursue<br />
The intended journey far; since by the way<br />
He will encounter with a frequent crew,<br />
And fierce, who serve as rampart to the fay,<br />
That block the road against the stranger, who<br />
Would break her bounds, and the deserter stay.’<br />
Rogero thanked the tree for all, and taught,<br />
Departed thence with full instructions fraught.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>The courser from the myrtle he untied,<br />
And by the bridle led behind him still;<br />
Nor would he, as before, the horse bestride,<br />
Lest he should bear him off against his will:<br />
He mused this while how safely he might find<br />
A passage to the land of Logistil;<br />
Firm in his purpose every nerve to strain,<br />
Lest empire over him Alcina gain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>He to remount the steed, and through the air<br />
To spur him to a new career again<br />
Now thought; but doubted next, in fear to fare<br />
Worse on the courser, restive to the rein.<br />
“No, I will win by force the mountain stair,”<br />
Rogero said; (but the resolve was vain)<br />
Nor by the beach two miles his way pursued,<br />
Ere he Alcina’s lovely city viewed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>A lofty wall at distance meets his eye<br />
Which girds a spacious town within its bound;<br />
It seems as if its summit touched the sky,<br />
And all appears like gold from top to ground.<br />
Here some one says it is but alchemy<br />
—And haply his opinion is unsound—<br />
And haply he more wittily divines:<br />
For me, I deem it gold because it shines.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>When he was nigh the city-walls, so bright,<br />
The world has not their equal, he the straight<br />
And spacious way deserts, the way which dight<br />
Across the plain, conducted to the gate;<br />
And by that safer road upon the right,<br />
Strains now against the mountain; but, in wait,<br />
Encounters soon the crowd of evil foes,<br />
Who furiously the Child’s advance oppose.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>Was never yet beheld a stranger band,<br />
Of mien more hideous, or more monstrous shape.<br />
Formed downwards from neck like men, he scanned<br />
Some with the head of cat, and some of ape;<br />
With hoof of goat that other stamped the sand;<br />
While some seemed centaurs, quick in fight and rape;<br />
Naked, or mantled in outlandish skin.<br />
These doting sires, those striplings bold in sin.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>This gallops on a horse without a bit;<br />
This backs the sluggish ass, or bullock slow;<br />
These mounted on the croup of centaur sit:<br />
Those perched on eagle, crane, or estridge, go.<br />
Some male, some female, some hermaphrodit’,<br />
These drain the cup and those the bungle blow.<br />
One bore a corded ladder, one a hook,<br />
One a dull file, or bar of iron shook.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>The captain of this crew, which blocked the road,<br />
Appeared, with monstrous paunch and bloated face;<br />
Who a slow tortoise for a horse bestrode,<br />
That passing sluggishly with him did pace:<br />
Down looked, some here, some there, sustained the load,<br />
For he was drunk, and kept him in his place.<br />
Some wipe his brows and chin from sweat which ran,<br />
And others with their vests his visage fan.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>One, with a human shape and feet, his crest,<br />
Fashioned like hound, in neck and ears and head,<br />
Bayed at the gallant Child with angry quest,<br />
To turn him to the city whence he fled.<br />
“That will I never, while of strength possessed<br />
To brandish this,” the good Rogero said:<br />
With that his trenchant faulchion he displayed,<br />
And pointed at him full the naked blade.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>That monster would have smote him with a spear,<br />
But swiftly at his foe Rogero sprung,<br />
Thrust at his paunch, and drove his faulchion sheer<br />
Through his pierced back a palm; his buckler flung<br />
Before him, and next sallied there and here:<br />
But all too numerous was the wicked throng.<br />
Now grappled from behind, now punched before,<br />
He stands, and plies the crowd with warfare sore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>One to the teeth, another to the breast,<br />
Of that foul race he cleft; since no one steeled<br />
In mail, his brows with covering helmet dressed,<br />
Or fought, secured by corslet or by shield;<br />
Yet is he so upon all quarters pressed,<br />
That it would need the Child, to clear the field,<br />
And to keep off the wicked crew which swarms,<br />
More than Briareus’ hundred hands and arms.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>If he had thought the magic shield to show,<br />
(I speak of that the necromancer bore,<br />
Which dazed the sight of the astonished foe,<br />
Left at his saddle by the wizard Moor)<br />
That hideous band, in sudden overthrow,<br />
Blinded by this, had sunk the knight before.<br />
But haply he despised such mean as vile,<br />
And would prevail by valour, not by guile.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>This as it may: the Child would meet his fate,<br />
Ere by so vile a band be prisoner led;<br />
When, lo! forth-issuing from the city’s gate,<br />
Whose wall appeared like shining gold I said,<br />
Two youthful dames, not born in low estate,<br />
If measured by their mien and garb, nor bred<br />
By swain, in early wants and troubles versed;<br />
But amid princely joys in palace nursed!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>On unicorn was seated either fair,<br />
A beast than spotless ermine yet more white;<br />
So lovely were the damsels, and so rare<br />
Their garb, and with such graceful fashion dight,<br />
That he who closely viewed the youthful pair,<br />
Would need a surer sense than mortal sight,<br />
To judge between the two. With such a mien<br />
Embodied Grace and Beauty would be seen.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>Into the mead rode this and the other dame,<br />
Where the foul crew opposed the Child’s retreat.<br />
The rabble scattered as the ladies came,<br />
Who with extended hand the warrior greet.<br />
He, with a kindling visage, red with shame,<br />
Thanked the two damsels for their gentle feat;<br />
And was content upon their will to wait,<br />
With them returning to that golden gate.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>Above, a cornice round the gateway goes,<br />
Somedeal projecting from the colonnade,<br />
In which is not a single part but glows,<br />
With rarest gems of India overlaid.<br />
Propp’d at four points, the portal did repose<br />
On columns of one solid diamond made.<br />
Whether what met the eye was false or true,<br />
Was never sight more fair or glad to view.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>Upon the sill and through the columns there,<br />
Ran young and wanton girls, in frolic sport;<br />
Who haply yet would have appeared more fair,<br />
Had they observed a woman’s fitting port.<br />
All are arrayed in green, and garlands wear<br />
Of the fresh leaf. Him these in courteous sort,<br />
With many proffers and fair mien entice,<br />
And welcome to this opening Paradise:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>For so with reason I this place may call,<br />
Where, it is my belief, that Love had birth;<br />
Where life is spent in festive game and ball,<br />
And still the passing moments fleet in mirth.<br />
Here hoary-headed Thought ne’er comes at all,<br />
Nor finds a place in any bosom. Dearth,<br />
Nor yet Discomfort, never enter here,<br />
Where Plenty fills her horn throughout the year.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>Here, where with jovial and unclouded brow,<br />
Glad April seems to wear a constant smile,<br />
Troop boys and damsels: One, whose fountains flow,<br />
On the green margin sings in dulcet style;<br />
Others, the hill or tufted tree below,<br />
In dance, or no mean sport the hours beguile.<br />
While this, who shuns the revellers’ noisy cheer,<br />
Tells his love sorrows in his comrade’s ear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>Above the laurel and pine-tree’s height,<br />
Through the tall beech and shaggy fir-tree’s spray,<br />
Sport little loves, with desultory flight:<br />
These, at their conquests made, rejoiced and gay:<br />
These, with the well-directed shaft, take sight<br />
At hearts, and those spread nets to catch their prey:<br />
One wets his arrows in the brook which winds,<br />
And one on whirling stone the weapon grinds.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>To good Rogero here was brought a steed,<br />
Puissant and nimble, all of sorel hue;<br />
Who was caparisoned with costly weed,<br />
Broidered with gold, and jewels bright to view.<br />
That other winged horse, which, at his need,<br />
Obedient to the Moorish wizard flew,<br />
The friendly damsels to a youth consigned,<br />
Who led him at a slower pace behind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>That kindly pair who, by the wicked band<br />
Offended fate, had saved the youthful knight;<br />
The wicked crew, that did the Child withstand,<br />
When he the road had taken on his right,<br />
Exclaimed, “Fair sir, your works already scanned<br />
By us, who are instructed of your might,<br />
Embolden us, in our behalf, to pray<br />
You will the prowess of your arm assay.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>“We soon shall reach a bottom which divides<br />
The plain into two parts: A cruel dame<br />
A bridge maintains, which there a stream bestrides,<br />
Eriphila the savage beldam’s name;<br />
Who cheats, and robs, and scathes, whoever rides<br />
To the other shore, a giantess in frame;<br />
Who has long poisonous teeth her prey to tear,<br />
And scratches with her talons like a bear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>“Besides that she infests the public way,<br />
Which else were free; she often ranging through<br />
All this fair garden, puts in disarray<br />
This thing or that. Of the assassin crew,<br />
That people who without the portal gay,<br />
Lately with brutal rage assaulted you,<br />
Many her sons, the whole her followers call,<br />
As greedy and inhospitable all.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>“For you not only her I would assail,<br />
But do a hundred battles, well content;<br />
Then of my person, where it may avail,<br />
Dispose (Rogero said) to you intent.<br />
Silver and land to conquer, plate or mail<br />
I swear not, I, in warlike cuirass pent;<br />
But to afford my aid to others due;<br />
And, most of all, to beauteous dames like you.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>Their grateful thanks the ladies, worthily<br />
Bestowed on such a valiant champion, paid:<br />
They talking thus the bridge and river see,<br />
And at her post the haughty dame arraid<br />
(Sapphire and emerald decked the panoply)<br />
In arms of gold: but I awhile delay<br />
Till other strain the issue of the fray.</td>
</tr>
