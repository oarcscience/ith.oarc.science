<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: center;"><span class="small"><em>Rinaldo from his courteous landlord hears<br />
What folly had destroyed his every good;<br />
Next learns another story, as he steers<br />
Toward Ravenna with the falling flood:<br />
Then last arrives where, conqueror o’er his foes<br />
Orlando was, but in no joyful mood.<br />
He, that the Child a Christian made whilere,<br />
Christens Sobrino, and heals Olivier.</em></span></td>

<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">I.</td>
<td>O Execrable avarice! O vile thirst<br />
Of sordid gold! it doth not me astound<br />
So easily thou seizest soul, immersed<br />
In baseness, or with other taint unsound;<br />
But that thy chain should bind, amid the worst,<br />
And that thy talon should strike down and wound<br />
One that for loftiness of mind would be<br />
Worthy all praise, if he avoided thee.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">II.</td>
<td>Some earth and sea and heaven above us square,<br />
Know Nature’s causes, works, and properties;<br />
What her beginnings, what her endings are;<br />
And soar till Heaven is open to their eyes:<br />
Yet have no steadier aim, no better care,<br />
Stung by thy venom, than, in sordid wise,<br />
To gather treasure: such their single scope,<br />
Their every comfort, and their every hope.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">III.</td>
<td>Armies by him are broken in his pride,<br />
And gates of warlike towns in triumph past:<br />
The foremost he to breast the furious tide<br />
Of fearful battle; to retire the last;<br />
Yet cannot save himself from being stied<br />
Till death, in thy dark dungeon prisoned fast.<br />
Of others that would shine thou dimm’st the praise;<br />
Whom other studies, other arts would raise.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IV.</td>
<td>What shall of high and beauteous dames be said?<br />
Who (from their lovers’ worth and charms secure)<br />
Against long service, I behold, more staid,<br />
More motionless, than marble shafts, endure:<br />
Then Avarice comes, who so her spells hath laid,<br />
I see them stoop directly to her lure.<br />
—Who could believe?—unloving, in a day<br />
They fall some elder’s, fall some monster’s prey.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">V.</td>
<td>Not without reason here I raise this cry:<br />
—Read me who can, I read myself—nor so<br />
I from the beaten pathway tread awry,<br />
Nor thus the matter of my song forego.<br />
Not more to what is shown do I apply<br />
My saying, than to what I have to show.<br />
But now return we to the paladine,<br />
Who was about to taste the enchanted wine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VI.</td>
<td>Fain would he think awhile, of whom I speak,<br />
(As said) ere to his lips the vase he bore;<br />
He thought; then thus: “When finding what we seek<br />
Displeases, this ’tis folly to explore,<br />
My wife’s a woman; every woman’s weak.<br />
Then let me hold the faith I held before.<br />
Faith still has brought, and yet contentment brings.<br />
From proof itself what better profit springs?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VII.</td>
<td>“From this small good, much evil I foresee:<br />
For tempting God moves sometimes his disdain.<br />
I know not if it wise or foolish be,<br />
But to know more than needs, I am not fain.<br />
Now put away the enchanted cup from me;<br />
I neither will, nor would, the goblet drain;<br />
Which is with Heaven’s command as much at strife,<br />
As Adam’s deed who robbed the tree of life.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">VIII.</td>
<td>“For as our sire who tasted of that tree,<br />
And God’s own word, by eating, disobeyed,<br />
Fell into sorrow from felicity,<br />
And was by misery evermore o’erlaid;<br />
The husband so, that all would know and see;<br />
Whatever by his wife is done and said;<br />
Passes from happiness to grief and pain,<br />
Nor ever can uplift his head again.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">IX.</td>
<td>Meanwhile the good Rinaldo saying so,<br />
And pushing from himself the cup abhorred,<br />
Beheld of tears a plenteous fountain flow<br />
From the full eyes of that fair mansion’s lord;<br />
Who cried, now having somewhat calmed his woe,<br />
“Accursed be he, persuaded by whose word,<br />
Alas! I of the fortune made assay,<br />
Whereby my cherished wife was reft away!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">X.</td>
<td>“Wherefore ten years ago wast thou not known,<br />
So that I counselled might have been of thee?<br />
Before the sorrows and the grief begun,<br />
That have nigh quenched my eyes; but raised shall be<br />
The curtain from the scene, that thou upon<br />
My pain mayst look, and mayst lament with me;<br />
And I to thee of mine unheard-of woe<br />
The argument and very head will show.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XI.</td>
<td>“Above, was left a neighbouring city, pent<br />
Within a limpid stream that forms a lake;<br />
Which widens, and wherein Po finds a vent.<br />
Their way the waters from Benacus take.<br />
Built was the city, when to ruin went<br />
Walls founded by the Agenorean snake.<br />
Here me of gentle line my mother bore,<br />
But of small means, in humble home and poor.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XII.</td>
<td>“If Fortune’s care I was not, who denied<br />
To me upon my birth a wealthy boon,<br />
Nature that went with graceful form supplied;<br />
So that in beauty rival had I none.<br />
Enamoured of me in youth’s early tide<br />
Erewhile was dame and damsel more than one:<br />
For I with beauty coupled winning ways;<br />
Though it becomes not man himself to praise.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIII.</td>
<td>“A sage within our city dwelled, a wight,<br />
Beyond belief, in every science great;<br />
Who, when he closed his eyes on Phœbus’ light,<br />
Numbered one hundred years, one score and eight:<br />
A savage life he led and out of sight,<br />
Until impelled by love, the senior late<br />
By dint of gifts obtained a matron fair,<br />
Who secretly to him a daughter bare;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIV.</td>
<td>“And to prevent the child from being won,<br />
As was erewhile the mother, that for gain<br />
Bartered her chastity, whose worth alone<br />
Excels what gold earth’s ample veins contain,<br />
With her he from the ways of man is gone,<br />
And where he spies the loneliest place, his train<br />
Of demons forces, in enchantment skilled,<br />
This dome so spacious, fair, and rich, to build.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XV.</td>
<td>“By ancient and chaste dames he there made rear<br />
This daughter, that in sovereign beauty grew;<br />
Nor suffered her to see or even hear<br />
A man beside himself; and, for her view,<br />
—Lest lights should lack, whereby her course to steer—<br />
The senior every modest lady, who<br />
E’er on unlawful love the barrier shut,<br />
Made limn in picture, or in sculpture cut.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVI.</td>
<td>“Nor he alone those virtuous dames, who, sage<br />
And chaste, had so adorned antiquity,<br />
Whose fame, preserved by the historic page,<br />
Is never doomed its dying day to see;<br />
But those as well that will in future age<br />
Everywhere beautify fair Italy,<br />
Made fashion in their well-known form and mien;<br />
As eight that round this fount by thee are seen.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVII.</td>
<td>“What time the damsel ripe for husband shows,<br />
So that the fruit may now be gathered, I<br />
(Did chance or my misfortune so dispose?)<br />
Am worthiest found; and those broad lands that lie<br />
Without the walls which that fair town enclose,<br />
—The fishy flat no less than upland dry—<br />
Extending twenty miles about that water,<br />
He gives me for a dowry, with his daughter.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XVIII.</td>
<td>“She was so mannered, was so fair of hue,<br />
None could desire she other gifts should bring;<br />
So well to broider was she taught, and sew,<br />
Minerva knew not better; did she sing,<br />
Or play, or walk, to those that hear and view,<br />
She seems a heavenly, and no mortal thing;<br />
And in the liberal arts was skilled as well<br />
As her own sire, or scarce behind him fell.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XIX.</td>
<td>“With genius high and beauty no less bright,<br />
Which might have served the very stones to move,<br />
Such love, such sweetness did the maid unite,<br />
Thinking thereof meseems my heart is clove.<br />
She had no greater pleasure or delight<br />
Than being with me, did I rest or rove.<br />
Twas long ere we had any strife; in fine<br />
We quarrelled; and the fault, alas! was mine.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XX.</td>
<td>“Five years my consort’s father had been dead,<br />
Since to that yoke I stooped, and pledged my vow;<br />
When in short time (the manner shall be said)<br />
Began the sorrows that I feel even now.<br />
While me with all his pinions overspread<br />
Love of the dame, whose praises thus I blow,<br />
A noble townswoman with love of me<br />
Was smit; more sorely smitten none could be.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXI.</td>
<td>“She, in all magic versed, was of such skill<br />
As never was enchantress; by her say<br />
Moved solid earth, and made the sun stand still,<br />
Illumined gloomy night and darkened day:<br />
Yet never could she work upon my will,<br />
With salve I could not give, except with scathe<br />
Of her to whom erewhile I pledged my faith.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXII.</td>
<td>“Not because she right gentle was and bright,<br />
Nor because I believed her love so true,<br />
Nor for large gift, nor promise often plight,<br />
Nor yet because she never ceased to sue,<br />
Could she from me obtain one spark of light<br />
From that first flame my gentle consort blew:<br />
So mates and masters every will in me<br />
The knowledge of my wife’s fidelity.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIII.</td>
<td>“I in the hope, belief, and certitude<br />
My wife to me was faithful evermore,<br />
Should with contempt the beauty have eschewed<br />
Of that famed daughter which fair Leda bore;<br />
And all the wit and wealth wherewith was wooed<br />
The illustrious shepherd upon Ida hoar.<br />
But no repulse withal with her avails,<br />
Who me, for ever at my side, assails.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIV.</td>
<td>“One day that me beyond my palace sees<br />
That weird enchantress, who Melissa hight,<br />
And where she can discourse with me at ease,<br />
She finds a way whereby my peace to blight;<br />
And, goading me with evil jealousies,<br />
The faith I nursed at heart, she puts to flight.<br />
She ’gan commending my intent to be<br />
Faithful to her who faithful was to me.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXV.</td>
<td>“ ‘But that she faithful is, ye cannot say,<br />
Save of her faith ye have assurance true;<br />
If she fails not withal, where fail she may,<br />
She faithful, modest may be deemed by you:<br />
But is she never from your side away,<br />
Is not permitted other man to view,<br />
How does this boldness come, that you would be<br />
The warrant of her untried modesty?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVI.</td>
<td>“ ‘Go forth awhile; go forth come from home alone;<br />
And be the bruit in town and village spread<br />
That she remains behind, and you are gone;<br />
Let lovers and let couriers have their head:<br />
If, unpersuaded still by prayer and boon,<br />
She does no outrage to the marriage bed;<br />
Though doing so she deem herself unseen,<br />
Then faithful you the dame may justly ween.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVII.</td>
<td>“I with such words and such-like words was plied,<br />
Till so on me the shrewd enchantress wrought,<br />
I wished to see my consort’s virtue tried<br />
By certain proof, and to the touchstone brought.<br />
—‘Now grant we (I to that witch-lady cried)<br />
She prove what cannot by myself be thought,<br />
How by some certain token can I read<br />
If she will merit punishment or meed?’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXVIII.</td>
<td>“ ‘A drinking-cup will I for that assay<br />
Give you (she said) of virtue strange and rare:<br />
Such was for Arthur made by Morgue the fay,<br />
To make him of Genevra’s fault aware.<br />
The chaste wife’s lord thereof may drink; but they<br />
Drink not, whose wedded partners wanton are:<br />
For, when they would the cordial beverage sup,<br />
Into their bosom overflows the cup.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXIX.</td>
<td>“ ‘Below departing, you the test shall try,<br />
And, to my thinking, now shall you drink clean;<br />
For clean as yet I think your consort, I:<br />
The event however shall by you be seen.<br />
Yet will I warrant not your bosom dry,<br />
Should you repeat the proof; for if, between<br />
The cup and lip, the liquor be not shed,<br />
You are the happiest wight that ever wed.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXX.</td>
<td>“The offer I accept, the vase to me<br />
Is given, and trial made with full success;<br />
For hitherto (as hoped) confirmed I see<br />
My gentle consort’s worth and faithfulness.<br />
‘Leave her awhile (Melissa said), and be<br />
A month or twain a truant, more or less:<br />
Then homeward wend; again the goblet fill;<br />
And prove if you the beverage drink or spill.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXI.</td>
<td>“I thought it hard to leave my consort’s side;<br />
Not as so much about her truth in pain,<br />
As that I could nor for two days abide,<br />
Nay, not an hour without her could remain.<br />
‘—You in another way (Melissa cried)<br />
Guided by me, the truth shall ascertain;<br />
Voice, vesture shall you change; and to her sight<br />
Present yourself, disguised like other wight.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXII.</td>
<td>“Sir, a fair city nigh at hand, defends<br />
Twixt fierce and threatening horns the foaming Po;<br />
Whose jurisdiction to the shore extends,<br />
Where the sea’s briny waters come and go:<br />
This yields in ancientry, but well contends<br />
With neighbouring towns in rich and gorgeous show:<br />
A Trojan remnant its foundations placed,<br />
Which scaped from Attila’s destructive waste.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIII.</td>
<td>“A rich, a youthful, and a handsome knight<br />
Bridles this city with his sovereign sway;<br />
Who, following a lost falcon in its flight,<br />
Entering by chance my dwelling on a day,<br />
Beheld my wife, who pleased him so at sight,<br />
He bore her impress in his heart away;<br />
Nor ceased to practise on her, with intent<br />
To incline the matron to his evil bent.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIV.</td>
<td>“So often she repels the cavalier<br />
That finally his courtship is foregone;<br />
But her fair image graved by Love will ne’er<br />
Be razed from memory; me Melissa won<br />
(So well she soothed and flattered) of that peer<br />
The face and figure to the sight to don;<br />
And changed me—nor well how can I declare—<br />
In voice and visage and in eyes and hair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXV.</td>
<td>“I, having to my lady made a show<br />
As eastward bound and gone,—like him that wooed,<br />
Her rich and youthful lover, altered so,<br />
His semblance, attended by Melissa, go,<br />
Into a page upon her side transmewed;<br />
Who the most costly jewels with her bore<br />
E’er brought form Ind, or Erithraean shore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVI.</td>
<td>“I enter safely, that my palace knew,<br />
And with me wends Melissa; and there I<br />
So wholly at her ease Madonna view,<br />
No woman or attendant squire is by.<br />
To her with suppliant prayer forthwith I sue,<br />
And next those goads to evil deed apply;<br />
Show emerald, ruby, diamond, that might serve;<br />
To make the firmest heart from honour swerve;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVII.</td>
<td>“And I declare to her the gift is small<br />
To that, which she may hope to make her own;<br />
Then of the vantage speak, that from his hall<br />
Her husband at the present time is gone;<br />
And I how long it was to her recall,<br />
Since, as she knew, to her my love was shown;<br />
And that my loving with such faith, in the end<br />
Might worthily to some reward pretend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXVIII.</td>
<td>“At first she was somedeal disturbed; became<br />
Like scarlet; nor would listen to my say;<br />
But seeing those bright jewels flash like flame,<br />
Her stubborn heart was softened, and gave way;<br />
And in brief speech and feeble said the dame<br />
What to remember takes my life away:<br />
She with my wishes, said, she would comply,<br />
If sure to be unseen of watchful eye.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XXXIX.</td>
<td>“Me my wife’s words like poisoned weapon thrill,<br />
And pierce my suffering spirit through and through:<br />
Through bones and veins there went a deadly chill;<br />
My tongue clave to my throat: The witch withdrew<br />
With that the magic mantle, and at will<br />
Transformed me to mine ancient shape anew.<br />
—Bethink thee of what hue my wife became,<br />
Taken by me in such notorious shame!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XL.</td>
<td>“Of deadly hue we both of us remain;<br />
We both stand silent; both with downcast eye.<br />
So feeble is my tongue, that I with pain,<br />
So faint my voice, that I with pain can cry;<br />
‘Thou wouldst betray me then, O wife, for gain,<br />
If there was one that would my honour buy!’<br />
She nought replies; nor save by tears she speaks,<br />
Which furrow, as they fall, her woeful cheeks.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLI.</td>
<td>“Shame stings her sore, but yet in sorer wise<br />
Wrath at the outrage I to her had done;<br />
And so without restraint it multiplies,<br />
And into rage and cruel hate is run,<br />
To fly from me forthwith does she devise;<br />
And, what time from his car dismounts the sun,<br />
Runs to the shore, aboard her pinnace wends,<br />
And all that night the stream in haste descends;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLII.</td>
<td>“And she at morn presents herself before<br />
Him that had loved her once, the cavalier,<br />
Whose semblance and whose borrowed face I wore<br />
When, to my shame, I tempted her whilere.<br />
To him that loved, and loves her evermore,<br />
Her coming, it may be believed, is dear.<br />
From thence she bade me never entertain<br />
The hope she’d love me or be mine again.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIII.</td>
<td>“Alas! with him she swells in mickle glee<br />
Even from that day, and makes of me a jest;<br />
And of that evil which I brought on me<br />
I languish yet, and find no place of rest.<br />
Justly this growing ill my death will be,<br />
Of little remnant now of life possest.<br />
I well believe I in a year had died,<br />
But that a single comfort aid supplied.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIV.</td>
<td>“That comfort was; of all which harboured were<br />
Here for ten years (for still to every guest<br />
Beneath my roof I bade the vessel bear)<br />
Was none but with the wine had bathed his breast.<br />
To have so many comrades in my care,<br />
Some little soothes the griefs that so molest.<br />
Thou only of so many hast been wise,<br />
Who wouldst forbear the perilous emprize.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLV.</td>
<td>“My wish, o’erpassing every fitting bound,<br />
To know what husband of his wife should know,<br />
Is cause, by me no quiet will be found,<br />
Whether my death be speedy of be slow.<br />
Thereat at first Melissa joys; but drowned<br />
Forthwith is her light mirth; for of my woe<br />
Esteeming her the cause, that dame so sore<br />
I hated, I would not behold her more.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVI.</td>
<td>“Impatient to be treated with disdain<br />
By me,—of her more loved than life, she said -<br />
Where she forthwith as mistress to remain<br />
Had hoped, when thence the other was conveyed,<br />
—Not to behold such present, cause of pain,<br />
Her own departure little she delayed;<br />
And went so far away, no further word<br />
By me was ever of that woman heard.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVII.</td>
<td>His tale the mournful cavalier so taught;<br />
And when he now had closed his history,<br />
With pity touched, somewhile immersed in thought<br />
Rinaldo mused, and after made reply:<br />
“Right ill advice to thee Melissa brought,<br />
Who moved three thus to anger wasps; and I<br />
Perceive in thee small wisdom, that wouldst sound<br />
A thing which thou wouldst gladly not have found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLVIII.</td>
<td>“If she, thy wife, by avarice was inclined<br />
To break her faith and be to thee untrue,<br />
Muse not: nor first nor last of womankind,<br />
She, worsted, from such cruel war withdrew;<br />
And by a meaner bribe yet firmer mind<br />
Is even tempted fouler deed to do.<br />
Of men, of how many we hear, that sold<br />
Their patrons and their friends for sordid gold?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XLIX.</td>
<td>“With such fierce arms thou ill didst her assail,<br />

Knowst thou not, against gold of no avail<br />

Meseems that thou in tempting her didst fail<br />
More than herself, that was so quickly caught.<br />
I know not, had she tempted thee as much,<br />
If thou, thyself, hadst better stood the touch.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">L.</td>
<td>Here ends Rinaldo, and—the parley done—<br />

Awhile will he repose; and then be gone,<br />
An hour or two before the daylight show.<br />
But little time has Aymon’s warlike son;<br />
Nor idly will that little time bestow.<br />
To him the mansion’s master made reply,<br />
He in his house might at his pleasure lie.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LI.</td>
<td>For bed and bower, within, were ready dight;<br />
But—would he take his counsel for his guide—<br />
In comfort might he sleep throughout the night.<br />
And yet advance some miles; “For thou,” he cried,<br />
“Shalt have a pinnace, that with rapid flight<br />
And without risque shall with the current glide.<br />
Therein shalt thou all night pursue thy way,<br />
And on thy journey gain withal a day.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LII.</td>
<td>Good seemed that proffer in Rinaldo’s eyes,<br />
And to the courteous host large thanks he paid;<br />
Then for the pinnace which that lord supplies,<br />
That waits him with her crew, the warrior made.<br />
Here, at full ease reclined, Rinaldo lies,<br />
While with the stream his frigate is conveyed;<br />
Which, by six oars impelled, flies fast and fair,<br />
And cleaves the water, as a bird the air.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIII.</td>
<td>As soon as he reclines his weary head,<br />
Asleep is Mount Albano’s cavalier;<br />
Having erewhile that they shall wake him, said,<br />
As soon as they Ferrara’s city near.<br />
Melara lies left of that river’s bed,<br />
Sermide to the right; they in their rear<br />
Next leave Stellata and Figarolo,<br />
Where his two horns are lowered by angry Po.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIV.</td>
<td>Of those two horns that which t’ward Venice goes<br />

Then the Bodeno past. Already shows<br />
Faintly the eastern blue, and fades from sight;<br />
For now Aurora from her basket throws<br />
All her rich flowers, and paints it red and white;<br />
When viewing the two castles of Tealdo,<br />
Again his head uplifts the good Rinaldo.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LV.</td>
<td>“O happy town! whereof” (the warrior cried)<br />
“Spake Malagigi, having, far and near,<br />
The fixt and wandering fires of heaven espied,<br />
And forced some subject spirit to appear,<br />
To me foretelling that in future tide,<br />
—What time with him I took his way whilere—<br />
Even to such pitch thy glorious fame should rise,<br />
Thou from all Italy wouldst bear the prize.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVI.</td>
<td>So saying, in his barge he all this while<br />
Hurries, as if the bark with pinions flew,<br />
Scowering the king of rivers, to that isle<br />
Nearest the town; and, though it not to view<br />
(Deserted and neglected then) doth smile,<br />
This yet rejoices to behold anew;<br />
Nor makes small mirth thereat; because aware<br />
Hereafter how adorned ’twill be and fair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVII.</td>
<td>Before when he with him that way had gone,<br />
From Malagigi, his cousin, did he hear<br />
That when seven hundred times his course had run,<br />
Circling the heaven in Aries, the fourth sphere,<br />
Of islands this should be the fairest one<br />
In sea, or pool, or river, far and near,<br />
So that who this beheld, would brook no more<br />
To hear that praised which fair Nausicaa bore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LVIII.</td>
<td>He heard, it in fair mansions would outdo<br />
That island which Tiberius held so dear;<br />
And trees that in Hesperian gardens grew<br />
Would yield to what this beauteous place should bear;<br />
—So rare its race of beasts—no fairer shew<br />
Herded or housed erewhile by Circe were;<br />
Venus with Loves and Graces there should sport,<br />
Nor more in Gnide and Cyprus keep her court;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LIX.</td>
<td>And so would flourish through his study and care,<br />
Who will with knowledge and with power should blend;<br />
And who so safely should that bright repair<br />
With circling wall and sheltering dyke defend,<br />
The united world’s assault it well might dare,<br />
Nor call on foreign power its aid to lend;<br />
And that Duke Hercules’ sire and Hercules’ son<br />
Was he by whom this marvel should be done.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LX.</td>
<td>So wends the warrior summing in his mind<br />
What erst to him had told his cousin wise;<br />
What time the sage of future things divined,<br />
Whereof with him he often wont devize;<br />
And aye contemplating that city blind,<br />
“How can it ever be,” Rinaldo cries,<br />
“That in all liberal and all worthy arts<br />
Shall flourish so these waste and watery parts?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXI.</td>
<td>“And that to city of such amplitude<br />
And beauty such a petty burgh should grow,<br />
And where but marsh and miry pool is viewed,<br />
Henceforth should full and fruitful harvests glow?<br />
Even now I rise, to hail the gentle blood,<br />
The love, the courtesy thy lords shall show,<br />
O thou fair city, in succeeding years;<br />
Thy burghers’ honours and thy cavaliers’.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXII.</td>
<td>“The grace ineffable of powers above,<br />
Thy princes’ wisdom and their love of right,<br />
Shall with perpetual peace, perpetual love<br />
Preserve thee in abundance and delight;<br />
And a defence from all the fury prove<br />
Of such as hate thee; and unmask their spite.<br />
Be thy content thy neighbours’ wide annoy,<br />
Rather than thou shouldst envy other’s joy!”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIII.</td>
<td>While thus Rinaldo speaks, so swiftly borne<br />
By the quick current flies that nimble yawl;<br />
Not to the lure more swiftly makes return<br />
The falcon, hurrying at his lord’s recall.<br />
Thenceforth the right-hand branch of the right horn<br />
Rinaldo takes; and hid are roof and wall:<br />
St. George recedes; recede from that swift boat<br />
The turrets OF GAIBANA and OF THE MOAT.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIV.</td>
<td>Montalban’s martial lord (as it befell,<br />
That thought moved thought, which others moved again)<br />
In memory chances on the knight to dwell,<br />
That him at supper late did entertain;<br />
That, through this city’s cause, the truth to tell,<br />
Hath reason evermore to be in pain;<br />
And of the magic vessel him bethinks<br />
Which shows his consort’s guilt to him that drinks;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXV.</td>
<td>And him bethinks therewith of what the knight<br />
Related; how of all that he had tried,<br />
Who of his goblet drank, there was no wight<br />
But split the wine he to his lips would guide.<br />
Now he repents him; now, “’Tis my delight,”<br />
(Mutters) “that I the proof would not abide:<br />
Succeeding I should prove but what I thought;<br />
And not succeeding, to what pass am brought!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVI.</td>
<td>“This my belief I deem a certainty;<br />
And faith could have but small increase in me:<br />
So, if I this should by the touchstone try,<br />
My present good would little bettered be:<br />
But small the evil would not prove, if I<br />
Saw of my Clarice what I would not see.<br />
This were a thousand against one to stake;<br />
To hazard much where I could nothing take.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVII.</td>
<td>The knight of Clermont buried in this mood,<br />
Who lifted not his visage from the floor,<br />
A mariner with much attention viewed,<br />
That overright was seated at his oar;<br />
And, for he deemed he fully understood<br />
The thought that prest the cavalier so sore,<br />
Made him (well-spoken was the man and bold)<br />
Wake from his muse, some talk with him to hold.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXVIII.</td>
<td>The substance of the talk between the two<br />
Was, that the husband little wit possest,<br />
Who, wishing to assay if she was true,<br />
Had tried his wife by too severe a test:<br />
For woman, proof to gold and silver, who,<br />
Armed but with modesty, defends her breast,<br />
This from a thousand faulchions will defend<br />
More surely, and through burning fires will wend.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXIX.</td>
<td>The mariner subjoined: “Thou saidest well;<br />
With gifts so rich he should not her have prest;<br />
For, these assaults, these charges, to repel,<br />
Not good alike is every human breast.<br />
I know not if of wife thou has heard tell<br />
(For haply not with us the tale may rest)<br />
That in the very sin her husband spied,<br />
For which she by his sentence should have died.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXX.</td>
<td>“My lord should have remembered, gold and meed<br />
Have upon every hardest matter wrought:<br />
But he forgot this truth in time of need;<br />
And so upon his head this ruin brought,<br />
Ah! would that he in proof, like me, a deed<br />
Done in this neighbouring city had been taught,<br />
His country and mine own; which lake and fen,<br />
Brimming with Mincius’ prisoned waters, pen.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXI.</td>
<td>“I of Adonio speak, that in a hound<br />
A treasure on the judge’s wife conferred.”<br />
“Thereof,” replied the paladin, “the sound<br />
Hath not o’erpast the Alps; for never word<br />
Of this neighbouring France, nor in my round<br />
Through far and foreign countries have I heard:<br />
So tell, if telling irks not,” said the peer,<br />
“What willingly I bown myself to hear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXII.</td>
<td>The boatman then: “Erewhile was of this town<br />
One Anselm, that of worthy lineage came;<br />
A wight that spent his youth in flowing gown,<br />
Studying his Ulpian: he of honest fame,<br />
Beauty, and state assorting with his own,<br />
A consort sought, and one of noble name:<br />
Nor vainly; in a neighbouring city, crowned<br />
With superhuman beauty, one he found.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIII.</td>
<td>“She such fair manners and so graceful shows,<br />
She seems all love and beauty; and much more<br />
Perchance than maketh for her lord’s repose;<br />
Then well befits the reverend charge he bore.<br />
He, wedded, strait in jealousy outgoes<br />
All jealous men that ever were before:<br />
Yet she affords not other cause for care<br />
But that she is too witty and too fair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIV.</td>
<td>“In the same city dwelt a cavalier,<br />
Numbered that old and honoured race among,<br />
Sprung from the haughty lineage, which whilere<br />
Out of the jaw-bone of a serpent sprung:<br />
Whence Manto, doomed my native walls to rear,<br />
Descended, and with her a kindred throng.<br />
The cavalier (Adonio was he named)<br />
Was with the beauties of the dame inflamed;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXV.</td>
<td>“And for the furtherance of his amorous quest,<br />
To grace himself, began his wealth to spend,<br />
Without restraint, in banquet and in vest,<br />
And what might most a cavalier commend:<br />
If he Tiberius’ treasure had possest,<br />
He of his riches would have made an end.<br />
I well believe two winters were not done,<br />
Ere his paternal fortune was outrun.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVI.</td>
<td>“The house erewhile, frequented by a horde<br />
—Morning and evening—of so many friends,<br />
Is solitary; since no more his board<br />
Beneath the partridge, quail, and pheasant bends.<br />
Of that once noble troop upon the lord,<br />
Save beggars, hardly any one attends.<br />
Ruined, at length he thinks he will begone<br />
To other country, where he is unknown.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVII.</td>
<td>“He leaves his native land with this intent,<br />
Nor letteth any his departure know;<br />
And coasts, in tears and making sad lament,<br />
The marshes that about his city go:<br />
He his heart’s queen, amid his discontent,<br />
Meanwhile forgets not, for this second woe.<br />
Lo! him another accident that falls,<br />
From sovereign woe to sovereign bliss recalls!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXVIII.</td>
<td>“He saw a peasant who with heavy stake<br />
Smote mid some sapling trunks on every side:<br />
Adonio stopt, and wherefore so he strake,<br />
Asked of the rustic, that in answer cried,<br />
Within that clump a passing ancient snake,<br />
Amid the tangled stems he had espied:<br />
A longer serpent and more thick to view<br />
He never saw, nor thought to see anew;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXIX.</td>
<td>“And that from thence he would not wend his way<br />
Until the reptile he had found and slain,<br />
When so Adonio heard the peasant say,<br />
He scarce his speech with patience could sustain,<br />
Aye reverence to the serpent wont to pay,<br />
The honoured ensign of his ancient strain;<br />
In memory that their primal race had grown<br />
Erewhile from serpent’s teeth by Cadmus sown;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXX.</td>
<td>“And by the churl the offended knight so said,<br />
And did withal, he made him quit the emprize;<br />
Leaving the hunted serpent neither dead,<br />
Nor injured, nor pursued in further wise.<br />
Thither, where he believes would least have spread<br />
The story of his woe, Adonio hies;<br />
And in discomfort and in sorrow wears,<br />
Far from his native land, seven weary years.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXI.</td>
<td>“Neither for distance nor for straitened cheer,<br />
Which will not let Thought run its restless round,<br />
Ceased Love, so wont to rein the cavalier,<br />
Aye to inflame his heart, aye vex his wound:<br />
At length those beauties, to his eyes so dear,<br />
Parforce must he revisit, homeward bound.<br />
Unshorn, afflicted, he, in poor array,<br />
Thither returns, from whence he went his way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXII.</td>
<td>“My city, at the time whereof I tell,<br />

That sometime near his holiness should dwell;<br />
And for how long a time could none foresee.<br />
Upon our judge the lot of envoy fell:<br />
O day, that ever wept by him will be!<br />
To be excused, Anselmo promised, prayed,<br />
And bribed; but at the last parforce obeyed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIII.</td>
<td>“As no less cruel and less hard to abide<br />

Dead-white with jealous fear his cheek is dyed,<br />
Through doubt of his fair consort while apart;<br />
And in the mode he deems may best avail,<br />
He supplicates her not in faith to fail,</td>
</tr>

<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">LXXXIV.</td>

The virtue that mankind most highly prize<br />

Of death; but with immortal being such woe<br />
Is coupled, death is not of direr sort.<br />
For every seventh day we all must take<br />
By certain law, the form of spotted snake.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">XCIX.</td>
<td>“ ‘So sad it is that loathsome coil to fill,<br />
And prone, at length, upon the ground to crawl;<br />
Equal to this here is no worldly ill;<br />
So that immortal life is cursed by all.<br />
And thou the debt I owe thee (for my will<br />
Is to inform thee of its cause withal)<br />
Shalt know as well; how on that fatal day<br />
Of change we are to countless ills a prey.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">C.</td>
<td>“ ‘So hated as the serpent beast is none;<br />
And we that wear its evil form, alarm,<br />
Outrage, and war endure from every one:<br />
For all that see us, hunt and do us harm:<br />
Unless we can to ground for shelter run,<br />
We feel how heavy falls man’s furious arm.<br />
Happier it were to die, than languish—broke,<br />
Battered, and crippled by the cruel stroke.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CI.</td>
<td>“ ‘My mighty obligation due to thee<br />
Is that, when once thou didst this greenwood thread,<br />
Thou from a rustic’s fury rescuedst me,<br />
By whose ill handling was I sore bested.<br />
But for thine aid, I should not have got free,<br />
Without a broken spine or battered head:<br />
With body crooked and crushed I should have lain,<br />
Albeit I could not by his arm be slain.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CII.</td>
<td>“ ‘Because thou hast to know upon the day<br />
We sprang from earth with scales of dragon dight,<br />
—Subject to us at other times—to obey<br />
The heavens refuse; and we are void of might:<br />
At other seasons, at our simple say<br />
The circling sun stands still, and dims its light:<br />
Fixt earth is moved, and in a circle wheels:<br />
Ice at our word takes fire, and fire congeals.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIII.</td>
<td>“ ‘Now here, prepared to render thee the meed<br />
Of benefit then done to me, I stand;<br />
For now, dismantled of my dragon weed,<br />
Vainly no grace of me wilt thou demand.<br />
Even now, thrice richer art thou by my deed,<br />
Than when thou heirdst erewhile thy father’s land:<br />
Now will I that henceforth thou shalt be poor;<br />
But wealth, the more ’tis spent, augment the more:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIV.</td>
<td>“ ‘And because with that ancient knot thou still,<br />
I know, art tangled, which by Love was tied,<br />
The mode and order, how thou mayst fulfil<br />
Thy wishes, shall by me be signified.<br />
Now that her lord is absent, ’tis my will<br />
My scheme without delay by thee be tried;<br />
Go forth the lady at her farm to find,<br />
Without the town; nor will I say behind.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CV.</td>
<td>“She her discourse continuing, ’gan advise<br />
What form he to that lady’s eyes should take:<br />
I say, what vesture wear, and in what wise<br />
Should speak, how tempt her; what entreaties make:<br />
And said, how she her figure would disguise;<br />
For, save the day wherein she was a snake,<br />
Upon all others went the fairy drest<br />
In whatsoever figure pleased her best.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVI.</td>
<td>“She in a pilgrim’s habit clothed the knight,<br />
Such as from door to door our alms entreat:<br />
Into a dog she changed herself to sight;<br />
The smallest ever seen, of aspect sweet,<br />
Long hair, than ermine’s fur more snowy white;<br />
And skilled withal in many a wondrous feat.<br />
Towards Agria’s villa, so transmewed,<br />
The fairy and the knight their way pursued;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVII.</td>
<td>“And at the labourer’s cabins in his round<br />
The stripling halts, before he stops elsewhere;<br />
And certain rustic reeds begins to sound;<br />
His dog is up, and dances to the air.<br />
The dame, that hears the voice and cry rebound,<br />
Is by the rumour moved to see the pair.<br />
Into her court she has the pilgrim brought,<br />
As Anselm’s evil destiny had wrought:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CVIII.</td>
<td>“And here Adonio gives the dog command;<br />
And here by that obedient dog is shown<br />
Dance of our country and of foreign land,<br />
With paces, graces, fashions of his own;<br />
And finally he does, amid that band,<br />
With winning ways what else is to be done,<br />
With such attention of the admiring crew,<br />
None winked their eyes, their breath they scarcely drew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CIX.</td>
<td>“Great marvel in the dame, then longing, bred<br />
That gentle dog: she one that her had nursed<br />
With no mean offer to his master sped.<br />
—‘If all the riches for which women thirst’<br />
(To her embassadress in answer said<br />
The wary pilgrim) ‘in my bags were pursued,<br />
There is not in that treasure what would boot<br />
To purchase of my dog one single foot’:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CX.</td>
<td>“And he, the truth of his discourse to show,<br />
Into a corner took the beldam old,<br />
And bade the dog in courtesy bestow<br />
Upon that messanger a mark of gold.<br />
The dog obeyed, and shook himself; and lo!<br />
The treasure! which he bade her have and hold:<br />
Thereto he added, ‘Thinkest thou by ought<br />
A dog so fair and useful can be bought?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXI.</td>
<td>“ ‘For whatsoever I of him demand,<br />
I empty-handed never go away;<br />
Now pearl, now ring will he shake from him, and<br />
Now gift me with some rich and fair array.<br />
Yet tell madonna he is at her command;<br />
But not for gold; for him no gold can pay;<br />
But if I for one night her arms may fill,<br />
Him may she take and do with him her will.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXII.</td>
<td>“So said, a gem, new-dropt, on her he prest,<br />
And bade her to the lady bear the boon.<br />
That in the costly produce she possest<br />
Ten, twenty ducats’ value deemed the crone.<br />
She bore the message to the dame addressed,<br />
And after wrought on her till she was won<br />
To buy the beauteous dog, who might be bought<br />
By payment of a prize which costeth nought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIII.</td>
<td>“Argia somewhat coy at first appears;<br />
Partly that she her faith will not forego;<br />
Partly that she believes not all she hears<br />
That beldam of the dog and pilgrim show.<br />
The nurse insists, and dins into her ears,<br />
That seldom such a chance occurs below;<br />
And makes her fix another day to see<br />
That dog, when fewer eyes on her shall be.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIV.</td>
<td>“The next appearance which Adonio made<br />
Was ruin to the doctor; for the hound<br />
Doubloons, by dozens and by dozens, braid<br />
Of pearl, and costly jewels scattered round.<br />
So that Argia’s pride of heart was laid;<br />
And so much less the dame maintained her ground,<br />
When she in him, who made the proffer, viewed<br />
The Mantuan cavalier that whilom wooed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXV.</td>
<td>“The harlot nurse’s evil oratory,<br />
The prayer and presence of the suitor lord,<br />
The occasion to acquire that mighty fee,<br />
Which wretched Anselm’s absence would afford,<br />
The hope that none would her accuser be,<br />
So vanquish her chaste thoughts, she makes the accord—<br />
Accepts the wondrous dog; and, as his pay,<br />
To her leman yields herself a willing prey.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVI.</td>
<td>The fruits of love long culled that cavalier<br />
With his lady fair; unto whom the fay<br />
Took such affection, whom she held so dear,<br />
That she obliged herself with her to stay.<br />
Through all the signs the sun had travelled, ere<br />
The judge had leave to wend his homeward way.<br />
He finally returned; but sore afraid<br />
Through what the astrologer erewhile had said.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVII.</td>
<td>“Arrived, his first employment is to run<br />
To that astrologer’s abode, and crave,<br />
If shame and evil to his wife be done;<br />
Of if she yet her faith and honor save.<br />
The heavens he figured; and to every one<br />
Of the seven planets its due station gave;<br />
Then to the judge replied that it had been<br />
Even as he feared, and as it was foreseen.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXVIII.</td>
<td>“By richest presents tempted to forego<br />
Her faith, a prey was she to other wight.<br />
This to the doctor’s heart was such a blow;<br />
Nor lance, nor spear, I deem, so sorely smite.<br />
To be more certified he wends (although<br />
He is too well assured the seer is right)<br />
To that old nurse; and, drawing her apart,<br />
To learn the truth employs his every art.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXIX.</td>
<td>“He in wide circles doth about her wind,<br />
Hoping now here, now there, to spy some trace:<br />
But nought in the beginning can he find,<br />
With whatsoever care he sifts the case.<br />
For she, as not unpractised in that kind,<br />
Denies, and fronts him with untroubled face;<br />
And, as well taught, above a month stands out,<br />
Holding the judge ’twixt certainty and doubt.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXX.</td>
<td>“How blest would doubt appear, had he that wound<br />
Foreseen, which would be given by certainty!<br />
When out of that false nurse at last he found<br />
He could not fish the truth by prayer or fee,<br />
Touching no chord but yielded a false sound,<br />
He shrewdly waits his time till there should be<br />
Discord between the beldam and his wife:<br />
For whereso women are, is stir and strife.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXI.</td>
<td>“And even that Anselmo waited, so<br />
Befell; since, angered by the first despite,<br />
Unsought of him, to him that nurse did go,<br />
To tell the whole; and nothing hid from sight.<br />
How sank his heart beneath that cruel blow,<br />
’Twere long to say; how prostrate lay his sprite.<br />
So was the wretched judge with grief opprest,<br />
He of his wits well-nigh was dispossest;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXII.</td>
<td>“And finally resolved to die, so burned<br />
His rage, but first would kill the faithless dame;<br />
And he with one destructive faulchion yearned<br />
To free himself from woe and her from shame.<br />
Stung by such blind and furious thoughts, returned<br />
Anselmo to the city, in a flame;<br />
And to the farm despatched a follower true,<br />
Charged with the bidding he was bound to do.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIII.</td>
<td>“He bids the servant to the villa go,<br />
And to Argia in his name pretend,<br />
He by a fever is reduced so low,<br />
She hardly can arrive before his end.<br />
Hence without waiting escort—would she show<br />
Her love—she with his man must backward wend,<br />
(Wend with him will she surely, nor delay)<br />
And bids him cut her throat upon the way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIV.</td>
<td>“The serving man to call his lady went<br />
Prepared his lord’s command on her to do.<br />
Having her little dog at starting hent,<br />
She mounted and began her journey, through<br />
The dog advised of Anselm’s ill intent,<br />
But bid no less her purpose to pursue;<br />
For he had taken thought for her; and aid<br />
Should in the time of peril be purveyed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXV.</td>
<td>“The servant from his pathway turns aside,<br />
And through bye-roads and solitary goes;<br />
Purposely lighting on a stream, whose tide<br />
From Apennine into our river flows;<br />
Where, both of farm and busy city wide,<br />
A holt, and dark and dismal greenwood grows.<br />
Silent appeared the gloomy place, and one<br />
Fitting the cruel deed which should be done.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVI.</td>
<td>“He drew his sword on her, and signified<br />
The mandate by her angry husband given;<br />
That so she might entreat, before she died,<br />
Forgiveness of her every sin from Heaven.<br />
I know not how; she vanished from his side,<br />
When through her flank the blade he would have driven.<br />
Vainly long time he seeks her, then remains<br />
Foiled and outscorned, for guerdon of his pains.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVII.</td>
<td>“He all astound and with bewildered face,<br />
And full of shame, to seek his lord returns;<br />
Who from the servant that unwonted case,<br />
Unweeting how the thing had happened, learns;<br />
Nor knows the fairy Manto fills a place<br />
About Argia, prompt to serve her turns.<br />
Because the nurse, that all the rest revealed<br />
(I know not wherefore, I), had this concealed.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXVIII.</td>
<td>“He knows not what to do: the outrage sore<br />
Avenged he has not, nor his pain allaid:<br />
What was a mote is now a beam; so sore<br />
It prest him; on his heart so heavy weighed.<br />
So plain is what was little known before,<br />
He fears that it will shortly be displaid.<br />
At first, he haply might have hid his woe;<br />
Which Rumour now throughout the world will blow.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXIX.</td>
<td>“Full well he wots, that since his evil vein<br />
He to his wife, unhappy wretch! hath shown,<br />
Not to be subject to his yoke again,<br />
She to some strong protector will have flown;<br />
Who to his ignominy will maintain,<br />
And utter scorn, the lady as his own:<br />
And haply may she to some losel flee,<br />
Who will her paramour and pander be.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXX.</td>
<td>“For remedy, he sends in haste a band<br />
Of messengers, with letters far and nigh.<br />
Some of Argia here, some there demand;<br />
Nor town unsearched is left in Lombardy.<br />
Next he in person goes; nor any land<br />
Leaves unexamined by himself or spy.<br />
Yet cannot he discover means or way<br />
For learning where concealed his consort lay.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXI.</td>
<td>“The servant last he called on whom was laid<br />
The ill hest, but who had served not his despite;<br />
And thither by his guidance was conveyed,<br />
Where (as ’twas said) she vanished from his sight;<br />
Who haply lurked by day in greenwood-shade,<br />
And to some friendly roof retired at night.<br />
He thither guided, where but forest-trees<br />
He thinks to find, a sumptuous palace sees.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXII.</td>
<td>“This while for bright Argia in that part<br />
The fay had made with speedy toil prepare<br />
An alabaster palace by her art,<br />
Gilded within, without, and everywhere.<br />
So wonderful, no tongue could tell, no heart<br />
Conceive, how rich within, without how fair:<br />
That, which thou deemed so fair, my master’s home,<br />
Is but a cottage to that costly dome.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIII.</td>
<td>“Curtain and cloth of arras deck the wall,<br />
Sumptuously woven and in different wise,<br />
In vaulted cellar and in littered stall;<br />
Not only spread in latticed galleries,<br />
Not only spread in lordly bower and hall.<br />
Vase, gold and silver, gems of many dyes,<br />
Carved into cup and charger, blue, red, green,<br />
And countless cloths of silk and gold are seen.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIV.</td>
<td>“He chanced upon the costly dome (as I<br />
To you was in my story making known)<br />
When he expected not a hut to spy,<br />
And but a weary waste of woodland lone.<br />
As he beheld the dome with wondering eye,<br />
Anselmo thought his intellects were gone:<br />
That he was drunk, or dreamed that wondrous sight<br />
He weened, of that his wits had taken flight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXV.</td>
<td>“An Aethiop woman posted at the door,<br />

Ill-favoured—such was Aesop feigned of yore:<br />

Nor half her hideousness have I exprest.</td>

<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVI.</td>

To the Aethiopian, and to her applied;<br />

And made that answer but in mockery:<br />
But with repeated oaths the negress swears;<br />
’Tis hers, and none with her the mansions shares;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVII.</td>
<td>“And would he see the palace, him invites<br />
To view it at his ease; and recommends<br />
If there be ought within which him delights,<br />
To take it for himself or for his friends.<br />
Anselmo hears, and from his horse alights,<br />
Gives it his man; and o’er the threshold wends;<br />
And by the hag conducted, mounts from hall<br />
Below to bower above, admiring all.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXVIII.</td>
<td>“Form, site, and sumptuous work doth he behold,<br />
And royal ornament and fair device;<br />

To buy the egregious mansion wound suffice.<br />
To him in answer said that negress old:<br />

If not in gold and silver, price less high<br />
Than gold and silver will the palace buy’:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXXXIX.</td>
<td>“And she to him prefers the same request,<br />
Which erst Adonio to Argia made.<br />
A fool he deemed the woman and possest,<br />
Who for a boon so foul and filthy prayed.<br />
Yet ceased she not, though more than thrice represt;<br />
And strove so well Anselmo to persuade,<br />
Proffering, for his reward, the palace still,<br />
She wrought on him to do her evil will.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXL.</td>
<td>“The wife Argia, that is hid fast by,<br />
When in such sin her husband she descries,<br />
Of doctor, that was deemed so passing wise,<br />
Springs forth and saith: ‘Ah! worthy deed! which I<br />
Found in such foul and filthy work, espy!’<br />
Bethink thee, if his kindling blushes rise;<br />
If he stands mute! why opens not thy hollow<br />
And central womb, O earth, the wretch to swallow?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLI.</td>
<td>“To clear herself and shame him, doth she stun<br />
Anselmo, never ceasing to upbraid.<br />
‘What pain should by thyself be undergone<br />
For this so filthy deed, (Argia said)<br />
If thou would’st take my life for having done<br />
What Nature prompted and a lover prayed;<br />
One that was fair and gentle, and who brought<br />
A gift, compared wherewith, this dome is nought?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLII.</td>
<td>“ ‘If worthy of one death thou deemest me,<br />
Worthy art thou a hundred deaths to die:<br />

So passing puissant in this place am I,<br />
No other or worse vengeance done shall be<br />
Upon my side, on thy delinquency.<br />
The give against the take, O husband, place;<br />
And, as ’twas granted thee, so grant me grace:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLIII.</td>

Nor me of mine, henceforward thou remind!’<br />
This seemed a goodly bargain to her lord;<br />
Nor to such pardon was he disinclined.<br />
Thus peace and concord they at home restore,<br />
And love each other dearly evermore.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLIV.</td>

Of laughter in Montalban’s master stirred;<br />
And made his visage burn, as if ’twas lit<br />
With fire, when of Anselmo’s shame he heard.<br />
Rinaldo greatly praised Argia’s wit,<br />
Who by such quaint device had trapped that bird;<br />
Who fell into the net wherein the dame<br />
Herself erewhile had fallen, but with less shame.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLV.</td>

Ordered the board with food to be supplied,<br />
Which the good Mantuan landlord overnight<br />
Took care with largest plenty to provide;<br />
While the fair town, upon the left, from sight<br />
Retired, and on the right that marish wide.<br />
Argenta is come and gone, with circling walls<br />
And stream into whose bed Santerno falls.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLVI.</td>
<td>Then was not fair Bastia built, deem I,<br />
Which little cause of boast affords to Spain<br />
(That there her banner has been raised on high),<br />
And causes deeper sorrow to Romagne.<br />
Thence in strait line their bark, that seems to fly,<br />
To the right shore the boatmen drive amain:<br />
Next through a stagnant channel make, that near<br />
Ravenna brings by noon the cavalier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLVII.</td>
<td>Though oft of money he had small supply,<br />
Then was the knight so well bested, he made<br />
The weary rowers, in his courtesy,<br />
A parting present, ere farewell was said.<br />
Here changing horse and guide, to Rimini<br />
Rinaldo rode that very eye, nor stayed<br />
In Montefiore till the night was done;<br />
And well nigh reached Urbino with the sun.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLVIII.</td>
<td>Then Frederick was not there of gentle lore,<br />
Nor was Elizabeth nor Guido good;<br />
Francis Maria nor sage Leonore;<br />
Who would in courteous, not in haughty mood,<br />
Have forced so famed a paladin for more<br />
Than one short eye, with them to make abode;<br />
As they long did, and do unto this day,<br />
By dames and cavaliers who pass that way.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXLIX.</td>
<td>Since here none takes his rein, Rinaldo bends<br />
His course an-end to Cagli; o’er the height,<br />
Rifted by Gaurus and Metaurus, wends<br />
Past Apennine, no longer on his right,<br />
Umbri and Tuscans; and at Rome descends.<br />
From Rome to Ostia goes Montalban’s knight:<br />
Thence to the city sails; wherein a grave<br />
His pious son to old Anchises gave.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CL.</td>
<td>There changes back; and thence in haste he goes<br />
Bound towards Lampedosa’s island-shore,<br />
That place of combat chosen by the foes,<br />
And where they had encountered Frank and Moor.<br />
Rinaldo grants his boatmen no repose;<br />
That do what can be done by sail and oar.<br />
But with ill wind and strong the warrior strives;<br />
And, though by little, there too late arrives.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLI.</td>
<td>Thither he came what time Anglante’s peer<br />
The useful and the glorious deed had done;<br />
Had slain those paynim kings in the career,<br />
But had a hard and bloody conquest won:<br />
Dead was Sir Brandimart; and Olivier,<br />
Dangerously hurt and sore, sate woe-begone,<br />
Somedeal apart, upon the sandy ground,<br />
Martyred and crippled by his cruel wound.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLII.</td>
<td>From tears could not the mournful Count refrain,<br />
When brave Rinaldo he embraced, and said,<br />
How in the battle Brandimart was slain.<br />
Such love, such faith endeared the warrior dead.<br />
Nor less Rinaldo’s tears his visage stain<br />
When he so cleft beholds their comrade’s head.<br />
Thence to embrace bold Oliviero, where<br />
He sits with wounded foot, he makes repair.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLIII.</td>
<td>All comfort that he could he gave; though none<br />
Could good Rinaldo to himself afford;<br />
Because he came but when the feast was done;<br />
Yea after the removal of the board.<br />
The servants wend to the demolished town,<br />
There hide the bones of either paynim lord<br />
Beneath Biserta’s ruined domes, and nigh<br />
And far, the fearful tidings certify.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLIV.</td>
<td>At the fair conquest won by Roland’s blade,<br />
Sansonet and Astolpho make great cheer;<br />
Yet other mirth whose warriors would have made<br />
Had Brandimart not perished; when they hear<br />
That he is dead, their joy is so allayed<br />
They can no more the troubled visage clear.<br />
Which of them now the tidings of such woe<br />
To the unhappy Flordelice shall show?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLV.</td>
<td>The night preceding that ill-omened day<br />
Flordelice dreamed the vest of sable grain<br />
That she had made, her husband to array,<br />
And woven with her hand and worked with pain,<br />
Before her eyes all sprinkled-over lay<br />
With ruddy drops, in guise of pattering rain.<br />
That she had worked it so the lady thought;<br />
And then was grieved at seeing what was wrought.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLVI.</td>
<td>And seemed to say, “Yet from my lord have I<br />
Command to make it all of sable hue;<br />
Now wherefore it is stained with other dye<br />
Against his will, in mode so strange to view?”<br />
She from that dream draws evil augury;<br />
And thither on that eve the tidings flew:<br />
But these concealed Astolpho from the dame<br />
Till he to her with Sansonetto came.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLVII.</td>
<td>When they are entered, and she sees no show<br />
Of joyful triumphs, she, without a word,<br />
Without a hint to indicate that woe,<br />
Knows that no longer living is her lord.<br />
With that her gentle heart was riven so,<br />
And so her harassed eyes the light abhorred,<br />
And so was every other sense astound,<br />
That, like one dead, she sank upon the ground.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLVIII.</td>
<td>She in her hair, when life returns again,<br />
Fastens her hand; and on her lovely cheeks,<br />
Repeating the beloved name in vain,<br />
With all her force her scorn and fury wreaks;<br />
Uproots and tears, her locks, and in her pain<br />
Like woman, smit by evil demon, shrieks,<br />
Or, as Bacchante at the horn’s rude sound,<br />
Erewhile was seen to run her restless round.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLIX.</td>
<td>Now to the one, to the other now her prayer<br />
She made for knife, wherewith her heart to smite;<br />
Now she aboard the pinnace would repair<br />
That brought the corse of either paynim knight,<br />
And would on either, lifeless as they were,<br />
Do cruel scathe, and vent her fierce despite.<br />
Now would she seek her lord, till at his side<br />
She rested from her weary search, and died.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLX.</td>
<td>“Ah! wherefore, Brandimart, did I let thee<br />
Without me wend on such a dire emprize?<br />
She ne’er before did thy departure see,<br />
But Flordelice aye followed thee,” she cries:<br />
“Well aided mightest thou have been by me;<br />
For I on thee should still have kept my eyes;<br />
And when Gradasso came behind thee, I<br />
Thee might have succoured with a single cry;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXI.</td>
<td>“And haply I so nimbly might have made<br />
Between you, that the stroke I might have caught,<br />
And with my head, as with a buckler, stayed:<br />
For little ill my dying would have wrought.<br />
Anyhow I shall die; and—that debt paid—<br />
My melancholy death will profit nought:<br />
When, had I died, defending thee in strife,<br />
I could not better have bestowed my life.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXII.</td>
<td>“Even is averse had been hard Destiny,<br />
And all heaven’s host, when thee I sought to aid,<br />
At least my tears had bathed thy visage, I<br />
Should the last kiss thereon, at least, have laid;<br />
And, ere amid the blessed hierarchy<br />
Thy spirit mixt, ‘Depart’—I should have said—<br />
‘In peace, and wait me in thy rest; for there,<br />
Where’er thou art, I swiftly shall repair.’</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXIII.</td>
<td>“Is this, O Brandimart, is this the reign,<br />
Whose honoured sceptre thou wast now to take?<br />
With thee to Dommogire, thy fair domain,<br />
Thus went I; me thus welcome dost thou make?<br />
Alas! what hope to-day thou renderest vain!<br />
Ah! what designs, fell Fortune, dost thou break!<br />
Ah! wherefore fear I, since a lot so blest,<br />
Is lost, to lose as well the worthless rest?”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXIV.</td>
<td>Repeating this and other plaint, so spite<br />
And fury waxed, that she in her despair<br />
Made new assault upon her tresses bright,<br />
As if the fault was wholly in her hair:<br />
Wildly her hands together doth she smite,<br />
And gnaw; with nails her lip and bosom tear.<br />
But I return to Roland and his peers;<br />
While she bemoans herself and melts in tears.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXV.</td>
<td>Roland with Olivier, who much requires<br />
Such leech’s care, his anguish to allay;<br />
And who, himself, some worthy place desires<br />
As much, wherein Sir Brandimart to lay,<br />
Steers for the lofty mountain, that with fires<br />
Brightens the night, with smoke obscures the day.<br />
The wind blows fair, and on the starboard hand,<br />
Not widely distant from them, lies that land.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXVI.</td>
<td>With a fresh wind, that in their favour blows,<br />
They loose their hawser at the close of day:<br />
In heaven above the silent goddess shows<br />
Her shining horn, to guide them on their way;<br />
And on the following morn before them rose<br />
The pleasant shores that round Girgenti lay.<br />
Here Roland orders for the ensuing night<br />
All that is needful for the funeral rite.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXVII.</td>
<td>He, when he saw his order duly done,<br />
And now the westering sun’s fair light was spent.<br />
With many nobles, who from neighbouring town,<br />
At his invital, to Girgenti went,<br />
—The shore with torches blazing up and down,<br />
And sounding wide with cries and loud lament,—<br />
Thither returned where late, of life bereft,<br />
His friends, beloved in life and death, was left.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXVIII.</td>
<td>There stands Bardino, weeping o’er the bier,<br />
Who under Age’s heavy burden bows;<br />
Who, in the tears on shipboard shed whilere.<br />
Might well have wept away his eyes and brows:<br />
Upbraiding skies and stars, the cavalier,<br />
Like lion, in whose veins a fever glows,<br />
Roars as he wreathes his wayward hands within<br />
His hoary hair, and rends his wrinkled skin.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXIX.</td>
<td>Upon the paladin’s return the cry<br />
Redoubled, and the mourning louder grew<br />
Orlando to the corse approached more nigh,<br />
And speechless stood awhile, his friends to view,<br />
Pale, as at eve is the acanthus’ dye<br />
Or lily’s, which were plucked at morn: he drew<br />
A heavy sigh, and on the warrior dead<br />
Fixing his stedfast eyes, the County said:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXX.</td>
<td>“O comrade bold and true, there here liest slain,<br />
And who dost live in heaven above, I know,<br />
Rewarded with a life, thy glorious gain,<br />
Which neither heat nor cold can take, my woe<br />
Forgive, if thou beholdest me complain:<br />
Because I sorrow to remain below,<br />
And not to share in such delights with thee;<br />
Not that thou art not left behind with me.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXI.</td>
<td>“Alone, without thee, there is nought I may<br />
Ever possess, without thee, that can please.<br />
If still with thee in tempest and affray,<br />
Ah wherefore not with thee in calm and ease?<br />
Right sore must be my trespass, since this clay<br />
Will not to follow thee my soul release.<br />
If in thy troubles still I bore a burden,<br />
Why am I not a partner of thy guerdon?</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXII.</td>
<td>“Thine is the guerdon; mine the loss; thy gain<br />
Is single; but not single is my woe:<br />
Partners with me in sorrow are Almayne,<br />
And grieving France and Italy; and oh!<br />
How will my lord and uncle, Charlemagne,<br />
How will his paladins lament the blow!<br />
How will the Christian church and empire moan,<br />
Whose best defence in thee is overthrown!</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXIII.</td>
<td>“Oh! how thy foes will by the death of thee<br />
Be freed henceforward from alarm and fear!<br />
Alas! how strengthened paynimry will be!<br />
What hardiment will now be theirs! what cheer!<br />
What of thy consort will become? I see<br />
Even here her mourning, and her outcries hear.<br />
Me she accuses, haply hates, I know;<br />
In that, through me, her every hope lies low.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXIV.</td>
<td>“Yet by one comfort, Flordelice, is followed<br />
His loss, for us that reft of him remain:<br />
His death, with such surpassing glory hallowed,<br />
To die all living warriors should be fain.<br />
Those Decii; Curtius, in Rome’s forum swallowed;<br />
Cordus, so vaunted by the Grecian train;<br />
Not with more honour to themselves, with more<br />
Profit to others, went to death of yore.”</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXV.</td>
<td>These sad laments and more Orlando made;<br />
And all this while white friars, and black, and gray,<br />
With other clerks, by two and two arrayed,<br />
Behind in long procession took their way;<br />
And they to God for the departed prayed,<br />
That he would to his rest his soul convey.<br />
Before and all about were torches reared,<br />
And changed to day the sable night appeared.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXVI.</td>
<td>They raise the warrior’s bier, and ranged to bear<br />
By turns that honoured weight were earl and knight.<br />
The pall was purple silk, with broidery rare<br />
Of gold, and pearls in costly circles dight.<br />
Thereon, of lordly work and no less fair,<br />
Cushions were laid, with jewels shining bright.<br />
On which was stretched the lifeless knight in view,<br />
Arrayed in vest of like device and hue.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXVII.</td>
<td>A hundred men had past before the rest,<br />
All taken from the poorest of the town;<br />
And in one fashion equally were drest<br />
Those beadsmen all, in black and trailing gown.<br />
A hundred pages followed them, who prest<br />
A hundred puissant steeds, for warfare bown;<br />
And by those pages backed, the portly steeds<br />
Went, sweeping wide the ground with sable weeds.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXVIII.</td>
<td>Banners in front and banners borne in rear,<br />
Whose fields with diverse ensignry is stained,<br />
Unfurled accompany the funeral bier;<br />
Which from a thousand vanquished bands were gained,<br />
For Caesar and for Peter’s church whilere,<br />
By that rare force, which now extinct remained.<br />
Bucklers by other followers carried are,<br />
Won from good warriors, whose device they bear.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXIX.</td>
<td>By hundreds and by hundreds followed more,<br />
Ordained for different tasks, the steps of those;<br />
Who burning torches like those others bore.<br />
Mantled, say rather closely muffled, goes<br />
Roland in sables next, and evermore<br />
His eyes suffused and red with weeping shows.<br />
Nor wears a gladder face Montalban’s peer.<br />
At home his wound detains Sir Olivier.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXX.</td>
<td>The ceremonies would be long to say<br />
In verse, wherewith Sir Brandimart was mourned;<br />
The mantles, black or purple, given away;<br />
The many torches which that eve were burned.<br />
Wending to the cathedral, where the array<br />
Past on its road, were no dry eyes discerned:<br />
All sexes, ages, ranks, in pitying mood<br />
Gazed upon him so youthful, fair, and good.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXI.</td>
<td>He in the church was placed; and, when with vain<br />
Lament the women had bemoaned the dead,<br />
And Kyrie Eleison, by the priestly train,<br />
And other holy orisons were said,<br />
In a fair ark, upraised on columns twain,<br />
Was reared, with sumptuous cloth of gold o’erspread.<br />
So willed Orlando; till he could be laid<br />
In sepulchre of costlier matter made:</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXII.</td>
<td>Nor out of Sicily the Count departs,<br />
Till porphyries he procures and alabasters,<br />
And fair designs; and in their several arts<br />
Has with large hire engaged the primest masters.<br />
Next Flordelice, arriving in those parts,<br />
Raises the quarried slabs and rich pilasters;<br />
Who, good Orlando being gone before,<br />
Is hither wafted from the Africk shore.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXIII.</td>
<td>She, seeing that her tears unceasing flow,<br />
And that of long lament she never tires;<br />
Nor she, for mass or service said, her woe<br />
Can ease, or satisfy her sad desires,<br />
Vows in her heart she thence will never go<br />
Till from the wearied corse her soul expires;<br />
And builds in that fair sepulchre a cell;<br />
There shuts herself; therein for life will dwell.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXIV.</td>
<td>Thither in person, having courier sent<br />
And letter, Roland goes, her thence to take;<br />
Her, would she wend to France, with goodly rent<br />
Would gift, and Galerana’s inmate make;<br />
As far as Lizza convoy her, if bent<br />
On journeying to her father; for her sake<br />
If wholly she to serve her God was willed,<br />
A monastery would the warrior build.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXV.</td>
<td>Still in that sepulchre she dwelt, and worn<br />
By weary penance, praying night and day,<br />
It was not long, ere by the Parcae shorn<br />
Was her life’s thread: already on their way<br />
Were the three Christian warriors, homeward borne,<br />
Sorrowing and afflicted sore in mind<br />
For their fourth comrade who remained behind.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXVI.</td>
<td>They would not go without a leech, whose skill<br />
Might ease the wound of warlike Olivier;<br />
Which, as in the beginning it could ill<br />
Be salved, is hard to heal. Meanwhile they hear<br />
The champion so complain, his outcries fill<br />
Orlando and all that company with fear.<br />
While they discoursed thereon, the skipper, moved<br />
By a new notion, said what all approved.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXVII.</td>
<td>A hermit not far distance hence, he said<br />
A lonely rock inhabits in this sea;<br />
Whose isle none, seeking succour, vainly tread,<br />
Whether for counsel or for aid it be:<br />
Who hath done superhuman deeds; the dead<br />
Restores to life; and makes the blind to see;<br />
Hushes the winds; and with a sign o’ the cross<br />
Lulls the loud billows when they highest toss;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXVIII.</td>
<td>And adds they need not doubt, if they will go<br />
To seek that holy man to God so dear,<br />
But he on Olivier will health bestow;<br />
Having his virtue proved by signs more clear.<br />
This counsel pleases good Orlando so,<br />
That for the holy place he bids him steer;<br />
Who never swerving from his course, espies<br />
The lonely rock, upon Aurora’s rise.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CLXXXIX.</td>
<td>Worked by good mariners, the bark was laid<br />
Safely beside the rugged rock and fell:<br />
The marquis there, with crew and servants’ aid,<br />
They lowered into their boat; and through the swell<br />
And foaming waters in that shallop made<br />
For the rude isle; thence sought the holy cell;<br />
The holy cell of that same hermit hoar,<br />
By whom Rogero was baptized before.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXC.</td>
<td>The servant of the Lord of Paradise<br />
Receives Orlando and the rest on land;<br />
Blesses the company in cheerful wise;<br />
And after of their errand makes demand;<br />
Though he already had received advice<br />
From angels of the coming of that band.<br />
That they were thither bound in search of aid<br />
For Oliviero’s hurt, Orlando said;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCI.</td>
<td>Who, warring for the Christian faith, in fight<br />
To perilous pass was brought by evil wound.<br />
All dismal fear relieved that eremite,<br />
And promised he would make him wholly sound.<br />
In that no unguents hath the holy wight,<br />
Nor is in other human medicine found,<br />
His church he seeks, his knee to Jesus bows,<br />
And issues from the fane with cheerful brows;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCII.</td>
<td>And in the name of those eternal Three,<br />
The Father, and the Son, and Holy Ghost,<br />
On Oliviero bade his blessing be.<br />
Oh! grace vouchsafed to faith! his sainted host<br />
From every pain the paladin did free;<br />
And to his foot restored its vigour lost.<br />
He moved more nimble than before, and sure;<br />
And present was Sobrino at the cure.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCIII.</td>
<td>Sobrino, so diseased that he described<br />
How worse with each succeeding day he grew,<br />
As soon as he that holy monk espied<br />
The manifest and mighty marvel do,<br />
Disposed himself to cast Mahound aside,<br />
And own in Christ a living God and true.<br />
He, full of faith, with contrite heart demands<br />
Our holy rite of baptism at his hands.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCIV.</td>
<td>So him baptized the hermit; and as well<br />
That monarch made as vigorous as whilere.<br />
At this conversion no less gladness fell<br />
On Roland and each Christian cavalier,<br />
Than when, restored from deadly wound, and well<br />
The friendly troop beheld Sir Olivier.<br />
Rogero more rejoiced than all that crew;<br />
And still in faith and grace the warrior grew.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCV.</td>
<td>Rogero from the day he swam ashore<br />
Upon that islet, there had ever been.<br />
That band is counselled by the hermit hoar,<br />
Who stands, benign, those warlike knights between,<br />
Eschewing in their passage mire and moor,<br />
To wade withal through that dead water, clean,<br />
Which men call life; wherein so fools delight;<br />
And evermore on heaven to fix their sight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCVI.</td>
<td>Roland on shipboard sends one from his throng,<br />
Who fetches hence good wine, hams, cheese, and bread;<br />
And makes the sage, who had forgotten long<br />
All taste of partridge since on fruits he fed,<br />
Even do for love, what others did, among<br />
Those social guests for whom the board was spread.<br />
They, when their strength by food was reinforced,<br />
Of many things amid themselves discoursed;</td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCVII.</td>
<td>And as in talk it often doth befall<br />
That one thing from another takes its rise,<br />
Roland and Olivier Rogero call<br />
To mind for that Rogero, in such wise<br />
Renowned in arms; whose valour is of all<br />
Lauded and echoed with accordant cries.<br />
Not even had Rinaldo known the knight<br />
For him whose prowess he had proved in fight.</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td style="text-align: right;" data-valign="top">CXCVIII.</td>
