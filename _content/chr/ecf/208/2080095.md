<span id="ix.xxiii-p1"></span> <span class="c18"><span
id="ix.xxiii-p1.1"></span>Letter XXII. <span
id="fr_1916"></span>[1917](#fn_1916) </span>

<span id="ix.xxiii-p3"></span>* Without address.  On the Perfection of
the Life of Solitaries*.

<span id="ix.xxiii-p4"></span>1.  <span class="c14"><span
id="ix.xxiii-p4.1"></span>Many</span> things are set forth by inspired
Scripture as binding upon all who are anxious to please God.  But, for
the present, I have only deemed it necessary to speak by <span
id="page_128">p. 128</span> <span id="ix.xxiii-Page_128"></span>way of
brief reminder concerning the questions which have recently been stirred
among you, so far as I have learnt from the study of inspired Scripture
itself.  I shall thus leave behind me detailed evidence, easy of
apprehension, for the information of industrious students, who in their
turn will be able to inform others.  The Christian ought to be so minded
as becomes his heavenly calling, <span
id="fr_1917"></span>[1918](#fn_1917) and his life and conversation ought
to be worthy of the Gospel of Christ. <span
id="fr_1918"></span>[1919](#fn_1918)   The Christian ought not to be of
doubtful mind, <span id="fr_1919"></span>[1920](#fn_1919) nor by
anything drawn away from the recollection of God and of His purposes and
judgments.  The Christian ought in all things to become superior to the
righteousness existing under the law, and neither swear nor lie. <span
id="fr_1920"></span>[1921](#fn_1920)   He ought not to speak evil; <span
id="fr_1921"></span>[1922](#fn_1921) to do violence; <span
id="fr_1922"></span>[1923](#fn_1922) to fight; <span
id="fr_1923"></span>[1924](#fn_1923) to avenge himself; <span
id="fr_1924"></span>[1925](#fn_1924) to return evil for evil; <span
id="fr_1925"></span>[1926](#fn_1925) to be angry. <span
id="fr_1926"></span>[1927](#fn_1926)   The Christian ought to be
patient, <span id="fr_1927"></span>[1928](#fn_1927) whatever he have to
suffer, and to convict the wrong-doer in season, <span
id="fr_1928"></span>[1929](#fn_1928) not with the desire of his own
vindication, but of his brother’s reformation, <span
id="fr_1929"></span>[1930](#fn_1929) according to the commandment of the
Lord.  The Christian ought not to say anything behind his brother’s back
with the object of calumniating him, for this is slander, even if what
is said is true. <span id="fr_1930"></span>[1931](#fn_1930)   He ought
to turn away from the brother who speaks evil against him; <span
id="fr_1931"></span>[1932](#fn_1931) he ought not to indulge in
jesting; <span id="fr_1932"></span>[1933](#fn_1932) he ought not to
laugh nor even to suffer laugh makers. <span
id="fr_1933"></span>[1934](#fn_1933)   He must not talk idly, saying
things which are of no service to the hearers nor to such usage as is
necessary and permitted us by God; <span
id="fr_1934"></span>[1935](#fn_1934) so that workers may do their best
as far as possible to work in silence; and that good words be suggested
to them by those who are entrusted with the duty of carefully dispensing
the word to the building up of the faith, lest God’s Holy Spirit be
grieved.  Any one who comes in ought not to be able, of his own free
will, to accost or speak to any of the brothers, before those to whom
the responsibility of general discipline is committed have approved of
it as pleasing to God, with a view to the common good. <span
id="fr_1935"></span>[1936](#fn_1935)   The Christian ought not to be
enslaved by wine; <span id="fr_1936"></span>[1937](#fn_1936) nor to be
eager for flesh meat, <span id="fr_1937"></span>[1938](#fn_1937) and as
a general rule ought not to be a lover of pleasure in eating or
drinking, <span id="fr_1938"></span>[1939](#fn_1938) “for every man that
striveth for the mastery is temperate in all things.” <span
id="fr_1939"></span>[1940](#fn_1939)   The Christian ought to regard all
the things that are given him for his use, not as his to hold as his own
or to lay up; <span id="fr_1940"></span>[1941](#fn_1940) and, giving
careful heed to all things as the Lord’s, not to overlook any of the
things that are being thrown aside and disregarded, should this be the
case.  No Christian ought to think of himself as his own master, but
each should rather so think and act as though given by God to be slave
to his like minded brethren; <span id="fr_1941"></span>[1942](#fn_1941)
but “every man in his own order.” <span
id="fr_1942"></span>[1943](#fn_1942)

<span id="ix.xxiii-p31"></span>2.  The Christian ought never to
murmur <span id="fr_1943"></span>[1944](#fn_1943) either in scarcity of
necessities, or in toil or labour, for the responsibility in these
matters lies with such as have authority in them.  There never ought to
be any clamour, or any behaviour or agitation by which anger is
expressed, <span id="fr_1944"></span>[1945](#fn_1944) or diversion of
mind from the full assurance of the presence of God. <span
id="fr_1945"></span>[1946](#fn_1945)

<span id="ix.xxiii-p35"></span>The voice should be modulated; no one
ought to answer another, or do anything, roughly or
contemptuously, <span id="fr_1946"></span>[1947](#fn_1946) but in all
things moderation <span id="fr_1947"></span>[1948](#fn_1947) and respect
should be shewn to every one. <span id="fr_1948"></span>[1949](#fn_1948)
  No wily glances of the eye are to be allowed, nor any behaviour or
gestures which grieve a brother and shew contempt. <span
id="fr_1949"></span>[1950](#fn_1949)   Any display in cloak or shoes is
to be avoided; it is idle ostentation. <span
id="fr_1950"></span>[1951](#fn_1950)   Cheap things ought to be used for
bodily necessity; and nothing ought to be spent beyond what is
necessary, or for mere extravagance; this is a misuse of our property. 
The Christian ought not to seek for honour, or claim precedence. <span
id="fr_1951"></span>[1952](#fn_1951)   Every one ought to put all others
before himself. <span id="fr_1952"></span>[1953](#fn_1952)   The
Christian ought not to be unruly. <span
id="fr_1953"></span>[1954](#fn_1953)   He who is able to work ought not
to eat the bread of idleness, <span id="fr_1954"></span>[1955](#fn_1954)
but even he who is busied in deeds well done for the glory of Christ
ought to force himself to the active discharge of such work as he can
do. <span id="fr_1955"></span>[1956](#fn_1955)   Every Christian, with
the approval of his superiors, ought so to do everything with reason and
assurance, even down to actual eating and drinking, as done to the glory
of God. <span id="fr_1956"></span>[1957](#fn_1956)   The Christian ought
not to change over from one work to another without the approval of
those who are appointed for the <span id="page_129">p. 129</span> <span
id="ix.xxiii-Page_129"></span>arrangement of such matters; unless some
unavoidable necessity suddenly summon any one to the relief of the
helpless.  Every one ought to remain in his appointed post, not to go
beyond his own bounds and intrude into what is not commanded him, unless
the responsible authorities judge any one to be in need of aid.  No one
ought to be found going from one workshop to another.  Nothing ought to
be done in rivalry or strife with any one.

<span id="ix.xxiii-p47"></span>3.  The Christian ought not to grudge
another’s reputation, nor rejoice over any man’s faults; <span
id="fr_1957"></span>[1958](#fn_1957) he ought in Christ’s love to grieve
and be afflicted at his brother’s faults, and rejoice over his brother’s
good deeds. <span id="fr_1958"></span>[1959](#fn_1958)   He ought not to
be indifferent or silent before sinners. <span
id="fr_1959"></span>[1960](#fn_1959)   He who shows another to be wrong
ought to do so with all tenderness, <span
id="fr_1960"></span>[1961](#fn_1960) in the fear of God, and with the
object of converting the sinner. <span
id="fr_1961"></span>[1962](#fn_1961)   He who is proved wrong or rebuked
ought to take it willingly, recognizing his own gain in being set
right.  When any one is being accused, it is not right for another,
before him or any one else, to contradict the accuser; but if at any
time the charge seems groundless to any one, he ought privately to enter
into discussion with the accuser, and either produce, or acquire,
conviction.  Every one ought, as far as he is able, to conciliate one
who has ground of complaint against him.  No one ought to cherish a
grudge against the sinner who repents, but heartily to forgive
him. <span id="fr_1962"></span>[1963](#fn_1962)   He who says that he
has repented of a sin ought not only to be pricked with compunction for
his sin, but also to bring forth fruits worthy of repentance. <span
id="fr_1963"></span>[1964](#fn_1963)   He who has been corrected in
first faults, and received pardon, if he sins again prepares for himself
a judgment of wrath worse than the former. <span
id="fr_1964"></span>[1965](#fn_1964)   He, who after the first and
second admonition <span id="fr_1965"></span>[1966](#fn_1965) abides in
his fault, ought to be brought before the person in authority, <span
id="fr_1966"></span>[1967](#fn_1966) if haply after being rebuked by
more he may be ashamed. <span id="fr_1967"></span>[1968](#fn_1967)   If
even thus he fail to be set right he is to be cut off from the rest as
one that maketh to offend, and regarded as a heathen and a
publican, <span id="fr_1968"></span>[1969](#fn_1968) for the security of
them that are obedient, according to the saying, When the impious fall
the righteous tremble. <span id="fr_1969"></span>[1970](#fn_1969)   He
should be grieved over as a limb cut from the body.  The sun ought not
to go down upon a brother’s wrath, <span
id="fr_1970"></span>[1971](#fn_1970) lest haply night come between
brother and brother, and make the charge stand in the day of judgment. 
A Christian ought not to wait for an opportunity for his own
amendment, <span id="fr_1971"></span>[1972](#fn_1971) because there is
no certainty about the morrow; for many after many devices have not
reached the morrow.  He ought not to be beguiled by over eating, whence
come dreams in the night.  He ought not to be distracted by immoderate
toil, nor overstep the bounds of sufficiency, as the apostle says,
“Having food and raiment let us be therewith content;” <span
id="fr_1972"></span>[1973](#fn_1972) unnecessary abundance gives
appearance of covetousness, and covetousness is condemned as
idolatry. <span id="fr_1973"></span>[1974](#fn_1973)   A Christian ought
not to be a lover of money, <span id="fr_1974"></span>[1975](#fn_1974)
nor lay up treasure for unprofitable ends.  He who comes to God ought to
embrace poverty in all things, and to be riveted in the fear of God,
according to the words, “Rivet my flesh in thy fear, for I am afraid of
thy judgments.” <span id="fr_1975"></span>[1976](#fn_1975)   The Lord
grant that you may receive what I have said with full conviction and
shew forth fruits worthy of the Spirit to the glory of God, by God’s
good pleasure, and the cooperation of our Lord Jesus Christ.
