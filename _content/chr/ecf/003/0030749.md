<span id="vi.iv.v-p1"></span> <span class="c1"><span
id="vi.iv.v-p1.1"></span>Chapter V.—The Fourth Clause.</span>

<span id="vi.iv.v-p2"></span>“Thy kingdom come” has also reference to
that whereto “Thy will be done” refers—*in us*, that is. For when does
God not reign, in whose hand is the heart of all kings? <span
id="fr_8789"></span>[8790](#fn_8789) But whatever we wish for ourselves
we augur for Him, and *to* Him we attribute what *from* Him we expect.
And so, if the *manifestation* of the Lord’s kingdom pertains unto the
will of God and unto our anxious expectation, how do some pray for some
protraction of the age, <span id="fr_8790"></span>[8791](#fn_8790) when
the kingdom of God, which we pray may arrive, tends unto the
consummation of the age? <span id="fr_8791"></span>[8792](#fn_8791) Our
wish is, that our reign be hastened, not our servitude protracted. Even
if it had not been prescribed in the Prayer that we should ask for the
advent of the kingdom, we should, unbidden, have sent forth that cry,
hastening toward the realization of our hope. The souls of the martyrs
beneath the altar <span id="fr_8792"></span>[8793](#fn_8792) cry in
jealousy unto the Lord, “How long, Lord, dost Thou not avenge our blood
on the inhabitants of the earth?” <span
id="fr_8793"></span>[8794](#fn_8793) for, of course, their avenging is
regulated by <span id="fr_8794"></span>[8795](#fn_8794) the end of the
age. Nay, Lord, Thy kingdom come with all speed,—the prayer of
Christians the confusion of the heathen, <span
id="fr_8795"></span>[8796](#fn_8795) the exultation of angels, for the
sake of which we suffer, nay, rather, for the sake of which we pray!
