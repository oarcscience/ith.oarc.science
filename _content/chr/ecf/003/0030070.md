<span id="iv.iv.xii-p1"></span> <span class="c1"><span
id="iv.iv.xii-p1.1"></span>Chapter XII.—Further Answers to the Plea, How
Am I to Live?</span>

<span id="iv.iv.xii-p2"></span>In vain do we flatter ourselves as to the
necessities of human maintenance, if—after faith sealed <span
id="fr_237"></span>[238](#fn_237) —we say, “I have no means to
live?” <span id="fr_238"></span>[239](#fn_238) For here I will now
answer more fully that abrupt proposition. It is advanced *too late*.
For after the similitude of that most prudent builder, <span
id="fr_239"></span>[240](#fn_239) who first computes the costs of the
work, together with his own means, lest, when he has begun, he
afterwards blush to find himself spent, deliberation should have been
made *before*. But even now you have the Lord’s sayings, as examples
taking away from you all excuse.  For what is it you say? “I shall be in
need.” But the Lord calls the needy “happy.” <span
id="fr_240"></span>[241](#fn_240) “I shall have no food.” But “think
not,” says He, “about food;” <span id="fr_241"></span>[242](#fn_241) and
as an example of clothing we have the lilies. <span
id="fr_242"></span>[243](#fn_242) “My work was my subsistence.” Nay, but
“all things are to be sold, and divided to the needy.” <span
id="fr_243"></span>[244](#fn_243) “But provision must be made for
children and posterity.” “None, putting his hand on the plough, and
looking back, is fit” for work. <span id="fr_244"></span>[245](#fn_244)
“But I was under contract.” “None can serve two lords.” <span
id="fr_245"></span>[246](#fn_245) If you wish to be the Lord’s disciple,
it is necessary you “take your cross, and follow the Lord:” <span
id="fr_246"></span>[247](#fn_246) *your cross*; that is, your own
*straits* and *tortures*, or your *body* only, which is after the manner
of a *cross*. Parents, wives, children, will have to be left behind, for
God’s sake. <span id="fr_247"></span>[248](#fn_247) Do you hesitate
about arts, and trades, and about professions likewise, for the sake of
children and parents? Even there was it demonstrated to us, that both
“dear pledges,” <span id="fr_248"></span>[249](#fn_248) and handicrafts,
and trades, are to be quite left behind for the Lord’s sake; while James
and John, called by the Lord, do leave quite behind both father and
ship; <span id="fr_249"></span>[250](#fn_249) while Matthew is roused up
from the toll-booth; <span id="fr_250"></span>[251](#fn_250) while even
burying a father was too tardy a business for faith. <span
id="fr_251"></span>[252](#fn_251) None of them whom the Lord chose to
Him said, “I have no means to live.” Faith fears not famine. It knows,
likewise, that hunger is no less to be contemned by it for God’s sake,
than every kind of death. It has learnt not to respect *life*; how much
more *food*? \[You ask\] “How many have fulfilled these conditions?” But
what with men is difficult, with God is easy. <span
id="fr_252"></span>[253](#fn_252) Let us, however, comfort ourselves
about the gentleness and clemency of God in such wise, as not to indulge
our “necessities” up to the point of affinities with idolatry, but to
avoid even from afar every breath of it, as of a pestilence. \[And
this\] not merely in the cases forementioned, but in the universal
series of human superstition; whether appropriated to its gods, or to
the defunct, or to kings, as pertaining to the selfsame unclean spirits,
sometimes through sacrifices and priesthoods, sometimes through
spectacles and the like, sometimes through holy-days.
