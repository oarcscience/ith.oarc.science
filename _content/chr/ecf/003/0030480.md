<span id="v.v.xi-p1"></span> <span class="c1"><span
id="v.v.xi-p1.1"></span>Chapter XI.—Hermogenes Makes Great Efforts to
Remove Evil from God to Matter. How He Fails to Do This Consistently
with His Own Argument.</span>

<span id="v.v.xi-p2"></span>But, after all, <span
id="fr_6226"></span>[6227](#fn_6226) by what proofs does Hermogenes
persuade us that Matter is evil? For it will be impossible for him not
to call that evil to which he imputes evil. Now we lay down this
principle, <span id="fr_6227"></span>[6228](#fn_6227) that what is
eternal cannot possibly admit of diminution and subjection, so as to be
considered inferior to another co-eternal Being. So that we now affirm
that evil is not even compatible with it, <span
id="fr_6228"></span>[6229](#fn_6228) since it is incapable of
subjection, from the fact that it cannot in any wise be subject to any,
because it is eternal.  But inasmuch as, on other grounds, <span
id="fr_6229"></span>[6230](#fn_6229) it is evident what is eternal as
God is the highest good, whereby also He alone is good—as being eternal,
and therefore good—as being God, how can evil be inherent in Matter,
which (since it is eternal) must needs be believed to be the highest
good? Else if that which is eternal prove to be also capable of evil,
this (evil) will be able to be also believed of God to His
prejudice; <span id="fr_6230"></span>[6231](#fn_6230) so that it is
without adequate reason that he has been so anxious <span
id="fr_6231"></span>[6232](#fn_6231) to remove evil from God; since evil
must be compatible with an eternal Being, even by being made compatible
with Matter, *as Hermogenes makes it*. But, as the argument now
stands, <span id="fr_6232"></span>[6233](#fn_6232) since what is eternal
can be deemed evil, the evil must prove to be invincible and
insuperable, as being eternal; and in that case <span
id="fr_6233"></span>[6234](#fn_6233) it will be in vain that we labour
“to put away evil from the midst of us;” <span
id="fr_6234"></span>[6235](#fn_6234) in that case, moreover, God vainly
gives us such a command and precept; nay more, in vain has God appointed
any judgment at all, when He means, indeed, <span
id="fr_6235"></span>[6236](#fn_6235) to inflict punishment with
injustice.  But if, on the other hand, there is to be an end of evil,
when the chief thereof, the devil, shall “go away into the fire which
God hath prepared for him and his angels” <span
id="fr_6236"></span>[6237](#fn_6236) —having been first “cast into the
bottomless pit;” <span id="fr_6237"></span>[6238](#fn_6237) when
likewise “the manifestation of the children of God” <span
id="fr_6238"></span>[6239](#fn_6238) shall have “delivered the
creature” <span id="fr_6239"></span>[6240](#fn_6239) from evil, which
had been “made subject to vanity;” <span
id="fr_6240"></span>[6241](#fn_6240) when the cattle restored in the
innocence and integrity of their nature <span
id="fr_6241"></span>[6242](#fn_6241) shall be at peace <span
id="fr_6242"></span>[6243](#fn_6242) with the beasts of the field, when
also little children shall play with serpents; <span
id="fr_6243"></span>[6244](#fn_6243) when the Father shall have put
beneath the feet of His Son His enemies, <span
id="fr_6244"></span>[6245](#fn_6244) as being the workers of evil,—if in
this way an *end* is compatible with evil, it must follow of necessity
that a *beginning* is also compatible with it; and Matter will turn out
to have a beginning, by virtue of its having also an end. For whatever
things are set to the account of evil, <span
id="fr_6245"></span>[6246](#fn_6245) have a compatibility with the
condition of evil.
