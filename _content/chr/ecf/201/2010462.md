<span id="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p1"></span> <span class="c11"><span
id="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p1.1"></span>Chapter XVII</span>.—*Constantine’s
Victory.*

<span id="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p2"></span><span class="c11"><span
id="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p2.1"></span>But</span> as soon as he perceived that
his adversaries persisted in their resolution, and were already drawing
their swords, he gave free scope to his indignation, and by a single
charge <span id="fr_3176"></span>[3177](#fn_3176) overthrew in a moment
the entire body of the enemy, thus triumphing at once over them and
their gods.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Footnotes

<span id="fn_3176"></span>[504:3177](2010462.htm#fr_3176) <span
id="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p2.2"></span>

<span id="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p3"></span> “With one shout and charge.” This
does not agree with the account of the final struggle by which Licinius
came into Constantine’s power, as generally given, and lends some
probability to the view that after he had been captured he again
revolted.
