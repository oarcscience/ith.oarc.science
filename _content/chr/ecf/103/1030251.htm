Chapter 1.—The Author Desires the Gift of True Wisdom for Laurentius.

I Cannot express, my beloved son Laurentius, the delight with which I witness your progress in knowledge, and the earnest desire I have that you should be a wise man: not one of those of whom it is said, “Where is the wise? where is the scribe? where is the disputer of this world? hath not God made foolish the wisdom of this world?” 1089 but one of those of whom it is said, “The multitude of the wise is the welfare of the world,” 1090 and such as the apostles wishes those to become, whom he tells,” I would have you wise unto that which is good, and simple concerning evil.” 1091 Now, just as no one can exist of himself, so no one can be wise of himself, but only by the enlightening influence of Him of whom it is written,” All wisdom cometh from the Lord.” 1092
