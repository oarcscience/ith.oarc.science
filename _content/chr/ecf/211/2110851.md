#### <span id="iv.v.iv.x-p0.1"></span>Chapter X.

<span id="iv.v.iv.x-p1"></span>On the weakness of free will.

<span id="iv.v.iv.x-p2"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="iv.v.iv.x-p2.1"></span>For</span> Holy Scripture supports the
freedom of the will where it says: “Keep thy heart with all
diligence,” <span id="fr_1783"></span>[1784](#fn_1783) but the Apostle
indicates its weakness by saying “The Lord keep your hearts and minds in
Christ Jesus.” <span id="fr_1784"></span>[1785](#fn_1784) David asserts
the power of free will, where he says “I have inclined my heart to do
Thy righteous acts,” <span id="fr_1785"></span>[1786](#fn_1785) but the
same man in like manner teaches us its weakness, by praying and saying,
“Incline my heart unto Thy testimonies and not to covetousness:” <span
id="fr_1786"></span>[1787](#fn_1786) Solomon also: “The Lord incline our
hearts unto Himself that we may walk in all His ways and keep His
commandments, and ordinances and judgments.” <span
id="fr_1787"></span>[1788](#fn_1787) The Psalmist denotes the power of
our will, where he says: “Keep thy tongue from evil, and thy lips that
they speak no guile,” <span id="fr_1788"></span>[1789](#fn_1788) our
prayer testifies to its weakness, when we say: “O Lord, set a watch
before my mouth, and keep the door of my lips.” <span
id="fr_1789"></span>[1790](#fn_1789) The importance of our will is
maintained by the Lord, when we find “Break the chains of thy neck, O
captive daughter of Zion:” <span id="fr_1790"></span>[1791](#fn_1790) of
its weakness the prophet sings, when he says: “The Lord looseth them
that are bound:”  and “Thou hast broken my chains: To Thee will I offer
the sacrifice of praise.” <span id="fr_1791"></span>[1792](#fn_1791) We
hear in the gospel the Lord summoning us to come speedily to Him by our
free will: “Come unto Me all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I
will refresh you,” <span id="fr_1792"></span>[1793](#fn_1792) but the
same Lord testifies to its weakness, by saying: “No man can come unto Me
except the Father which sent Me draw him.” <span
id="fr_1793"></span>[1794](#fn_1793) The Apostle indicates our free will
by saying: “So run that ye may obtain:” <span
id="fr_1794"></span>[1795](#fn_1794) but to its weakness John Baptist
bears witness where he says: “No man can receive anything of himself,
except it be given him from above.” <span
id="fr_1795"></span>[1796](#fn_1795) We are commanded to keep our souls
with all care, when the Prophet says: “Keep your souls,” <span
id="fr_1796"></span>[1797](#fn_1796) but by the same spirit another
Prophet proclaims: “Except the Lord keep the city, the watchman waketh
but in vain.” <span id="fr_1797"></span>[1798](#fn_1797) The Apostle
writing to the Philippians, to show that their will is free, says “Work
out your own salvation with fear and trembling,” but to point out its
weakness, he adds: “For it is God that worketh in you both to will and
to do of His good pleasure.” <span id="fr_1798"></span>[1799](#fn_1798)
