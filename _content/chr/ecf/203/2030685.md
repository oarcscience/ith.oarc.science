<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p1"></span> <span id="page_460">p. 460</span>
<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-Page_460"></span><span class="c21"><span
id="vi.xi.iii.i-p1.1"></span>Book II.</span>

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p2"></span>1. Jerome says that the defenders of
Origen are united in a federation of perjury.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p3"></span>2. Jerome’s commentaries on Ephesians
follow Origen’s interpretation of the texts about a secret federation to
whom higher truths are to be told.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p4"></span>3. But I follow Christ in condemning
all falsehood.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p5"></span>4. Jerome has not only allowed perjury
but has practised it.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p6"></span>5. His treatise on Virginity (Ep. xxii
to Eustochium) defames all orders of Christians.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p7"></span>6. In his anti-Ciceronian dream he
promised never to read or possess heathen books.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p8"></span>7. Yet his works are filled with
quotations from them.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p9"></span>8. In his “Best mode of Translation” he
relies on the opinions of Cicero and Horace.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p10"></span>9. He confesses his obligations to
Porphyry.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p11"></span>8 (2). Jerome at Bethlehem had heathen
books copied and taught them to boys.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p12"></span>9 (2). He condemns as heathenish
unobjectionable views which he himself holds.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p13"></span>10 (2). He spoke of Paula impiously as
the mother-in-law of God.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p14"></span>11. Such impiety is unpardonable.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p15"></span>12. Jerome’s boast of his teachers,
Didymus and the Jew Baranina.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p16"></span>13. His extravagant praises of Origen.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p17"></span>14. Preface to Origen on Canticles.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p18"></span>15. Preface to Commentary on Micah.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p19"></span>16. Book of Hebrew Names.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p20"></span>17. A story of Origen.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p21"></span>18. Pamphilus the Martyr and his
Library.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p22"></span>19. Jerome praises Origen but condemns
others for doing the same.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p23"></span>20. Jerome praises the dogmatic as
well as the expository works of Origen.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p24"></span>21. Contrast of Jerome’s earlier and
later attitude towards Origen.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p25"></span>22. The Book of Hebrew Questions.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p26"></span>23. Jerome’s attack upon Ambrose.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p27"></span>24. Preface to Didymus on the Holy
Spirit.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p28"></span>25. Jerome attacks one Christian
writer after another.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p29"></span>26. His treatment of Melania.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p30"></span>27. I never followed Jerome’s errors,
for which he should do penance.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p31"></span>27 a. But I followed his method of
translation.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p32"></span>28. Jerome in condemning me condemns
himself.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p33"></span>29. He says I shew Origen to be
heretical, yet condemns me.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p34"></span>30. His pretence that the Apology for
Origen is not by Pamphilus needs no answer.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p35"></span>31. Others did not translate the <span
class="Greek"><span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p35.1"></span>Περὶ ᾽Αρχῶν</span>
because they did not know Greek.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p36"></span>32. Jerome’s translation of the
Scriptures impugned.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p37"></span>33. Authority of the LXX.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p38"></span>34. Has the Church had spurious
Scriptures?

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p39"></span>35. Danger of altering the Versions of
Scripture.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p40"></span>36. Origen’s Hexapla—Its object.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p41"></span>37. St. Paul’s method of dealing with
erring brethren.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p42"></span>38. How Jerome should have replied to
Pammachius.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p43"></span>39. The Books against Jovinian.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p44"></span>40. My translation of the <span
class="Greek"><span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p44.1"></span>Περὶ ᾽Αρχῶν</span> was
meant to aid in a good cause.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p45"></span>41, 42, 43. Recapitulation of the
Apology.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p46"></span>44. An appeal to Pammachius.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p47"></span>45, 46. Why my translations of Origen
had created offence, but Jerome’s not.

<span id="vi.xi.iii.i-p48"></span>47. A Synod, if called on to condemn
Origen, must condemn Jerome also.
