<span id="v.i.iv-p3"></span>(c) *General History of the Church*. It is
unnecessary to enumerate the well-known general histories, all <span
id="page_xiii">p. xiii</span> <span id="v.i.iv-Page_xiii"></span>of
which devote special pains to Athanasius and the Arian controversy. This
is especially the case with <span class="c10"><span
id="v.i.iv-p3.1"></span>Schaff</span>, *Nicene Christ*. ii. 616–678,
884–893, with full bibliography. See also supra §3. <span
class="c10"><span id="v.i.iv-p3.2"></span>Bright’s</span> *Notes on the
Canons* (Oxf. 1882), and <span class="c10"><span
id="v.i.iv-p3.3"></span>Hefele</span>, vol. 2 (E. Tra.), are most
useful: also <span class="c10"><span
id="v.i.iv-p3.4"></span>Kaye</span>, *Council of Nicæa* (Works, vol. v.
ed. 1888). Card. <span class="c10"><span
id="v.i.iv-p3.5"></span>Hergenröther’s</span> *Kirchengeschichte*
(allowing for the natural bias of the writer) is fair and able, with
good bibliographical references in the notes (ed. 1884). By far the best
modern historical monograph on the Arian period is that of <span
class="c10"><span id="v.i.iv-p3.6"></span>Gwatkin</span>, *Studies of
Arianism*, 1882, constantly referred to in this volume, and
indispensable. His *Arian Controversy*, 1889, is an abridgement, but
with supplementary discussions of importance on one or two points; very
useful bibliography prefixed to both. (Cf. also below, Chap. v. §1)
<span class="c10"><span id="v.i.iv-p3.7"></span>Kölling’s</span>
*Geschichte der Arianischen Häresie* (1st vol., 1874, 2nd, 1883) is
pretentious and uncritical.
