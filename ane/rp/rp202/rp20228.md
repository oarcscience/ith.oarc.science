### Footnotes

<span id="fn_305"></span>[120:1](rp20228.htm#fr_306) *Turtanu*,
"commander-in-chief;" see Isaiah xx. I, 2 Kings xviii. 17.

<span id="fn_306"></span>[120:2](rp20228.htm#fr_307) Perhaps "the chief
of the cup-bearers."

<span id="fn_307"></span>[120:3](rp20228.htm#fr_308) Nisibis.

<span id="fn_308"></span>[121:1](rp20228.htm#fr_309) The Chaldæans, at
this time a tribe in the marshes of Southern Babylonia.

<span id="fn_309"></span>[121:2](rp20228.htm#fr_310) Arrapakhitis.

<span id="fn_310"></span>[121:3](rp20228.htm#fr_311) That is to say, the
troops stayed at home; no military expedition took place.

<span id="fn_311"></span>[121:4](rp20228.htm#fr_312) On the river
Khabour; see 2 Kings xix. 52.

<span id="fn_312"></span>[122:1](rp20228.htm#fr_313) The Minni of the
Old Testament, the Manâ of the Vannic inscriptions, whose territory
extended from the Kotur mountains, the eastern frontier of the kingdom
of Ararat or Van, towards Lake Urumiyeh. The name has no connection with
that of Van.

<span id="fn_313"></span>[122:2](rp20228.htm#fr_314) *Abarakku*, from
the Accadian *abrik;* in Genesis xli. 43 Joseph is called *abrek*, a
word erroneously supposed to be of Egyptian origin. See my Hibbert
Lectures on *Babylonian Religion*, p. 183, where, however, I have
erroneously translated *abrikku* or *abarakku* "vizier." Joseph's cup of
divination is referred to in Genesis xliv. 5.

<span id="fn_314"></span>[122:3](rp20228.htm#fr_315) The Rezeph of
Isaiah xxxvii. 12.

<span id="fn_315"></span>[122:4](rp20228.htm#fr_316) Amida, now
Diarbekir.

<span id="fn_316"></span>[122:5](rp20228.htm#fr_317) *Rab-saki*, "the
chief of the princes," or Vizier.

<span id="fn_317"></span>[123:1](rp20228.htm#fr_318) Or perhaps "the
prefect" (*saladh*).

<span id="fn_318"></span>[123:2](rp20228.htm#fr_319) "The country of the
cedar-trees," *i.e.* Mount Amanus.

<span id="fn_319"></span>[124:1](rp20228.htm#fr_320) The Hadrach of
Zech. ix. 1.

<span id="fn_320"></span>[124:2](rp20228.htm#fr_321) The eclipse was
visible at Nineveh on the 15th of June.

<span id="fn_321"></span>[125:1](rp20228.htm#fr_323) "City" in another
copy.

<span id="fn_322"></span>[125:2](rp20228.htm#fr_324) Or "the prefect."

<span id="fn_323"></span>[126:1](rp20228.htm#fr_325) I cannot explain
the grammatical construction of *tsabtat*.

<span id="fn_324"></span>[126:2](rp20228.htm#fr_326) Or "the prefect."

<span id="fn_325"></span>[126:3](rp20228.htm#fr_327) Probably the Calneh
of Genesis x. 10; Isaiah x. 9.

<span id="fn_326"></span>[126:4](rp20228.htm#fr_328) The Philistines.

<span id="fn_327"></span>[126:5](rp20228.htm#fr_329) This ceremony was
performed at Babylon, and implied that the king was recognised as
legitimate sovereign of Babylonia.

<span id="fn_328"></span>[127:1](rp20228.htm#fr_330) According to the
text published in W. A. I. ii. 69, Dur-Sargon (now Khorsabad). The text
published by Dr. Bezold, however (*Proceedings of the Society of
Biblical Archæology*, xi. 7), gives Dur-yakin, the ancestral capital of
Merodach-baladan in the southern marshes of Babylonia.
