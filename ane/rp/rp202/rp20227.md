### Footnotes

<span id="fn_261"></span>[113:1](rp20227.htm#fr_261) From the form of
the fragment on which this and the following twelve names are preserved,
it has been conjectured by George Smith that the first year of the reign
of Rimmon-nirari II, the father and predecessor of Tiglath-Uras II, was
B.C. 911.

<span id="fn_262"></span>[113:2](rp20227.htm#fr_262) Or perhaps Abu-A,
like the eponym of B.C. 887.

<span id="fn_263"></span>[113:3](rp20227.htm#fr_263) According to George
Smith.

<span id="fn_264"></span>[113:4](rp20227.htm#fr_264) Or Tiglath-Baru. He
is the second king of the name known to us.

<span id="fn_265"></span>[113:5](rp20227.htm#fr_265) Not Malik. For the
god or goddess A, the wife of the Sun-god, see my Hibbert Lectures on
*The Religion of the Ancient Babylonians*, pp 177 *sqq*.

<span id="fn_266"></span>[113:6](rp20227.htm#fr_266) The Biblical
Elimelech, "El is Moloch."

<span id="fn_267"></span>[113:7](rp20227.htm#fr_267) "O Assur save me!"

<span id="fn_268"></span>[113:8](rp20227.htm#fr_268) The reading of the
name is doubtful. It is differently written in the *Annals of
Assur-natsir-pal*, ii. 49. Perhaps it should be pronounced Belaku.

<span id="fn_269"></span>[114:1](rp20227.htm#fr_269) "May the priest of
Assur live long!"

<span id="fn_270"></span>[114:2](rp20227.htm#fr_270) Also given as
Samas-yubla.

<span id="fn_271"></span>[114:3](rp20227.htm#fr_271) Also written
Bir-Raman (Bir-Rimmon).

<span id="fn_272"></span>[114:4](rp20227.htm#fr_272) Or perhaps
Ner-Istar.

<span id="fn_273"></span>[114:5](rp20227.htm#fr_273) "(Born) in the
month Elul."

<span id="fn_274"></span>[114:6](rp20227.htm#fr_274) Shalmaneser was
twice eponym.

<span id="fn_275"></span>[114:7](rp20227.htm#fr_275) "The Sun-god is
Rimmon," like the name of Hadad- Rimmon, "Hadad is Rimmon," in Zech.
xii. 11.

<span id="fn_276"></span>[115:1](rp20227.htm#fr_276) The ideograph
*khal* represents *asâpu*, "to prophesy" or "divine." See the name of
the eponym for B.C. 670.

<span id="fn_277"></span>[115:2](rp20227.htm#fr_277) According to other
lists, Nebo-sarra-utsur. The proper eponym of the year may have died
during his term of office, and a supplementary eponym appointed in his
place.

<span id="fn_278"></span>[115:3](rp20227.htm#fr_279) Omitted in the
Chronicle.

<span id="fn_279"></span>[115:4](rp20227.htm#fr_280) Shalmaneser III.

<span id="fn_280"></span>[115:5](rp20227.htm#fr_281) "The Sun-god is El"
or "god," like Jiphthah-el in Josh. xix. 14, or the Palestinian town of
Ya’aqab-el ("Jacob is El," ? Hebron) and Yesephel ("Joseph is El"),
mentioned by the Egyptian king Thothmes III.

<span id="fn_281"></span>[115:6](rp20227.htm#fr_282) Or more probably
Pan-Assur-la’mur, "I see not the face of Assur;" cf. Exodus xxxiii. 20.

<span id="fn_282"></span>[116:1](rp20227.htm#fr_283) "(He is) my son."

<span id="fn_283"></span>[116:2](rp20227.htm#fr_284) *I.e.* Zadkiel,
Comp. the Hebrew name Zedekiah.

<span id="fn_284"></span>[116:3](rp20227.htm#fr_285) Also written
Beli-taggil, "he trusts in Bel."

<span id="fn_285"></span>[116:4](rp20227.htm#fr_286) Also written Qi’su.

<span id="fn_286"></span>[116:5](rp20227.htm#fr_287) Also written
Assur-bela-yukin.

<span id="fn_287"></span>[116:6](rp20227.htm#fr_288) The line is drawn
here by List IV.

<span id="fn_288"></span>[116:7](rp20227.htm#fr_289) The line is drawn
here by Lists II and III. Probably Tiglath-pileser III seized the crown
in B.C. 745, but was not universally recognised as king until B.C. 743.

<span id="fn_289"></span>[116:8](rp20227.htm#fr_290) "O Bel of Harran
(Genesis xi. 31) protect the lord."

<span id="fn_290"></span>[117:1](rp20227.htm#fr_291) The line is drawn
here by List III.

<span id="fn_291"></span>[117:2](rp20227.htm#fr_292) The name of
"Sennacherib the king" is inserted here in List II. In List IV the
dividing-line is drawn after the name of Yupakhirra-Belu, and is
followed by the name of Sennacherib.

<span id="fn_292"></span>[117:3](rp20227.htm#fr_293) "He who belongs to
the god of fertility," who was the god of Andakhu according to W. A. I.,
v. 16, 38.

<span id="fn_293"></span>[117:4](rp20227.htm#fr_294) Sin-akhi-erba "the
Moon-god has increased the brethren." In List III the name is written by
error Assur-akhi-\[erba\] and a line is drawn both before and after it.

<span id="fn_294"></span>[117:5](rp20227.htm#fr_295) Nabu-sarra-utsur,
"O Nebo protect the king!"

<span id="fn_295"></span>[117:6](rp20227.htm#fr_296) "The father (Bel)
is exalted": the name is identical with the Biblical Abram.

<span id="fn_296"></span>[118:1](rp20227.htm#fr_297) "Atar is El." Atar
or Athar, as Schrader has shown, was the name of the goddess of the
North Arabian tribe of Kedar, and enters into that of Atar-samain or
"Athar of heaven" mentioned by Assur-bani-pal.

<span id="fn_297"></span>[118:2](rp20227.htm#fr_298) "Born in the month
of Tebet."

<span id="fn_298"></span>[118:3](rp20227.htm#fr_299) The date is taken
from George Smith.

<span id="fn_299"></span>[118:4](rp20227.htm#fr_300) "The Arbelite."

<span id="fn_300"></span>[118:5](rp20227.htm#fr_301) List I. ends here.
The names which follow are derived from List III.

<span id="fn_301"></span>[118:6](rp20227.htm#fr_302) Assigned to the
year B.C. 656 by George Smith.

<span id="fn_302"></span>[118:7](rp20227.htm#fr_303) List III ends here.
The names which follow are derived by George Smith from various dated
documents.

<span id="fn_303"></span>[119:1](rp20227.htm#fr_304) "Rimmon have mercy
on me," Barku or Barqu, "the lightning," the Hebrew Baraq, being a name
of Rimmon.

<span id="fn_304"></span>[119:2](rp20227.htm#fr_305) Daddi, whose name
indicates his Syrian origin, was eponym in the reign of Sin-sar-iskun,
one of the last kings of Assyria.
