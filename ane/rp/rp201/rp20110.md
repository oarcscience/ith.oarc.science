### Footnotes

<span id="fn_89"></span>[32:1](rp20110.htm#fr_89.md) That is,
Accado-Sumerian.

<span id="fn_90"></span>[32:2](rp20110.htm#fr_90.md) The name of the king
was really Sarganu (perhaps of the same origin as the Biblical Serug),
but his Accadian subjects misunderstood it, turning it into Sarru-kinu,
"established king," which was written in Sumerian Lugal-ginna.

<span id="fn_91"></span>[32:3](rp20110.htm#fr_91.md) That is, Kassite or
Kossæan.

<span id="fn_92"></span>[33:1](rp20110.htm#fr_92.md) Literally "the
messenger of the treasury (of heaven)."

<span id="fn_93"></span>[34:1](rp20110.htm#fr_93.md) The correct reading of
this word is doubtful.

<span id="fn_94"></span>[35:1](rp20110.htm#fr_94.md) The Assyro-Babylonian
translation is a paraphrase, as in some other instances. The
Accado-Sumerian compound is literally: "The Moon-god has established a
head."
