BOOK THE FOURTH

CHAPTER I

PROEM

ALL those circumstances have now been set forth, which occur previously to the birth, as well as at the actual birth, and after it, and which it seemed necessary to mention, as conducing to a knowledge of the general quality of the contemperament produced. And of the other points, now remaining, by which extrinsic events 1 are contemplated, those regarding the several fortunes of wealth and of rank claim to be taken first into consideration. Each of these fortunes has a distinct relationship; for instance, that of wealth relates to the body, and that of rank to the mind.

Footnotes

117:1 That is to say, such events as are independent of the will, and not necessarily consequent on any peculiar conformation of the mind or body.
