### SEVENTH KHA*ND*A.

1\. He begins with the hymn, Tad id âsa, bhuvaneshu *g*yesh*th*am (Rv.
X, 120). Verily, *g*yesh*th*a, the oldest, is mahat, great. Endowed with
mahat the form of this day is perfect.

2\. Then follows the hymn, Tâ*m* su te kîrtim maghavan mahitvâ (Rv. X,
54), with the auspicious word mahitvâ.

3\. Then follows the hymn, Bhûya id vav*ri*dhe vîryâya (Rv. VI, 30),
with the auspicious word vîrya.

4\. Then follows the hymn, N*rin*âm u tvâ n*ri*ta*m*a*m* gobhir
ukthai*h* (Rv. I, 51, 4), with the auspicious word uktha.

5\. He extends the first two pâdas, which are too small, by one syllable
(Rv. X, 120, 1 a, and Rv. VIII, 69, 2 a) <span
id="fr_450"></span>[2](#fn_448). Into the small heart the vital spirits
are placed, into the small stomach food is placed. It

<span id="page_184">p. 184</span>

serves for the attainment of these desires. He who knows this, obtains
these desires.

6\. The two feet, each consisting of ten syllables (Rv. X, 120, 1 a, b),
serve for the gaining of both kinds of food <span
id="fr_451"></span>[1](#fn_449), of what has feet (animal food), and
what has no feet (vegetable food).

7\. They come to be of eighteen syllables each <span
id="fr_452"></span>[2](#fn_450). Of those which are ten, nine are the
prâ*n*as (openings of the body) <span id="fr_453"></span>[3](#fn_451),
the tenth is the (vital) self. This is the perfection of the (vital)
self; Eight syllables remain in each. He who knows them, obtains
whatever he desires.
