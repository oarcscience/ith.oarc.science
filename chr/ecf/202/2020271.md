<span id="iii.ii.iii-p27"></span>B. *Texts*.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p28"></span>I. *Manuscripts.*—It would indeed be a
desirable work to have a uniform apparatus of the codices, not only of
Sozomen, but of all the Greek Church historians. Admirable as is
Heinchen’s survey of Eusebian <span class="c13"><span
id="iii.ii.iii-p28.1"></span>mss.</span>, it is neither uniform nor
complete. No editor of Sozomen from Stephen down, has deemed it
necessary to work up the detail even as well as Heinchen. Nolte
evidently had the material in hand, but the labor remains to be done.
The numbers and positions of many codices have been changed since the
days of Valesius, Montfaucon, and Hænelius, and it is impossible to
bring harmony out of the differences without direct inspection. It would
seem as if no one had consulted some of those mentioned by Montfaucon;
e.g. in the Inventarium <span class="c11"><span
id="iii.ii.iii-p28.2"></span>mss.</span> monasterii S. Petri
Carnutensis, the title is given without number; Socratis, Sozomenis et
Theodoreti historia Ecclesiastica, vol. in fol. notat P. sæculo ix. (ii.
1246); and the two described by Hænelius (*a*) Socratis, Sozomenis
historia ecclesiastica memb. fol. exemp. vetus., at Chartres (Fasc. i.
col. 130). (*b*) iv. 2, Hermiæ Sozomeni Salaminis, historicæ
ecclesiasticæ lib. ix. (Fasc. iii. c. 93) in the Escorial.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p29"></span>II. *Editions of Text.*—1. The first
printed text without translation was by Robert Stephen: Eusebii
Pamphili, Ecclesiasticæ historiæ, libri x.; ejusdem de vita Constantini,
libri v.; Socratis, libri vii.; Theodoreti episc. Cyrenensis, libri v.;
Collectaneorum ex historia ecclesiastica Theodori lectoris, libri ii.;
Hermiæ Sozomeni, libri ix.; Evagrii, libri vi.; Græce Excud. Rob. Steph.
Lutetiæ Parisior. (pridie Cal. Jul.) 1544. Fol.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p30"></span><span id="page_226">p. 226</span> <span
id="iii.ii.iii-Page_226"></span>The sole manuscript at the basis of this
edition is the Codex Regius bibliothecæ Parisiensis, n. 1437 (Nolte,
1444) Possevin (App. crit.) says: A Græca vero edito proviget ann. 1545,
but this seems a mistake.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p31"></span>2. The next edition of the text was
accompanied with a Latin translation; Græca et Latine ex interpretatione
J. Christophorsoni et recognitione Suffredi Petri una cum variis
lectionibus, J. Christophorsoni, Jos. Scaligeri, Jac. Cuiaci, Jan.
Gruteri, Jac. Bongarsii, Col. All. (Geneva) 1612. 2 vols. in fol. This
was the text of Stephen with marginal notes of the above \[see Hussey,
Nolte\]. The sources of the notes are not sure.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p32"></span>3. Reprint of Geneva edition:
Bibliotheca magna veterum patrum et antiq. Scriptorum eccles. primo a
Margarino de la Bigne collecta. Tom. v. has Soc. and Soz. with the Latin
of Christophorson and Suff. Petrus. Colon. 1618–22.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p33"></span>4. The edition of Valesius, 1659–1668,
with a new translation by himself. Socratis Scholastici et Hermiæ
Sozomeni historia ecclesiastica. Henricus Valesius græcum textum
collatis <span class="c13"><span
id="iii.ii.iii-p33.1"></span>mss.</span> Codicibus emendavit, Latine
vertit, et annotationibus illustravit. Adjecta est ad calcem disputatio
Archelai Episcopi adversus Manichæum. Parisiis, 1668. Fol. In this
edition there are the preface to the reader, explaining his sources; an
essay on the life and writings of the historians; and the text is
followed by annotations. This edition is conspicuous for the number of
codices, more or less accurately collated. The Codex Fuketanus is his
chief reliance; the previous annotations were used; he claims to have
made no alteration without warrant.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p34"></span>5. Biblioteca Maxima Veterum Patrum M.
de la Bigne, Lugd. 1677. In vol. vii. of this series; this reproduces
the Genevan edition.

Pamph. Ep. Cæsar. et Theodoreti, Evagrii, Socratis et Sozomeni Historia

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p54"></span><span id="page_227">p. 227</span> <span
id="iii.ii.iii-Page_227"></span>C. *Textual Criticism.*

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p55"></span>We have here (1) the apparatus
mentioned in the greater editions; (2) the marginal notes and papers of
various readings by Bishop Christophorson, Scaliger, Casaubon, Curicius,
Gurterius, etc. (3) The solitary work of Dr. Nolte, who, in 1860, wrote
a recension of Hussey’s edition of Sozomen. <span
id="iii.ii.iii-p55.1"></span>Theolog. Q. Schrift. 1861. iii. 417–451; as
he had done for Socrates, and did later for Evagrius. He dwells
especially on the valuable readings which could be derived from the
translation of Epiphanius Scholasticus and from Nicephorus Callistus.

<span id="iii.ii.iii-p56"></span>This shows the urgency of collation de
novo, and a new edition of the text.
