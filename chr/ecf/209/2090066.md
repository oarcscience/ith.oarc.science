<span id="iii.iv.ii.xv-p1"></span> <span class="c12"><span
id="iii.iv.ii.xv-p1.1"></span>Chapter XV</span>.—*Concerning Fear.*

<span id="iii.iv.ii.xv-p2"></span>Fear is divided into six varieties:
viz., shrinking <span id="fr_1824"></span>[1825](#fn_1824.md) , shame,
disgrace, consternation, panic, anxiety <span
id="fr_1825"></span>[1826](#fn_1825.md) . Shrinking <span
id="fr_1826"></span>[1827](#fn_1826.md) is fear of some act about to take
place. Shame is fear arising from the anticipation of blame: and this is
the highest form of the affection. Disgrace is fear springing from some
base act already done, and even for this form there is some hope of
salvation. Consternation is fear originating in some huge product of the
imagination. Panic is fear caused by some unusual product of the
imagination. Anxiety is fear of failure, that is, of misfortune: for
when we fear that our efforts will not meet with success, we suffer
anxiety.
