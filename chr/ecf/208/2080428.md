<span id="ix.ccclvi-p1"></span> <span class="c14"><span
id="ix.ccclvi-p1.1"></span>Letters</span> CCCLXI. and CCCLXIII., to
Apollinarius, and Letters CCCLXII. and CCCLXIV., from Apollinarius to
Basil, are condemned as indubitably spurious, not only on internal
evidence, but also on the ground of Basil’s asseveration that he had
never written but once to Apollinarius, and that “as layman to
layman.” <span id="fr_3292"></span>[3293](#fn_3292.md)   Letter CCCLXV.,
“to the great emperor Theodosius,” on an inundation in Cappadocia, is
also condemned by the Ben. Ed. as spurious, and contains nothing of
ecclesiastical or theological interest.  Tillemont however (vol. v., p.
739) thought its style not unworthy of a young man and a rhetorician,
and conjectures the Theodosius to whom it is addressed to be not the
great emperor, but some magistrate of Cappadocia.
