#### <span id="iv.iv.x.xii-p0.1"></span>Chapter XII.

<span id="iv.iv.x.xii-p1"></span>Of Prayer.

<span id="iv.iv.x.xii-p2"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="iv.iv.x.xii-p2.1"></span>Prayers</span> are those by which we offer
or vow something to God, what the Greeks call <span class="Greek"><span
id="iv.iv.x.xii-p2.2"></span>εὐκή</span>, i.e., a vow. For where we read
in Greek <span class="Greek"><span
id="iv.iv.x.xii-p2.3"></span>ἰ</span><span class="Greek"><span
id="iv.iv.x.xii-p2.4"></span>ὰ</span><span class="Greek"><span
id="iv.iv.x.xii-p2.5"></span>ς ἐυκάς μου τῶ κυρίῶ ἀποδώσω</span>, in
Latin we read: “I will pay my vows unto the Lord;” <span
id="fr_1593"></span>[1594](#fn_1593) where according to the exact force
of the words it may be thus represented: “I will pay my prayers unto the
Lord.” And this which we find in Ecclesiastes: “If thou vowest a vow
unto the Lord do not delay to pay it,” is written in Greek likewise:
<span class="Greek"><span id="iv.iv.x.xii-p3.2"></span>ἐ</span><span
class="Greek"><span id="iv.iv.x.xii-p3.3"></span>ά</span><span
class="Greek"><span id="iv.iv.x.xii-p3.4"></span>ν ἐύξῃ ἐυχὴν τῶ
κυρίῶ,</span> i.e., “If thou prayest a prayer unto the Lord, do not
delay to pay it,” <span id="fr_1594"></span>[1595](#fn_1594) which will
be fulfilled in this way by each one of us. We pray, when we renounce
this world and promise that being dead to all worldly actions and the
life of this world we will serve the Lord with full purpose of heart. We
pray when we promise that despising secular honours and scorning earthly
riches we will cleave to the Lord in all sorrow of heart and humility of
spirit. We pray when we promise that we will ever maintain the most
perfect purity of body and steadfast patience, or when we vow that we
will utterly root out of our heart the roots of anger or of sorrow that
worketh death. And if, enervated by sloth and returning to our former
sins we fail to do this we shall be guilty as regards our prayers and
vows, and these words will apply to us: “It is better not to vow, than
to vow and not to pay,” which can be rendered in accordance with the
Greek: “It is better for thee not to pray than to pray and not to
pay.” <span id="fr_1595"></span>[1596](#fn_1595)
