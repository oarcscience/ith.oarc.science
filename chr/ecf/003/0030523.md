<span id="v.vi.viii-p1"></span> <span class="c1"><span
id="v.vi.viii-p1.1"></span>Chapter VIII.—The Names and Descent of Other
Æons; First Half a Score, Then Two More, and Ultimately a Dozen Besides.
These Thirty Constitute the Pleroma. But Why Be So Capricious as to Stop
at Thirty?</span>

<span id="v.vi.viii-p2"></span>For, behold, when the second Tetrad—Sermo
and Vita, Homo and Ecclesia <span id="fr_6696"></span>[6697](#fn_6696.md)
—had borne fruit to the Father’s glory, having an intense desire of
themselves to present to the Father something similar of their own, they
bring other issue into being <span id="fr_6697"></span>[6698](#fn_6697.md)
—conjugal of course, as the others were <span
id="fr_6698"></span>[6699](#fn_6698.md) —by the union of the twofold
nature. On the one hand, Sermo and Vita pour out at a birth a half-score
of Æons; on the other hand, Homo and Ecclesia produce a couple more, so
furnishing an equipoise to their parents, since this pair with the other
ten make up just as many as they did themselves procreate. I now give
the names of the half-score whom I have mentioned:  Bythios (*Profound*)
and Mixis (*Mixture*), Ageratos (*Never old*) and Henosis (*Union*),
Autophyes (*Essential nature*) and Hedone (*Pleasure*), Acinetos
(*Immoveable*) and Syncrasis (*Commixture*,) Monogenes (*Only-begotten*)
and Macaria (*Happiness*). On the other hand, these will make up the
number twelve (to which I have also referred):  Paracletus (*Comforter*)
and Pistis (*Faith*), Patricas (*Paternal*) and Elpis (*Hope*), Metricos
(*Maternal*) and Agape (*Love*), Ainos (*Praise*) <span
id="fr_6699"></span>[6700](#fn_6699) and Synesis (*Intelligence*.md),
Ecclesiasticus (*Son of Ecclesia*) and Macariotes (*Blessedness*),
Theletus <span id="fr_6700"></span>[6701](#fn_6700) (*Perfect*.md) and
Sophia (*Wisdom*).  I cannot help <span
id="fr_6701"></span>[6702](#fn_6701.md) here quoting from a like example
what may serve to show the import of <span id="page_508">p. 508</span>
<span id="v.vi.viii-Page_508"></span>these names. In the schools of
Carthage there was once a certain Latin rhetorician, an excessively cool
fellow, <span id="fr_6702"></span>[6703](#fn_6702.md) whose name was
Phosphorus. He was personating a man of valour, and wound up <span
id="fr_6703"></span>[6704](#fn_6703.md) with saying, “I come to you,
excellent citizens, from battle, with victory for myself, with happiness
for you, full of honour, covered with glory, the favourite of fortune,
the greatest of men, decked with triumph.” And forthwith his scholars
begin to shout for the school of Phosphorus, <span class="Greek"><span
id="v.vi.viii-p10.1"></span>φεῦ</span> <span
id="fr_6704"></span>[6705](#fn_6704) (*ah!*.md).  Are you a believer
in <span id="fr_6705"></span>[6706](#fn_6705.md) Fortunata, and Hedone, and
Acinetus, and Theletus? Then shout out your <span class="Greek"><span
id="v.vi.viii-p12.1"></span>φεῦ</span> for the school of Ptolemy. <span
id="fr_6706"></span>[6707](#fn_6706.md) This must be that mystery of the
Pleroma, the fulness of the thirty-fold divinity. Let us see what
special attributes <span id="fr_6707"></span>[6708](#fn_6707.md) belong to
these numbers—four, and eight, and twelve. Meanwhile with the number
thirty all fecundity ceases. The generating force and power and desire
of the Æons is spent. <span id="fr_6708"></span>[6709](#fn_6708.md) As if
there were not still left some strong rennet for curdling numbers. <span
id="fr_6709"></span>[6710](#fn_6709.md) As if no other names were to be got
out of the page’s hall! <span id="fr_6710"></span>[6711](#fn_6710.md) For
why are there not sets of fifty and of a hundred procreated? Why, too,
are there no comrades and boon companions <span
id="fr_6711"></span>[6712](#fn_6711.md) named *for them*?
