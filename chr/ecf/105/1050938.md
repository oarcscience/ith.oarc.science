<span id="xix.iv.iv-p1"></span> <span class="c2"><span
id="xix.iv.iv-p1.1"></span>Chapter 4.—The Divine Commands Which are Most
Suited to the Will Itself Illustrate Its Freedom.</span>

<span id="xix.iv.iv-p2"></span>What is the import of the fact that in so
many <span id="page_445">p. 445</span> <span
id="xix.iv.iv-Page_445"></span>passages God requires all His
commandments to be kept and fulfilled? How does He make this
requisition, if there is no free will? What means “the happy man,” of
whom the Psalmist says that “his will has been the law of the
Lord”? <span id="fr_2958"></span>[2959](#fn_2958.md) Does he not clearly
enough show that a man by his own will takes his stand in the law of
God? Then again, there are so many commandments which in some way are
expressly adapted to the human will; for instance, there is, “Be not
overcome of evil,” <span id="fr_2959"></span>[2960](#fn_2959.md) and others
of similar import, such as, “Be not like a horse or a mule, which have
no understanding;” <span id="fr_2960"></span>[2961](#fn_2960.md) and,
“Reject not the counsels of thy mother;” <span
id="fr_2961"></span>[2962](#fn_2961.md) and, “Be not wise in thine own
conceit;” <span id="fr_2962"></span>[2963](#fn_2962.md) and, “Despise not
the chastening of the Lord;” <span id="fr_2963"></span>[2964](#fn_2963.md)
and, “Forget not my law;” <span id="fr_2964"></span>[2965](#fn_2964.md)
and, “Forbear not to do good to the poor;” <span
id="fr_2965"></span>[2966](#fn_2965.md) and, “Devise not evil against thy
friend;” <span id="fr_2966"></span>[2967](#fn_2966.md) and, “Give no heed
to a worthless woman;” <span id="fr_2967"></span>[2968](#fn_2967.md) and,
“He is not inclined to understand how to do good;” <span
id="fr_2968"></span>[2969](#fn_2968.md) and, “They refused to attend to my
counsel;” <span id="fr_2969"></span>[2970](#fn_2969.md) with numberless
other passages of the inspired Scriptures of the Old Testament. And what
do they all show us but the free choice of the human will? So, again, in
the evangelical and apostolic books of the New Testament what other
lesson is taught us? As when it is said, “Lay not up for yourselves
treasures upon earth;” <span id="fr_2970"></span>[2971](#fn_2970.md) and,
“Fear not them which kill the body;” <span
id="fr_2971"></span>[2972](#fn_2971.md) and, “If any man will come after
me, let him deny himself;” <span id="fr_2972"></span>[2973](#fn_2972.md)
and again, “Peace on earth to men of good will.” <span
id="fr_2973"></span>[2974](#fn_2973.md) So also that the Apostle Paul says:
“Let him do what he willeth; he sinneth not if he marry. Nevertheless,
he that standeth stedfast in his heart, having no necessity, but hath
power over his own will, and hath so decreed in his heart that he will
keep his virgin, doeth well.” <span id="fr_2974"></span>[2975](#fn_2974.md)
And so again, “If I do this willingly, I have a reward;” <span
id="fr_2975"></span>[2976](#fn_2975.md) while in another passage he says,
“Be ye sober and righteous, and sin not;” <span
id="fr_2976"></span>[2977](#fn_2976.md) and again, “As ye have a readiness
to will, so also let there be a prompt performance;” <span
id="fr_2977"></span>[2978](#fn_2977.md) then he remarks to Timothy about
the younger widows, “When they have begun to wax wanton against Christ,
they choose to marry.” So in another passage, “All that will to live
godly in Christ Jesus shall suffer persecution;” <span
id="fr_2978"></span>[2979](#fn_2978.md) while to Timothy himself he says,
“Neglect not the gift that is in thee.” <span
id="fr_2979"></span>[2980](#fn_2979.md) Then to Philemon he addresses this
explanation: “That thy benefit should not be as it were of necessity,
but of thine own will.” <span id="fr_2980"></span>[2981](#fn_2980.md)
Servants also he advises to obey their masters “with a good will.” <span
id="fr_2981"></span>[2982](#fn_2981.md) In strict accordance with this,
James says: “Do not err, my beloved brethren . . . and have not the
faith of our Lord Jesus Christ with respect to persons;” <span
id="fr_2982"></span>[2983](#fn_2982.md) and, “Do not speak evil one of
another.” <span id="fr_2983"></span>[2984](#fn_2983.md) So also John in his
Epistle writes, “Do not love the world,” <span
id="fr_2984"></span>[2985](#fn_2984.md) and other things of the same
import. Now wherever it is said, “Do not do this,” and “Do not do that,”
and wherever there is any requirement in the divine admonitions for the
work of the will to do anything, or to refrain from doing anything,
there is at once a sufficient proof of free will. No man, therefore,
when he sins, can in his heart blame God for it, but every man must
impute the fault to himself. Nor does it detract at all from a man’s own
will when he performs any act in accordance with God. Indeed, a work is
then to be pronounced a good one when a person does it willingly; then,
too, may the reward of a good work be hoped for from Him concerning whom
it is written, “He shall reward every man according to his works.” <span
id="fr_2985"></span>[2986](#fn_2985.md)
