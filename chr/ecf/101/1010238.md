<span id="vi.XII.V-p1"></span><span class="c2"><span
id="vi.XII.V-p1.1"></span>Chapter V.—What May Have Been the Form of
Matter.</span>

<span id="vi.XII.V-p2"></span>5. So that when herein thought seeketh
what the sense may arrive at, and saith to itself, “It is no
intelligible form, such as life or justice, because it is the matter of
bodies; nor perceptible by the senses, because in the invisible and
formless there is nothing which can be seen and felt;—while human
thought saith these things to itself, it may endeavour either to know it
by being ignorant, or by knowing it to be ignorant.
