<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p1"></span> <span id="page_99">p. 99</span>
<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-Page_99"></span><span class="c4"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p1.1"></span>Canon XVI.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p2"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p2.1"></span>If</span>, under any pretence of piety,
any children shall forsake their parents, particularly \[if the parents
are\] believers, and shall withhold becoming reverence from their
parents, on the plea that they honour piety more than them, let them be
anathema.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p3"></span><span class="c4"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p3.1"></span>Notes.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p4"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p4.1"></span>Ancient Epitome of Canon XVI.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p5"></span>If children leave their parents who
are of the faithful let them be anathema.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p6"></span>Zonaras notes that the use of the
word “particularly” shews that the obligation is universal.  The
commentators all refer here to [Matt.
15](../../../bib/kjv/mat015.htm#000.md)<span
id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p6.1"></span>, where our Lord speaks of the subterfuge
by which the Jews under pretext of piety defrauded their parents and
made the law of God of none effect.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p7"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p7.1"></span>Van Espen.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p8"></span>Of the last clause this is the
meaning; that according to the Eustathians “piety towards God” or
“divine worship,” or rather its pretence, should be preferred to the
honour and reverence due to parents.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xvi-p9"></span>This canon, in Isidore’s version, is
found in the *Corpus Juris Canonici*, Gratian’s *Decretum*, Pars I.,
Dist. xxx., c. i.  The Roman correctors advertize the reader that the
version of Dionysius Exiguus “is much nearer to the original Greek,
although not altogether so.”
