<span id="viii.v.iv.xii"></span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p1"></span> <span class="c4"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xii-p1.1"></span>Canon XII.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p2"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xii-p2.1"></span>If</span> any one, under pretence of
asceticism, should wear a *peribolæum* and, as if this gave him
righteousness, shall despise those who with piety wear the *berus* and
use other common and customary dress, let him be anathema.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p3"></span><span class="c4"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xii-p3.1"></span>Notes.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p4"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xii-p4.1"></span>Ancient Epitome of Canon XII.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p5"></span>Whoso despises those who wear
beruses, let him be anathema.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p6"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xii-p6.1"></span>Hefele.</span>

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p7"></span>The <span class="Greek"><span
id="viii.v.iv.xii-p7.1"></span>βήροι</span> (*lacernæ*) were the common
upper garments worn by men over the tunic; but the <span
class="Greek"><span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p7.2"></span>περιβόλαια</span>
were rough mantles worn by philosophers to show their contempt for all
luxury.  Socrates (*H. E.*, ii. 43) and the Synodal Letter of Gangra in
its third article say that Eustathius of Sebaste wore the philosopher’s
mantle.  But this canon in no way absolutely rejects a special dress for
monks, for it is not the distinctive dress but the proud and
superstitious over-estimation of its worth which the Synod here blames.

<span id="viii.v.iv.xii-p8"></span>This canon is found in the *Corpus
Juris Canonici*, Gratian’s *Decretum*, Pars I., Dist. xxx., c. xv.
