<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p1"></span> <span class="c4"><span
id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p1.1"></span>Canon XXIII.</span>

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p2"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p2.1"></span>The</span> readers and singers have
no right to wear an orarium, and to read or sing thus \[habited\].

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p3"></span><span class="c4"><span
id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p3.1"></span>Notes.</span>

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p4"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p4.1"></span>Ancient Epitome of Canon
XXIII.</span>

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p5"></span>Cantors and lectors shall not
wear the orarium.

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p6"></span><span class="sc"><span
id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p6.1"></span>Van Espen.</span>

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p7"></span>Rightly Zonoras here remarks,
“for the same reason (that they should not seem to wish to usurp a
ministry not their own) it <span id="page_144">p. 144</span> <span
id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-Page_144"></span>is not permitted to these to
wear the stole, for readers are for the work of reading, and singers for
singing,” so each one should perform his own office.

<span id="viii.vii.iii.xxvii-p8"></span>This canon is found in the
*Corpus Juris Canonici*, Gratian’s *Decretum*, Pars I., Dist. xxiii.,
can. xxviij.
