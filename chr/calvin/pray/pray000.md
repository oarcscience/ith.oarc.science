## <span id="i-p0.1"></span>INSTITUTES OF THE CHRISTIAN RELIGION

### <span id="i-p0.2"></span>John Calvin

# <span id="i-p0.3"></span>OF PRAYER

<span id="i-p1"></span>

### <span id="i-p1.2"></span>Translated by Henry Beveridge

### <span id="i-p1.3"></span>1845

### <span id="i-p1.4"></span>BOOK III.

### <span id="i-p1.5"></span>CHAPTER XX.

## <span id="i-p1.6"></span>OF PRAYER — A PERPETUAL EXERCISE OF FAITH. THE DAILY BENEFITS DERIVED FROM IT.

<span id="i-p2"></span>The principal divisions of this chapter are, —

<span id="i-p3"></span>I. Connection of the subject of prayer with the
previous chapters. The nature of prayer, and its necessity as a
Christian exercise, sec. 1, 2.

<span id="i-p4"></span>II. To whom prayer is to be offered. Refutation
of an objection which is too apt to present itself to the mind, sec. 3.

<span id="i-p5"></span>III. Rules to be observed in prayer, sec. 4–16.

<span id="i-p6"></span>IV. Through whom prayer is to be made, sec.
17–19.

<span id="i-p7"></span>V. Refutation of an error as to the doctrine of
our Mediator and Intercessor, with answers to the leading arguments
urged in support of the intercession of saints, sec. 20–27.

<span id="i-p8"></span>VI. The nature of prayer, and some of its
accidents, sec. 28–33.

<span id="i-p9"></span>VII. A perfect form of invocation, or an
exposition of the Lord's Prayer, sec. 34–50.

<span id="i-p10"></span>VIII. Some rules to be observed with regard to
prayer, as time, perseverance, the feeling of the mind, and the
assurance of faith, sec. 50–52.
