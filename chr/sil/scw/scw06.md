## *VI SELF-ABANDONMENT*

<span id="page_144">p. 144</span>

 

139 (I. 24)  
THOU MUST BE NAUGHT, WILL NAUGHT

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>If thou art Somewhat to thyself,<br />
If Somewhat thou dost love and will,<br />
If Somewhat knowest, Somewhat hast—<br />
Thou carriest thy Burden still.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

140 (I. 58)  
SELF-SEEKING

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>If seeking God thou seekest Rest,<br />
Then is thy reckoning out of trim:<br />
God's hireling art thou, not His child,<br />
Thou seekest thine own Self, not Him.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

141 (II. 57)  
MAN MUST GROW FREE FROM HIMSELF

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Grow free from self, from all created things grow free,<br />
Then God will graft His Godlike nature on to thee.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

142 (I. 143)  
'TIS SELFHOOD THAT DAMNS

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Could but the Devil quit his His-ness, thereupon<br />
The Devil thou wouldst see sitting upon God's throne<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<span id="page_145">p. 145</span>

 

143 (V. 32)  
SELFWILL RUINS ALL

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>If even in Christ himself there were Selfwill at all,<br />
Despite his Blessedness, believe me, he would fall.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

144 (I. 138)  
THE MORE THOU GOEST OUT, THE MORE GOD COMETH IN

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>The more thyself out of thyself<br />
Thou canst dischannel and outpour,<br />
The more must God flow into thee<br />
With all His Godhead more and more.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

145 (II. 140)  
SELF-NULLIFYING

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Naught bringeth thee beyond thyself<br />
So surely as Self-nullity:<br />
The more thou canst annul thyself<br />
The more thou hast of Deity.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

146 (II. 136)  
ABANDONMENT

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Go out—and so God goeth in;<br />
Die to thyself—thou hast begun<br />
To live to God; Be not—He is;<br />
Do naught—His bidding's done.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<span id="page_146">p. 146</span>

 

147 (V. 220)  
HOW GOD IS FOUND

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Seekest thou God, then must thou, Man,<br />
First lose thy Self-identity,<br />
Nor ever find again the trace<br />
Of Self in all eternity.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

148 (V. 186)  
OWNHOOD IS THE CAUSE OF ALL EVIL

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Communion engendereth Peace;<br />
 But Ownhood giveth rise<br />
To Persecution, War and Strife,<br />
 And all Calamities.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

149 (V. 238)  
"MINE" AND "THINE" ARE DAMNABLE

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Two words, and nothing else in all the world, combine<br />
To plunge thee into Hell—two hateful words, Mine, Thine.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

150 (II. 72)  
HE WHO CAN SING WITH THE ANGELS

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>He who can soar above himself<br />
 One instant at the most,<br />
He too can sing the Gloria<br />
 With the angeleic host.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<span id="page_147">p. 147</span>

 

151 (V. 33)  
WHEN GOD MOST DELIGHTS TO BE WITH US

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>God, whose extreme delight it is<br />
 To dwell with thee, doth come<br />
Most willingly into thy house<br />
 When thou art not at home.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

152 (I. 46)  
THE BLESSED UNTHING

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>I am a blessed Thing if I<br />
Can but unthing myself, forgo<br />
All my community with things,<br />
My cognizance of things unknow.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

153 (I. 92)  
HE WHO IS WHOLLY DEIFIED

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Who is as were he not, nor yet had come to be,<br />
He is become—O Happiness!—sheer Deity.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

154 (IV. 139)  
THE HAPPY DROWNING

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>If thou dost sail thy little ship<br />
Upon the Sea of Deity,<br />
It were indeed a happy chance<br />
Shouldst thou be drowned in that great Sea.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<span id="page_148">p. 148</span>

 

155 (II. 92)  
THE MYSTICAL ABANDONMENT

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Abandonment ensnareth God:<br />
But the Abandonment supreme,<br />
Which few there be can comprehend,<br />
Is to abandon even Him.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

156 (VI. 172)  
IN THE SEA NO DROP IS DISTINGUISHABLE

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>If thou canst designate a drop<br />
Lost in the Sea's immensity,<br />
Then wilt thou in the Sea of God<br />
Divine my soul's identity.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

157 (II. 25)  
THOU MAKEST THY OWN DISQUIET

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>Thee into thy disquiet nor God nor Creature brings,<br />
Thou dost disquiet thyself caring for many things.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

158 (III. 177)  
THE LONG MARTYRDOM

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>The Martyrs fare exceeding well:<br />
Swift passage through the mortal fires,<br />
Then God's embrace. But we lifelong<br />
Are martyred. How?—By our Desires.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<span id="page_149">p. 149</span>

WHO DRINKETH THE SPRING OF LIFE?

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">

</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

</table>

165 (I. 39)  
IMPERFECT ABANDONMENT

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">

Even in Hell itself and find in Hell no Hell.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

 

166 (VI. 170)  
TWO WAYS OF LOSING ONE'S SELF

<table data-align="center">
<colgroup>
<col style="width: 100%" />
</colgroup>
<tbody>
<tr class="odd">
<td>So I may yet be lost? O ay!<br />
If lost in death, loss limitless.<br />
But if thou lose thyself in God<br />
Thy loss is then all happiness.<br />
</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
